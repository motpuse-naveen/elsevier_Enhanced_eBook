Chapter 52: Thorax

overview and surface anatomy
The thorax is the upper part of the trunk. It consists of an external musculoskeletal cage, the thoracic wall and an internal cavity that contains the heart, lungs, oesophagus, trachea and main bronchi, thymus, vagus and phrenic nerves, sympathetic trunks and ganglia, thoracic duct, lymph nodes, and major systemic and pulmonary vasculature (Fig. 52.1). Superiorly, the thoracic cavity communicates with the neck and the upper limbs via the superior thoracic aperture. Inferiorly, it is separated from the abdominal cavity by the respiratory diaphragm. The thoracic cage offers protection to some of the superiorly located abdominal viscera: the majority of the liver lies inferior to the right dome (cupula) of the respiratory diaphragm; the stomach, spleen and left lobe of the liver lie inferior to the left dome of the diaphragm; and the posterior surfaces of the suprarenal glands and kidneys contact the anteroinferior surface of the lumbar part of the diaphragm.
The shape, dimensions and proportions of the thoracic cage vary between individuals, particularly with age and sex (Joshua et al 2014). In an adult, the dimensions of the thoracic cavity change continuously with the movements of the ribs and diaphragm during respiration (Ch. 55) and also with distension of the abdominal viscera and movement of the vertebral column. In a normal healthy adult, both the thoracic cage and abdominal wall move outwards on inspiration and inwards on expiration. Changes to this pattern, including paradoxical movements, may be seen in respiratory disease (Goldman 1982). Thoracic capacity is less in females than it is in males, both absolutely and proportionately; in the female, the sternum is normally shorter and the thoracic inlet more oblique.
In neonates and young infants, the thoracic cage has the shape of a truncated cone and is relatively round in cross-section, the ribs have an almost horizontal alignment, and the domes of the respiratory diaphragm and sternal ends of the clavicles sit in a more superior position than is the case in older children and adults (Openshaw et al 1984). Thoracic expansion is consequently limited and contributes little to breathing, which is reliant on the respiratory diaphragm. In a neonate, diaphragmatic contraction during inspiration pulls the thoracic cage inwards and moves the abdomen outwards, the thoraco-abdominal paradox. The neonatal thoracic wall is highly compliant and is easily deformed. By 2 years of age, as standing and walking begin, adult thoracic proportions and geometry have developed. These include downward sloping ribs, an oval cross-sectional shape to the thoracic cage, and a more inferior position of the domes of the respiratory diaphragm and the sternal ends of the clavicles (Openshaw et al 1984, Joshua et al 2014). This means that the thorax is able to move outwards during inspiration and the thoraco-abdominal paradox diminishes until it is no longer observed.

Musculoskeletal Framework

Bones and joints

The thoracic cage consists of 12 thoracic vertebrae and the intervening intervertebral discs, 12 pairs of ribs and their anteriorly located costal cartilages, and the sternum (Fig. 52.2). When articulated, they form an irregularly shaped osseocartilaginous frame (see Fig. 53.8). Laterally, the outer surface of the thoracic cage is convex and is formed by the ribs; anteriorly, it is slightly convex and is formed by the sternum and the distal parts of the ribs and their costal cartilages. The costal cartilages of the first seven pairs of ribs are connected to the sternum at the sternocostal joints, the costal cartilages of the eighth to tenth ribs usually join the inferior border of the superiorly located costal cartilage at the interchondral joints, and the eleventh and twelfth (sometimes the tenth) ribs and their cartilage are free (floating) at their anterior ends and project into the musculature of the abdominal wall. The posterolateral curvature of the ribs, from their head (vertebral end) to their angle (see Fig. 53.9), produces a deep internal groove, the paravertebral gutter, located on either side of the vertebral column. The ribs and costal cartilages are separated by intercostal spaces, which are deeper anteriorly and between the more superiorly located ribs. Each space is occupied by two or three layers of flat muscles and their aponeuroses (membranes), neurovascular bundles and lymphatic channels.
The superior thoracic aperture (thoracic inlet) typically measures 5 cm anteroposteriorly and 10 cm transversely (see Fig. 52.1). It is bounded by the body of the first thoracic vertebra posteriorly, the internal (medial) borders of the left and right first ribs, and the superior border (jugular notch) of the manubrium of the sternum anteriorly. It slopes anteriorly and inferiorly from the vertebral column, so that the apex of the lung is located in the inferior neck, above the level of the first rib and the medial one-third of the clavicle, and level with the body of the seventh cervical or first thoracic vertebra, or the intervening C7–T1 intervertebral disc; structures that pass between the thorax and the upper limb therefore pass over the first rib and the suprapleural membrane covering the pleurae and lung apex.
The inferior thoracic aperture (thoracic outlet) is bordered posteriorly by the body of the twelfth thoracic vertebra, posterolaterally by the twelfth rib and its costal cartilage and the distal end of the eleventh rib and its costal cartilage, anterolaterally by the cartilaginous ends of the seventh to tenth ribs (which unite and ascend as the costal arch or margin), and anteriorly by the xiphoid process of the sternum (see Fig. 52.2). In adults the dimension of the transverse plane of the aperture is larger than the sagittal plane, and the aperture slopes obliquely inferiorly and posteriorly towards the vertebral column, so that the thoracic cavity is deeper posteriorly than anteriorly.
The sternum is formed from three parts (manubrium, body and xiphoid process) that articulate at the manubriosternal and xiphisternal joints. The sternal plane passes horizontally through the manubriosternal joint (sternal angle, angle of Louis) at the level of the second costal cartilage anteriorly, intersecting the vertebral column posteriorly in a region that extends from the body of the fourth thoracic vertebra to the superior half of the body of the fifth thoracic vertebra (Mirjalili et al 2012a). The sternal plane separates the superior mediastinum from the inferior mediastinum and can be used as a reference plane to position intrathoracic mediastinal structures.

Muscles

Intrinsic and extrinsic muscles

The intrinsic muscles of the chest wall are the intercostal muscles, subcostalis, transversus thoracis, levatores costarum and serratus posterior superior and inferior (see Figs 46.71, 53.15). Transversus thoracis may mimic pleural thickening on CT imaging: the uncommon variants, sternalis and rectus thoracis bifurcalis, are important clinically because they may mimic a focal density in medial breast craniocaudal mammograms and may be encountered during reconstructive surgery of the breast and chest wall.
The intercostal muscles are arranged in consecutive layers in each intercostal space and are named according to their positional relationship to each other, i.e. external, internal and innermost (see Fig. 53.17). All the intrinsic muscles except levatores costarum are innervated segmentally by the intercostal nerves derived from the ventral rami of the thoracic nerves. Levatores costarum are innervated segmentally by lateral branches of the dorsal rami of the thoracic spinal nerves. The intrinsic muscles can elevate or depress a rib depending upon their location and are particularly active in forced (deep) breathing; some also help to stiffen the chest wall, thereby preventing paradoxical movement during inspiration and optimizing inspiratory/expiratory reserve volume.
The skeletal framework of the thoracic wall provides attachment sites for muscles associated functionally with the neck, back, upper limbs and abdomen. Some (e.g., scalenes, infrahyoid strap muscles, sternocleidomastoid, serratus anterior, pectoralis major and minor, external and internal abdominal obliques and rectus abdominis) may also function as accessory muscles of respiration; they are usually active only during forced breathing. Muscles with thoracic attachment are described in more detail in Chapters 35, 48 and 60.

Respiratory diaphragm

The respiratory diaphragm is a curved musculotendinous sheet attached to the circumference of the inferior thoracic aperture and the bodies of the superior two or three lumbar vertebrae. It forms the inferior boundary (floor) of the thoracic cavity and separates it from the abdominal cavity (Ch. 55). The diaphragm is relatively flat centrally, in the region inferior to the heart, and domed peripherally, rising higher on the right side than on the left, an asymmetry reflecting the differences in relative densities of the underlying abdominal viscera. From its most superior point on each side, the diaphragm slopes inferiorly to its peripheral costovertebral attachments. Posteriorly, the space between the diaphragm and the thoracic wall (costodiaphragmatic recess) is long and narrow. The position of the domes varies with body position; they are higher in the supine compared to the erect position. During quiet respiration in the erect position, diaphragmatic excursion is about 2 cm in both sexes, increasing to around 7 cm during deep breathing (Boussuges et al 2009); the range of excursion is often greater in men and may increase further whilst in the supine position (Takazakura et al 2004).
Diaphragmatic openings
The respiratory diaphragm is traversed by structures that pass between the thorax and abdomen, including the right and left vagus, phrenic, thoracic splanchnic and subcostal nerves, the thoracic duct, sympathetic trunks, right and left superior epigastric arteries (continuations of the internal thoracic arteries), and the azygos and hemiazygos veins. The inferior vena cava and oesophagus pass through the tendinous and muscular parts of the diaphragm, respectively (see Fig. 55.1). CT-based studies have shown the vertebral level at which they pass through the diaphragm is more inferior than reported conventionally.

Thoracic Cavity

Fascia and spaces

The endothoracic fascia (parietal fascia of the thorax) is a mixture of tissue types including connective, adipose, lymphoid and smooth muscle. It lines the inner surface of the thoracic wall and the superior surface of the respiratory diaphragm, separating them from the respective region (part) of the parietal pleura. It is attached posteriorly to the periosteum of the thoracic vertebrae and becomes continuous with the thoracic prevertebral fascia; superiorly it forms the suprapleural membrane; inferiorly it is continuous with the endo-abdominal fascia via apertures in the respiratory diaphragm, e.g., the aortic hiatus (Dugan and Samson 1975).
More recent descriptions define the endothoracic fascia as the outermost connective tissue layer within the extrapleural space, a space situated between the parietal pleura and the inner surface of the thoracic wall and superior (thoracic) surface of the respiratory diaphragm (Santamarina et al 2017). The extrapleural space also contains a layer of adipose tissue in contact with the parietal pleura, loose connective tissue, vessels and lymphoid tissue. A range of pathologies may occur in this tissue plane, including liposarcoma, leiomyosarcoma, fibrosarcoma and lymphoma (Morelli et al 2007). An increased accumulation of extrapleural fat may be confused for pleural thickening on CT imaging (Im et al 1989).
The superficial subcutaneous fascia of the thorax, formed mainly by fat, is attached loosely to the skin and includes the breast tissue. Deeper fascial layers include those surrounding the muscles of the thoracic wall: some layers are dense and supportive, e.g. the clavipectoral fascia (Ch. 49) and the thoracolumbar fascia (Ch. 46).
The extrathoracic fascia is located posteriorly between the muscles that attach the scapula to the posterior thoracic wall and the underlying posterior thoracic wall (Latarjet and Juttin 1953). The extrafascial space is superficial to the extrathoracic fascia and is relatively sealed, whereas the subfascial space deep to the extrathoracic fascia communicates freely with the cervical region superiorly and with the lumbar region inferiorly.

Pleural cavities

The right and left pleural cavities are separate compartments within the thoracic cavity, located on either side of the mediastinum. Each surrounds a lung and its associated bronchial tree, vessels, nerves and lymphatics (Ch. 54). The walls are formed by a serous membrane, the pleura, arranged as a closed sac. Each cavity represents a potential space between the parietal and visceral pleura, two serous membranous layers that are continuous on the medial aspect of the lung root and pulmonary ligament at the pleural reflection. The outermost layer, the parietal pleura, lines the inner surface of the corresponding half of the thoracic wall, the superior surface of the ipsilateral hemidiaphragm and structures along the ipsilateral lateral side of the mediastinum. The parietal pleura covers part of the lung root, close to the hilum, then folds internally on itself to become continuous with the innermost layer, the visceral pleura, which adheres to the outer surface of the lobes of the lung, following the interlobar fissures. The two layers of pleura are in close contact throughout all phases of breathing, yet are able to slide freely over each other due to the presence of an intervening thin layer of serous fluid. The pleural cavity is maintained normally at a negative pressure by the inward elastic recoil of the lung and the outward pull of the thoracic wall. The lungs do not occupy the full extent of the pleural cavity during quiet breathing, but are free to move into recesses such as the costodiaphragmatic and costomediastinal recesses, during deep breathing.

Mediastinum

The mediastinum is a visceral compartment of the thoracic cavity, located within and adjacent to the median sagittal plane, between the right and left pleural cavities (see (Figs 52.1, Fig 56.1): the mediastinal part of the parietal pleura forms its lateral boundaries. The mediastinum extends from the sternum anteriorly to the thoracic vertebral column posteriorly, and from the superior thoracic aperture superiorly to the respiratory diaphragm inferiorly. The sternal plane (which passes horizontally through the manubriosternal joint at the level of the second costal cartilage anteriorly, intersecting the vertebral column posteriorly in a region that extends from the body of the fourth thoracic vertebra to the superior half of the body of the fifth thoracic vertebra) is used to divide the mediastinum into superior and inferior parts. The sternal plane is sometimes defined as a fixed plane that passes from the manubriosternal joint to the most inferior part of the body of the fourth thoracic vertebra. Regional mediastinal content is reviewed in Ch. 56.

Superior mediastinum

The superior mediastinum lies between the manubrium of the sternum anteriorly and the anterior longitudinal ligament covering the bodies of the first four or five thoracic vertebrae and their intervening intervertebral discs posteriorly (depending upon the level at which the sternal plane intersects the vertebral column). It contains the lower ends of sternohyoid, sternothyroid and longus colli on each side; the aortic arch, brachiocephalic trunk and thoracic portions of the left common carotid and left subclavian arteries; the brachiocephalic veins and upper half of the superior vena cava; the supreme and superior intercostal veins; the vagus, cardiac, phrenic and left recurrent laryngeal nerves; and the trachea, oesophagus, thoracic duct, thymic remnants and lymph nodes (e.g. upper paratracheal, para-aortic, subaortic and prevascular nodes).

Inferior mediastinum

The inferior mediastinum, between the sternal plane and diaphragm, is divided into three parts: anterior, middle and posterior.

Anterior mediastinum

The anterior mediastinum is primarily located on the left side where the point of reflection between the left costal and mediastinal part of the parietal pleura diverges from the median plane. It is bounded anteriorly by the sternum, laterally by the left and right mediastinal parietal pleura and posteriorly by the fibrous pericardium. It is approximately triangular in shape, being wider inferiorly, and may be absent above the level of the fourth costal cartilage if the left and right parietal pleural layers meet. Its anterior wall is formed mainly by the left transversus thoracis and the fifth to seventh left costal cartilages and intervening intercostal muscles. The anterior mediastinum contains loose areolar tissue, lymphatic vessels from the liver, thymic remnants (or fatty replacement), parasternal lymph nodes and mediastinal branches of the internal thoracic artery.

Middle mediastinum

The middle mediastinum is the broadest part of the mediastinum. It contains the heart within the pericardial coverings and cavity; the ascending aorta; the inferior part of the superior vena cava with the azygos vein joining it; the tracheal bifurcation and right and left main bronchi; the pulmonary trunk dividing into the left and right pulmonary arteries; the right and left pulmonary veins (superior and inferior) and phrenic nerves; and hilar, subcarinal and lower paratracheal (tracheobronchial) lymph nodes.

Posterior mediastinum

The posterior mediastinum is a relatively long and narrow space. It is bounded anteriorly by the fibrous pericardium, pulmonary vessels, tracheal bifurcation, left and right main bronchi and the posterior (or posterosuperior) surface of the respiratory diaphragm; posteriorly, depending on the level of the sternal plane, by the anterior longitudinal ligament covering the vertebral column from the bodies of the fourth or the superior half of the body of the fifth to the twelfth thoracic vertebrae and intervening intervertebral discs; and laterally by the mediastinal part of the parietal pleura. It contains multiple structures passing to/from the abdominal cavity and posterior thoracic wall including the descending thoracic aorta; the oesophagus and the anterior and posterior vagal trunks; the azygos venous system; the thoracic duct and lymph nodes (e.g., paraoesophageal and retrotracheal); the right and left sympathetic trunks and ganglia; and the left and right vagus nerves.

Vascular Supply and Lymphatic Drainage

Arteries

The skin of the thorax is supplied by direct cutaneous vessels and musculocutaneous perforators, which reach the skin primarily via the intercostal muscles, pectoralis major, latissimus dorsi and trapezius. The positions of the cutaneous vascular territories and their arterial supply guide the placement of free flap grafts for reconstructive surgery. Branches from the thoraco-acromial, lateral and superficial thoracic, internal thoracic, anterior and posterior intercostal, thoracodorsal, transverse cervical, dorsal scapular and circumflex scapular arteries are the major contributing vessels to the skin of the thoracic wall (see Figs 46.4, 53.1, 53.2).
The muscles of the thoracic wall receive their blood supply from the internal thoracic artery (either directly or via the musculophrenic artery), the supreme (highest) intercostal artery (from the costocervical trunk), the superior thoracic artery (from the axillary artery) and the descending thoracic aorta (via the posterior intercostal and subcostal arteries) (Fig. 52.3). The main anterior and posterior intercostal arteries pass around the superior aspect of each intercostal space, their anastomoses provide a potential route for collateral circulation in cases of aortic coarctation. Collateral branches of the intercostal arteries pass around the inferior aspect of each space. Additional contributions come from vessels that supply the proximal muscles of the upper limb, namely the suprascapular, superficial cervical, thoraco-acromial and subscapular arteries (see Fig. 46.4).
The detailed arterial supply of the thoracic viscera is described with the viscera.

Veins

The posterior intercostal veins and their collateral branches accompany their similarly named arterial counterparts in the superior and inferior aspect of each intercostal space (Fig. 52.4). The anterior intercostal veins are tributaries of the internal thoracic and musculophrenic veins; the internal thoracic veins drain into the ipsilateral brachiocephalic veins. The posterior intercostal veins drain directly or indirectly into the azygos vein on the right and the hemiazygos or accessory hemiazygos veins on the left. In some cases the veins from the upper two or three intercostal spaces on the left join and drain directly into the left brachiocephalic vein. The azygos venous network shows great variation in its formation, origin, course, tributaries, anastomoses and termination (Loukas and Tubbs 2016) (see Figs 56.2–56.3).

Lymphatic drainage

Superficial lymphatic vessels of the thoracic wall ramify subcutaneously and converge on the axillary nodes (see Figs 46.5, 53.22), similarly lymphatic vessels from the breast tissue drain mainly to axillary nodes, with some passing initially to parasternal, interpectoral and supraclavicular nodes (see Fig. 53.24). The extent of drainage of the breast is of particular importance in metastatic breast cancer. Lymph vessels from deeper tissues of the thoracic walls drain mainly to the parasternal, intercostal and diaphragmatic nodes. Lymph from the thoracic viscera drains to consecutive node groups (stations) situated throughout the mediastinum (see Fig. 54.12).

Innervation

Thoracic spinal nerves

There are 12 pairs of thoracic spinal nerves. The ventral rami, unlike their cervical and lumbar counterparts, have retained a largely segmental distribution to the body wall (see Figs 24.10, 47.7, 53.3). The first to the eleventh intercostal nerves pass in the intercostal spaces between adjacent ribs, and the twelfth (subcostal nerve) passes inferior to the twelfth rib. Each is connected with an adjacent ganglion of the sympathetic trunk by pre- and postganglionic axons that form the white and grey rami communicantes, respectively (see Figs 24.13, 53.21). The majority of the intercostal nerves are distributed to the thoracic and, in the case of the more inferiorly located nerves, abdominal walls. The greater part of the first thoracic ventral ramus passes into the brachial plexus (see Fig. 48.6), together with variable proportions of the second and sometimes third thoracic ventral rami via the intercostobrachial nerves. The fourth to sixth intercostal nerves supply only the thoracic wall, whereas the seventh to eleventh nerves supply the thoracic and abdominal walls. The subcostal nerve is distributed to the abdominal wall and skin covering the superior part of the lateral aspect of the gluteal region and thigh. Communicating branches may link the intercostal nerves across the intercostal spaces posteriorly (see Fig. 56.6). The dorsal rami of the thoracic spinal nerves divide into medial and lateral branches that supply the intrinsic muscles of the back and the overlying postvertebral skin on a segmental basis (see Figs 46.3, 47.8).
The breast is mainly innervated by the anterior and lateral cutaneous branches of the fourth to sixth intercostal nerves. The nipple is innervated by a plexus formed primarily from cutaneous branches of the fourth intercostal nerve.
The supraclavicular branches of the cervical plexus (derived from the ventral ramus of the fourth cervical spinal nerve) innervate the skin over the clavicle and may communicate with the anterior cutaneous branches of the second thoracic ventral ramus.

Regional anaesthesia

Regional anaesthesia may be used to alleviate the pain associated with rib fracture, thoracostomy, thoracotomy or breast surgery. Nerve blocks performed in the thoracic paravertebral space target the ventral rami of the thoracic spinal nerves (or the posterior intercostal nerves) as they travel through the fat-filled space (Bouman et al 2017). Various fascial plane blocks are used to target segmental and cutaneous branches of regional nerves as they pass through specific fascial planes or compartments (Elsharkawy et al 2018), for example, serratus anterior plane (Biswas et al 2018), pectoralis and serratus anterior plane (Pecs1 or Pecs2) and erector spinae plane blocks (Forero et al 2017). Anaesthetic agents are able to track through the fascial lined space or compartment into which they are injected: effective anaesthesia of multiple nerves can therefore be achieved via a single point of access.

Autonomic innervation

The autonomic nervous system in the thorax consists of the left and right sympathetic trunks and left and right vagus nerves, and the cardiac, oesophageal and pulmonary plexuses (see Figs 56.6, 56.7).

Sympathetic trunks

The left and right thoracic sympathetic trunks and associated ganglia (commonly 11 in number) sit posterior to the costal part of the parietal pleura on either side of the vertebral column. The majority of the thoracic sympathetic ganglia lie anterior to the head of a rib; the first thoracic and inferior cervical ganglia are fused, and the inferior two or three thoracic ganglia are often located lateral to their correspondingly numbered vertebral bodies (see Fig. 56.6). A tumour of the superior pulmonary sulcus (apex of lung) may compress the ventral ramus of the first thoracic spinal nerve, the cervicothoracic ganglion or the sympathetic trunk (see Fig. 54.2) resulting in a loss of sympathetic innervation to the ipsilateral side of the head (Horner’s syndrome) (Nikolaos et al 2014) and pain radiating to the medial aspect of the ipsilateral forearm (Bruzzi et al 2008).
Preganglionic sympathetic axons originate from neuronal somata in the intermediolateral nucleus (lateral horn) of the spinal cord from T1 to L2. They travel initially with the corresponding ventral roots (rami) and leave the latter as white rami communicantes to enter the adjacent sympathetic ganglion in the sympathetic trunk. Their targets vary; some synapse in the sympathetic ganglion that they enter, whereas others ascend or descend in the sympathetic trunk to synapse in cervical or lumbar ganglia, respectively. Many of the preganglionic axons that originate in the lower thoracic spinal segments (T5–T12) do not synapse locally but enter the abdominal cavity as the thoracic splanchnic nerves. These nerves pass through the posterior mediastinum and respiratory diaphragm to enter the abdominal cavity where they synapse either in prevertebral ganglia that surround the origin of the major visceral arteries arising from the abdominal aorta, e.g. the coeliac and superior mesenteric ganglia (Ch. 59), or around the medullary chromaffin cells of the suprarenal gland (Ch. 70). Axons destined for the cervical ganglia are mainly derived from preganglionic neurones in the first thoracic spinal nerve.

Vagus nerves in the thorax

Preganglionic parasympathetic axons arise from neuronal cell bodies in the dorsal motor nucleus of the vagus in the medulla oblongata. Those destined for the thoracic viscera travel in the pulmonary, cardiac and oesophageal branches of the vagus nerve (see Fig. 56.7) and synapse in minute ganglia in the walls of the target viscera. Axons travelling in cardiac branches join the cardiac plexuses and synapse in ganglia distributed over both atria, the interatrial septum and peri-nodal subepicardial tissue: when stimulated, they normally slow the cardiac cycle and constrict coronary arteries, although low level stimulation may increase heart rate (Ch. 57). Pulmonary branches contain axons that synapse in ganglia of the pulmonary plexuses; they are motor (broncho constrictor) to the circular non-striated muscle fibres of the bronchi and bronchioles, and secretomotor to the mucous glands of the respiratory epithelium.

Autonomic plexuses in the thorax

Cardiac plexus

The cardiac plexus has a superficial component inferior to the aortic arch, lying between the arch and the pulmonary trunk, and a deep part between the aortic arch and tracheal bifurcation (Ch. 57, see Fig. 57.61).
The plexus has coronary, pulmonary, atrial and aortic extensions. The superficial (ventral) part is formed by the cardiac branch of the left superior cervical sympathetic ganglion and the inferior of the two cervical cardiac branches of the left vagus nerve. The deep (dorsal) part is formed by the cardiac branches of the cervical and upper thoracic sympathetic ganglia and of the vagus and recurrent laryngeal nerves. The only cardiac nerves that do not join it are those that join the superficial part of the plexus. Branches from the cardiac plexuses subsequently form the left and right coronary and atrial plexuses.

Pulmonary plexus

The pulmonary plexuses are located both anterior and posterior to the hila of the lungs (Ch. 54 and see Fig. 56.7), and connect with the cardiac plexuses (Ch. 57).
The posterior plexus provides the majority of the autonomic innervation to the lung and is formed by approximately 12 (range, 8–18) branches from the vagus nerve (Weijs et al 2015) and cardiac branches from the second to fifth (or sixth) thoracic sympathetic ganglia. The smaller anterior plexus is formed by 3 (range, 2–4) branches of the vagus nerve and cervical sympathetic cardiac nerves via the superficial and deep parts of the cardiac plexus. Branches of the recurrent laryngeal nerves form a plexus on the membranous posterior wall of the trachea; the plexus primarily innervates the trachea and proximal parts of the main bronchi, with branches to the oesophagus. Vagal nerve innervation is involved in the cough reflex, mucous production and control of bronchial diameter; inadvertent transection of the pulmonary vagal nerves during oesophagectomy may be related to the high incidence of postoperative pneumonia and pulmonary complications following oesophagectomy (Weijs et al 2017); preservation of vagal pulmonary branches is therefore desirable (Weijs et al 2016).

Oesophageal plexus

The oesophageal plexus surrounds the oesophagus below the level of the hila of the lungs (Ch. 56, see Fig. 56.6).
Preganglionic parasympathetic axons from the vagus and recurrent laryngeal nerves travel to ganglia in the oesophageal wall, either directly or through the oesophageal plexus and are motor to the smooth muscle in the lower oesophagus and secretomotor to mucous glands in the oesophageal mucosa. Vasomotor sympathetic axons arise from the first to sixth (sometimes fewer) thoracic segments of the spinal cord. Those passing from the more superior cord segments often synapse in the middle and inferior cervical sympathetic ganglia; their postganglionic axons are vasomotor to the vessels of the cervical and superior part of the thoracic oesophagus. Preganglionic axons from the fifth or sixth thoracic spinal cord segments pass directly to the oesophageal plexus or to the coeliac ganglion, where they synapse; postganglionic axons innervate the vessels of the distal oesophagus.

Surface Anatomy

Thoracic surface anatomy underpins proficient physical examination and clinical procedures such as thoracostomy, placing drains or catheters and siting surgical incisions. Previous cadaver-based descriptions of surface landmarks are now being revised based upon CT imaging of living subjects. These studies are demonstrating a wider range of variation in positional and surface anatomy than appears in the current anatomical literature (Hale et al 2010, Mirjalili et al 2012a, Keough et al 2016, Pak et al 2016, Shen et al 2016, Uzun et al 2016, Badshah et al 2017).

Skeletal landmarks

Anteriorly, the clavicle, sternoclavicular and acromioclavicular joints are palpable and visible in most individuals (Fig. 52.5). The sternum may be felt throughout its length in the midline; laterally it may be covered by pectoralis major. The jugular (suprasternal) notch is palpable in the midline at the superior border of the manubrium of the sternum and commonly sits horizontally level with the vertebral bodies of T2 or T3. The cartilaginous rings and lateral borders of the trachea are palpable immediately superior to the notch. The sternal angle (of Louis) is more pronounced in males than in females, and is palpable at the junction of the manubrium with the body of the sternum. It marks the level of the horizontal sternal plane and the medial ends of the second costal cartilages, and so offers a reference point at which to start counting ribs. In the majority of adults the sternal plane intersects the vertebral column in a region that extends from the vertebral body of T4 to the superior half of the vertebral body of T5. The xiphisternal joint and xiphoid process are palpable at the inferior end of the sternum; the joint usually lies at the level of the vertebral body of T9, although it may be slightly higher. The costal margin (arch) is palpable passing inferolaterally from either side of the xiphoid process of the sternum. Posteriorly, the free ends of the eleventh and twelfth ribs may be palpable. Tracing the twelfth rib superomedially aids identification of the spinous process of T12 (Fig. 52.6). In thin individuals, it is possible to palpate all ribs anteriorly, from the first to the costal margin, and laterally, from the fourth rib at the floor of the axilla to the costal margin. Posteriorly, the spinous processes of the thoracic vertebrae are palpable in the midline; the spinous process of T1 sits below that of C7 (vertebra prominens) and is often more prominent. The prominent and distinct angles of the ribs are palpable several centimetres lateral to the spinous processes of the vertebrae. The surface anatomy of the back is described in Chapter 46.

Musculotendinous landmarks

In a lean, muscular subject, pectoralis major, the digitations of serratus anterior, latissimus dorsi, trapezius, external abdominal oblique and rectus abdominis are all visible and palpable. In females, the breasts may obscure part of the anterior thoracic wall.

Breast

In females the base of the breast is relatively constant in position; it extends vertically from the second or third rib to the sixth rib, and the associated costal cartilage, and horizontally from the lateral sternal border medially to around the mid-axillary line laterally. The tail of the breast extends towards the axilla along the inferolateral border of pectoralis major (anterior axillary fold) (see Figs 53.22–53.23). In adult females, the location of the nipple is dependent on the size and shape of the breasts: the pigmented areola surrounding the nipple also varies in size. In adult males the nipple is often located either in the fourth intercostal space or over the fifth rib in the mid-clavicular line, approximately 20 cm from the jugular notch and 18 cm from the mid-clavicular point (Beckenstein et al 1996). The position of the nipple should never be relied upon for accurate landmarking.

Intrathoracic viscera

The sternal plane represents the inferior border of the superior mediastinum. It is typically described as marking the position of the tracheal bifurcation, the concavity of the aortic arch and the point where the azygos vein joins the superior vena cava. CT-based studies show these major structures are more commonly located below the sternal plane, from the level of the vertebral body of T5 to the vertebral body of T6 (Mirjalili et al 2012a) (Fig. 52.7; Tables 52.1–52.2): sometimes the aortic arch may be located in or above the sternal plane (Uzun et al 2016, Badshah et al 2017). The pulmonary trunk bifurcation is commonly located about 2–3 cm inferior to the level of the sternal plane, around the level of the vertebral body of T6 (Keough et al 2016, Pak et al 2016, Shen et al 2016, Uzun et al 2016); it may be located more often at a higher level in females.

Heart

The surface projection of the cardiac borders on to the anterior thoracic wall, between four landmark points, forms a trapezoid. The area of cardiac dullness may be mapped out via percussion. The description below applies to an average adult with a normal heart; this may be modified by age, sex, stature, ventilation, the position of the diaphragm, posture and pathology.
The superior cardiac border is marked by a curved line joining the second left costal cartilage to the third right costal cartilage (each point about 1 cm lateral to the sternum) (see Fig. 52.5), the latter point representing the junction between the superior vena cava and right atrium. In some study populations this junction is located more commonly at the right third costal cartilage or third intercostal space level (Keough et al 2016, Pak et al 2016), whereas in others it is located at the fourth to the fifth right costal cartilage level (Mirjalili et al 2012a, Shen et al 2016). The right border is represented by a curved line joining the point marking the junction of the superior vena cava and right atrium with the sixth right costal cartilage, usually 1–2 cm lateral to the sternal edge. The inferior cardiac border runs leftwards from the sixth right costal cartilage to the cardiac apex, located approximately 8–9 cm lateral to the midline, commonly in the left fifth intercostal space or level with the fifth or sixth ribs (Mirjalili et al 2012a, Keough et al 2016, Pak et al 2016, Shen et al 2016), close to the mid-clavicular line. Clinicians vary considerably when locating the mid-clavicular line (Naylor et al 1987); a measurement from the midline is therefore preferred. The cardiac apex beat is usually palpable near the cardiac apex and represents the most inferolateral point at which cardiac pulsation is visible and palpable.
The left cardiac border gently curves superomedially from the cardiac apex to meet the second left costal cartilage approximately 1 cm from the left sternal edge. An oblique line joining the sternal end of the third left and sixth right costal cartilages represents the anterior part of the coronary (atrioventricular) sulcus, which separates the right atrium from the right ventricle. The projections of the cardiac valves, although in different planes, are sited along or close to this line (see Fig. 57.19). The orifice of the pulmonary valve is represented by an approximately horizontal 2.5 cm long line that lies over the superior border of the left third costal cartilage and the corresponding part of the left side of the sternum. The pulmonary trunk is delineated by two parallel lines drawn perpendicular to the ends of the pulmonary valve line, commonly up to the level of the left second intercostal space. The orifice of the aortic valve is located inferior and to the right of the orifice of the pulmonary valve, and is represented by a line approximately 2.5 cm long running inferolaterally and to the right from the medial end of the left third intercostal space. Two parallel lines, drawn perpendicular to the ends of the aortic valve line and slanting superolaterally towards the right side of the manubriosternal joint, delineate the location of the ascending aorta.
The orifice of the tricuspid valve is represented by a line approximately 4 cm long that starts near the midline just inferior to the level of the right fourth costal cartilage and passes down and slightly to the right. The centre of this line is level with the middle of the right fourth intercostal space. The orifice of the mitral valve is level with the left half of the sternum opposite the left fourth costal cartilage and is represented by a line approximately 3 cm long and descending to the right.
The direction of blood flow through a valve carries the valve sound with it, consequently the sites of cardiac valve auscultation do not correspond to the surface anatomy of the valves. Common sites at which to auscultate using a stethoscope are: the sternal end of the left second intercostal space (pulmonary area); the sternal end of the right second intercostal space (aortic area); near the cardiac apex in the fifth intercostal space (mitral area); and adjacent to the left lower sternal border, at the level of the fifth intercostal space (tricuspid area) (see Fig. 57.19).

Great vessels

Starting at the aortic valve (see above), the ascending aorta curves anteriorly, superiorly and to the right, and becomes the aortic arch posterior to the right half of the manubrium or the superior part of the right side of the sternal body, at the level of the right second costal cartilage or second intercostal space, respectively. The aortic arch lies mainly posterior to the manubrium of the sternum (see Fig. 52.7). The concavity of the arch is located commonly up to 1 cm inferior to the sternal plane (range, 2.7 cm superior to 4.8 cm inferior), approximately level with the superior half of the vertebral body of T5 (Mirjalili et al 2012a, Shen et al 2016), although it may be located in or superior to the sternal plane (Uzun et al 2016, Badshah et al 2017) (see Tables 52.1, 52.2). It then arches to the left within the superior mediastinum and descends such that the distal part of the arch protrudes just to the left of the manubrium around the level of the first intercostal space, where it is visible on thoracic X-ray (aortic knuckle). The brachiocephalic trunk arises approximately posterior to the centre point (or just to its left) of the manubrium and ascends toward the right sternoclavicular joint. The left and right brachiocephalic veins are commonly formed posterior to the sternoclavicular joints (Mirjalili et al 2012a); the right brachiocephalic vein descends almost vertically, whereas the left brachiocephalic vein passes inferiorly and to the right, posterior to the manubrium and across the midline. The position of formation of the brachiocephalic veins varies between study populations; for example, the right brachiocephalic vein may form lateral to the sternoclavicular joint (Shen et al 2016) or both brachiocephalic veins may form superolateral to the sternoclavicular joints (Keough et al 2016). According to conventional descriptions, the superior vena cava forms posterior to the right first costal cartilage. The majority of in vivo CT-based studies show it forms posterior to the right second costal cartilage or first intercostal space and the associated part of the manubrium (Mirjalili et al 2012a, Pak et al 2016, Shen et al 2016). The level of formation may be higher in females, children and younger adults, and in some adult study populations (Keough et al 2016). The position of the junction of the superior vena cava and right atrium is described above with the heart. The azygos vein joins the superior vena cava up to 2 cm inferior to the sternal plane at the level of the inferior part of the vertebral body of T5 (Mirjalili et al 2012a, Keough et al 2016, Shen et al 2016).

Lungs and pleural reflections

Anteriorly, the apex of the lung sits posterior, and up to 2 cm superior, to the medial one-third of the clavicle; posteriorly, it lies level with the vertebral body of C7 or T1, or the C7–T1 intervertebral disc (see Fig. 52.5). The surface markings of both the apex, costal surface and vertebral part of the lung correspond to those of the parietal pleura. Conventionally, the surface projection of the lower border of the lung is represented by a curved line that crosses the mid-clavicular line at the sixth rib, the mid-axillary line at the eighth rib, and is just lateral to the vertebral column at the tenth rib. CT data show this border, at the end of tidal inspiration, is located at the fifth rib or the fifth intercostal space on the left, or the sixth intercostal space or the seventh rib on the right, in the mid-clavicular line, and adjacent to the vertebral body of T12 posteriorly (range: vertebral bodies of T9–L1) (Mirjalili et al 2012a, Pak et al 2016, Shen et al 2016). In full expiration, the lower margin of the lung may rise 5 cm above the line of reflection of the parietal pleural within the costodiaphragmatic recess and may fully leave the retrosternal costomediastinal recess.
Trachea and bronchi
The trachea originates at the level of the inferior border of the cricoid cartilage, which usually sits at the level of the vertebral body of C7 (range, vertebral bodies of C5–T1) (Mirjalili et al 2012b). It passes inferiorly in the midline and bifurcates a mean of 1.4–3.1 cm inferior to the sternal plane, often level with the vertebral bodies of T5–T6 (Mirjalili et al 2012a, Shen et al 2016, Uzun et al 2016, Badshah et al 2017). The tracheal bifurcation may be more superior in location in females (Keough et al 2016). The trachea is palpable immediately superior to the jugular notch either by rolling one finger over its anterior surface or by defining its lateral borders with two fingers. A shift to one side indicates that the mediastinum has either been pulled over to that side by loss of lung volume, or pushed over by a pneumothorax or large pleural effusion. The level at which the trachea bifurcates appears to move inferiorly, on average, by a mean of 3 mm per decade.
Pulmonary fissures
The superior and inferior lobes of the left and right lung are normally separated by the oblique fissure; this is marked by a line that curves around the thoracic wall, from the posterior part (neck) of the fourth rib (level with the spinous process of T3) on the left side and the posterior part of the fifth rib (level with the spinous process of T4) on the right side, to the sixth rib in the mid-axillary line on both sides (Heřmanová et al 2012) (see Figs 52.5–52.6, 54.3). Continuing along the same trajectory of descent, the line marking the oblique fissure passes towards the inferior border of the lung near to the mid-clavicular line (i.e. 7–8 cm lateral to the anterior median plane), where it terminates. The lowest margin of the lung in the mid-clavicular line is located at the sixth intercostal space or the seventh rib on the right or at the fifth rib or the fifth intercostal space on the left (Mirjalili et al 2012a). As a convenient approximation, posteriorly the oblique fissure follows the medial border of the scapula when the upper limb is in full abduction. The left oblique fissure may be slightly more vertical than the right. The surface position of the pulmonary fissures show little change during inspiration and expiration (Heřmanová et al 2012).
The horizontal fissure lies between the superior and middle lobes of the right lung (see Figs 52.5, 54.3; Fig. 52.8 ). It extends from the right fourth costal cartilage at the right sternal border to intersect the oblique fissure, approximately in its middle one third. The right middle lobe may be projected on to the thoracic wall using three points: the fourth costal cartilage at the right sternal border (sternocostal joint), the fifth rib in the mid-axillary line and the sixth intercostal space or seventh rib in the mid-clavicular line. The superior lobe, and middle lobe on the right, lie anterior to the oblique fissure and are best examined from the anterior and lateral wall of the thorax. The inferior lobes lie posteriorly and should be examined via the posterior thoracic wall, below the level of the oblique fissure. The arrangement of the pulmonary fissures can vary widely (Wantanabe 2016).
Pleural margin and reflections
Starting in the midline at the sternal angle, the anterior line of reflection of the parietal pleura may be traced superiorly along a curved line that diverges from the midline and passes superiorly and laterally to the apex of the lung (see Fig. 52.5). The line lies 3–4 cm above the anterior end of the first rib; the surface marking is posterior, and up to 2 cm superior, to the medial one third (sternal end) of the clavicle. The right and left parietal pleurae are in contact retrosternally in the midline from the level of the second to the fourth costal cartilages, at which point they often diverge (see Fig. 53.4). Above this level and posterior to the manubrium the diverging lines of pleural reflection border a triangular shaped region which is occupied by the thymus, or its remnants. The pleura on the right descends almost vertically, often remaining near the midline, to the xiphoid process, whereas the pleura on the left deviates laterally by 3–5 cm and then passes inferolaterally to cross the anterior end of the sixth rib. This deviation produces an area, roughly triangular in shape (the pericardial triangle), between the heart and the sternum that is free of pleura; a needle puncture of the pericardium may be performed at this site and on the left lateral sternal edge with minimal risk of damaging the pleura.
The reflection of the parietal pleura (costal and diaphragmatic parts), forming the inferior limit of the costodiaphragmatic recess, may be followed around the chest wall along a line sitting just superior to the costal attachment of the respiratory diaphragm (see Fig. 52.1); passing from the sixth costal cartilage (sternocostal joint) lateral to the sternum, to the eighth rib in the mid-clavicular line, the tenth rib in the mid-axillary line and the twelfth rib, or just below, adjacent to the vertebral column. Viewed from posteriorly, the medial edges of the pleurae pass along a line joining the transverse processes of T12 to T2 on either side. The diaphragmatic part of the parietal pleura is fused to fascia on the superior (thoracic) surface of the respiratory diaphragm, with which it can be landmarked.

Respiratory diaphragm

The highest point of the dome of the respiratory diaphragm is commonly located level with the fifth intercostal space or the sixth rib on the right and the sixth rib or the sixth intercostal space on the left: these levels vary widely, from the fourth rib to the seventh intercostal space, and in some cases may even sit inferior to the costal margin (Mirjalili et al 2012a, Shen et al 2016). Conventionally, the inferior vena cava, oesophagus and descending thoracic aorta are said to traverse the respiratory diaphragm at the levels of the vertebral bodies of T8, T10 and T12, respectively. However, CT-based studies have shown that at end tidal inspiration, the inferior vena cava, oesophagus and aorta more commonly pass through the respiratory diaphragm at the levels of the vertebral bodies of T11 (sometimes T10) and T12, respectively (Mirjalili et al 2012a, Pak et al 2016, Shen et al 2016, Badshah et al 2017).

Paediatric thoracic surface anatomy

CT-based assessment of paediatric thoracic surface anatomy in subjects from birth to 12 years of age has shown that some structures are similar in position to those seen in adults, whereas others change position with increasing age (Tarr et al 2016, Fischer et al 2017).
The sternal angle in the majority of children from birth to 12 years of age is located level with the vertebral body of T4 (range, inferior half of the vertebral body of T1 to the inferior half of the vertebral body of T5), and the sternal plane marks the level at which the azygos vein joins the superior vena cava. Relative to the sternal plane, the concavity of the aortic arch is located approximately 0–5 mm inferior and the bifurcation of the trachea and pulmonary artery are located approximately 10–15 mm inferior. The cardiac apex is located close to the mid-clavicular line from birth to 12 years of age; specifically, it is located approximately 4 cm (0–1 year of age), 5 cm (1–4 years of age) and 6 cm (4–12 years of age) to the left of the midline. The cardiac apex is typically located posterior to the fifth intercostal space (range, fourth intercostal space to the sixth rib) from birth to 4 years of age and posterior to the fourth intercostal space or the fifth rib from 4 to 12 years of age. The highest (most superior) part of the dome of the respiratory diaphragm is commonly located at the level of the fifth rib or the fifth intercostal space on the right and the sixth rib or the fifth intercostal space on the left; the left dome is slightly lower than the right, and both domes are flatter in children compared to adults. On average, the respiratory diaphragm is traversed by the inferior vena cava, oesophagus and descending thoracic aorta at the levels of the vertebral bodies of T9, T10 and T11, respectively, although some variability occurs. The apex of the lung is commonly situated posterior to the medial one third of the clavicle across all age groups, although it is sometimes located more laterally, i.e. posterior to the middle one third of the clavicle, in younger children.
The right and left brachiocephalic veins are formed posterior to the ipsilateral sternal head of the clavicle in the majority of children from birth to 11 years of age (Tarr et al 2016). The vertebral level of the formation of the superior vena cava and the junction between the superior vena cava and right atrium are variable in position (Fischer et al 2017). The superior vena cava forms most commonly posterior to the second right costal cartilage in children from 0–3 years of age, and moves superiorly to sit posterior to the right first costal cartilage or right first intercostal space during subsequent growth. The junction between the superior vena cava and right atrium is commonly located posterior to the right fourth costal cartilage in children from birth to 3 years of age, moving superiorly to sit posterior to the right third costal cartilage or right third intercostal space during subsequent growth in childhood. In adults the junction is commonly located posterior to the right fourth costal cartilage.
