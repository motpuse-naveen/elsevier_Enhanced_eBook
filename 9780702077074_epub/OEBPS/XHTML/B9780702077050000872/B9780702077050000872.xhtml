Commentary 1.6: A critical evaluation of the current status of myofascial chains

Jan Wilke, and Winfried Banzer

Introduction

From a morphological point of view, most anatomy textbooks have described the skeletal muscles of the human body as being discrete actuators with clear origins and insertions (van der Wal 2009). Recent analyses of published anatomical cadaveric studies have challenged this assumption, revealing that the active components of the locomotor system are directly linked by fibrous connective tissue (Wilke et al 2016c) (Fig. 1.6.1). The bridging structures include: ligaments (hamstrings – sacrotuberous ligament – erector spinae muscle) (Martin 1968, Vleeming et al 1995); fasciae1 (gastrocnemius muscle – hamstrings) (Tuncay et al 2009); aponeuroses (gluteus maximus/tensor fasciae latae – iliotibial tract – calf muscles) (Wilke et al 2016b); tendons (gastrocnemius muscle – Achilles tendon – plantar aponeurosis – toe abductors) (Snow et al 1995, Stecco et al 2013).
A common feature of the listed elements is their considerable tensile strength, conferred by a large quantity of collagen type I fibres. Moreover, comparisons between samples of the fascia lata and different tendons located around the knee joint revealed that these structures share similar biomechanical, biochemical and histological properties (Butler et al 1984, Derwin et al 2008). Therefore, in addition to the classical distinctive terminology, it has been suggested that the entirety of soft collagenous connective tissues should be grouped under the umbrella terms ‘fascial tissues/fascial system’ when describing the body from a holistic and functional point of view (Adstrum et al 2017).

Myofascial Chains

Concepts of myofascial chains assume that the connectivity of muscles and stretch-resistant soft tissue creates systemic lines of pull with the potential to transmit mechanical force to distant body regions. The selection of the components of a myofascial chain is based on the criterion of continuity between their longitudinal axes (Fig. 1.6.2): it is assumed that this construction maximizes the amount of transmittable force. Another relevant factor is the common fibre orientation of neighbouring myofascial chain components. In tendons, the collagen fibrils are oriented parallel to each other, almost directly following the muscle’s line of pull. A similar architecture with mostly parallel fibres running in a longitudinal direction can be found in ligaments. Although the investing fasciae often display a bidirectional, lattice-like fibre arrangement, biomechanical studies reveal a markedly higher longitudinal stiffness (Eng et al 2014), which might indicate a more pronounced strain transmission capacity in this direction.
Based on these thoughts, a variety of concepts have suggested body maps with hypothesized myofascial chains (Fig. 1.6.3) (for an overview, see Richter (2012)). Only three continuous lines, the posterior longitudinal chain, the posterior diagonal chain and the anterior diagonal chain, have been systematically verified so far (Fig. 1.6.4A, Table 1.6.1) (Wilke et al 2016c). Two other possible chains, the lateral longitudinal chain and the spiral chain, still exhibit some unresolved inconsistencies but are at least partially backed by scientific evidence (Fig. 1.6.4B, Table 1.6.2).

Limitations and perspectives

Myofascial continuity is not restricted to in-series coupling as at first glance might be implied by chain concepts. Direct morphological linkages have also been clearly documented between muscles lying parallel to each other, e.g. synergists or antagonists (Yucesoy 2010). Myofascial continuity therefore represents a global feature of body architecture, rather than a distinct hallmark of serially arranged skeletal muscles. The factors determining and influencing the amount of structural continuity are a matter of debate. While the plantar aponeurosis – also called plantar fascia or plantar ligament by other authors – fuses strongly with the Achilles tendon in young individuals, no (or only a few) bridging fibres can be detected in older persons (Snow et al 1995, Stecco et al 2013). Besides age, physical activity and gender represent other potential predictors of the degree of myofascial force transmission, but as yet have not been investigated.

Myofascial Force Transmission

The existence of myofascial continuity does not necessarily imply mechanical significance. Clinical relevance depends on whether or not substantial amounts of force can be transferred across the connections. In fact, although considerable inter-individual variation exists, results of biomechanical cadaveric studies suggest notable mutual interactions between serially arranged muscles and/or their connective tissue, respectively (Krause et al 2016). In the low back region, this appears to be of particular relevance: the lumbar fascia represents the mechanical hub of the posterior diagonal myofascial chain and can receive tension from both latissimus dorsi and the contralateral gluteus maximus muscle (Vleeming et al 1995, Barker et al 2004). In the posterior longitudinal chain, the lumbar fascia and erector spinae may be affected by the hamstring muscles via connectivity with the sacrotuberous ligament (Vleeming et al 1989a, 1989b, 1995). Further interactions for this chain have been demonstrated concerning the more caudal transitions between (1) the hamstrings and gastrocnemius (Cruz-Montecinos et al 2015) and (2) gastrocnemius and the plantar aponeurosis (Carlson et al 2000, Erdemir et al 2004). A mechanical force transmission has been observed between rectus abdominis and adductor longus in the anterior diagonal myofascial chain (Norton-Old et al 2013).

Limitations and perspectives

Myofascial continuity includes both parallel and serial linkages of skeletal muscles. There is evidence of mechanical force transmission in the course of myofascial chains. Numerous studies suggest strong interactions between several lower leg muscles (Wilke et al 2018). For instance, length changes of gastrocnemius at fixed ankle position are associated with substantial strains in soleus and the fibular muscles (Yaman et al 2013), indicative of a mutual interaction between these muscles. It is yet to be elucidated if in-series force transmission across myofascial chains exceeds that of structures located in parallel (Fig. 1.6.5).

Remote Exercise Effects and Non-Local Symptom Manifestations

A few studies have attempted to translate the findings of cadaveric trials into in vivo conditions. Initial evidence suggests that exercise protocols based on the concept of myofascial chains lead to non-local effects: stretching of the gastrocnemius and hamstring muscles, both of which represent a part of the longitudinal posterior chain, increases the cervical range of motion (ROM) (Wilke et al 2016a, Wilke et al 2016d). Similarly, self-massage of the plantar aponeurosis enhances hamstring extensibility (Grieve et al 2015, Wilke et al 2019).

Limitations and perspectives

In most cases, non-local exercise effects have been examined using functional outcomes (e.g. ROM of neighbouring joints), which are paramount in order to estimate clinical significance. However, there is a caveat: although likely, without simultaneous imaging it cannot be concluded without doubt that a passive myofascial force transmission caused the remote effects. Alternative explanations include cortical adaptations (e.g. reduced systemic stretch tolerance) or a mechanical force transfer via peripheral neurovascular bundles that also cross multiple joints (Ellis et al 2012).

The Role of Myofascial Chains in Musculoskeletal Disorders

Besides optimizing motor function, e.g. through enhanced ROM, force transmission across myofascial chains might contribute to musculoskeletal diseases. In some disorders, remote functional or tissue-related abnormalities can be found in addition to the typical local symptoms. Patients with sacroiliac and chronic low back pain may display hyperactivity of latissimus dorsi and the contralateral gluteus maximus, which represent the major muscular parts of the diagonal posterior chain (Mooney et al 2001, Kim et al 2014). Furthermore, although the relationship between the viscoelastic properties of the posterior thigh muscles and back pain remains controversial (Halbertsma et al 2001), individuals with chronic lumbar pain have been reported to display decreased extensibility and increased stiffness of the hamstrings (Marshall et al 2009), a muscle group belonging to the longitudinal posterior chain. The latter chain might also be of importance in plantar fasciitis, a condition in which increased stiffness of the hamstrings and gastrocnemius has been observed (Labovitz et al 2011, Bolívar et al 2013).

Limitations and perspectives

Although cross-sectional studies indicate several non-local symptom manifestations in the course of myofascial chains, little knowledge exists regarding the success of corresponding interventions. Randomized, controlled trials of treatments of musculoskeletal disorders based on the concept of myofascial chains are therefore warranted.

Conclusion

The classical conception of skeletal muscles as independent actuators requires a thorough revision. Fascial tissue links the skeletal muscles of the body and acts as a mechanical force transmitter. Although it is not known (1) to what degree cortical adaptations play a role in the genesis of non-local exercise effects under in vivo conditions; and (2) whether the extent of mutual interaction between serially connected muscles exceeds that of muscles arranged parallel to each other, it is reasonable to suggest that clinical focus should not be restricted to one joint or soft tissue.
