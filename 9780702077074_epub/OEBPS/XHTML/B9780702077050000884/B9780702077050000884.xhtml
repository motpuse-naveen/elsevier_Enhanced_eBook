Commentary 2.1: Head–trunk interface in the vertebrate embryo

Shigeru Kuratani
The vertebrate body is characterized by the possession of an overt head. However, the definition of ‘head’ in this context is enigmatic because the boundary between the head and trunk is not always self-evident. The head is usually defined as the rostral region of the vertebrate body where the brain, various sensory organs and a well-developed oral apparatus are located, but this definition can vary according to morphological context (reviewed by Kuratani 2019).
In the vertebrate embryo, the head is composed of various germ-layer derivatives. Head-specific mesodermal components include the head mesoderm in the paraxial domain, and the pharyngeal arch mesoderm in the ventrolateral domain of the pharyngula. The endoderm develops into the pharynx and the segmental pharyngeal pouches, and further head-specific ectodermal components include ectodermal placodes and populations of cranial neural crest cells (Fig. 2.1.1A). These head-specific components are distinct from those of the trunk, such as the unsegmented lateral plate, pharyngeal endoderm, segmented somites and trunk neural crest (TNC; see Fig. 2.1.1A).
During early embryogenesis, cranial neural crest cells migrate under the epidermis to form the ectomesenchyme, which comprises three distinct neural crest cell populations (these are, from rostral to caudal: trigeminal, hyoid and circumpharyngeal).
Dorsal migration of cranial neural crest cells is permitted by the absence of somites, which also allows the cranial neural crest cells to adhere selectively to rhombomeres 2, 4 and 6 and to the posterior region of the hindbrain, thus prefiguring the morphology of the trigeminal (V), facial (VII), glossopharyngeal (IX) and vagus (X) cranial nerves (i.e., the branchiomeric nerves that innervate pharyngeal arches). The morphological pattern of these nerves is different from that of the spinal nerves in the trunk, the precursors of which are segmented by somites.
Ventrally, the cranial neural crest cells migrate into the pharyngeal pouches, where they come into contact with the epibranchial placodes (primordia of the inferior ganglia of the branchiomeric nerves) (Fig. 2.1.1B). The cranial neural crest cells also migrate ventrally to fill the pharyngeal arches and craniofacial region, thereby establishing the (pseudo-)segmental organization (branchiomeres and rhombomeres) of the branchiomeric cranial nerves, pharyngeal arches and hindbrain.
The posterior boundary of the head mesoderm (at roughly the position of the otic vesicle) does not line up with the caudal-most pharyngeal arch, and so the posterior boundary of the cranial neural crest cell distribution forms an S-shape (see Fig. 2.1.1). This boundary was first recognized by Froriep (1885), who interpreted it as representing the interface between the somatic and visceral parts of the body (see Figs 17.7, 18.12). Developmentally, the interface coincides with the first few somites and lateral plates (Kuratani 1997). In other words, there is a domain in the vertebrate pharyngula (a stage in embryonic development after head and tail folding with a specified body plan, about stage 11 in human embryos, see Chapter 12) where somites and pharyngeal arches coexist at the same rostrocaudal level. This means that the somites in this domain – the suprapharyngeal somites (see Fig. 2.1.1A) – develop in a unique environment, surrounded both by cells that are unique to the head and by cells that are unique to the trunk.
During the evolutionary process, the shoulder girdle developed as the posterior-most part of the dermal shield of the head of jawless vertebrates; it arises during embryogenesis along the S-shaped head–trunk interface. In jawed vertebrates, the shoulder girdle began to move away from the head, eventually resulting in the formation of an aperture (Trinajstic et al 2013), around which several unique structures that also develop along the head–trunk interface – including the hypobranchial muscles (including the muscles of the tongue and geniohyoid and infrahyoid), vagus, hypoglossal (occipitospinal) and accessory nerves and cucullaris (trapezius and sternocleidomastoid) muscles – then form and elongate to produce the neck (Fig. 2.1.1C). Some somite-derived muscles are also referred to as ‘neck muscles’ because their connective tissues are, in part, derived from cranial neural crest cells, unlike most other postcranial skeletal muscles, the connective tissues of which are mesodermal in origin. The developmental and morphological characteristics of the cucullaris muscles remain to be elucidated (reviewed by Tada and Kuratani 2015).
The neck is particularly conspicuous in amniotes, whose hearts are shifted posteriorly (Hirasawa et al 2016). In this context, the neck can be defined as the domain between the head and the shoulder girdle, where, during embryogenesis, the posterior-most part of the ectomesenchyme contributes to organogenesis, including that of the connective tissues of the neck muscles (Noden 1983, Matsuoka et al 2005, Nagashima et al 2016; reviewed by Hirasawa and Kuratani 2015). It is important to note that the neck has partially lost its trunk-like nature, not only with respect to the distribution of cranial neural crest cells, but also because of the disappearance of the coelom, as seen in the head. Furthermore, the neck muscles are not considered to be hypaxial skeletal muscles because they are not located in the lateral body wall (see Fig. 2.1.1C).
The head–trunk interface is not present in non-vertebrate chordates such as amphioxi and tunicates. In cyclostomes (modern jawless vertebrates), lamprey and hagfish embryos both show an S-shaped interface between the prospective head and trunk, but the hagfish embryo shows an additional caudal shift of the posterior half of the pharynx. The hagfish hypoglossal nerve violates the head–trunk interface and has lost the typical shape of this nerve in vertebrates: this peculiar morphology is assumed to be a derived condition (Oisi et al 2015, Kuratani et al 2019). Despite these differences, the cyclostome hypobranchial muscles are coextensive with the pharynx, thereby being a prototype of the human neck. Thus, the head–trunk interface appears to have been already obtained by the latest common vertebrate ancestor. This interface can be seen as a synapomorphy that defines vertebrates as a unique group among chordates with a clearly differentiated head and trunk (Irie et al 2018).
