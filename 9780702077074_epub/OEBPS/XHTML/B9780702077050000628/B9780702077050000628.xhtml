Chapter 62: Peritoneum, mesentery and peritoneal cavity

The peritoneum is the largest serous membrane in the body. In males the peritoneum forms a closed sac, but in females it is open at the abdominal ostia of the uterine tubes. Its smooth appearance is unremarkable, but its conformation varies. Directly beneath the single layer of mesothelium is a well-developed basement membrane, outside which is a rich lymphatic plexus. Microscopic mesothelial pores or peritoneal stomata are distributed throughout the peritoneum but particularly on the undersurface of the respiratory diaphragm and anterior abdominal wall (Wassilev et al 1998). At the peritoneal stomata the mesothelium is close to underlying lymphatic endothelial cells and there is no intervening basement membrane. Both peritoneal stomata and sites of macrophage and lymphoid aggregates (‘milky spots’) in the omenta contribute to peritoneal fluid absorption, and their occlusion by malignant cells can result in ascites (diZerega and Rodgers 1992, Hagiwara et al 1994). Neighbouring mesothelial cells are joined by junctional complexes but permit macrophages to pass. Some regions of the submesothelial connective tissue also contain macrophages, lymphocytes and adipocytes.
The normal pressure within the peritoneal cavity is about 2–10 mmHg (higher in pregnant and obese individuals) (Sanchez et al 2001). It can be measured directly using a catheter inserted into the abdomen, or indirectly by monitoring the pressure in the urinary bladder or stomach. The pressure inside the abdomen can increase after trauma as a result of the accumulation of blood, fluid or oedema, leading to ‘abdominal compartment syndrome’. In such patients, monitoring of intra-abdominal pressure can identify major increases (above about 25 mmHg) that could jeopardize blood flow to vital organs and dictate the need for urgent decompression.

Peritoneal Fluid

The peritoneal cavity lies between the parietal peritoneum, which lines the abdominal walls, the visceral peritoneum (covering abdominal viscera and mesentery) and the peritoneal reflection. It contains a small amount of peritoneal fluid, rarely more than 5 ml in healthy males and postmenopausal females (Yoshikawa et al 2013). Up to 25 ml of fluid can be present in healthy young females, depending on the phase of the menstrual cycle (Koninckx et al 1980). The fluid allows the viscera covered with peritoneum, i.e. intermesenteric, to glide freely within the peritoneal cavity. Normal peritoneal fluid contains water, proteins (less than 30 g per litre), electrolytes and solutes derived from interstitial fluid in the adjacent tissues and from plasma in local capillaries. It also contains a few cells, including desquamated mesothelium, nomadic peritoneal macrophages, mast cells, fibroblasts, lymphocytes and other leukocytes. Macrophages migrate freely between the peritoneal cavity and the surrounding connective tissue. In females, blood or fluid can enter the peritoneal cavity from a ruptured ovarian follicle or from the retrograde flow of menstrual fluid along the uterine tubes (which can cause endometriosis). Under normal circumstances, the peritoneal cavity contains no air.
The peritoneum and its fluid have important defensive properties (Autio 1964). Peritoneal fluid flows in a predominantly clockwise direction around the peritoneal cavity and gravitates to dependent sites within the cavity: diaphragmatic respiratory movements, negative intrathoracic pressure and intestinal peristalsis encourage flow from the pelvis to the subphrenic regions, even when the individual is erect (Meyers et al 1987, Meyers 1994). Fluid preferentially ascends from the inferior abdomen and pelvis to the right subhepatic and subphrenic spaces via the right paracolic gutter (deeper than the left paracolic gutter in the supine position and there is no phrenicocolic ligament or reflection to obstruct flow) (Meyers 1973). Passage across the midline to the left subphrenic space is prevented by the falciform ligament. Malignant cells can become trapped within peritoneal recesses or in the milky spots of the greater omentum, where they proliferate and produce visible and palpable tumour deposits. These sites must be deliberately examined during searches for peritoneal metastases (Zonca et al 2008). Gross infiltration of the omentum by cancer is often referred to as an ‘omental cake’. The movement of peritoneal fluid explains the presence of disease at sites such as the undersurface of the right half of the respiratory diaphragm and the pelvic pouches. An example is Fitz–Hugh–Curtis syndrome, in which organisms causing gonorrhoea or chlamydia organisms enter the peritoneal cavity through the uterine tubes and are transported in the peritoneal fluid to the right upper quadrant, where they cause perihepatic inflammation. Disruption of the integrity of the peritoneum by percutaneous puncture, laparoscopy or open surgery profoundly affects its defensive function and can create ‘sticky’ sites where pathogens or malignant cells can settle and proliferate.
Rarely there are direct communications (pleuroperitoneal fenestrae) between the peritoneal and pleural cavities (Simmons and Mir 1989, Pestieau et al 2000).

Peritoneal Attachments

The parietal peritoneum is attached to the muscular layers of the abdominal wall by extraperitoneal connective tissue. It detaches from the abdominal wall to continue as the visceral peritoneum of nearby viscera (including the mesentery). The region of detachment is referred to as the peritoneal reflection (Fig. 62.1A). The parietal peritoneum is only loosely attached around the urinary bladder and rectum so as to accommodate alterations in their size, whereas it adheres firmly to the inferior surface of the respiratory diaphragm and linea alba. The extraperitoneal tissue over the posterior abdominal wall frequently contains large amounts of fat, especially in obese males. Visceral peritoneum adheres firmly to the underlying viscera, often blends with connective tissue in the wall of the organ and rarely contains loose connective or adipose tissue.

General Arrangement of the Peritoneum

The gastrointestinal tract develops as a single tube suspended in the abdominal cavity by ventral and dorsal regions of the mesentery (Ch. 21). The mesentery is derived from the dorsal and ventral mesenteries. The rotation and subsequent attachment of parts of the gastrointestinal tract to the posterior abdominal wall create the impression that these structures are retroperitoneal. However, it is now acknowledged that the mesentery is continuous and that all regions of the intestine are centrally connected via the attached mesentery (Byrnes et al 2019a–2019d) (see Fig. 62.1A). The intestines can be divided into attached and non-attached regions. Attached regions include the duodenum, ascending and descending colons and the rectum. Non-attached regions include the stomach, small intestine, and the transverse and sigmoid colon.
The mesentery is continuous from the gastro-oesophageal to the anorectal junctions (Byrnes et al 2019a–2019d). All abdominal digestive organs are continuous with a region of the mesenteric frame. Some regions (the greater and lesser omenta) differ considerably in appearance from others, including the mesocolon and mesosigmoid (sigmoid mesocolon). Where the parietal peritoneum detaches from the abdominal wall as the peritoneal reflection, and extends to adjacent viscera such as the liver, it is often termed a ‘ligament’. The mesentery contains neurovascular bundles and lymphatic vessels that supply digestive system organs. In individuals with obesity, extensive adipose tissue within the mesentery can obscure neurovascular bundles. In contrast, in the very young, elderly or malnourished, the mesentery lacks adipose tissue and neurovascular bundles are more obvious within ‘mesenteric pedicles’ that are routinely used as anatomical landmarks to guide abdominal surgeons.
The surfaces of viscera attached to the mesentery contribute to the visceral component of the peritoneal landscape. The latter is often subdivided into potential ‘spaces’ where fluids such as blood or purulence can accumulate under pathological conditions.

Surgical planes are defined by anatomical structures

Abdominal surgeons rigorously follow an anatomical roadmap when conducting resections, in order to minimize blood loss and trauma to tissues. The map is created by the mesentery and its continuity with abdominal digestive organs, and the surrounding peritoneal reflection. The surgeon incises the peritoneum to access the plane between the mesentery and underlying fascia (Fig. 62.1B). When the intact mesentery has been detached from the fascia back to the midline, it can be disconnected by dividing major vascular trunks. These activities are repeated at all levels from the oesophagus to the rectum and are based on the anatomical foundation provided by the mesentery and associated peritoneum (Figs 62.2-62.4).

Peritoneum of the upper abdomen

The abdominal part of the oesophagus, stomach, liver and spleen all lie within a region that runs from the posterior to the anterior abdominal walls and is broadly termed the mesogastrium (Coakley and Hricak 1999). Its attachments are divisible into regions such as the falciform ligament, coronary ligament, lesser omentum (hepatogastric and hepatoduodenal ligaments), greater omentum (including the gastrocolic ligament), gastrosplenic ligament, splenorenal ligament and phrenicocolic ligament (see Fig. 62.1A; Fig. 62.5).

Falciform ligament

The falciform ligament is a thin anteroposterior double fold of peritoneum that represents an anterior portion of the mesentery connecting the liver to the posterior aspect of the anterior abdominal wall. It extends inferiorly to the level of the umbilicus and superiorly it narrows to a depth of 1–2 cm as the distance between the liver and anterior abdominal wall decreases. Its two peritoneal layers separate to enclose the liver, to which it adheres firmly. Superiorly, the two peritoneal layers are continuous with the parietal peritoneum on the undersurface of the respiratory diaphragm, from which they are reflected laterally to form the superior layer of the coronary ligament of the liver on the right and the left triangular ligament of the liver on the left. The inferior aspect of the falciform ligament forms a free border, where the two peritoneal layers are continuous with each other as they enclose the round ligament of the liver (ligamentum teres hepatis), a remnant of the umbilical vein. Because the peritoneum of the falciform ligament is continuous with that covering the posterior abdominal wall and with the parietal peritoneum of the anterior abdominal wall, blood from a retroperitoneal haemorrhage (commonly, acute haemorrhagic pancreatitis) can track beneath the peritoneum and appear as haemorrhagic discoloration around the umbilicus (Cullen’s sign). The blood-tinged fluid reaches the peri-umbilical region via the lesser omentum and the falciform ligament.

Peritoneal and mesenteric connections of the liver

The liver is almost completely covered in visceral peritoneum, and only the ‘bare area’ posteriorly is directly apposed to the right half of the respiratory diaphragm. Peritoneal ligaments (also referred to as peritoneal reflections) and regions of mesentery run from the liver to the surrounding viscera and to the abdominal wall and respiratory diaphragm (see Figs 62.1A, 62.2); they are described in detail in Chapter 66.
The coronary ligament is formed by the reflection of the peritoneum from the respiratory diaphragm onto the superior and posterior surfaces of the right lobe of the liver. Between the two layers of this ligament, a large triangular area, the bare area of the liver, is devoid of peritoneal covering. Here, the liver is connected to, but separated from, the respiratory diaphragm by a thin layer of areolar tissue. On the right, the two layers of the coronary ligament converge laterally to form the right triangular ligament. On the left, the two layers of peritoneal reflection fuse to form the left triangular ligament. The superior layer of the coronary ligament is reflected superiorly onto the undersurface of the diaphragm and inferiorly onto the right and superior surfaces of the liver. The inferior layer of the coronary ligament is reflected inferiorly from the posterior surface of the liver onto the posterior abdominal wall, where it is continuous with the parietal peritoneum overlying the right kidney, and continues to the superior (first) part of the duodenum and right colic flexure (see Fig. 62.3). Medially, it passes in front of a short segment of the inferior vena cava lying between the superior part of the duodenum inferiorly and the caudate process of the liver superiorly, forming the posterior wall of the omental foramen. It continues to the left as the peritoneum overlying the anterior surface of the dorsal mesogastrium, turns anteriorly and continues as the undersurface of the greater omentum.
The peritoneal recess between the inferior surface of the liver and the colic component of the right colic flexure is known as the hepatorenal recess (pouch of Morison) (Stringer 2009). In the supine position, this is the most dependent part of the peritoneal cavity in the upper abdomen and is a site toward which fluid or peritoneal metastases can gravitate.
The right triangular ligament is a short, V-shaped fold at the right lateral limit of the bare area of the liver where the two layers of the coronary ligament converge. As it is reflected onto the abdominal wall it is continuous with the parietal peritoneum of the right posterolateral abdominal wall. Surgical division of the right triangular and coronary ligaments allows the right lobe of the liver to be retracted anteriorly, exposing the lateral aspect of the inferior vena cava posterior to the liver.
The left triangular ligament is a double layer of peritoneum that extends for a variable distance over the superior border of the left lobe of the liver. Medially, the anterior leaf is continuous with the left layer (or left surface) of the falciform ligament, while the posterior leaf is continuous with the lesser omentum. The left triangular ligament lies anterior to the abdominal part of the oesophagus, the superior limits of the lesser omentum, and part of the fundus of the stomach. It is continuous with the parietal peritoneum lining the undersurface of the left side of the respiratory diaphragm, which is continuous with the parietal peritoneum lining the posterior abdominal wall. Division of the left triangular ligament allows the left lobe of the liver to be mobilized; the ligament is an important stabilizing factor for the left lobe after excision of the right lobe of the liver.

Lesser omentum

The lesser omentum is a region of mesentery derived from the ventral mesogastrium. Formed by two layers of peritoneum separated by a variable amount of connective tissue and fat, it runs between the inferior, visceral surface of the liver and the abdominal part of the oesophagus, stomach, pylorus and superior part of the duodenum. Superiorly, its attachment to the inferior surface of the liver forms an L shape. The vertical component of the L shape is formed by the fissure for the ligamentum venosum, and inferiorly, the attachment turns horizontally to complete the L in the porta hepatis. The part of the lesser omentum running between the liver and the stomach is the hepatogastric ligament and the part between the liver and duodenum is the hepatoduodenal ligament. The right and left gastric vessels, branches of the vagal trunks and lymph nodes are contained within the two layers of the hepatogastric ligament close to its attachment to the stomach. An accessory or replaced left hepatic artery is sometimes present. The anterior layer of the lesser omentum descends from the fissure for the ligamentum venosum on to the anterior surface of the abdominal part of the oesophagus, stomach and duodenum. The posterior layer descends from the fissure and runs on to the posterior surface and pylorus of the stomach: it forms part of the anterior surface of the omental bursa (see Fig. 62.4). At the lesser curvature of the stomach, the peritoneal layers of the lesser omentum become continuous with the visceral peritoneum covering the anterior and posterior surfaces of the stomach.
The free right lateral border of the lesser omentum (i.e. the hepatoduodenal ligament) is thickened to form a mesenteric pedicle that extends from the porta hepatis to the junction between the superior and descending (second) parts of the duodenum. It forms the anterior boundary of the omental foramen and contains the hepatic portal vein (posteriorly), the bile duct (anteriorly to the right) and proper hepatic artery (anteriorly to the left) (see Figs 62.3, 62.5B), together with lymphatic vessels, lymph nodes and hepatic autonomic nerves. An accessory or replaced right hepatic artery sometimes arises from the superior mesenteric artery in the free edge of the lesser omentum, usually running posterior to the hepatic portal vein. Often the free margin of the lesser omentum extends to the right as the cystoduodenal ligament that contains the gallbladder, cystic artery and duct and lymph nodes (Ashaolu et al 2011). This is of considerable clinical significance: this mesenteric appendage must be visualized in order to remove the gallbladder safely (i.e. cholecystectomy). The hepatoduodenal ligament continues distally posterior to the superior part of the duodenum and merges with the mesentery of the duodenum.
The superior border of the lesser omentum is short and runs over the inferior surface of the respiratory diaphragm between the liver and the medial aspect of the abdominal part of the oesophagus. The lesser omentum is thinner in this region and contains a variable amount of connective tissue, especially fat. It has a translucent appearance where it is thinnest, a feature that guides surgeons in identifying regions that can be divided safely.

Greater omentum

The greater omentum is the largest peritoneal fold and hangs inferiorly from the greater curvature of the stomach. When the undisturbed abdomen is opened, the greater omentum is frequently draped over the superior abdominal organs, but often not in the even distribution shown in many illustrations. It is usually thin but always contains some adipose tissue and is a common site for storage of fat in obese individuals, particularly males (Coulier 2009). The greater omentum is a double sheet: each sheet consists of two layers of peritoneum separated by a small amount of connective tissue. The two sheets are folded back on themselves and adhere firmly to each other inferior to the transverse colon. Above this level the anterior sheet separates from the posterior sheet and is attached to the greater curvature of the stomach (see Fig. 62.4); this supracolic part of the greater omentum is known as the gastrocolic ligament. The most anterior layer of peritoneum of the greater omentum is continuous with the visceral peritoneum over the anterior surface of the stomach and duodenum. The anterior sheet descends from the greater curvature of the stomach and superior part of the duodenum for a variable distance into the peritoneal cavity and then turns sharply backwards on itself to ascend as the posterior sheet. The posterior sheet is extremely thin where it attaches to the transverse colon and superior surface of the transverse mesocolon. It extends cephalad, attached to (but not fused with) the superior surface of the transverse mesocolon, until it reaches the inferior border of the body and head of the pancreas (see Figs 62.4, 62.5C). At the pancreas the anterior layer envelops the pancreas and continues cephalad as the dorsal mesogastrium (see above). During surgery, the transverse mesocolon and greater omentum are separated by dividing through the posterior layer of the greater omentum, where this first attaches to the transverse colon. Alternatively, surgeons frequently divide directly through the greater omentum at the level where it approaches the greater curvature of the stomach. These activities are essential for gaining entry into the omental bursa.
On the left side of the abdomen, the greater omentum is attached to the gastrosplenic ligament or the peritoneal reflection in this region; on the right side, it extends to the beginning of the duodenum (see Fig. 62.4). A region of peritoneal reflection, the hepatocolic ligament, always extends from the inferior surface of the right lobe of the liver to the right colic flexure. The right border of the greater omentum usually adheres to the anterior surface of this region of reflection and thereafter to the right colic flexure and superior part of the ascending colon. In individuals, with obesity the right border of the greater omentum often extends laterally to adhere to the lateral border of the ascending colon and its associated region of peritoneal reflection (Jackson’s membrane). Less commonly, the left border of the greater omentum adheres to the anterior surface of the descending colon.
The greater omentum has a rich blood supply derived from the right and left gastro-omental (gastro-epiploic) vessels that run between the two peritoneal layers of the anterior sheet of greater omentum, close to the greater curvature of the stomach. These vessels give off numerous omental branches that run the length of the omentum. Microscopic capillary convolutions, sometimes termed ‘omental glomeruli’, lie directly beneath the mesothelium of the greater omentum. They are surrounded by aggregations of macrophages and lymphocytes (milky spots) that drain into lymphatic vessels (Shimotsuma et al 1993); the macrophages form clusters near the mesothelial surface, while B and T lymphocytes are grouped around vessels. The fenestrated endothelium lining the omental glomeruli and the mesothelium overlying the milky spots form sites of leukocyte migration and peritoneal fluid absorption (Platell et al 2000). The number and size of milky spots increase when there is peritoneal inflammation, highlighting their defensive role.
The greater omentum serves several important functions. It is highly mobile and frequently adheres to inflamed viscera or foreign bodies within the abdominal cavity. This can limit the spread of infection, as in the walling-off of acute appendicitis, and promote haemostasis. However, it can also block peritoneal drains and catheters (such that omentectomy is sometimes performed when intraperitoneal catheters are placed). It absorbs peritoneal fluid. It contains macrophages and lymphoid tissue capable of destroying pathogens; for example, macrophages and neutrophils readily migrate from the greater omentum into the peritoneal cavity when there is peritonitis. It promotes neovascularization of the structures to which it adheres and thus promotes healing of ischaemic tissues and supports splenic autotransplantation (Ch. 69).
The remarkable capacity of the greater omentum to participate in reparative processes is highlighted by its use as an omental pedicle flap or free flap in reconstructive surgery: it has been used to support closure of gastrointestinal perforations, fill ‘dead space’ after surgical excision of pathology, and cover wound defects. A greater omental pedicle or free flap based on the right gastro-omental vessels can be mobilized by dividing the gastric branches of these vessels close to the surface of the stomach and the left gastro-omental vessels near their origin.

Peritoneal connections of the spleen

The peritoneal connections of the spleen anchor it in the left upper quadrant of the abdomen. Although they are called ligaments, they are regions of peritoneal reflection from the parietal peritoneum of the left upper quadrant to the spleen. They include the gastrosplenic, splenorenal and phrenicocolic ligaments. The gastrosplenic ligament runs between the greater curvature of the stomach and the splenic hilum, and is the cephalad continuation of the greater omentum. The peritoneal layers of the gastrosplenic ligament separate to enclose the spleen. The outer layer then detaches from the spleen to form the splenorenal and phrenicocolic ligaments that continue posteriorly as the dorsal mesogastrium, forming the posterior wall of the omental bursa. The splenorenal ligament extends from the spleen to the posterior abdominal wall and the phrenicocolic ligament connects the left colic flexure to the respiratory diaphragm.
The splenorenal ligament (see Figs 62.1, 62.5B) is continuous medially with the peritoneum of the posterior wall of the omental bursa over the left kidney and runs up to the splenic hilum, where it is continuous with the posterior layer of the gastrosplenic ligament. The posterior layer of the splenorenal ligament is continuous laterally with the peritoneum over the inferior surface of the respiratory diaphragm and runs onto the splenic surface over the renal impression. The splenic vessels lie between the layers of the splenorenal ligament; the tail of the pancreas is usually present in its inferior portion.
As the gastrosplenic ligament is a cephalad extension of the greater omentum, it also consists of two layers of peritoneum (see Fig. 62.5B). The posterior layer is continuous with the peritoneum of the splenic hilum and the peritoneum over the posterior surface of the stomach. This layer continues medially as the anterior surface of the dorsal mesogastrium. The anterior layer is continuous with the peritoneum reflected off the gastric impression of the spleen and with the peritoneum over the anterior surface of the stomach. The short gastric and left gastro-omental branches of the splenic artery, with their corresponding veins, pass between the layers of the gastrosplenic ligament.
The phrenicocolic ligament is a region of peritoneal reflection extending from the colic component of the left colic flexure to the adjacent parietal peritoneum. Medially, it is attached to the left border of the greater omentum. Inferiorly, it continues as the left lateral reflection. Superiorly, it continues as the splenorenal ligament or region of reflection.
Undue traction on the peritoneal attachments of the spleen at surgery can cause a capsular tear and serious bleeding. A clear understanding of peritoneal anatomy in this region is therefore essential for the surgeon. Downward traction on the phrenicocolic ligament during mobilization of the left colic flexure is particularly hazardous; medial traction on the greater omentum can also injure the spleen if the omentum adheres to the fibrous capsule of the spleen. Unfortunately, surgical trauma is the main cause for splenectomy.

Peritoneum of the lower abdomen

The posterior surface of the inferior anterior abdominal wall is lined by parietal peritoneum, which extends from the linea alba centrally to the lateral paracolic gutters, where it is reflected over the front and sides of the ascending colon on the right and the descending colons on the left (see Fig. 62.5C). The ascending and descending colons are now regarded as having attached regions of mesentery (i.e. the right and left mesocolon respectively). The surface of the viscus is considered to be lined by visceral peritoneum that is continuous with the parietal peritoneum at regions of peritoneal reflection. In keeping with this, the parietal peritoneum is reflected onto the small intestine mesentery where this approaches the posterior abdominal wall, and continues laterally as the right mesocolon. The anatomical organization of the small and large intestines and associated mesentery explain the organization of their surrounding peritoneal reflection at all levels from the duodenojejunal flexure to the mesorectum.

Transverse mesocolon

The mesentery of the transverse colon is a region of the mesentery associated with the large intestine that suspends the transverse colon in the peritoneal cavity. The transverse mesocolon attaches centrally and obliquely from the anterior aspect of the descending part of the duodenum, over the head and neck of the pancreas superior to the duodenojejunal junction, and over the superior pole of the left kidney to the left colic flexure (see Figs 62.2–62.4). It contains the middle colic vessels and their branches, together with autonomic nerves from the aortic plexus, lymphatic vessels and lymph nodes. Near the left colic flexure, the ascending branch of the left colic artery terminates within the transverse mesocolon. Its two peritoneal layers pass to the posterior surface of the transverse colon, where they separate to invest it. In between the flexures, the superior layer of mesocolic peritoneum is attached to the posterior layer of the greater omentum (see Fig. 62.4). The inferior layer of peritoneum is continuous with the anterior surface of the mesentery of the small intestine and right mesocolon in the midline and to the right, and with the anterior surface of the left mesocolon to the left. The transverse and right mesocolon and the mesentery of the small intestine converge centrally to form a region of considerable technical and pathological importance that contains the superior mesenteric artery and vein.

Mesentery of the small intestine

The mesentery of the small intestine is formed from two layers of peritoneum (anterosuperior and posteroinferior) that fan out towards an intestinal margin from a diagonal origin running across the posterior abdominal wall from the duodenojejunal flexure to the ileocaecal junction (see Fig. 62.1A). It continues as the right mesocolon, and where it does so it is attached to the posterior abdominal wall, although the attachment of mesentery to posterior abdominal wall is obscured by the peritoneal reflection at this level. The mesentery of the small intestine and the right and transverse mesocolon converge on a central region that is more appropriately termed the root of the mesentery. Jejunal and ileal branches of the superior mesenteric vessels, nerves related to the superior mesenteric plexus, lymphatic vessels and regional lymph nodes, all lie in the adipose tissue of the mesentery of the small intestine. The length and mobility of this mesentery can make it difficult to determine the site and orientation of a loop of small intestine through a small surgical incision. Tracing the continuity of a peritoneal layer of mesentery onto the posterior abdominal wall can be useful for orientating an isolated loop if the duodenojejunal flexure or ileocaecal junction is not readily accessible.

Right mesocolon

The right mesocolon is a continuation of the mesentery of the small intestine. It is apposed to the posterior abdominal wall from which it is separated by a fascial layer. Laterally it is directly connected to the caecum and ascending colon. It continues as the hepatic component of the transverse mesocolon. Where the small intestinal region of the mesentery continues as the right mesocolon, a mesothelial bridge (reflection) between the visceral peritoneum of the mesentery and the parietal peritoneum of the posterior abdominal wall is an important anatomical landmark defining the medial boundary of the right mesocolon (Byrnes et al 2019a–2019d).

Meso-appendix

The mesentery of the appendix, the meso-appendix, arises from the posterior surface of the mesentery in the ileocaecal region. It attaches to the appendix along its length, but it can end just short of the distal end of the appendix. It encloses the blood vessels, nerves and lymph vessels of the appendix and usually contains a lymph node. A fold of peritoneum is sometimes observed between the terminal ileum and the anterior layer of the meso-appendix (the ‘bloodless fold of Treves’). The position of the meso-appendix is of considerable clinical importance. As it arises from the posterior surface of the main body of the mesentery, the appendix frequently takes up a retrocaecal position. Its positioning in this location means that appendicitis is often a difficult diagnosis, and that the appendix and meso-appendix can be difficult to expose and divide during surgery.

Left mesocolon

The left mesocolon is the region of mesocolon between the transverse mesocolon proximally and the sigmoid mesocolon distally. It is apposed to the posterior abdominal wall from which it is separated by a fascial layer. A mesothelial bridge (reflection) occurs at the medial boundary of the left mesocolon and links the parietal peritoneum of the posterior abdominal wall with the visceral peritoneum of the left mesocolon. The left mesocolon is directly connected to the medial border of the descending colon and is the distal continuation of the splenic component of the transverse mesocolon.

Sigmoid mesocolon

The sigmoid mesocolon (mesosigmoid) is the distal continuation of the left mesocolon (see Fig. 62.1A). At the rectosigmoid junction it continues distally into the pelvis as the mesorectum. Medially, it is attached to the posterior abdominal wall. Laterally, this region separates from the posterior abdominal wall as the mobile region of the sigmoid mesocolon. The medial and lateral boundaries of the attached region of sigmoid mesocolon are obscured from direct visualization by the peritoneal reflection at this level. Bands of peritoneum can run from the proximal sigmoid colon to the abdominal wall in the left iliac fossa: they are congenital adhesions that are often mistaken for the peritoneal reflection at this level. The sigmoid mesocolon contains the inferior mesenteric artery and the superior anorectal artery (i.e. the continuation of the inferior mesenteric artery). Sigmoid arteries arise primarily from the inferior mesenteric artery and travel in the sigmoid mesocolon to the sigmoid colon.

Peritoneum of the inferior anterior abdominal wall

The peritoneum of the inferior anterior abdominal wall is raised into five folds (sometimes referred to as ‘ligaments’) that diverge as they descend from the umbilicus. They are the median, right and left medial, and right and left lateral, umbilical folds (Fig. 62.6; see Figs 73.5, 73.6). The median umbilical fold extends from the umbilicus to the apex of the bladder and contains the urachus or its remnant. The medial umbilical fold overlies the obliterated umbilical artery. The lateral umbilical fold covers the inferior epigastric vessels inferior to their entry into the rectus sheath. The supravesical fossa lies between the medial and median umbilical folds on each side of the midline. The medial and lateral inguinal fossae lie on either side of a lateral umbilical fold. The lateral inguinal fossa overlies the deep inguinal ring, and the medial inguinal fossa overlies the femoral ring (Healy and Reznek 1999).

Peritoneum of the pelvis

The parietal peritoneum of the abdominal wall is continuous with the parietal peritoneum that lines the side walls of the pelvis. The arrangement of the pelvic visceral peritoneum differs between the sexes.

Peritoneum of the male pelvis

In males, peritoneum is reflected off the pelvic side walls and on to the rectum and mesorectum. These are the pararectal regions of the reflection and are surgically important because they must be divided to access the plane between the mesorectum and underlying fascia. They coalesce anteriorly in the distal pelvis to form the anterior reflection. A gulley, the rectovesical pouch, is created by the organization of the peritoneum at this level. The urinary bladder, seminal glands and ductus deferens are positioned deep to the anterior region of peritoneum. The peritoneum covering the posterosuperior surface of the urinary bladder forms a paravesical fossa on each side that is limited laterally by a ridge of peritoneum containing the ductus deferens (Fig. 62.7); the depths of the paravesical fossae depend on the volume of urine in the bladder. When the urinary bladder is empty, a transverse vesical fold is sometimes visible at laparoscopy; its medial portion overlies the superior
vesical artery or arteries (Boaz et al 2011). The ureters and internal iliac vessels can cause slight elevations in the peritoneum between the paravesical and pararectal fossae. The median umbilical fold extends superiorly from the apex of the bladder on the posterior surface of the inferior anterior abdominal wall to the umbilicus: when the urinary bladder distends, the overlying peritoneum is lifted so that part of the underlying surface of the bladder comes into direct contact with the anterior abdominal wall.

Peritoneum of the female pelvis

In females, peritoneum covers the anterolateral surface of the proximal rectum and mesorectum, as it does in males, but it descends further over the anterior surface of the rectum. The pararectal and paravesical fossae are limited laterally by the peritoneum that covers the uterosacral ligaments and round ligaments of the uterus, respectively (see Figs 75.11B, 75.18). The presence of the uterus and vagina means that there are two pelvic pouches instead of the single rectovesical pouch seen in males. The peritoneum from the rectum is reflected anteriorly on to the posterior surface of the posterior part of the vaginal fornix and the uterus, forming the recto-uterine pouch (of Douglas). The depth of the pouch, i.e. the extent to which it descends on the posterior surface of the vagina, is variable (Baessler and Schuessler 2000). The peritoneum covers the fundus and body of the uterus, descending on its anterior surface as far as the junction of the body and cervix, and from there it is reflected forwards on to the posterosuperior surface of the urinary bladder, forming a shallow vesico-uterine pouch. As in males, the peritoneum over the urinary bladder is reflected on to the posterior surface of the inferior anterior abdominal wall. Recto-uterine folds containing the uterosacral ligaments pass posteriorly from the sides of the cervix of the uterus to the sacrum, running lateral to the rectum.
Peritoneum is also reflected from the anterior and posterior uterine surfaces to the lateral pelvic walls as the broad ligament of the uterus (see Fig. 75.18). This consists of anterior and posterior layers that are continuous at the superior border of the ligament. The broad ligament contains the uterine tubes in its free superior peritoneum and it merges with the pelvic parietal peritoneum at its base. The ovarian fossa is a shallow depression in the peritoneal lining of the lateral pelvic wall between the peritoneal ridges formed by the occluded part of the umbilical arteries anteriorly, the ureter posteriorly, and the external iliac vessels above; it lies posterior to the lateral attachment of the broad ligament of the uterus and usually contains the ovary in nulliparous females.

Vascular and lymphatic drainage

Parietal and visceral peritonea develop from the somatopleural and splanchnopleural layers, respectively, of the lateral plate mesoderm. Parietal peritoneum is supplied by the somatic blood vessels of the abdominal and pelvic walls, and its lymphatics join vessels in the body wall and drain to parietal nodes. Visceral peritoneum is best considered as an integral part of the viscus it covers; its blood supply and lymphatic drainage therefore correspond to those of the associated viscus. The region between, i.e. the peritoneal reflection, represents a transitional zone in terms of the origins of its vascular and lymphatic drainage and innervation.

Innervation

The parietal peritoneum is innervated by somatic efferent and afferent nerves that also supply the muscles and skin of the overlying body wall. The visceral peritoneum is innervated by afferent nerves that travel with the autonomic supply in the mesentery to the underlying viscera. Sensations arising from pathologies that affect the parietal or visceral peritoneum reflect these different patterns of innervation. Well-localized pain is elicited by mechanical, thermal or chemical stimulation of nociceptors in the parietal peritoneum; the pain is usually limited to the affected region. Somatic nerves that innervate the parietal peritoneum also supply the corresponding segmental skin and muscles: when the parietal peritoneum is irritated there is local reflex muscle contraction, resulting in clinical signs of guarding or even rigidity of the anterior abdominal wall. The parietal peritoneum covering the respiratory diaphragm is supplied centrally by afferent fibres from the phrenic nerves and peripherally by the inferior intercostal nerves and subcostal nerves: peripheral irritation of the diaphragm can therefore result in pain localized in the distribution of the inferior thoracic spinal nerves, while central irritation causes referred pain in the cutaneous distribution of the third to fifth cervical spinal nerves (the shoulder region). The innervation of the parietal peritoneum of the lesser pelvis is poorly documented.
Irritation or stretching of the visceral peritoneum causes poorly localized discomfort. The sensation of pain is referred to a diffuse area of the abdominal wall. Discomfort from structures derived from the foregut is felt in the epigastric region; that from structures derived from the midgut in the umbilical region; and that from structures derived from the hindgut in the hypogastric region. None of these sensations is significantly lateralized. Pain from the pancreas, gallbladder and small intestine can radiate to the back. Stretching or irritation of the visceral peritoneum can also elicit profound reflex vasomotor and cardiac changes mediated by autonomic nerves, including a vasovagal response. This is of considerable clinical relevance. Painful responses to manipulation of the parietal peritoneum can be abolished by local or regional anaesthesia. In marked contrast, the direct central connections of visceral afferents, particularly via the vagus nerve, mean that stretching the visceral peritoneum can have profound effects, including acute haemodynamic instability, despite local or regional (including spinal) anaesthesia.

General Arrangement of the Peritoneal Cavity

The peritoneum is organized into parietal and visceral parts and intervening areas of reflection (where the parietal peritoneum is reflected onto adjacent viscera with which it merges). Together, these form the peritoneal cavity. The region of the cavity that is attached to the lateral and anterior abdominal wall is relatively featureless. In contrast, while the contour of the reflection and visceral peritoneum appears more complex, it is explained by the shape of the underlying digestive system organs, including the mesentery. The cavity can be subdivided into the greater sac and the omental bursa. The greater sac is the main peritoneal cavity surrounding most of the abdominal and pelvic viscera. The omental bursa is a substantial diverticulum of the dorsal mesentery at this level and is situated posterior to the stomach and lesser omentum. The greater sac and omental bursa communicate via the omental foramen.
For clinical purposes, the peritoneal cavity can be divided into several ‘spaces’ (see below); pathological processes are often contained within these spaces and their anatomy can influence diagnosis and treatment. It has also been divided into two regions separated by the transverse mesocolon: the supramesocolic (often simply called supracolic) region superior to the transverse mesocolon and the infracolic region inferior to the transverse mesocolon. It should be noted that this classification is little used in current clinical practice.

Supramesocolic region

The supramesocolic region lies between the respiratory diaphragm and the transverse mesocolon and can be arbitrarily divided into right and left supramesocolic spaces. The right supramesocolic space can be subdivided into the right subphrenic space, the right subhepatic space and the omental bursa. The left supramesocolic space can be divided into the left subphrenic space and the left perihepatic space. These ‘spaces’ usually communicate but can nevertheless be sites of localized fluid collection.

Right subphrenic space

The right subphrenic space lies between the respiratory diaphragm and the anterior, superior and right lateral surfaces of the right lobe of the liver. It is bounded on the left by the falciform ligament and posteriorly by the superior layer of the coronary ligament (see Fig. 62.3).

Hepatorenal recess (right subhepatic space)

The hepatorenal recess (right subhepatic space) lies between the inferior surface of the right lobe of the liver and the superior pole of the right kidney. It is bounded superiorly by the inferior layer of the coronary ligament, laterally by the right lateral abdominal wall, posteriorly by the anterior surface of the superior pole of the right kidney, and inferomedially by the right colic flexure, transverse mesocolon, descending part of the duodenum and part of the head of the pancreas. In the supine position, the hepatorenal recess is more dependent than the right paracolic gutter. Pathological fluid collections can develop at this site.

Omental bursa

The omental bursa is a cavity lined with peritoneum and connected to the main peritoneal cavity (greater sac) by the omental foramen (of Winslow). It develops under the left half of the respiratory diaphragm, providing a mesenteric platform into which the stomach, spleen and pancreas can emerge. It has posterior and anterior walls and superior, inferior, right and left borders. The bursa varies in size according to the volume of the viscera making up its walls, and it can be partially obliterated by natural adhesions between its anterior and posterior walls.
The anterior wall of the omental bursa consists of the posterior peritoneal layer of the lesser omentum, the peritoneum over the posterior wall of the stomach and superior part of the duodenum, and the posterior superior part of the anterior layer of the greater omentum (see above) (see Fig. 62.4). At its right border, the anterior wall is mostly formed by the lesser omentum, but towards the left, the lesser omentum becomes progressively shorter and more of the anterior wall is formed by the posterior surface of the stomach and greater omentum. The spleen is positioned at the left lateral extent of the bursa.
The greater omentum curves posterolaterally at the lateral border of the bursa and is continuous posteriorly with the posterior wall of the bursa, which is the dorsal mesogastrium, and is attached to the posterior abdominal wall under the left half of the respiratory diaphragm. The body of the pancreas is located in the inferior part of the posterior wall. The posterior wall detaches at the inferior margin of the pancreas to continue distally over the superior surface of the transverse mesocolon. It detaches from the transverse colon to continue as the greater omentum (see Fig. 62.4).
The inferior phrenic, splenic, left gastric and common hepatic arteries are located in the mesentery of the omental bursa: they use the frame of the bursa to travel to their target organs. The superior border of the omental bursa is narrow and lies between the right side of the abdominal part of the oesophagus and the superior end of the fissure for the ligamentum venosum and is sometimes referred to as the superior recess of the omental bursa. Here, the peritoneum of the posterior wall of the omental bursa is reflected anteriorly from the respiratory diaphragm to join the posterior layer of the lesser omentum.
The right border of the omental bursa is where the peritoneum overlying the head and neck of the pancreas is reflected onto the inferior aspect of the superior part of the duodenum. The line of this reflection ascends to the left, along the medial side of the gastroduodenal artery. Near the superior border of the duodenum, the right border of the omental bursa joins the inferior aspect of the omental foramen round the proper hepatic artery. The omental foramen therefore interrupts the right border. Superior to the omental foramen, the right border is formed by the peritoneal reflection from the respiratory diaphragm to the right margin of the caudate lobe of the liver, which then crosses the inferior vena cava. The left border of the omental bursa runs from the left end of the transverse mesocolon and is mostly formed by the inner peritoneal layers of the splenorenal and gastrosplenic ligaments. The region of the omental bursa between the splenorenal and gastrosplenic ligaments is referred to as the splenic recess. Superior to the spleen, the two ligaments merge to form the short gastrophrenic ligament, which passes anteriorly from the respiratory diaphragm to the posterior aspect of the fundus of the stomach and forms part of the superior left border of the omental bursa. The two layers of the gastrophrenic ligament diverge near the abdominal part of the oesophagus, leaving part of the posterior surface of the stomach devoid of peritoneum. The left gastric artery runs forwards here into the lesser omentum.
The omental bursa is indented by a crescentic mesenteric arch that runs from the superior border of the neck of the pancreas to the superior part of the lesser curvature of the stomach: it is variously described but commonly known as the gastropancreatic fold. The superior part of this arch contains the left gastric artery as it runs from the coeliac trunk to the lesser curvature of the stomach. The inferior part contains the common hepatic artery as it runs from the coeliac trunk to the lesser omentum. When the arch is prominent, it divides the omental bursa into a smaller superior and a larger inferior recess. The superior recess lies posterior to the lesser omentum and encloses the caudate lobe of the liver; it extends superiorly into the fissure for the ligamentum venosum and lies adjacent to the right crus of the respiratory diaphragm posteriorly. The inferior recess of the omental bursa lies between the stomach and pancreas, and is contained within the double sheet of greater omentum.
Acute pancreatitis is probably the most common cause of a fluid collection within the omental bursa (or just posterior to its posterior wall). Bleeding from trauma or a ruptured aneurysm of the splenic artery and perforation of a posterior gastric ulcer are other causes of omental bursa fluid collections.
Omental foramen
The omental foramen is a short, vertical slit, about 3 cm in height in adults, posterior to the free right border of the lesser omentum, and is the entrance to the omental bursa from the greater sac. The hepatoduodenal ligament, the thickened free right margin of the lesser omentum that extends between the porta hepatis above and the superior border of the superior part of the duodenum below, forms the anterior boundary of the omental foramen. Within this free border lie the bile duct (anteriorly on the right), the proper hepatic artery (anteriorly on the left) and the hepatic portal vein (posteriorly), together with nerves and lymphatics. Rapid control of the proper hepatic artery and hepatic portal vein can be exerted by compression of the free edge of the lesser omentum (a ‘Pringle’ manoeuvre), which is a potentially useful technique during surgery. Superiorly, the peritoneum of the posterior layer of the hepatoduodenal ligament runs over the caudate process of the liver, forming the roof of the omental foramen. This layer of peritoneum is then reflected onto the inferior vena cava, forming the posterior border of the foramen. The floor of the foramen is formed by the peritoneal reflection overlying the superior border of the superior part of the duodenum as it runs anteriorly, superior to the head of the pancreas. A narrow passage, the vestibule of the omental bursa, can be formed to the left of the foramen between the caudate process superiorly and the superior part of the duodenum inferiorly. To the right, the rim of the foramen is continuous with the peritoneum of the greater sac. The anterior and posterior walls of the foramen are normally apposed, which partly explains why patients can develop large fluid collections isolated to the greater sac or omental bursa (Shrestha et al 2010).

Left subphrenic space

The left subphrenic space lies between the respiratory diaphragm, the anterior and superior surfaces of the left lobe of the liver, the anterosuperior surface of the stomach and the diaphragmatic surface of the spleen. To the right it is bounded by the falciform ligament. It is expanded in the absence of the spleen and is a common site for fluid collection after splenectomy. The left subphrenic space is substantially larger than the right and can be divided into a left perihepatic space and anterior and posterior subphrenic spaces, all of which are in continuity in the absence of disease. The definitions of the boundaries of these spaces vary.
The left anterior subphrenic space is large and lies between the superior and anterolateral surfaces of the spleen and the respiratory diaphragm. Inferiorly and medially, this space is bounded by the phrenicocolic and splenorenal regions of peritoneal reflection and the gastrosplenic ligament. The left subphrenic space is a relatively common site of fluid collection after upper abdominal surgery, particularly surgery involving the spleen or adjacent pancreas. The left posterior subphrenic space is small and lies between the fundus of the stomach and the diaphragm superior to the origin of the splenorenal region of peritoneal reflection.
The left perihepatic space is sometimes subdivided into anterior and posterior spaces. The left anterior perihepatic space lies between the anterosuperior surface of the left lobe of the liver and the respiratory diaphragm. The left posterior perihepatic space, also known as the gastrohepatic recess, lies inferior to the left lobe of the liver. It extends into the fissure for the ligamentum venosum on the right. Posteriorly, it is separated from the superior recess of the omental bursa by the lesser omentum.

Extraperitoneal subphrenic spaces

There are two potential subphrenic spaces that actually lie outside the peritoneum but are clinically relevant because they can be sites of fluid accumulation. The right extraperitoneal space is bounded by the two layers of the coronary ligament, the bare area of the liver and the inferior surface of the right half of the respiratory diaphragm. The left extraperitoneal space lies anterior to the left suprarenal gland and superior pole of the left kidney.

Inframesocolic region

The inframesocolic region (also called the infracolic region) lies inferior to the transverse mesocolon and transverse colon and is divided into two unequal spaces by the mobile region of the mesentery of the small intestine. It contains the right and left paracolic gutters lateral to the ascending and descending colons, respectively.

Right and left infracolic spaces

The right and left infracolic spaces are conceptual zones without defined anatomical boundaries and are little referenced in current clinical practice. The right space is the region of the peritoneal cavity inferior to the transverse mesocolon and lateral to the right side of the mobile region of the mesentery of the small intestine. The left space is the corresponding space on the opposite side of the mobile region of the mesentery of the small intestine.

Paracolic gutters

The right and left paracolic gutters are peritoneal depressions on the posterior abdominal wall alongside the ascending and descending colons, respectively. The principal paracolic gutter lies lateral to the colon on each side. The right paracolic gutter runs from the superolateral aspect of the right colic flexure down the lateral aspect of the ascending colon and caecum and is continuous with the peritoneum of the pelvic cavity inferiorly. Superiorly, it is continuous with the hepatorenal recess and with the omental bursa through the omental foramen. Bile, purulence, blood or other fluids can run along the gutter and collect in sites distant to the viscus of origin. In supine patients, infected fluid from the right iliac fossa can ascend in the gutter to the right subphrenic space. In erect or semi-recumbent positions, fluid from the stomach, duodenum or gallbladder can run down the gutter to collect in the right iliac fossa (mimicking acute appendicitis) or pelvis to form an abscess. The right paracolic gutter is deeper than the left, which could explain why subphrenic collections are more common on the right.

Recesses of the peritoneal cavity

Peritoneal fossae or recesses are difficult to standardize, so they are less discussed in the literature than they were in the past. However, these recesses are occasionally sites of internal herniation. If a loop of intestine becomes trapped in a fossa or recess, it can become obstructed or strangulated from constriction at the entrance to the recess. The contents of the peritoneal fold forming the fossa/recess must be considered when such a hernia is repaired.

Duodenal recesses

There can be several folds of peritoneum around the ascending (fourth) part of the duodenum and the duodenojejunal junction. They form a number of named recesses, which are highly variable in anatomical organization, and that arise after the duodenojejunal junction (and associated mesentery) adopts a final position in utero. Although it has been suggested that these recesses are sites of internal herniation, this is extremely rare (Zonca et al 2008).
Superior duodenal recess
The superior duodenal recess is occasionally present, often in association with an inferior duodenal recess. It lies directly to the left of the ascending part of the duodenum, adjacent to the second lumbar vertebra, and posterior to a crescentic superior duodenal fold of peritoneum. This fold has a semilunar free inferior margin that merges laterally with the peritoneum overlying the left kidney. The inferior mesenteric vein lies within the left mesocolon and is positioned close to the recess (when present).
Inferior duodenal recess
The inferior duodenal recess is usually present, often in association with a superior recess, with which it can share an orifice. It lies to the left of the ascending part of the duodenum, adjacent to the third lumbar vertebra, posterior to an avascular, triangular inferior duodenal fold, which has a sharp superior edge. It usually admits one or two fingertips and extends inferiorly, sometimes posterior to the ascending part of the duodenum and to the left, anterior to the ascending branch of the left colic artery and the inferior mesenteric vein.
Paraduodenal recess
The paraduodenal recess can occur in conjunction with superior and inferior duodenal recesses. It lies a little to the left and slightly posterior to the ascending part of the duodenum, posterior to a falciform fold of peritoneum. The free right edge of this fold contains the inferior mesenteric vein and ascending branch of the left colic artery, and is part of the left mesocolon. Herniation here is extremely rare (Khan et al 1998).
Retroduodenal recess
The retroduodenal recess is the largest of the duodenal recesses but is rarely present. It lies posterior to the horizontal and ascending parts of the duodenum and anterior to the abdominal aorta, is up to 10 cm deep, and has a wide orifice flanked by duodeno-parietal peritoneal regions of reflection. The recess arises because the confluence between the mesentery of the small intestine and right mesocolon has failed to attach to the posterior abdominal wall and incomplete attachment creates the impression of a recess.
Duodenojejunal recess
The duodenojejunal or mesocolic recess occurs in a minority of adults; when present, it is almost never associated with other duodenal recesses. It is approximately 3 cm deep and lies to the left of the abdominal aorta, between the duodenojejunal junction and the central region of the mesentery. It is bounded superiorly by the pancreas, on the left by the kidney, and inferiorly by the left renal vein. Its circular opening lies between two peritoneal folds and faces inferiorly and to the right.
Mesentericoparietal recess
The mesentericoparietal recess or fossa is rarely present. It lies just inferior to the horizontal part of the duodenum and invaginates the superior part of the mesentery of the small intestine towards the right. Its orifice is large and faces to the left, posterior to a fold of mesentery raised by the superior mesenteric artery. This recess is the site of the rare ‘right paraduodenal hernia’.

Caecal recesses

Folding of the peritoneal reflection at the ileocaecal level generates a variety of recesses that have the potential to become sites of internal herniation (Rivkind et al 1986). Paracaecal recesses are common sites for abscess formation after acute appendicitis.
Superior ileocaecal recess
The superior ileocaecal recess is usually best developed in children and is often absent in the aged or obese. It is a narrow slit bounded anteriorly by a vascular fold of peritoneum containing the anterior caecal vessels, posteriorly by the ileal mesentery, inferiorly by the terminal ileum and on the right by the ileocaecal junction. Its orifice opens to the left.
Inferior ileocaecal recess
The inferior ileocaecal recess is similarly well developed in the young but frequently obliterated by fat in adults. It is formed by the ileocaecal fold, which extends from the antero-inferior aspect of the terminal ileum to the anterior aspect of the meso-appendix (or to the appendix or caecum). It is also known as the ‘bloodless fold of Treves’, although it sometimes contains blood vessels that bleed if divided during surgery. If inflamed, especially when the appendix and its mesentery are retrocaecal, it can be mistaken for the meso-appendix. The recess is bounded anteriorly by the ileocaecal fold, superiorly by the terminal ileum and associated mesentery, to the right by the caecum, and posteriorly by the superior part of the meso-appendix. Its orifice opens inferiorly and to the left.
Retrocaecal recess
The retrocaecal recess lies posterior to the caecum. It ascends posterior to the ascending colon to a variable extent, often being large enough to admit an entire finger. It is bounded anteriorly by the caecum (and sometimes the ascending colon), posteriorly by the parietal peritoneum, and on each side by caecal folds (parietocolic folds) passing from the caecum to the posterior abdominal wall. The appendix frequently occupies this retrocaecal recess. There can also be one or more shallow paracaecal or paracolic recesses: they are simply depressions within the right paracolic gutter and are not sites of internal herniation.

Intersigmoid recess

The intersigmoid recess is present in fetal life and infancy but often subsequently disappears. It lies lateral to the mobile region of the sigmoid mesocolon, between it and the parietal peritoneum of the left iliac fossa. As these structures are its anatomical determinants, it is highly variable in appearance.

Internal hernias

An internal hernia is a protrusion of a viscus through a normal or abnormal aperture (which includes a surgically created opening) within the confines of the abdominal cavity (Martin et al 2006). It is possible for any of the peritoneal recesses, foramina or cul-de-sacs described in this chapter to be associated with the development of an internal hernia; but with a clinically apparent internal hernia, the relevant peritoneal recess is usually enlarged and its aperture narrower than normal. Although they are rare, the most common sites for internal hernia (in the absence of adhesions) are the paraduodenal recess, followed by the recesses around the caecum and omental foramen.

Additional clinical aspects of the peritoneal cavity

Peritoneal fluid collections

Fluid collections frequently develop within the peritoneal cavity in response to a wide range of pathological processes. In the absence of inflammation, peritoneal adhesions or previous surgery, serous fluid tends to be distributed widely between the peritoneal spaces. Simple ascites can therefore be drained freely from any convenient dependent part of the peritoneal cavity; drainage is most commonly performed by blind puncture or ultrasound-guided insertion of a catheter into the inferior left or right paracolic gutters. The mobility of the small intestine makes iatrogenic injury during this procedure very unlikely.
Fluid collections caused by inflammatory processes are often much thicker because they contain purulence, fibrin or blood. Furthermore, they are usually associated with peritoneal adhesions arising from peritoneal inflammation. These factors predispose to the formation of localized fluid collections that can become walled-off as the inflammatory process progresses. Any region of the peritoneum can develop a localized collection, but the subphrenic and subhepatic spaces and pelvic pouches are the most common sites because they are well defined by the peritoneal folds and viscera that form their boundaries and they are the most dependent regions within the peritoneal cavity.
Surgical access to the peritoneum is less often needed today because advances in radiologically guided percutaneous drainage using fluoroscopy, ultrasound, computed tomography (CT) or magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) offer reliable and versatile methods for accessing the peritoneum, including relatively inaccessible subhepatic, perihepatic or intermesenteric collections. Posterolateral approaches or approaches through the greater sciatic foramen can be used to access the retroperitoneum or pelvis, respectively. Occasionally, a surgical approach is required, for example, a subcostal or lateral intercostal incision to drain a subphrenic abscess or an inguinal incision to drain a pelvic abscess.

Peritoneal dialysis

The mean surface area of the peritoneum in the adult female has been estimated to be about 1.4 m2 (the visceral peritoneum accounting for about 80% of the total) (Albanese et al 2009). Mesothelium resembles vascular endothelium in that it allows the passage of ions and small molecules. Normally, the volume of fluid transmitted by peritoneal surfaces is small, but large volumes can be instilled into the peritoneal cavity and then siphoned out, using the peritoneum as a dialysing membrane, for example to support individuals with acute or chronic renal failure.

Ventriculoperitoneal shunts

The absorptive capabilities of the peritoneum can be exploited to absorb excess transitional fluids from several sites in the body. The most common of these is the absorption of cerebrospinal fluid in patients with hydrocephalus, diverted into the peritoneal cavity using a shunt (a fine catheter) from the cerebral ventricles. The catheter placed within the peritoneal cavity is connected to a one-way valve placed over the skull so that reflux of peritoneal fluid into the cerebral ventricles is prevented. The cerebrospinal fluid is continuously absorbed by the peritoneum, maintaining normal pressure within the ventricles.

Intraperitoneal cancer chemotherapy

In patients with peritoneal metastases, attempts to optimize the benefits of cytoreductive surgery can involve the administration of chemotherapeutic agents (diluted in large volumes of fluid) directly into the peritoneal cavity. The peritoneum can be accessed via an intraperitoneal catheter connected to a subcutaneous port or by repeated paracentesis. The infused chemotherapeutic agents can be retained locally for longer than systemically administered agents. Targeting drugs to the peritoneal space can improve the results of treatment if complete tumour resection is not possible.
