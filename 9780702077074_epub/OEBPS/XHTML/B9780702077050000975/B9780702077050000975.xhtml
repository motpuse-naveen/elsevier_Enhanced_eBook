Commentary 9.3: Anterolateral ligament of the knee

Robert F. LaPrade
There has been significant interest over the past decade concerning the ‘rediscovery’ of the anterolateral ligament (ALL) of the knee. The structure was initially defined by Paul Segond in 1879 as a pearly, fibrous band that became tensioned during internal rotation of the knee (Segond 1879). Strictly speaking, the ‘rediscovery’ of the ALL by Claes et al (2013) was a clarification of the previous literature and reflected increasing recognition that anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) reconstruction alone did not always eliminate a patient’s pivot shift test. This fibrous band along the anterolateral aspect of the knee has been variously identified as retrograde iliotibial tract fibres (Lobenhoffer et al 1987), the capsulo-osseous layer of the iliotibial tract (Terry et al 1993), the mid-third lateral capsular ligament (LaPrade et al 2000), and more recently, as the ALL (Vieira et al 2007).
It is important to realise that while the ALL has been recognized as a thickening within the mid-third of the lateral capsular ligament, there is still no clear definition of its anatomical attachment sites. The femoral attachment of the ALL has been identified proximal and posterior to the lateral epicondyle (Kennedy et al 2015) and the tibial attachment has been located consistently midway between the anterior aspect of the fibular head and Gerdy’s tubercle (Claes et al 2013, Helito et al 2013, Dodds et al 2014, Caterine et al 2015, Kennedy et al 2015).
The femoral attachment of the ALL has been difficult to define. Some of the first descriptions indicated a femoral attachment on or near the insertion of the popliteus tendon (Vincent et al 2012), or a shared femoral origin with the fibular collateral ligament (FCL), adjacent to the lateral epicondyle (Claes et al 2013). Helito et al (2013) described a femoral attachment anterior and distal to the FCL femoral attachment site, while Dodds et al (2014) reported the femoral attachment as proximal and posterior to the femoral FCL attachment site. Caterine et al (2015) reported significant anatomical variability, identifying attachments at either the proximal and posterior or the anterior and distal attachment sites. Most recently, Kennedy et al (2015) consistently located a femoral attachment approximately 7.0 mm from the lateral epicondyle and 4.7 mm posterior and proximal to the FCL femoral attachment, with an overall attachment area of 67.7 mm2 (Fig. 9.3.1). Lateral radiographs yielded a relative distance of 8.4  mm posterior and proximal to the lateral epicondyle, and an approximate distance of 22.3 mm proximal to the joint line.
The tibial attachment site of the ALL has been fairly consistently described across multiple studies over the years as a thickening of the lateral capsule, traversing anterodistal from its origin, and coming under tension when an internal rotation load is applied to the knee at 30° of flexion. This insertion has an area of approximately 64.9 mm2, and is strongly correlated with the location of a bony avulsion on the anterolateral aspect of the knee (Segond fracture) that occasionally accompanies ACL injuries. The attachment is approximately midway between the anterior aspect of the fibular head and Gerdy’s tubercle, with relative distances of 26.1 mm and 24.7 mm from these points, respectively (Kennedy et al 2015). It has been estimated radiographically as 13.1 mm distal to the lateral tibial plateau, with anterior–posterior radiographs yielding a planar distance of 19.0 mm posterior and superior to the centre of Gerdy’s tubercle. Additional attachments of the ALL to the meniscotibial portion of the mid-third lateral capsular ligament, the anterior arm of the short head of biceps femoris, and the capsulo-osseous layer of the iliotibial tract, have all been identified.
The occurrence of Segond fractures together with ACL injuries and the high failure rate of ACL reconstructions prompted the idea that perhaps the structures responsible for the kinematics associated with these injuries were not being fully restored. Upon concomitant sectioning of the ACL and ALL in a robotic study, Rasmussen et al (2016) found a significant increase in axial plane tibial translation during a simulated pivot shift relative to isolated ACL-deficient states, in addition to increases in internal rotation. This study supported the proposal that the ALL was acting as a secondary stabilizer throughout knee flexion during applied internal rotation torque and pivot shift testing and that its absence altered the stability of the knee and ultimately overburdened grafts utilized for ACL reconstructions. Nitri et al (2016) showed that concurrent reconstruction of both ACL and ALL was successful in improving rotary stability relative to isolated ACL reconstruction in ALL–ACL deficient knees. This was supported by Schon et al (2016) who also showed that ALL reconstructions resulted in a significant overconstraint of internal rotation beyond 30° of knee flexion.
In conclusion, the ALL is a capsular thickening of the lateral knee, originating proximal and posterior to the lateral epicondyle and coursing anterodistal to the anterolateral tibia, which can be reproducibly identified by reference to surrounding anatomical and bony landmarks. Imaging correlates ALL failure with the identical location of Segond fractures on the tibia, reinforcing its importance as a stabilizer of internal rotation in the ACL-deficient knee. Although the ALL appears to be vital for secondary stabilization of internal rotation, especially in conditions of ACL deficiency, further studies are necessary when considering ALL-supplemented reconstructions in the treatment of ACL injuries.
