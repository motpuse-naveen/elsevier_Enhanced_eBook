Commentary 1.3: Metaplasia

Jonathan M.W. Slack
Metaplasia is the appearance, in adult life, of a patch of tissue that normally belongs elsewhere in the body (Slack 1985, 2007). Generally it is understood that a metaplasia has originated in situ and has not migrated from elsewhere; so, for example, metastatic tumour deposits are not considered to be metaplasias, nor are tissues misplaced during embryonic development, such as thyroid residues in the thyroglossal duct.
Metaplasias are very diverse (Willis 1962). They include ectopic bone and cartilage arising from connective tissue, squamous metaplasia of glandular epithelia, and substitution of one glandular epithelial tissue for another. The latter group is of particular interest from a biological point of view. These transformations are normally between tissue types that originated in embryonic development as neighbours in a common cell sheet. Such tissues may remain neighbours into adult life or may become separated. An example of metaplasia between tissues that are still neighbours in the adult is intestinal metaplasia of the stomach, involving patches of small intestine-like epithelium which are found in the stomach (Stemmerman and Hayashi 1968, Gutierrez-Gonzalez and Wright 2008 ). An example of metaplasia between tissues that have become anatomically separated is intestinal metaplasia found in the urinary bladder that typically comprises cell types and markers normally found in the colon (Sung et al 2006). The bladder is no longer part of a common cell sheet with the intestine in the adult, but it is in the embryo, where it originates from the allantoic diverticulum of the hind gut. Metaplasias of glandular epithelia are particularly common in the gastrointestinal tract and in the female reproductive tract (Kurita 2011), perhaps because these are both examples of structures in which a number of different tissue types arise from a simple epithelial tube in the embryo.
The metaplasias are clinically important because they often predispose to cancer: for example, carcinomas of the bronchus usually arise within areas of squamous metaplasia (Auerbach 1961, Rigden 2016) and carcinomas of the stomach usually arise in regions of intestinal metaplasia (Correa 1992, Busuttil and Boussioutas 2009, Spence et al 2017). Metaplasias arise because the combination of gene activities that normally defines the tissue type in embryonic development becomes altered in adult life. The genes in question encode a limited number of key transcription factors. For example, the transcription factor p63 is essential for the formation of squamous epithelia (Koster et al 2004) and is always found to be expressed in patches of squamous metaplasia. The transcription factor CDX2 is required for the formation of intestinal epithelium, and is expressed in patches of intestinal metaplasia (Barros et al 2012). Epithelial tissues like these are maintained by continuous renewal from a population of stem cells, and the initiating event, which may be a somatic mutation or an epigenetic change of gene expression, changes the character of the stem cells: once the change has occurred, it is normally irreversible.
Barrett’s metaplasia, also known as Barrett’s oesophagus, is a well-studied example of metaplasia (Falk 2002, Gilbert et al 2011, Naini 2016, Colleypriest et al 2017) (Fig. 1.3.1). It is characterized by the presence of columnar epithelium in the distal oesophagus, some of which resembles intestinal epithelium because of the presence of goblet cells. Barrett’s metaplasia is a risk factor for the development of oesophageal adenocarcinoma, a cancer whose incidence rose considerably in the late twentieth century (Hvid-Jensen et al 2011). In less developed countries most oesophageal cancers are squamous cell carcinomas, arising from the normal squamous lining of the oesophagus, but in the Western world adenocarcinomas are now more common.
Some metaplasias are monoclonal, meaning that a particular focus of ectopic tissue is derived exclusively from a single cell, as would be expected if the cause is the somatic mutation of key transcription factor genes. Monoclonality has been demonstrated for individual foci of intestinal metaplasia of the stomach, where neighbouring metaplastic crypts arise by fission (Gutierrez-Gonzalez et al 2011). In Barrett’s metaplasia, patches of crypts are also monoclonal (Nicholson et al 2012) although the lesion as a whole is polyclonal, being derived from several original progenitors, with various combinations of abnormalities arising from somatic mutation (Leedham et al 2008). A key property of metaplastic foci is that the new tissue is in a situation of competitive growth with the old tissue that surrounds it. Unless the metaplastic tissue can outgrow the surrounding tissue then the transformation of a single stem cell would not give rise to a macroscopic lesion: this aspect of epithelial biology remains poorly understood.
The exact cell of origin of Barrett’s metaplasia is still not known (McDonald et al 2015). Theories include a migration of glandular tissue from the stomach; an origin from transitional or primitive cells located at the squamo-columnar junction; metaplasia of the squamous epithelium of the oesophagus; an origin from submucosal oesophageal glands. The plausibility of transformation of the squamous epithelium or the oesophageal glands is increased by the fact that the normal oesophagus originates from an endodermal tube lined with columnar epithelium that becomes squamous between about 6–7 weeks of gestation (Yu et al 2005, Zhang et al 2017).
The predisposing cause of Barrett’s metaplasia is gastro-oesophageal reflux, in which stomach acid and bile from the intestine enters the oesophagus (Souza 2010). It is known that this environment can induce expression of some of the key genes involved in intestinal development, such as those encoding CDX factors and hepatocyte nuclear factors, in oesophageal cells (Eda et al 2003, Debruyne et al 2006, Piessen et al 2007). Obesity is a well-known risk factor for reflux, partly because of effects on intra-abdominal pressure and probably also through the effects of cytokines released from fat (Corley et al 2007). Barrett’s metaplasia may progress to dysplasia and to adenocarcinoma. Progression is associated with loss of the tumour suppressor gene CDKN2A, encoding the protein p16 which is an inhibitor of cell division, and TP53, encoding the protein p53 required for death of abnormal cells (Spechler et al 2010, Chen et al 2011).
Gastro-oesophageal reflux is normally treated with proton pump inhibitors that reduce the levels of stomach acid. This improves associated oesophagitis but does not usually result in regression of any Barrett’s metaplasia. Barrett’s metaplasia as such is not normally treated: if it progresses to dysplasia, it may be extirpated by a variety of endoscopic techniques of which radiofrequency ablation has become the most usual (Lim and Fitzgerald 2013, Spechler and Souza 2014).
