Chapter 75: Female reproductive system

The female reproductive system consists of the lower genital tract (vulva and vagina) and the upper tract (uterus and cervix with associated uterine (Fallopian) tubes and ovaries).

Lower Genital Tract

Vulva

The female external genitalia or vulva (pudendum) include the mons pubis, labia majora, labia minora, clitoris, vestibule of the vagina, vestibular bulbs and the greater vestibular glands (Bartholin’s glands) (Fig. 75.1).

Mons pubis

The mons pubis is the rounded, hair-bearing area of skin and adipose tissue over the pubic symphysis and adjacent pubic bone. Before puberty, it is relatively flat and hairless, and the labia minora are poorly formed. Through adolescence and into adult life the mons pubis becomes prominent and develops coarse hair. It atrophies slightly after menopause.

Labia majora

The labia majora are two prominent, longitudinal folds of skin that extend back from the mons pubis to the perineum (see Fig. 75.1B). They form the lateral boundaries of the vulva. Each labium has an external pigmented surface covered with hairs and a smooth pink internal surface with large sebaceous follicles. Between these surfaces there is loose connective and adipose tissue intermixed with smooth muscle (resembling the dartos muscle of the scrotum), vessels, nerves and glands. The subcutaneous layer consists of a superficial fatty layer similar to the superficial fatty layer (Camper’s fascia) of the anterior abdominal wall, and a deep membranous layer (Colles’ fascia) continuous with the deep membranous layer (Scarpa’s fascia) of the anterior abdominal wall. The round ligament of the uterus may end in the adipose tissue and skin in the anterior part of the labium majus. A patent processus vaginalis may also reach a labium majus. The labia majora join anteriorly to form the anterior commissure below the mons pubis. Posteriorly they do not join but instead merge into neighbouring skin, ending near and almost parallel to each other. The connecting skin between them posteriorly forms a ridge, the posterior commissure, which overlies the perineal body and is the posterior limit of the vulva.

Labia minora

The labia minora are two small cutaneous folds, devoid of fat and hair, that lie between the labia majora. They extend from the clitoris obliquely down, laterally and back, flanking the vaginal orifice. Anteriorly, each labium minus bifurcates. The upper layer of each side passes above the clitoris to form the prepuce, while the lower layer passes below the clitoris to form the frenulum of the clitoris (Fig. 75.1A–C). There are numerous sebaceous follicles on the medial surfaces. Sometimes, an extra labial fold (labium tertium) is found on one or both sides between the labia minora and majora. Adhesions between the labia minora are common in prepubertal girls; in postmenopausal females with severe atrophy they can predispose to urinary infections (Leung and Robson 1992, Gandhi et al 2016).

Vestibule of the vagina

The vestibule of the vagina is the cavity between the labia minora. It contains the vaginal and external urethral orifices and the openings of the two greater vestibular glands and of numerous mucous secreting, lesser vestibular glands. There is a shallow vestibular fossa between the vaginal orifice and the frenulum of the labia minora (Lee et al 2015). The posterior part of the vestibule of the vagina is a classic site where a fistulous opening of the rectum may be located in girls born with an imperforate anus (Bill et al 1975).
Urethra
The urethra opens into the vestibule of the vagina about 2.5 cm below the clitoris and above the vaginal orifice via the external urethral orifice, a short, sagittal cleft with slightly raised margins. The external urethral orifice is very distensible and varies in shape: the aperture can be rounded, slit-like, crescentic or stellate. The ducts of the para-urethral glands (Skene’s glands) open on each side of the lateral margins of the urethra.
Bulbs of the vestibule
The bulbs of the vestibule lie on each side of the vestibule of the vagina (Fig. 75.2B). They are two elongated masses of erectile tissue, 3 cm long, which flank the vaginal orifice and unite anterior to it by a narrow commissure of the bulbs. Their posterior ends are expanded and are in contact with the greater vestibular glands. Their deep surfaces contact the inferior aspect of the perineal membrane; superficially, each is covered by bulbospongiosus (Fig. 75.2A) (Delancey 2011).
Greater vestibular glands (Bartholin’s glands)
The greater vestibular glands are homologues of the male bulbo-urethral glands. They consist of two small, round or oval, reddish-yellow bodies that flank the vaginal orifice, in contact with and often overlapped by the posterior end of the vestibular bulb. Each opens into the vestibule of the vagina by a 2 cm long duct, situated in the groove between the hymen and the labium minus at approximately the 5 and 7 o’clock positions (Corton 2012). The glands are composed of tubulo-acinar tissue; the secretory cells are columnar and secrete a clear or whitish mucus for lubrication during sexual arousal.

Clitoris

The clitoris is an erectile structure, partially enclosed by the anterior bifurcated ends of the labia minora. It has a crus, root, body and glans (see Fig. 75.2). The body can be palpated through the skin. It contains two corpora cavernosa, composed of erectile tissue and enclosed in dense fibrous tissue, and separated medially by an incomplete fibrous pectiniform septum (septum of the corpora cavernosa). The fibrous tissue forms a suspensory ligament that is attached superiorly to the pubic symphysis. Each corpus cavernosum is attached to its ischiopubic ramus by a crus that extends from the root of the clitoris. The glans clitoris is a small, round tubercle of spongy erectile tissue at the end of the body, connected to the bulbs of the vestibule by thin bands of erectile tissue (pars intermedia). It is exposed between the anterior ends of the labia minora. Its epithelium has high cutaneous sensitivity, important in sexual responses. Congenital absence of the clitoris is very rare.

Vascular supply and lymphatic drainage of the vulva

Arteries
The arterial blood supply of the female external genitalia is derived from the superficial and deep external pudendal arteries which arise from the femoral artery superiorly and the internal pudendal artery inferiorly (Figs 75.3A, 75.4).
The blood supply to the adipose tissue of the labia majora must be carefully preserved during vaginal surgery, e.g. in creating a Martius fat pad flap to repair a vesicovaginal fistula where the blood supply has already been compromised by radiation or fibrosis (Delancey 2011).
Veins
Venous drainage of the skin of the external genitalia is via external pudendal veins to the long saphenous vein. Venous drainage of the clitoris is via deep dorsal veins to the internal pudendal vein, and via superficial dorsal veins to the external pudendal and long saphenous veins.
Lymphatic drainage
A meshwork of connecting vessels from the skin of the labia, clitoris and perineum join to form three or four collecting trunks that drain to superficial inguinal nodes lying on the cribriform fascia covering the femoral artery and vein; these nodes drain through the cribriform fascia to the deep inguinal nodes lying medial to the femoral vein (Corton 2012). The deep inguinal nodes drain via the femoral canal to the pelvic nodes (Table 75.1) . The last of the deep inguinal nodes lies under the inguinal ligament within the femoral canal and is often called Cloquet’s node. Lymph vessels from the clitoris drain directly to the deep inguinal nodes, and direct clitoral lymphatics may pass to the internal iliac nodes (Fig. 75.3B). Lymph vessels in the perineum and lower part of the labia majora drain primarily to inguinal nodes.

Innervation

The main nerve supply of the vulva is the pudendal nerve (S2, S3 and S4) and its branches, the inferior anal and perineal nerves and the dorsal nerve of the clitoris (Table 75.2, Fig. 75.5; see Fig. 75.4; Shoja et al 2013, Jackson et al 2019). The sensory innervation differs between the anterior and posterior parts of the labium majus. The anterior third of the labium majus is supplied by the ilioinguinal nerve (anterior labial nerve) (L1), the posterior two-thirds are supplied by the pudendal nerve through the posterior labial branches of the perineal nerve (S3), and the lateral aspect is also innervated by the perineal branch of the posterior femoral cutaneous nerve (S2). Nerves of the external genitalia are susceptible to trauma and inflammation, leading to external genitalia pain syndromes or vulvodynia (Shoja et al 2013). The intimate relationship of the vagina to the urinary bladder, rectum and nerves of the external genitalia predispose to pelvic pain and dyspareunia.

Vagina

The vagina is a fibromuscular tube lined by non-keratinized stratified epithelium. It extends from the vestibule of the vagina (the opening between the labia minora) to the uterus. The upper end of the vagina surrounds the vaginal projection of the cervix of the uterus. The recess formed between the vaginal part of the cervix and vagina is the fornix; the different parts of the fornix are given separate names, i.e. anterior, posterior and lateral, but they are continuous (Fig. 75.6).
In the standing position, the vagina ascends posteriorly and superiorly, forming an angle of 60–70° with the horizontal plane (Corton 2012). It forms an angle of over 90° to the uterine axis (see Fig. 75.12); this angle varies with the contents of the urinary bladder and rectum. The proximal vagina is directed posteriorly towards the ischial spines. The width of the vagina increases as it ascends. The inner surfaces of the anterior and posterior vaginal walls are in contact with each other, forming a transverse slit. The vaginal mucosa is attached to the cervix of the uterus higher on the posterior cervical wall than on the anterior; the anterior wall is approximately 7.5 cm long and the posterior wall is approximately 9 cm long. The fibromuscular anterior wall of the vagina supports the fundus of the urinary bladder in its middle and upper portions, and the urethra (which is embedded in it) inferiorly. The fibromuscular posterior wall of the vagina supports the rectum. The upper quarter of the posterior vagina is separated from the rectum by the peritoneum of the recto-uterine pouch (pouch of Douglas), and by moderately dense fibromuscular tissue in its middle half (Delancey 2011). In its lower quarter, it is separated from the anal canal by the fibromuscular perineal body. The proximal vagina is supported laterally by levator ani, together with such ligaments as the cardinal and uterosacral. Pubovaginalis provides a U-shaped muscular sling around the mid-vagina. The distal vagina is surrounded by bulbospongiosus (see Fig. 75.4). As the ureters pass anteromedially to reach the fundus of the urinary bladder they pass close to the lateral parts of the fornix, where care must be taken to avoid injury during hysterectomy (Fig. 75.7). As they enter the urinary bladder, the ureters are usually anterior to the vagina; at this point, each ureter is crossed transversely by a uterine artery (Fig. 75.8).
The vagina opens externally via a sagittal orifice positioned below the external urethral orifice (see Fig. 75.1C). The size of the vaginal orifice varies; it is capable of great distension during childbirth and, to a lesser degree, during sexual intercourse. The hymen is a thin fold of mucous membrane situated just within the vaginal orifice. The internal surfaces of the folds are normally in contact with each other and the vaginal orifice appears as a cleft between them. The hymen varies greatly in shape and dimensions. When stretched, it is anular and widest posteriorly; it can also be semilunar and concave towards the mons pubis, cribriform, fringed, absent or complete and imperforate. The hymenal ring normally ruptures after first sexual intercourse, but can rupture earlier during non-sexual physical activity. Small round carunculae hymenales are its remnants after it has been ruptured. It has no established function.
Remnants of the caudal end of the mesonephric duct (Gartner’s duct) (see Figs 22.13, 22.19) are occasionally seen protruding through the lateral parts of the fornix or lateral parts of the vagina and can cause cysts (Gartner’s cysts). Prepubertal distal longitudinal folds are common within the vagina and they disappear during puberty (Altchek et al 2008).

Vascular supply and lymphatic drainage

Arteries
The arterial supply of the vagina is derived from branches of the internal iliac arteries. Vaginal arteries anastomose with cervical branches of the uterine artery to form the midline azygos arteries of the vagina, which descend anterior and posterior to the vagina; these vessels also supply the bulb of the vestibule, fundus of the urinary bladder, and adjacent part of the rectum (see Fig. 75.3A). The uterine, internal pudendal and middle anorectal arteries may contribute to the blood supply of the vagina.
Veins
The vaginal veins, one on each side, arise from lateral venous plexuses that communicate with uterine, vesical and anorectal venous plexuses and drain to the internal iliac veins. The uterine and vaginal venous plexuses can provide collateral venous drainage for the lower limb.
Lymphatic drainage
Vaginal lymphatic vessels link with those of the cervix, rectum and vulva. They form three groups but the regions drained are not sharply demarcated. Upper vessels accompany the uterine artery to the internal and external iliac nodes; intermediate vessels accompany the vaginal artery to the internal iliac nodes; and vessels draining the vagina below the hymen, and from the vulva and perineal skin, pass to the superficial inguinal nodes (see Table 75.1).

Innervation

The distal vagina is supplied by the pudendal nerve (S2, S3 and S4). The proximal vagina is supplied by the pelvic splanchnic nerves (S2, S3 and, sometimes, S4) (see Fig. 75.5 and Table 75.2).

Developmental anomalies of the vagina

Developmental anomalies of the vagina include vaginal agenesis, absent hymen, transverse vaginal septum and persistent cloaca. Vaginal and renal agenesis, in the presence of other paramesonephric duct (Müllerian duct) anomalies, is termed Mayer–Rokitansky-Küster Hauser syndrome. An absent hymen in patients with vaginal agenesis is associated with renal agenesis (Kimberley et al 2012). A congenital transverse septum within the vagina manifests clinically during adolescence with primary amenorrhoea and haematocolpos. Children with a persistent cloaca have a congenital defect characterized by fusion of the rectum, vagina and urethra into a single common channel that varies in length from 1 to 7 cm.

Microstructure

The vagina has an external epithelial and an internal muscular layer: the epithelium adheres firmly to the muscular layer. There are two median longitudinal ridges on its epithelial surface, one anterior and the other posterior. Numerous transverse bilateral vaginal rugae extend from these vaginal columns, divided by sulci of variable depth, giving an appearance of conical papillae. These transverse rugosities are most numerous on the posterior wall and near the orifice; they increase under the influence of oestrogen during puberty and pregnancy, are especially well developed before parturition, and decrease after the menopause (Corton 2012). The surface cells are stratified squamous non-keratinized epithelium, similar to and continuous with that of the ectocervical mucosa. After puberty the epithelium thickens and its superficial cells accumulate glycogen, which gives them a clear appearance in histological preparations.
The vaginal epithelium does not change markedly during the menstrual cycle, but its glycogen content increases after ovulation and then diminishes towards the end of the cycle. Natural vaginal bacteria, particularly Lactobacillus acidophilus, break down glycogen in the desquamated cellular debris to lactic acid. This produces a highly acidic (pH 3) environment that inhibits the growth of most other micro-organisms. There is less glycogen before puberty and after the menopause, when vaginal infections are more common. There are no mucous glands, but a fluid transudate from the lamina propria, supplemented by mucus production from the cervical glands (Fig. 75.9). The muscular layers are composed of smooth muscle and consist of a thick outer longitudinal and an inner circular layer; the two layers are not distinct but are connected by oblique interlacing fibres. The longitudinal fibres are continuous with the superficial muscle fibres of the uterus. A layer of loose connective tissue containing extensive vascular plexuses surrounds the muscle layers.

Upper Genital Tract

Uterus

The uterus is a thick-walled, muscular organ situated in the pelvis between the urinary bladder and the rectum (Figs 75.10–75.12). It lies posterior to the urinary bladder and vesico-uterine pouch and anterior to the rectum and recto-uterine pouch; it is mobile, which means that its position varies with distension of the urinary bladder and rectum. The broad ligaments are lateral.
The uterus is divided structurally and functionally into two main regions: the muscular body forms the upper two-thirds, and the fibrous cervix forms the lower third. In the adult nulliparous state, the cervix usually tilts forwards relative to the axis of the vagina (anteversion), and the body of the uterus tilts forwards relative to the cervix (anteflexion) (see Fig. 75.12). In 10–15% of females the whole uterus leans posteriorly at an angle to the vagina and is said to be retroverted. A uterus that angles posteriorly on its cervix is described as retroflexed.

Body

The body of the uterus is pear-shaped and extends from the fundus superiorly to the isthmus inferiorly. The uterine tubes enter the uterus on both sides at the utero-tubal junction: the round ligament of the uterus and proper ovarian ligament are inferoanterior and inferoposterior, respectively, to each junction. The dome-like fundus is superior to the entry points of the uterine tubes and is covered by peritoneum continuous with that of neighbouring surfaces. The fundus of the uterus is in contact with the small intestine and, occasionally, with a distended sigmoid colon. The lateral margins of the body of the uterus are convex; on each side, their peritoneum is reflected laterally to form the broad ligament, which extends as a flat sheet to the pelvic wall (see Figs 75.11B, 75.16). The anterior surface of the body of the uterus is covered by peritoneum reflected on to the urinary bladder at the vesico-uterine fold (see Fig. 75.14). This normally occurs at the level of the anatomical internal os, the most inferior margin of the body of the uterus. The vesico-uterine pouch, between the urinary bladder and uterus, is obliterated when the bladder is distended, but may be occupied by small intestine when the bladder is empty. The posterior surface of the uterus is convex transversely. Its peritoneal covering continues down to the cervix and proximal vagina and is then reflected back to the rectum along the surface of the recto-uterine pouch, which lies posterior to the uterus (see Fig. 75.15). The sigmoid colon and occasionally the terminal ileum lie posterior to the uterus.
The uterine cavity usually measures 6 cm from the external os of the uterus to the wall of the fundus and is flat in its anteroposterior plane (Salardi et al 1985). In coronal section, it is triangular, broad above where the two uterine tubes join the uterus, and narrow below at the anatomical internal os of the uterus. There is no change in the size of the uterus until approximately 7 years of age, when the body of the uterus enlarges more than the cervix.

Developmental anomalies of the uterus

The paramesonephric (Müllerian) ducts can fail to fuse, resulting in a uterus that is not pear-shaped. There may only be a septum (septate uterus) or partial clefting of the uterus (bicornuate uterus); the most extreme example is a septate vagina, two cervices and two discrete uteri, each with one uterine tube (uterus didelphys) (Minto et al 2001) (see Fig. 22.15).

Cervix of the uterus

The adult, non-pregnant cervix is narrower and more cylindrical than the body of the uterus and is typically 2.5 cm long. The proximal end communicates with the isthmus of the uterus, and the distal end opens into the vagina at the external os (see Fig. 75.6). In nulliparous females the external os is usually a circular aperture, whereas it is a transverse slit after childbirth. Two longitudinal ridges, one each on its anterior and posterior walls, give off small, oblique, palmate folds that ascend laterally like the branches of a tree (arbor vitae uteri); the folds on opposing walls interdigitate to close the cervical canal. Once considered part of the cervix, the isthmus of the uterus (approximately 1 cm long) demarcates the body of the uterus from the cervix. Although unaffected in the first month of pregnancy, it is gradually taken up into the body of the uterus during the second month to form the ‘lower uterine segment’ (see below). In non-pregnant females the isthmus undergoes menstrual changes, although these are less pronounced than those occurring in the body of the uterus. The distal end of the cervix enters the proximal end of the vagina, thereby dividing the cervix into supravaginal and vaginal parts. The supravaginal part is separated anteriorly from the urinary bladder by connective tissue, the parametrium, which also passes to the sides of the cervix and laterally between the two layers of the broad ligaments.

Peritoneal folds and ligaments of the pelvis

The uterus is connected to a number of ‘ligaments’. Some are true ligaments, in that they have a fibrous composition and provide support to the uterus; some provide no support to the uterus; and others are simply folds of peritoneum.
Peritoneal folds
The parietal peritoneum is reflected over the upper genital tract to produce anterior (vesico-uterine), posterior (recto-uterine) and lateral peritoneal folds. The lateral folds are commonly called the broad ligaments (Fig. 75.13).
Vesico-uterine and rectovaginal folds
The anterior or vesico-uterine fold consists of peritoneum reflected onto the urinary bladder from the uterus at the junction of its cervix and body (Fig. 75.14). The recto-uterine fold extends lower than the anterior fold and consists of peritoneum reflected from the posterior part of the vaginal fornix on to the anterior surface of the rectum, thereby creating the recto-uterine pouch. The recto-uterine pouch is bounded anteriorly by the uterus, supravaginal part of the cervix and posterior part of the vaginal fornix; posteriorly by the rectum; and laterally by the uterosacral ligaments (Fig. 75.15).
Broad ligament
The lateral folds, or broad ligaments, extend on each side from the uterus to the lateral pelvic walls, where they become continuous with the peritoneum covering those walls (Figs 75.16–75.18). The upper border is free and the lower border is continuous with the peritoneum over the urinary bladder, rectum and side wall of the pelvis. The borders are continuous with each other at the free edge via the fundus of the uterus and diverge below near the superior surface of levator ani. A uterine tube lies in the upper free border on either side. The broad ligament is divided into an upper mesosalpinx, a posterior mesovarium and an inferior mesometrium.

Mesosalpinx and mesovarium

The mesosalpinx is attached above to the uterine tube and posteroinferiorly to the mesovarium (see Fig. 75.17). Superior and laterally it is attached to the suspensory ligament of the ovary; medially it is attached to the proper ovarian ligament. The fimbria of the uterine tube projects from its free lateral end. Between the ovary and uterine tube, the mesosalpinx contains vascular anastomoses between the uterine and ovarian vessels, the epoophoron and the paroophoron. The mesovarium projects from the posterior aspect of the broad ligament, of which it is the smaller part. It is attached to the hilum of the ovary and carries vessels and nerves to the ovary (see Fig. 75.17 and page 1321).

Mesometrium

The mesometrium is the largest part of the broad ligament and extends from the pelvic floor to the proper ovarian ligament and body of the uterus. The uterine artery passes between its two peritoneal layers typically 1.5 cm lateral to the cervix; it crosses the ureter shortly after its origin from the internal iliac artery and gives off a branch that passes superiorly to the uterine tube, where it anastomoses with the ovarian artery (Fig. 75.19). Between the pyramid formed by the infundibulum of the uterine tube, the upper pole of the ovary and the lateral pelvic wall, the mesometrium contains the ovarian vessels and nerves lying within the suspensory ligament of the ovary. This ligament continues laterally over the external iliac vessels as a distinct fold. The mesometrium also encloses the proximal part of the round ligament of the uterus, as well as smooth muscle and loose connective tissue.
Visceral ligaments of the pelvis
The visceral ligaments of the female pelvis include the round ligament of the uterus and the uterosacral, cardinal and pubocervical ligaments.
Round ligament of the uterus
Each round ligament of the uterus is a narrow band of smooth muscle 10–12 cm long, that extends from the utero-tubal junction through the broad ligament to enter the deep inguinal ring lateral to the inferior epigastric artery (see Figs 75.13, 75.16, 75.18). Although conventionally described as ending in the labium majus, a cadaveric dissection study showed that the round ligament of the uterus ended just outside the superficial inguinal ring in approximately 50% of specimens, with neither attachment nor extension to the caudal labium in any specimen (Bellier et al 2018). Near the uterus, the round ligament contains a considerable amount of smooth muscle, but this gradually diminishes and the terminal portion is purely fibrous. It also contains skeletal muscle, blood vessels, nerves and lymphatics that drain the uterine region around the utero-tubal junction to the superficial inguinal lymph nodes.
In the fetus, a projection of peritoneum (processus vaginalis) is carried with the round ligament of the uterus for a short distance into the inguinal canal. Although generally obliterated in adults, it is sometimes patent even in old age. A patent processus vaginalis in the inguinal canal is often referred to as the canal of Nuck; it can be asymptomatic or give rise to an indirect inguinal hernia or hydrocele of the canal of Nuck. In the canal, the ligament receives the same coverings as the spermatic cord, although they are thinner and blend with the ligament itself, which might not reach the mons pubis.
Uterosacral, cardinal and pubocervical ligaments
The uterosacral, cardinal and pubocervical ligaments are condensations of the visceral or endopelvic connective tissue that connect the pelvic viscera to the side wall of the pelvis; they radiate like the spokes of a wheel around the hub of the cervix of the uterus, providing it with considerable support (Fig. 75.20) (Delancey 2011). The connective tissue lateral to the uterus and the cervix, the parametrium, continues alongside the vagina as the paracolpium: it contains the vascular and connective tissues associated with the vagina. The uterosacral ligaments contain fibrous tissue and smooth muscle. They pass back from the cervix and body of the uterus on both sides of the rectum and are attached to the anterior aspect of the sacrum. They can be palpated laterally on rectal examination and can be felt as thick bands of tissue passing downwards on both sides of the posterior part of the vaginal fornix on vaginal examination. The cardinal ligaments (ligaments of Mackenrodt) extend from the sides of the cervix and lateral parts of the vaginal fornix, and are attached extensively on the pelvic wall (Eid et al 2018). The lower parts of the ureters and pelvic blood vessels traverse the cardinal ligaments. Fibres of the pubocervical ligament pass forwards from the anterior aspect of the cervix and upper vagina to diverge around the urethra, and are attached to the posterior aspect of the pubic bones.
The cardinal and uterosacral ligaments are almost vertically orientated in the standing position and maintain the near-horizontal axis of the upper vagina. The uterus and vagina are supported by the close interaction of the uterosacral and cardinal ligaments with the muscles of the pelvic floor, including the pelvic diaphragm, the perineal membrane and the perineal body. The support of the pelvic floor has been reviewed in detail by Delancey (2011) (see also Ch. 71).

Vascular supply and lymphatic drainage

Arteries
The arterial supply to the uterus comes primarily from the uterine artery (see Fig. 75.19; Fig. 75.21), which arises as a branch of the anterior division of the internal iliac artery. From its origin, the uterine artery crosses the ureter anteriorly in the broad ligament before branching as it reaches the uterus at the level of the cervico-isthmic junction (see Fig. 72.20B). One major branch ascends the uterus tortuously within the broad ligament until it reaches the region of the hilum of the ovary, where it anastomoses with branches of the ovarian artery. Another branch descends to supply the cervix and anastomoses with branches of the vaginal artery to form two median longitudinal vessels, the azygos arteries of the vagina, which descend anterior and posterior to the vagina. Although there are anastomoses with the ovarian and vaginal arteries, the dominance of the uterine artery is indicated by its marked hypertrophy during pregnancy.
The tortuosity of the vessels as they ascend in the broad ligaments is repeated in their branches within the uterine wall. Each uterine artery gives off numerous branches. These enter the uterine wall, divide and run circumferentially as groups of anterior and posterior arcuate arteries. They ramify and narrow as they approach the anterior and posterior midline so that no large vessels are present in these regions. However, the left and right arterial trees anastomose across the midline and unilateral ligation can be performed without serious effects. Terminal branches in the uterine muscle are tortuous and are called helicine arterioles. They provide a series of dense capillary plexuses in the myometrium and endometrium. From the arcuate arteries, many helical arteriolar rami pass into the endometrium. Their detailed appearance changes during the menstrual cycle. Helical arterioles are less prominent during the proliferative phase, whereas they grow in length and calibre, becoming even more tortuous, during the secretory phase.
Veins
The uterine veins extend laterally within the broad ligaments, running adjacent to the arteries and passing over the ureters. They drain into the internal iliac veins (see Fig. 75.3). The uterine venous plexus anastomoses with the vaginal and ovarian venous plexuses.
Lymphatic drainage
There are uterine lymphatics in the superficial (subperitoneal) and deep parts of the uterine wall. Collecting vessels from the body of the uterus and cervix pass laterally in the parametrium to three main groups of lymph nodes: the external and internal iliac and the obturator nodes (see Table 75.1 ). The external and internal iliac nodes surround their corresponding arteries. The obturator nodes lie in the so-called obturator fossa between the external and internal iliac vessels; the obturator nerve passes through the lower part of this group of lymph nodes.
Lymph vessels from the fundus of the uterus and the uterine tubes can accompany the lymph drainage of the ovaries to lateral aortic nodes (see Fig. 75.3B). The region surrounding the isthmus of the uterine tube can drain along the round ligament of the uterus to the superficial inguinal nodes.

Innervation

The nerve supply to the uterus is predominantly from the inferior hypogastric plexus (see Fig. 75.5) (Shoja et al 2013). Some branches ascend with, or near, the uterine arteries in the broad ligament. They supply the body of the uterus and uterine tubes and connect with tubal branches from the inferior hypogastric plexus and with the ovarian plexus. The branches to the uterus terminate in the myometrium and endometrium and usually accompany the vessels (see Table 75.2 ). Nerves to the cervix form a plexus that contains small paracervical ganglia. Branches can pass directly to the cervix of the uterus or be distributed along the vaginal arteries.
Efferent preganglionic sympathetic fibres are derived from neurones in the last thoracic and first lumbar spinal cord segments; the sites where they synapse on their postganglionic neurones are unknown but are presumably in the superior and/or inferior hypogastric plexus (Lee et al 1973). Preganglionic parasympathetic fibres arise from neurones in the second to fourth sacral spinal cord segments and relay in the paracervical ganglia. Sympathetic activity can produce uterine contraction and vasoconstriction, and parasympathetic activity can produce uterine inhibition and vasodilation, but these activities are complicated by hormonal control of uterine functions.

Microstructure

Body of the uterus
The uterus is composed of three main layers. From its lumen outwards, these are the endometrium (mucosa), myometrium (smooth muscle layer) and serosa (or adventitia) (Fig. 75.22).
Endometrium
The endometrium is formed by a layer of connective tissue, the endometrial stroma, which supports a single-layered columnar epithelium. Before puberty, the epithelium is ciliated and cuboidal. It contains glands that are composed largely of columnar cells secreting glycoproteins and glycogen. After puberty, the structure of the endometrium varies with the stage of the menstrual cycle (see below). The glands are tubular, run perpendicular to the luminal surface and penetrate up to the myometrial layer. The stroma consists of a highly cellular connective tissue between the endometrial glands and contains blood and lymphatic vessels.
Myometrium
The myometrium is composed of smooth muscle and loose connective tissue and contains blood vessels, lymphatic vessels and nerves. It is dense and thick at the uterine midlevel and fundus but thin at the uterine ostium of the uterine tube. The body of the uterus has four muscular layers, submucosal, vascular, supravascular and subserosal. The submucosal (innermost) layer is composed of longitudinal and some oblique smooth muscle fibres. The vascular layer is external to the submucosal layer and is rich in blood vessels and longitudinal muscle. It is succeeded by the supravascular muscle layer where the muscle fibres are predominantly circular. The outer, thin longitudinal muscle layer, the subserosal muscle layer, lies adjacent to the serosa.
The muscular fibres of the supravascular and subserosal layers converge at the lateral angles of the uterus and continue into the uterine tubes. Some fibres enter the broad ligaments as the round ligament of the uterus and the proper ovarian ligament, while others turn back into the uterosacral ligament. At the junction between the body and the cervix, the smooth muscle merges with dense, irregular connective tissue containing both collagen and elastin and forms most of the wall of the cervix. Bilateral longitudinal fibres extend in the lateral submucosal layer to the cervix. Their muscle fibres differ structurally from those of typical myometrium, and they may provide fast conducting pathways that coordinate the contractile activities of the uterine wall.
Serosa
The body of the uterus is covered by serosa, which continues downwards posteriorly to cover the supravaginal part of the cervix. The anterior cervix and the lateral surfaces of the body and cervix are not covered by peritoneum.
Cervix of the uterus
The cervix consists of fibroelastic connective tissue and contains relatively little (10%) smooth muscle. The elastin component of the cervical stroma is essential for the stretching capacity of the cervix during childbirth. The cervical canal is lined by a deeply folded mucosa with a surface epithelium of columnar mucous cells (Fig. 75.23). Branched tubular glands present within the mucosa are lined by a similar secretory epithelium. These glands extend obliquely upwards and outwards from the canal. They secrete clear, alkaline mucus, which is relatively viscous except at the midpoint of the menstrual cycle, when it becomes more copious and less viscous to encourage the passage of sperm. At the vaginal end of the cervix, the aperture of a gland may be blocked and it then fills with mucus to form a Nabothian follicle (or cyst). None of the mucosa is shed during menstruation, so unlike the body of the uterus it is not divided into functional and basal layers and lacks spiral arteries. The surface of the vaginal part of the cervix (ectocervical mucosa) is covered by stratified squamous non-keratinized epithelium, which contains glycogen.
The squamocolumnar junction, where the columnar secretory epithelium of the endocervical canal meets the stratified squamous covering of the ectocervical mucosa, is located at the external os of the uterus before puberty. As oestrogen levels rise during puberty the cervical os opens, exposing the endocervical columnar epithelium on to the ectocervical mucosa. This area of columnar cells on the ectocervical mucosa forms an area that is red and raw in appearance, called an ectropion (cervical erosion). It is then exposed to the acidic environment of the vagina and, through a process of squamous metaplasia (Ch. 2), transforms into stratified squamous epithelium. This area is thus known as the ‘transformation zone’ (Fig. 75.24). Other hyperoestrogenic states, such as pregnancy and the use of oral contraceptive pills, can also result in an ectropion. This area is the most usual site of cervical intraepithelial neoplasia (CIN), which can progress to malignancy. In postmenopausal females, the squamocolumnar junction recedes into the cervical canal.

Magnetic resonance imaging of the uterus and vagina

Although MRI has limited clinical value for viewing the uterus and vagina, on T2-weighted magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) the uterus displays a zonal anatomy with three distinct zones: the endometrium, junctional zone and myometrium (Fig. 75.25) (Minto et al 2001). The endometrium and uterine cavity appear as a high-signal stripe; its thickness varies with the stage of the menstrual cycle. In the early proliferative phase it measures up to 5 mm, and widens to up to 1 cm in the mid-secretory phase. A low signal band, the junctional zone, borders the endometrium and represents the inner myometrium. It is of constant thickness and signal throughout the menstrual cycle and usually measures 5 mm. The outer myometrium is of medium-signal intensity during the proliferative phase and of high-signal intensity during the mid-secretory phase because of the increased vascularity and prominence of the arcuate vessels.
The uterus is smaller (only 4 cm in length) in prepubertal females, and on T2-weighted MRI the endometrium is minimal or absent and the junctional zone indistinct. In postmenopausal females the body of the uterus shrinks and the zonal anatomy is indistinct.
On T2-weighted MRI, the cervix has an inner, low-signal stroma continuous with the junctional zone of the uterus. This is often surrounded by an outer zone of intermediate signal intensity continuous with the outer myometrium. The appearances do not change with the menstrual cycle or oral contraceptive pill use. The central stripe is very high-signal and is a consequence of the secretions produced by the cervical glands.
The vagina is best seen on T2-weighted MRI as a thin layer of high-signal intensity of the mucosa and an outer low-signal layer of the submucosa and muscular layer (see Fig. 75.25). MRI accurately demonstrates congenital and acquired Müllerian duct anomalies including uterine and vaginal aplasia, duplication and septa (Grant et al 2010). Vaginal and perivaginal cysts are also well visualized with T2-weighted MRI.

Uterine (Fallopian) tubes

The uterine tubes are attached to the upper part of the body of the uterus and their ostia open into the uterine cavity (Figs 75.26–75.28). The medial opening of the tube (uterine ostium of the uterine tube) is located at the superior angle of the uterine cavity. The tube passes laterally and superiorly and consists of four parts: uterine part, isthmus, ampulla and fimbria (see Fig. 75.22). The uterine part is 0.7 mm wide and 1 cm long and lies within the myometrium. It is continuous laterally with the isthmus, which is 1–5 mm wide and 3 cm long; it is rounded, muscular and firm. The isthmus is continuous laterally with the ampulla, the widest portion of the uterine tube with a maximum luminal diameter of 1 cm. The ampulla is 5 cm long and has a thin wall and a tortuously folded luminal surface. Typically, fertilization takes place in its lumen. The ampulla opens into the trumpet-shaped infundibulum at the abdominal ostium of the uterine tube. Fimbriae, numerous mucosal finger-like folds 1 mm wide, are attached to the ends of the infundibulum and extend from its inner circumference beyond the muscular wall of the uterine tube. One of these, the ovarian fimbria, is longer and more deeply grooved than the others and is typically applied to the tubal extremity of the ovary. At the time of ovulation, the fimbriae swell and extend as a result of engorgement of the vessels in the lamina propria, which aids capture of the released oocyte. All fimbriae are covered, like the mucosal lining throughout the tube, by a ciliated epithelium whose cilia beat towards the ampulla of the uterine tube.

Vascular supply and lymphatic drainage

Arteries
The blood supply to the uterine tubes is derived from ovarian and uterine arteries (see Fig. 75.21A). The lateral third of the tube is supplied by the ovarian artery, which continues in the mesosalpinx to anastomose with branches from the uterine artery. The medial two-thirds of the uterine tube are supplied by the uterine artery.
Veins
Venous drainage is similar to the arterial supply. The venous drainage of the lateral two-thirds of the uterine tube is via the pampiniform plexus to the ovarian veins, which open into the inferior vena cava on the right side and the renal vein on the left. The medial two-thirds of the uterine tube drain via the uterine plexus to the internal iliac vein.
Lymphatic drainage
Lymph drainage is via ovarian vessels to the lateral aortic nodes and uterine vessels to the internal iliac nodes. It is possible for lymph to reach the inguinal nodes via the round ligament of the uterus (see Table 75.1 ).

Innervation

The uterine tubes are innervated by autonomic fibres that are distributed mainly with the ovarian and uterine arteries. Most of the tube has a dual sympathetic and parasympathetic supply (see Table 75.2 ). Preganglionic parasympathetic fibres are derived from the pelvic splanchnic nerves. The preganglionic sympathetic supply is derived from neurones in the intermediolateral nuclei of the tenth thoracic to the second lumbar spinal cord segments; postganglionic sympathetic fibres are most likely derived from the superior hypogastric plexus via the hypogastric nerves (see Fig. 75.5) (Chen et al 2014). Visceral afferent fibres travel with the sympathetic nerves and enter the spinal cord through corresponding dorsal roots; they can also travel with parasympathetic fibres. The ampullary submucosa contains modified Pacinian corpuscles.

Microstructure

The walls of the uterine tubes show typical visceral mucosal, muscular and serosal layers (Fig. 75.29). The mucosa is thrown into longitudinal folds, which are most pronounced distally at the infundibulum and decrease to shallow bulges in the uterine part. The mucosa is lined by a single-layered, tall, columnar epithelium, which contains mainly ciliated cells and secretory (peg) cells (so called because they project into the lumen further than their ciliated neighbours), and occasional intraepithelial lymphocytes. In the uterine tube, ciliated cells predominate distally and secretory cells proximally. Their activities vary with the stage in the menstrual cycle and with age. Secretory cells are most active around the time of ovulation. Their secretions include nutrients for the gametes and aid capacitation of the spermatozoa. Ciliated cells increase in height and develop more cilia during the oestrogenic first half of the menstrual cycle. Their cilia waft the oocyte from the open-ended infundibulum towards the uterus in fluids secreted by the peg cells. The epithelium regresses in height towards the end of the cycle and postmenopausally, when ciliated cells are reduced in number.
The lamina propria provides vascular connective tissue support and abundant lymphatic drainage vessels. The smooth muscle of the muscular layer is arranged as an inner circular or spiral layer and an outer, thinner longitudinal layer. Together, their contractile activity produces peristaltic movements of the uterine tube, which assist propulsion of the gametes and the fertilised ovum. The uterine tubes are covered externally by a highly vascular serosa.

Ovaries

In the non-pregnant adult, the ovaries lie on each side of the uterus close to the lateral pelvic wall. They are suspended in the pelvic cavity by a double fold of peritoneum, the mesovarium, which is attached to the upper limit of the posterior aspect of the broad ligament. They are dull white in colour and consist of dense fibrous tissue, in which ova are embedded. Before regular ovulation begins they have a smooth surface, but their surfaces are distorted by scarring that follows the degeneration of successive corpora lutea, thereafter (Fritz and Speroff 2011) (Fig. 75.30). In the neonate, their dimensions are 1.3×0.6×0.4 cm. Before the first menstrual period (menarche) the ovaries are about one-third of the normal reproductive adult size. They gradually increase in size with body growth (Sforza et al 1993, Badouraki et al 2008): their average dimensions are 4×2×3 cm in reproductively mature females. The ovaries more than double in size during pregnancy and shrink significantly after menopause.
The lateral surface of the ovary contacts parietal peritoneum in the ovarian fossa. Behind the ovarian fossa are retroperitoneal structures including the ureter, internal iliac vessels, obturator vessels and nerve, and the origin of the uterine artery. The medial surface faces the uterus and uterine vessels in the broad ligament. Above the superior extremity are the fimbria and distal section of the uterine tube. The inferior extremity points downwards towards the pelvic floor. The mesovarian border faces the posterior leaf of the broad ligament. The free border faces the peritoneum, which overlies the upper part of the internal iliac artery and vein, and the ureter. On the right side, superior and lateral to the ovary, are the ileocaecal junction, caecum and appendix. On the left side, the sigmoid colon passes over the superior aspect of the ovary and joins the rectum, which lies between the medial surfaces of the two ovaries. The mesosalpinx lies below the uterine tube. The proper ovarian ligament is inferior and medial. The mesovarium and ovary lie inferiorly at its fimbrial end. The round ligament of the uterus is anterior to the uterine tube. The superior and posterior surfaces of the uterine tube lie free in the peritoneal cavity.
During embryonic and early fetal life the ovaries are situated in the lumbar region near the kidneys. They gradually descend along the gubernaculum, stopping at the lesser pelvis. The relationship between the location of the ovaries and surface landmarks is important for enabling the ovaries to be shielded from exposure to radiation during radiological procedures. In girls the ovaries lie at or below the iliac crest, just medial to the anterior superior iliac spine, and they lie above the pubic symphysis at all ages (Bardo et al 2009). There may be accessory ovarian tissue in the mesovarium or along the course of the gubernacula; rarely, the ovaries descend along the whole course of the gubernacula and are found in the labia majora. During pregnancy the ovaries are lifted high in the pelvis; by 14 weeks of gestation they become partly abdominal structures, and by the third trimester they are totally abdominal structures and lie vertically behind and lateral to the parous uterus.

Peritoneal and ligamentous supports of the ovary

The peritoneal and ligamentous supports of the ovary consist of the suspensory ligament of the ovary, proper ovarian ligament and the mesovarium.
Suspensory ligament of the ovary
The suspensory ligament of the ovary is a peritoneal fold attached to the upper part of the lateral surface of the ovary, which contains the ovarian vessels and nerves (see Fig. 75.18). On the right side, the suspensory ligament of the ovary is attached to a fold of peritoneum that is posterior and inferior to the caecum and appendix. The peritoneal attachment is higher on the left side than the right and is lateral to the junction of the descending and sigmoid colons. As it joins the peritoneum covering psoas major, the suspensory ligament of the ovary passes superiorly over the external iliac vessels, genitofemoral nerve and ureter.
Proper ovarian ligament
The proper ovarian ligament attaches the uterine (inferomedial) extremity of the ovary to the lateral angle of the uterus, posteroinferior to the uterine tube (see Fig. 75.22). It lies in the posterior leaf of the broad ligament and contains some smooth muscle cells. It is continuous with the medial border of the round ligament.
Mesovarium
The mesovarium is a short peritoneal fold that attaches the ovary to the back of the broad ligament. It carries blood vessels and nerves to the hilum of the ovary. The uterine tube arches over the ovary and ascends in relation to its mesovarian border, then curves over its tubal end and passes down on its posterior, free, border and medial surface.

Vascular supply and lymphatic drainage

Arteries
The ovarian arteries are branches of the abdominal aorta and originate below the renal arteries. Each descends behind the peritoneum and, at the linea terminalis, crosses the external iliac artery and vein to enter the lesser pelvis. Here, the artery turns medially in the suspensory ligament of the ovary and splits into a branch to the mesovarium that supplies the ovary and a branch that continues into the broad ligament, below the uterine tube, and supplies the tube. There are variations in the site of the division to the ovary and tube (see Fig. 75.21). On each side a branch passes lateral to the uterus to unite with the uterine artery. Other branches accompany the round ligaments through the inguinal canal to the skin of the labium majus and the inguinal region. Early in intrauterine life, the ovaries flank the vertebral column inferior to the kidneys, so the ovarian arteries are relatively short; they gradually lengthen as the ovaries descend into the pelvis.
Veins
The ovarian veins emerge from the ovary as a plexus (pampiniform plexus) in the mesovarium and suspensory ligament of the ovary (see Fig. 75.3A). Two veins emerge from the plexus and ascend with the ovarian artery; they usually merge into a single vessel before entering either the inferior vena cava on the right side or the renal vein on the left.
Lymphatic drainage
The main lymphatic drainage of the ovaries is along vessels that follow the ovarian veins to lateral aortic nodes situated near the origin of the renal arteries (see Table 75.1 ). There can also be drainage via pelvic nodes into lower lateral aortic nodes and, less commonly, via the round ligament of the uterus to the inguinal nodes (Kleppe et al 2015).

Innervation

The ovarian innervation is derived from autonomic plexuses (see Table 75.2 ). The upper part of the ovarian plexus is formed from branches of the renal and aortic plexuses and the lower part is reinforced from the superior and inferior hypogastric plexuses. These plexuses consist of postganglionic sympathetic fibres, preganglionic parasympathetic fibres from the pelvic splanchnic nerves, and visceral afferent fibres (Lee et al 1973). The efferent preganglionic sympathetic fibres are derived from the tenth and eleventh thoracic spinal segments.

Microstructure

In young females the surface of the ovary is covered by a single layer of cuboidal epithelium, which contains some flatter cells. It appears dull white, in contrast to the shiny, smooth peritoneal mesothelial covering of the mesovarium, with which it is continuous. A white line around the anterior mesovarian border usually marks the transition between peritoneum and ovarian epithelium. The surface epithelium is also termed the germinal epithelium, but this is a misnomer because it is not the source of germ cells. Immediately beneath the epithelium there is a tough collagenous coat, the tunica albuginea. The ovarian tissue it surrounds is divisible into a cortex, containing the ovarian follicles, and a medulla, which receives the ovarian vessels and nerves at the hilum.
Ovarian cortex
Before puberty the cortex forms 35%, the medulla 20% and interstitial cells up to 45% of the volume of the ovary. After puberty, the cortex forms the major part of the ovary, enclosing the medulla except at the hilum. The cortex contains the ovarian follicles at various stages of development, and corpora lutea and their degenerative remnants, depending on age or stage of the menstrual cycle. The follicles and the structures derived from them are embedded in a dense stroma comprising a meshwork of thin collagen fibres and fusiform fibroblast-like cells, arranged in characteristic whorls. Stromal cells differ from fibroblasts in general connective tissue in that they contain lipid droplets, which accumulate during pregnancy. Stromal cells give rise to the thecal layers of maturing ovarian follicles.
Ovarian follicles

Primordial follicles

The development of ovarian follicles can be divided into different stages (Fig. 75.31). At birth, the ovarian cortex contains a superficial zone of primordial follicles. These consist of primary oocytes 25 μm in diameter, each surrounded by a single layer of flat follicular cells. The oocyte nuclei are slightly eccentric and have a characteristically prominent nucleolus. They contain the diploid number of chromosomes (duplicated as sister chromatids), arrested at the diplotene stage of meiotic prophase after 16–20 weeks of fetal life (Fritz and Speroff 2011) (Fig. 75.32). Many follicles degenerate either during prepubertal (including prenatal) life, or through atresia at some stage after beginning the process of cyclical maturation during the child-bearing period. Their remnants are visible as atretic follicles, the remains of which accumulate throughout the period of reproductive life. After puberty, cohorts of up to 20 primordial follicles become activated in each menstrual cycle (fewer are activated with advancing age). Their development takes a number of cycles. Among the follicles activated in each cohort, usually only one from one or the other ovary becomes dominant, reaches maturity and releases its oocyte at ovulation.
Abnormal, non-growing follicles with indistinct germinal vesicle membranes and absent nucleoli are sometimes found in prepubertal ovaries and occasionally in pubertal ovaries, but not in adult ovaries (Anderson et al 2014).

Primary follicles

Primary follicles develop from primordial follicles. The first sign is a change in the follicle cells from flattened to cuboidal, followed by their proliferation to give rise to a follicle consisting of a wall of granulosa cells surrounded by a thick basal lamina (Fig. 75.33).

Secondary follicles

Granulosa cell proliferation continues to form a multilayered follicular wall. Stromal cells immediately surrounding the follicle begin to differentiate into spindle-shaped cells that constitute the theca folliculi and which will become the theca interna. They are later surrounded by a more fibrous layer, the theca externa. At the same time, the oocyte increases in size and secretes a thick layer of extracellular proteoglycan-rich material – the zona pellucida – between its plasma membrane and the surrounding granulosa cells of the early follicle; this is important for the process of fertilization. The granulosa cells in contact with the zona pellucida send cytoplasmic processes radially inwards; these contact and communicate with oocyte microvilli at gap junctions (Motta et al 2003).

Antral follicles

Antral follicles develop from secondary follicles; the transition is not well defined and is a continuous process. The number of granulosa cells continues to increase. Cavities begin to form between them during a pre-antral phase and are filled with a clear fluid (follicular fluid), derived largely as an exudate from the thecal vasculature, containing hyaluronate, growth factors and steroid hormones secreted by the granulosa cells. The follicle is now about 200 μm in diameter and usually lies deep in the cortex. The cavities coalesce to form one large fluid-filled space, the antrum, which is surrounded by a thin uniform layer of granulosa cells, except at one pole of the follicle where a thickened granulosa layer envelops the eccentrically placed oocyte to form the cumulus oophorus. The oocyte has now reached its maximum size of about 160 μm (including the zona pellucida) and the inner and outer thecae are clearly differentiated (Fig. 75.34). As follicles mature, the theca interna becomes more prominent and its cells more rounded and typical of steroid-secreting endocrine cells. They produce androstenedione, from which the granulosa cells synthesize oestrogens (primarily oestradiol). Follicular development is stimulated by follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH).
Preovulatory (tertiary, Graafian) follicle
Although a number of follicles can progress to the secondary stage by about the first week of a menstrual cycle, usually only one follicle develops to maturity; the remainder become atretic. The surviving follicle increases considerably in size as the antrum takes up fluid from the surrounding tissues and expands to a diameter of 2 cm. The term ‘Graafian follicle’ is often used to describe this mature follicular stage (vesicular ovarian follicle). The oocyte with a surrounding ring of tightly adherent cells, the corona radiata, breaks away from the follicle wall and floats freely in the follicular fluid. The primary oocyte, which has remained in the first meiotic prophase since fetal life (see Fig. 1.24), completes its first meiotic division to produce the almost equally large secondary oocyte and a small first polar body with very little cytoplasm. The secondary haploid oocyte immediately begins its second meiotic division, but when it reaches metaphase the process is arrested until fertilization has occurred. The follicle moves to the superficial cortex, causing the surface of the ovary to bulge. The tissues at the point of contact (the stigma) with the tough tunica albuginea and ovarian surface epithelium are eroded until the follicle ruptures and its contents are released into the peritoneal cavity for capture by the fimbriae of the uterine tube. The oocyte at ovulation is still surrounded by its zona pellucida and corona radiata of granulosa cells. If fertilization does not occur, it begins to degenerate after 24–48 hours.

Corpus luteum

After ovulation, the remainder of the follicle becomes the corpus luteum (Fig. 75.35). The walls of the empty follicle collapse and fold. The granulosa cells increase in size and synthesize a cytoplasmic carotenoid pigment (lutein), giving them a yellowish colour (hence, corpus luteum). These large (30–50 μm) granulosa lutein cells form most of the corpus luteum. The basal lamina surrounding the follicle breaks down and numerous smaller theca lutein cells infiltrate the folds of the cellular mass, accompanied by capillaries and connective tissue. Extravasated blood from thecal capillaries accumulates in the centre as a small clot, but this rapidly resolves and is replaced by connective tissue. All lutein cells have a cytoplasm filled with abundant smooth endoplasmic reticulum, characteristic of steroid-synthesizing endocrine cells. Granulosa lutein cells secrete progesterone and oestradiol (from aromatization of the androstenedione synthesized by theca lutein cells). The two cell types also respond differently to circulating gonadotropins: theca lutein cells respond to luteinizing hormone (LH) and granulose lutein cells to FSH. Theca lutein cells express receptors for human chorionic gonadotrophin (hCG). If the oocyte is not fertilized, the corpus luteum (of menstruation) functions for 12–14 days after ovulation, then atrophies. The lutein cells undergo fatty degeneration, autolysis, removal by macrophages and gradual replacement with fibrous tissue. Eventually, after 2 months, a small, whitish, scar-like corpus albicans is all that remains.
If fertilization does occur, implantation of the blastocyst into the uterine endometrium usually begins 7 days later and the embryonic trophoblast then starts to produce hCG. The chorionic gonadotrophin stimulates the corpus luteum of menstruation to grow and it becomes a corpus luteum of pregnancy. It normally increases in diameter from 10 mm to 25 mm at 8 weeks of gestation and can be seen clearly on ultrasound. It secretes progesterone, oestrogen and relaxin and functions throughout pregnancy, although it gradually regresses as its endocrine functions are largely replaced by the placenta after 8 weeks’ gestation. Its diameter is reduced by the end of pregnancy to 1 cm. Over the next few months it degenerates, like the corpus luteum of menstruation, to form a corpus albicans.
Ovarian medulla
The ovarian medulla is highly vascular. It contains numerous veins and spiral arteries that enter the hilum from the mesovarium and lie within a loose connective tissue stroma (Delson et al 1948). Small numbers of cells (hilus cells) with characteristics similar to interstitial (Leydig) cells in the testis are found in the medulla at the hilum; they may be a source of androgens.
Menopause
At the menopause, ovulation ceases and various microscopic changes ensue within the ovarian tissues. The stroma becomes denser, the tunica albuginea thickens and the ovarian surface epithelium thins. However, many follicles persist within the cortex.

Menstrual Cycle

The onset of menstruation (menarche) occurs between the tenth and seventeenth years of life, with a peak between 12 and 14 years. The uterus, ovaries and vagina undergo changes during the menstrual cycle, which normally lasts approximately 28 days. Usually, a single mature follicle ruptures mid-cycle (approximately day 14 of a 28-day cycle) to release a secondary oocyte. The wall of the follicle is then transformed into the corpus luteum, which secretes progesterone (luteal phase). About 10 days after ovulation the corpus luteum begins to regress, then it ceases to function and is replaced by fibrous tissue. The breakdown of the endometrium that follows this cessation of function is due to the falling levels of progesterone and oestrogen as the corpus luteum degenerates.
The changes that occur in the endometrium during the menstrual cycle can be divided into three phases: proliferative, secretory and menstrual (Fig. 75.36).

Proliferative phase

During the early proliferative phase, even before the menstrual flow ceases, the epithelium from the persisting basal parts of the uterine glands grows over the surface of the endometrium, which has been denuded by menstruation. Re-epithelialization is complete 5–6 days after the start of menstruation. Initially, the tissue is only 1–2  mm thick and is lined by low cuboidal epithelium. The glands are straight and narrow and their lining cells are low columnar. The apical cell surface contains microvilli; some cells are ciliated. The stroma is dense and contains small numbers of lymphocytes. During days 10–12 of the proliferative phase, the endometrium thickens. Cells divide in response to rising levels of oestrogen, which is produced by the ovary and acts through receptors present on both the stromal and the epithelial cells. The glands become tortuous and their lining epithelial cells become tall columnar in nature (Fritz and Speroff 2011).

Secretory phase

The secretory phase coincides with the luteal phase of the ovarian cycle. The endometrial changes are driven by progesterone and oestrogen, secreted by the corpus luteum. Steroid receptors in the endometrium activate a programme of new gene expression that over the following 7 days produces a highly regulated sequence of differentiative events, presumably required to prepare the tissue for blastocyst implantation. Part of the response is direct, but there is evidence that some of the effects are mediated through growth factors (Fritz and Speroff 2011). The first morphological effects of progesterone are evident 24–36 hours after ovulation (which occurs approximately 14 days before the next menstrual flow). During the early secretory phase, glycogen masses accumulate in the basal cytoplasm of the epithelial cells lining the glands, where they are often associated with lipids; giant mitochondria appear. There is an obviously increased polarization of the gland cells: nuclei are displaced towards the centre of the cells, and Golgi apparatus and secretory vesicles accumulate in the supranuclear cytoplasm. The nuclear channel system is prominent and the nuclei enlarge. Nascent secretory products can be detected immunohistochemically within the cells. Progestational effects on the stroma (known as the decidual reaction) are also evident in the early secretory phase. The nuclei are enlarged and the packing density of the resident stromal cells increases, due in part to the increase in volume of gland lumina and the onset of secretory activity in the epithelial compartment.
By the mid-secretory phase, the endometrium can be up to 6 mm deep. The basal epithelial glycogen mass is progressively transferred to the apical cytoplasm and nuclei return to the cell bases. The Golgi apparatus dilates and produces glycogen, mucin and other glycoproteins, secreted from the glandular epithelium into the lumen by a combination of apocrine and merocrine mechanisms; this activity reaches a maximum 6 days after ovulation. These secretory changes are considerably less pronounced in the basal gland cells and the luminal epithelium than in the glandular cell population of the functional layer. There is a notable stromal oedema and a corresponding decrease in the density of collagen fibrils. At the same time, the endoplasmic reticulum and Golgi apparatus become more prominent, and collagen is synthesized and degraded, presumably reflecting ongoing matrix remodelling.
During the late secretory phase, glandular secretory activity declines. Decidual differentiation occurs in the superficial stromal cells that surround blood vessels; this transformation includes nuclear rounding and an increased cytoplasmic volume, reflecting an increase in and dilation of the rough endoplasmic reticulum and Golgi systems, and cytoplasmic accumulation of lipid droplets and glycogen. The cells begin to produce basal lamina components including laminin and type IV collagen.

Ultrasound imaging of the uterus and its contents

Ultrasound is frequently used in the clinical evaluation of the endometrium. During the early secretory phase the endometrium is identified as a thin echogenic line, a consequence of specular reflection from the interface between its opposing surfaces. During the late proliferative phase the endometrium appears as a triple layer: a central echogenic line (produced by the apposed endometrial surfaces), surrounded by a thicker hypoechoic functional layer and bounded by an outer echogenic basal layer. During the secretory phase, the functional layer surrounding the echogenic line becomes more hyperechoic because of the increased mucus and glycogen within the glands and the increased number of interfaces caused by the development of tortuous spiral arteries (Fig. 75.37).

Menstrual phase

The premenstrual endometrium consists of two layers, an upper two-thirds, the functional layer (which will be shed), overlying the basal layer (which is retained). In the upper part of the functional layer (the compact layer), the necks of the glands are only slightly expanded and the stromal cells show a distinct decidual reaction. In the deeper part of the functional layer (the spongy layer) the uterine glands are tortuous, dilated and, ultimately, separated from one another by a small amount of connective tissue. The basal layer, next to the myometrium, is thin and contains the tips of the uterine glands embedded in an unaltered stroma.
As the corpus luteum regresses, those parts of the stroma showing a decidual reaction, together with the glandular epithelium, undergo degenerative changes and the endometrium often thins. Blood escapes from the superficial vessels of the endometrium, forming small haematomata beneath the surface epithelium (see below). The functional layer, next to the free surface, is shed piecemeal, leaving mainly the basal layer adjacent to the myometrium; 65–75% of the thickness of the endometrium can be shed. Blood and necrotic endometrium then begin to appear in the uterine lumen, to be discharged from the uterus through the vagina as the menstrual flow, which usually lasts 3–6 days.

Vascular changes within the uterus during the menstrual cycle

The vascular bed of the endometrium undergoes significant changes during the menstrual cycle. The arteries to the endometrium arise from a myometrial plexus and consist of short, straight vessels to the basal portion of the endometrium and more muscular spiral arteries to its superficial two-thirds. The venous drainage, consisting of narrow perpendicular vessels that anastomose by cross-branches, is common to both the superficial and the basal layers of the endometrium. The capillary bed consists of an endothelium with a basal lamina that is discontinuous during the proliferative phase but becomes more distinct by the mid-secretory phase. Pericytes make contact with the endothelial cells via cytoplasmic extensions that project through the basal lamina. Enlargement of the pericytes starts early in the secretory phase and leads to a conspicuous cuff of cells in the mid- and late secretory phases. The arterial supply to the basal part of the endometrium remains unchanged throughout the menstrual cycle. However, the spiral arteries supplying the compact (superficial) layer lengthen disproportionately, becoming increasingly coiled as their tips approach the uterine epithelium. This leads to a slowing of the circulation in the compact layer with some vasodilation. Immediately before the menstrual flow, these vessels begin to constrict intermittently, causing stasis of the blood and anaemia of the compact layer. During the periods of relaxation of the vessels, blood escapes from the devitalized capillaries and veins, causing the menstrual blood loss. The breakdown of the endometrium is a consequence of the falling levels of progesterone and oestrogen as the corpus luteum degenerates.

Pregnancy and Parturition

During pregnancy many morphological changes occur in the female reproductive system and associated abdominal structures. The uterus enlarges to accommodate the developing fetus and placenta, and various alterations take place in the pelvic walls, floor and contents that allow for this expansion, and which anticipate parturition. At the end of gestation dramatic changes occur that facilitate the passage of the baby through the birth canal and, subsequently, allow the pelvic organs to return to the non-pregnant state (involution).
On average, human gestation is approximately 280 days (or 40 weeks) from the date of the last menstrual period (LMP): the duration of pregnancy can be expressed in postmenstrual weeks (Ch. 23). However, cycle irregularity or inaccurate recollection can lead to an inaccurate menstrual history: an accurate gestational age can be calculated by ultrasound measurement of the fetal crown–rump length during the first trimester (Fig. 75.38). A discrepancy of more than 5–7 days from the LMP-estimated age is usually reason to revise the estimated date of confinement. For females presenting after the first trimester, gestational dating can be based on other sonographically obtained fetal biometric measurements, such as the fetal biparietal diameter, head circumference and femur length, although precision gradually decreases in later pregnancy (see Fig. 23.5). The increased accuracy of gestational dating using sonographic fetal biometry rather than menstrual history alone has significantly reduced the number of pregnancies requiring induction of labour for post-term females.

Uterus in pregnancy

The function of the uterus in pregnancy is to retain the developing fetus and to provide a protected environment until a stage at which the fetus is capable of surviving ex utero. The uterus must grow, facilitate delivery of the fetus and then involute.
The uterus grows dramatically during pregnancy, increasing in weight from about 50 g at the beginning of pregnancy to up to 1 kg at term. Most of the weight gain is the result of increased vascularity and fluid retention by the myometrium. The increased growth of the uterine wall is driven by a combination of mechanical stretching and endocrine input. The mechanical load imposed on the uterine wall as gestation advances induces hypertrophy of uterine smooth muscle cells and is the major stimulus for increasing smooth muscle mass. There is some hyperplasia early in pregnancy, mainly from the growth of the media of the myometrial arteries and veins. The myometrium is relatively unresponsive to additional endocrine stimulation during most of pregnancy, a relative quiescence that is in part attributed to progesterone. However, a number of growth factors, e.g. insulin-like growth factor-1 (IGF-1), have been identified, which interact with oestrogen in promoting uterine growth. The myometrium thins with advancing gestation from 2–3 cm thick in early pregnancy to 1–2 cm at term.
The upper third of the cervix is gradually taken up into the body of the uterus during the second month to form the ‘lower segment’ (Fig. 75.39). The isthmus of the uterus hypertrophies like the body of the uterus during the first trimester and triples in length to about 3 cm. From the second trimester, the wall of the isthmus and that of the body are the same thickness and their junction is no longer visible externally. This condition persists until the middle of the third trimester, when the lower uterine segment thins to less than 1 cm in thickness. The thicker myometrium above this transition is the main contractile portion of the uterus that will generate the expulsive forces during labour. The thin lower uterine segment, which is thought to correspond to the level of the anatomical internal os, is just superior to the bladder and the reflections of the vesico-uterine pouch. It is more compliant and allows for the descent of the fetus in late pregnancy and during labour: it is the preferred site of incision during a caesarean section because it is thinner and less vascular than the upper uterus. In addition, because it is less contractile, there is a lower risk of uterine rupture in subsequent pregnancies than with an incision made in the body of the uterus during a ‘classic’ caesarean section.
The growing uterus generally emerges from the pelvis by postmenstrual week 12 and can be palpated on the maternal abdomen just above the pubic symphysis. By the 20th week of pregnancy the uterine fundus is at the level of the umbilicus and it reaches the xiphoid process of the sternum by 36 weeks. After 24 weeks, the distance between the pubic symphysis and the fundus of the uterus generally corresponds to the number of postmenstrual weeks and is often used in clinical care as a screening method to detect a pregnancy that is measuring suspiciously larger or smaller than expected. If there is more than a 2 cm discrepancy between uterine size and gestational week, a more accurate, sonographic assessment of fetal size and amniotic fluid volume is indicated.

Cervix of the uterus during pregnancy

During pregnancy the cervix of the uterus is a relatively rigid, fibromuscular structure that retains the developing fetus within the uterus. The rigidity of the cervix appears to be related to the orientation of its collagen fibres within a regular connective tissue matrix. The cervix gradually softens and shortens in the weeks preceding labour. During active labour, the cervix dilates to allow the fetus to descend through the birth canal. The exact processes that allow softening, effacement and dilation of the cervix are unclear, but are believed to include remodelling of the connective tissue matrix, probably mediated by increased activities of enzymes such as matrix metalloproteinase-1, reduced collagen concentration, a significant increase in the water content in the cervix, macrophage and neutrophil infiltration, and an increased level of apoptosis.
Transvaginal ultrasound examination allows for a robust evaluation of the cervix during pregnancy. Cervical length, dilation of the anatomical internal os and bulging of the membranes into the cervical canal can all be assessed. A shortened cervical length in mid-gestation detected by transvaginal ultrasound is a strong risk factor for premature delivery and has been used clinically to identify females at high risk of preterm delivery in order to plan appropriate management (Fonseca et al 2007, Owen et al 2009, Hassan et al 2011). Emerging sonographic and MRI technologies are being used to investigate both the biomechanical properties and the microstructure of the cervix in pregnancy and are beginning to shed light on the poorly understood mechanisms involved in both normal and pathological cervical shortening in human pregnancy (Feltovich et al 2012, House et al 2013). The advent of commercially available histochemical studies such as those testing for fetal fibronectin also look for substances known to increase with dilation of the cervix (Daskalakis et al 2000).

Relations of the uterus in pregnancy

With uterine expansion, the ovaries and uterine tubes are displaced upwards and laterally. The round ligaments of the uterus become hypertrophied and their course from the uterus down to the deep inguinal ring becomes more vertical. The broad ligaments tend to open out to accommodate the massive increase in sizes of the uterine and ovarian vessels. The uterine veins in particular can reach about 1 cm in diameter and appear to act as a significant reservoir for blood during uterine contractions. Lymphatics and nerves expand their territories (the significance of this increased innervation is not clear because paraplegic females are able to labour normally, albeit painlessly). Later in pregnancy, the increase in intra-abdominal pressure produced by the gravid uterus can cause eversion of the umbilicus. On the skin over the abdomen, a combination of stretching and hormonal changes can produce stretch marks (striae gravidarum). In multiparous patients, diastasis (divarification) of right and left rectus abdominis can occur to accommodate the enlarging fundus of the uterus further. In the supine position, the gravid uterus can cause aortocaval compression, leading to reduced venous return to the heart and decreased cardiac output. In some females this can result in symptomatic hypotension and symptoms of nausea and syncope. These symptoms are minimized if the patient lies on her left side, thereby deviating the gravid uterus and reducing compression of the inferior vena cava.
The jejunum, ileum and transverse colon tend to be displaced upwards by the enlarging uterus, whereas the caecum and appendix are displaced to the right and the sigmoid colon posteriorly and to the left. Upward and lateral displacement of the appendix in later pregnancy can cause difficulties in the diagnosis of appendicitis. The ureters are pushed laterally by the enlarging uterus and, in late pregnancy, can be compressed at the level of the linea terminalis, resulting in hydronephrosis and pain in the lower back. However, mild ureteric dilation is normal in pregnancy and is caused by progesterone-induced relaxation of smooth muscle in the ureteric walls. The axis of the uterus is often dextrorotated by the presence of the sigmoid colon; this rotation must be considered when a hysterotomy is performed at the time of caesarean section to avoid injuring the large uterine vessels located bilaterally.

Pelvic changes in pregnancy

A pregnant uterus changes the centre of gravity of the body, especially in late pregnancy. In order to compensate for this, the mother tends to straighten her cervical and thoracic spine and throw her shoulders back, resulting in a compensatory increase in lumbar lordosis. There is also a softening of the pubic symphysis and sacroiliac joints, caused by production of relaxin and other pregnancy hormones, leading to mild pelvic instability that can result in a waddling gait. Softening also produces an increase in pelvic diameter, which is beneficial during labour. Significant joint relaxation can lead to pubic symphysis diastasis, which in severe cases can produce debilitating pain during walking.
Advances in three-dimensional ultrasound imaging permit real-time visualization of the anatomical relations and adaptations of the pelvic floor during pregnancy. They also make it possible to identify a pelvic diaphragm injury that can lead to symptomatic postpartum pelvic organ prolapse (Dietz and Lanzarone 2005, Shek et al 2012).

Placental development

The placenta is attached to the uterus at the point where the blastocyst becomes embedded, which is determined by a multitude of molecular signalling pathways (Ch. 9). In early pregnancy, the placental disc occupies a large proportion of the uterine cavity and will often appear to be situated near the anatomical internal os of the uterus. In most cases, growth and stretching of the uterus will usually draw the placenta upwards, away from the cervix of the uterus, by the end of pregnancy.
In about 1% of pregnancies, the position of the placenta will remain over or in close proximity to the anatomical internal os of the uterus at the end of pregnancy (Fig. 75.40A–B). This condition is called placenta praevia and can be associated with vaginal bleeding during pregnancy and labour. If the placenta covers the anatomical internal os of the uterus, or the lower edge is less than 1–2 cm from the anatomical internal os (marginal placental praevia), delivery by caesarean section is generally indicated.
Normal trophoblastic development includes the remodelling of maternal spiral arterioles to allow for low-resistance flow into the intervillous space. The uteroplacental vasculature is thus a low-resistance, high-capacitance system. Antenatal ultrasound Doppler velocimetry can be used during pregnancy to assess the resistance to flow in both the uterine and the umbilical arteries and can help in the management of high-risk pregnancies (Fig. 75.41).
Abnormally invasive placentation can occur when trophoblastic tissue invades through the decidua basalis and reaches the myometrium; this can lead to a morbidly adherent placenta and significant maternal morbidity. This condition, placenta accreta, occurs in approximately 3 per 1000 births. When the trophoblastic tissue invades the myometrium it is referred to as placenta increta; if the placental tissue reaches the uterine serosa it is called a placenta percreta. In extreme cases, a placenta percreta can also invade as far as the maternal bladder mucosa. These conditions often require a hysterectomy. Although several characteristics are known to increase the risk for placental invasion (such as placenta praevia, smoking, and multiple gestation), prior uterine surgery, especially multiple prior caesarean sections, is the most significant risk factor (Fig. 75.42).

Labour

The onset of labour is defined as the combination of regular uterine contractions that are of sufficient intensity to produce progressive effacement and dilation of the cervix of the uterus. The process of labour is described in three main stages.

First stage

The first stage of labour is the period during which the cervix progressively dilates until the fetus is able to descend into the birth canal and the cervix of the uterus is no longer palpable on vaginal examination. There is little change in the uterine volume because myometrial contractions are isometric, so there is minimal shortening of the muscle fibres. Progress is determined by the equilibrium between forces generated by myometrial contractions, especially from the fundus, and the resistance of the cervix.
Second stage
The second stage of labour begins once the cervix is fully dilated, and ends with the delivery of the fetus. Cervical resistance is lost and myometrial activity results in isometric contractions that aid descent of the fetus through the birth canal. The head of the fetus usually enters the pelvis with the occiput facing laterally. As the head descends further, the occiput contacts the gutter-shaped levator ani and this promotes flexion and rotation of the occiput to the anterior position. With further descent, the occiput escapes under the pubic symphysis and the head is born by extension. At this point, the head of the fetus regains its normal relationship with its shoulders, and there is slight rotation (or restitution) of the head. Further external rotation occurs as the leading shoulder is directed medially by the maternal pelvic diaphragm. The body of the fetus is now born by lateral flexion as one shoulder slips underneath the pubic symphysis and the posterior shoulder is drawn over the frenulum of the labia minora.
Third stage
The third stage of labour is the time from delivery of the fetus until delivery of the placenta. Prior to separation of the placenta, a large proportion of the mother’s cardiac output passes through the uterine circulation. After separation in the third stage of labour, maternal exsanguination is only prevented by marked uterine contraction; the criss-crossing myometrial fibres act as a tourniquet, restricting blood flow to the area that was the placental site. This process is usually expedited clinically by the administration of oxytocic drugs in an attempt to limit maternal blood loss. Any condition that predisposes to poor uterine contraction, such as retained placental tissue, polyhydramnios or multiple gestations, will increase the likelihood of postpartum haemorrhage (Salafia et al 2005).

Placenta after delivery

The placenta separates from the uterine wall along the plane of the functional layer and extends beyond the placental area, detaching the villous placenta with associated fibrinoid matrix and small amounts of basal decidua, the chorioamnion, together with a superficial layer of the fused capsular decidua, and the parietal decidua. When the placenta and membranes have been expelled, a thin functional layer remains above the uterine basal layer; it soon degenerates and is cast off in the early part of the puerperium. A new epithelial lining is regenerated from the remaining basal layer.
The expelled placenta is a flattened discoid mass with an approximately circular or oval outline (Fig. 75.43). It has an average volume of 500 ml (range 200–950 ml), a mean weight of 470 g (range 200–800 g), a diameter of 185 mm (range 150–200 mm), a thickness of 23 mm (range 10–40 mm), and a total villous surface area of 12–14 m2. It is thickest at its centre (the original embryonic pole), and rapidly thins towards its periphery, where it continues as the chorion laeve (smooth chorion). The size of the placenta correlates well with the birth weight of the neonate.
Macroscopically, the fetal or inner surface, covered by amnion, is smooth, shiny and transparent, so that the mottled appearance of the subjacent chorion, to which it is closely applied, can be seen. The umbilical cord is usually attached near the centre of the fetal surface, and branches of the umbilical vessels radiate out under the amnion from this point; the veins are deeper and larger than the arteries. The maternal surface of the placenta is finely granular and mapped into some 15–30 lobes by a series of fissures or grooves. The placental lobes, which are often somewhat loosely termed cotyledons, correspond in large measure to the major branches of the umbilical vessels. The grooves correspond to the bases of incomplete placental septa, which become increasingly prominent from the end of the first trimester. They extend from the maternal aspect of the intervillous space towards the chorionic plate (see Fig. 9.5) but do not quite reach it. The septa are complex structures composed of components of the cytotrophoblastic shell and residual syncytium, together with maternally-derived material including decidual cells, occasional blood vessels and gland remnants, collagenous and fibrinoid extracellular matrix, and, in the later months of pregnancy, foci of degeneration.
In multiple pregnancies, the number of placentas is determined by the zygosity; for example, in twin gestations, dizygotic pregnancies will always have two placentas (dichorionic). Monozygotic pregnancies usually have a single placenta (monochorionic), but about one-third will have two; the number is determined by the timing of splitting of the embryonic mass (see Ch. 8 and Fig. 8.9). If the split occurs within 3 days of fertilization, each fetus will have its own placenta and amniotic sac (dichorionic diamniotic); splitting after the third day following fertilization results in a monochorionic diamniotic pregnancy (two amniotic sacs) and vascular communication within the two placental circulations (Fig. 75.44); splitting after the ninth day results in a single placenta and single amniotic sac (monochorionic monoamniotic); and splitting after the twelfth day results in conjoined twins.

Placental variations

The umbilical cord is usually attached near the centre of the placenta, although it can occasionally insert more laterally and closer to the placental margin; this condition is known as a battledore placenta (Fig. 75.45). Occasionally, the cord fails to reach the placenta itself and ends in the membranes as a velamentous insertion. When insertion of the cord is velamentous, the larger branches of the umbilical vessels traverse the membranes before they reach and ramify on the placenta (Fig. 75.46). They travel unprotected through the membranes to the placenta, and this puts the fetus at risk because compression or tearing of the vessels can disrupt blood flow to and from it. This can be particularly problematic when the vessels present themselves across the external os of the uterus, a condition called vasa praevia. An accessory (succenturiate) placental lobe is occasionally present, connected to the main organ by membranes and blood vessels. If it is inadvertently retained in utero after delivery of the main placental mass it can cause postpartum haemorrhage or infection. Other variations include membranous placenta, in which villous stems and their branches persist over the whole chorion; and vallate (circumvallate) placenta, where the placental margin is undercut by a deep groove caused by an abnormally central insertion of the membranes (Fig. 75.47).
