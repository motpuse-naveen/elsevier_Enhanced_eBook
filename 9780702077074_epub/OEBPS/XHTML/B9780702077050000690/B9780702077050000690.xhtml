Chapter 69: Spleen

The spleen is a large, encapsulated, complex mass of vascular and lymphoid tissues situated in the upper left quadrant of the abdominal cavity between the fundus of the stomach and the respiratory diaphragm. It is mainly concerned with phagocytosis and immune responses but is also important in immunological defence, metabolism and maintenance of circulating blood elements (Steiniger 2015, Tarantino et al 2013; Table 69.1). In the fetus, the spleen is a major site of haemopoiesis and can resume this role postnatally in certain pathological conditions (Üngör et al 2007). However, it is not essential for life; if the spleen is removed, many of its functions can be assumed by the liver and other tissues of the mononuclear phagocytic system.
The most common clinical manifestations of splenic disorders are splenomegaly and a decrease in the number of cellular elements in the blood (cytopenias). The most serious long-term consequence of removal of the spleen is severe sepsis. One study of children who underwent splenectomy found that 7% were hospitalized for sepsis and 38% of these occurred within the first year following the procedure (Madenci et al 2018).

Gross anatomy

The size and weight of the spleen vary with age and sex, and can also vary slightly in the same individual under different conditions. The adult spleen is usually 9–14 cm long, 6–8 cm wide and 3–5 cm thick. It reaches its largest dimension in puberty and diminishes in size thereafter (DeLand 1970, Sprogøe-Jakobsen and Sprogøe-Jakobsen 1997). Although its weight increases during puberty, it is comparatively largest in the young child and tends to diminish in size and weight in old age (Skandalakis et al 1993, Üngör et al 2007, Coquet et al 2010). The average weight of an adult spleen depends on the volume of blood it contains; emptied of blood it weighs between 70 and 120 g, whereas in vivo its weight ranges from 150 to 350 g (Nakamura et al 1989, Skandalakis et al 1993, Petroianu 2011).

Spleens in men are proportionally heavier than those in women. In pathological conditions, the spleen may expand and retain blood; weights rarely exceed 700 g, but gigantic spleens exceeding 10 kg have been reported (DeLand 1970, Skandalakis et al 1993, Sprogøe-Jakobsen and Sprogøe-Jakobsen 1997). Splenomegaly compresses and displaces adjacent organs, causing abdominal discomfort, dyspepsia, respiratory restriction and difficulty walking (Coetzee 1982, Petroianu 2011).
The shape of the spleen is also variable: it is mostly determined by its relations to neighbouring structures during development and often appears as a slightly curved wedge. The superolateral aspect is covered by the respiratory diaphragm and the inferomedial aspect mostly by the neighbouring stomach, left kidney and left colic flexure. In the fetus, the spleen is lobulated (Ch. 21); the adult spleen usually retains a notch(es) on its superior border, although additional surface notches can persist (Mikhail et al 1979). In a study of 113 post mortem, i.e. unfixed, specimens, Parsons (1901) observed 1–7 splenic notches in over 90% of specimens; two notches were the most common finding and the majority were located on the superior border of the spleen.
A splenic lobule that fails to coalesce with the developing spleen can persist as an accessory (supernumerary) spleen. This fully functional island of splenic tissue is found in approximately 14% of individuals and may be located in any part of the abdomen, or even outside it, but is most commonly present near the splenic hilum within the gastrosplenic ligament or greater omentum (Faller 1985, Vikse et al 2017; Fig. 69.1). Other anatomical variations of the spleen include asplenia, hyposplenia, and splenogonadal and splenopancreatic fusions.
A normal adult spleen is not palpable on abdominal examination and has to triple in size before it becomes palpable below the left costal arch (Mikhail et al 1979). In supine healthy adults it is most frequently located between the tenth and twelfth ribs, with its long axis along the eleventh rib (Gupta et al 1976, Mirjalili et al 2012).

Relations

The spleen has superolateral diaphragmatic and inferomedial visceral surfaces (Fig. 69.2), superior and inferior borders, and anterior and posterior extremities. The convex, smooth diaphragmatic surface faces mostly superiorly and laterally. The diaphragmatic surface is separated from the left costodiaphragmatic recess, lower lobe of the left lung and the left tenth to twelfth ribs by the underside of the respiratory diaphragm (Figs 69.3–69.4).
The visceral surface is irregular, faces inferomedially towards the abdominal cavity and is marked by gastric, renal and colic impressions. A pancreatic impression is sometimes present. Michels (1942) found that the tail of the pancreas contacts the spleen in approximately one-third of individuals. The gastric impression faces anteromedially and is broad and concave where the spleen lies adjacent to the posterior aspect of the fundus, upper body and upper greater curvature of the stomach. It is separated from the stomach by a peritoneal recess, limited by the gastrosplenic ligament. The renal impression is slightly concave and lies on the posteroinferior part of the visceral surface, separated from the gastric impression above by a ridge of splenic tissue and the splenic hilum. It faces inferomedially and slightly backwards and is related to the upper lateral area of the anterior surface of the left kidney and sometimes to the superior border of the left suprarenal gland. The colic impression is usually flat; it lies at the anterior extremity of the spleen and is related to the left colic flexure and the phrenicocolic ligament. The splenic hilum is a long fissure pierced by the splenic vessels, nerves and lymphatics, and lies on the visceral surface closer to the inferior border.
The superior border separates the diaphragmatic surface from the gastric impression and is usually convex. The inferior border separates the renal impression from the diaphragmatic surface and is more rounded and blunt than the superior border. The posterior extremity usually faces the vertebral column. The anterior extremity is longer and less angulated than the posterior extremity and connects the superior and inferior borders anteriorly; it is related to the colic impression and often lies adjacent to the left colic flexure and phrenicocolic ligament.
The anterior extremity of the spleen is particularly at risk of injury from blunt abdominal trauma or during surgical procedures on the stomach, tail of the pancreas, left kidney, left suprarenal gland and colon. Excessive traction of the stomach, transverse colon or greater omentum can tear the splenic fibrous capsule and superficial parenchyma by way of their peritoneal attachments, causing bleeding that can be difficult to control (Merchea et al 2012).

Splenic ligaments

The spleen develops between the two leaves of the dorsal mesogastrium (see Figs 21.6–21.7) and so it is almost entirely invested in visceral peritoneum that is firmly adherent to its fibrous capsule (Üngör et al 2007). The dorsal mesogastric attachments persist as peritoneal ligaments. The superior part of the splenic hilum is connected to the stomach via the gastrosplenic ligament, and to the posterior abdominal wall by a variably developed phrenicosplenic ligament. A presplenic fold connecting the spleen to the stomach is sometimes seen anterior to the gastrosplenic ligament and may contain the left gastro-omental (gastro-epiploic) vessels (Skandalakis et al 2004). The inferior part of the splenic hilum is further connected to the posterior abdominal wall by the splenorenal ligament and to the left colic flexure by the splenocolic ligament. A spleno-omental fold may connect the spleen to the greater omentum: this is known as the ‘criminal’ fold of Morgenstern because careless surgical traction on it can traumatize the spleen (Skandalakis et al 2004). Each of these ligaments is made up of two layers of peritoneum containing fat, blood and lymphatic vessels and nerves. The phrenicosplenic ligament runs between the spleen and the peritoneum covering the undersurface of the respiratory diaphragm. The anterior layer of the splenorenal ligament is continuous with the peritoneum of the posterior wall of the omental bursa over the left kidney, and with the posterior layer of the gastrosplenic ligament at the splenic hilum. The posterior layer of the splenorenal ligament is continuous with the peritoneum over the inferior surface of the respiratory diaphragm and anterior surface of the left kidney. The terminal portions of the splenic artery and vein, and, more inferiorly, the tail of the pancreas, lie between the two peritoneal layers of the splenorenal ligament. The tail of the pancreas can be injured during dissection when ligating and dividing the splenic vessels, resulting in bleeding, local pancreatitis and pancreatic fistula formation (Skandalakis et al 1993).
The gastrosplenic ligament is continuous with the fibrous capsule of the spleen, the serosa of the stomach and the greater omentum. It contains the short gastric artery and its branches and the left gastro-omental artery, all of which arise from the splenic artery, together with their corresponding veins. During splenectomy or mobilization of the fundus of the stomach, the short gastric vessels must not be ligated too close to the stomach because there is a risk of local gastric necrosis, perforation and their consequences.
The phrenicocolic ligament connects the left colic flexure to the respiratory diaphragm and runs inferior and lateral to the anterior extremity of the spleen (see Fig. 69.4). It is continuous with the peritoneum of the left lateral end of the transverse mesocolon at the end of the tail of pancreas, and the splenorenal ligament at the splenic hilum (Merchea et al 2012, Skandalakis et al 1993). When the phrenicocolic ligament is being divided, particularly when electrocautery is used, the colon is at risk of injury.
Operating on a normal-sized spleen is a relatively straightforward procedure for most surgeons. Mobilization of the spleen requires division of the phrenicocolic, gastrosplenic and phrenicosplenic ligaments. Undue traction on the phrenicocolic ligament during mobilization of the left colic flexure can tear the splenic fibrous capsule, causing bleeding: this is less likely if the phrenicocolic ligament is retracted laterally rather than inferiorly and medially (Merchea et al 2012). The diaphragmatic surface of the spleen is occasionally adherent to the peritoneum on the undersurface of the respiratory diaphragm: adhesions can be a consequence of inflammation of the spleen or may be congenital (Skandalakis et al 1993, Petroianu 2011).

Mobile spleen

The peritoneal ligaments associated with the spleen vary in length; longer ligaments, especially the splenorenal ligament, afford the spleen greater mobility, and can stretch its vascular pedicle. While this facilitates surgical mobilization, it can render the spleen more susceptible to injury from shear forces during trauma.
A floating or wandering spleen is characterized by excessive mobility and migration of the spleen outside the left hypochondriac region. In such cases, the spleen can undergo torsion or cause urinary bladder or rectal symptoms from local pressure; imaging usually confirms the diagnosis. If surgical treatment is necessary, the spleen is freed from local adhesions and returned to the left hypochondriac region, the surgeon shortening and suturing its ligaments to the respiratory diaphragm (McFee et al 1995, Petroianu 2011).

Vascular supply and lymphatic drainage

Arteries

The spleen is supplied by the splenic artery, one of the most tortuous arteries in the body (Fig. 69.5, see Figs 63.10, 63.14B, 69.6). The reason for the tortuosity, which can become more pronounced with advancing age, is not understood, although there have been several proposals (Cortés and Pellico 1988, García-Porrero and Lemes 1988, Borley et al 1995, Sylvester et al 1995, Liu et al 1996, Pandey et al 2004). The splenic artery almost always arises from the coeliac trunk, in common with the left gastric and common hepatic arteries. However, it can originate from the common hepatic artery or the left gastric artery, or, rarely, directly from the abdominal aorta either in isolation or as a splenomesenteric trunk (Tubbs et al 2016).
From its origin, the artery runs a little way inferiorly before turning to the left behind the stomach to run horizontally posterior to the superior border of the body and tail of the pancreas. Multiple loops or even coils of the artery appear above the superior border of the pancreas (McFee et al 1995, Pandey et al 2004). The splenic artery courses anterior to the left kidney and left suprarenal gland and runs in the splenorenal ligament behind or above the tail of the pancreas. In its course it gives off numerous branches to the pancreas (dorsal pancreatic, great pancreatic artery, and artery to the tail of the pancreas) and, near its termination, it gives off the short gastric arteries and the left gastro-omental artery (Skandalakis et al 1993). Additional variant branches include a posterior gastric artery and small retroperitoneal branches.
The splenic artery is 8–32 cm long and its calibre usually exceeds that of the common hepatic and left gastric arteries, ranging from 3 to 12 mm. Splenic arterial blood flow is approximately 3 ml/sec/100 g, corresponding to approximately 7% of cardiac output (Trubel et al 1985, Cortés and Pellico 1988, García-Porrero and Lemes 1988, Nakamura et al 1989, Skandalakis et al 1993, Pandey et al 2004).
The splenic artery usually divides into two, or occasionally three, branches before entering the splenic hilum. The superior and inferior branches are sometimes known as superior and inferior polar arteries; as they enter the hilum they divide into four or five segmental arteries, each of which supplies a segment of splenic tissue. There is relatively little arterial collateral circulation between segments, which means that occlusion of a segmental vessel often leads to infarction of part of the spleen. Segmental arteries divide within the splenic trabeculae, becoming trabecular arteries. These leave the trabeculae and give rise to central arterioles that are surrounded by thick lymphoid sheaths of white pulp. Central arterioles ramify into several penicillar arterioles that feed the sinusoids of the red pulp. There is considerable communication among arterioles (Mikhail et al 1979, García-Porrero and Lemes 1988, Trubel et al 1988, Sow et al 1991, Liu et al 1996).
The short gastric arteries in the gastrosplenic ligament provide an additional arterial supply to the posterior extremity of the spleen that is distinct from the splenic hilar vessels. These short gastric vessels connect the posterior extremity of the spleen to the fundus of the stomach and preserve the viability of this region of the spleen after surgical ligation of the splenic pedicle (Skandalakis et al 1993).

Veins

Blood from the spleen is collected by trabecular veins that join to form the segmental veins draining individual splenic segments. In general, there are no anastomoses between intrasegmental venous tributaries. The segmental veins join to form two (superior and inferior) or three (superior, middle and inferior) ‘lobar’ veins that emerge from the length of the splenic hilum and unite to form the splenic vein within the splenorenal ligament (see Fig. 69.2). Communicating veins may interconnect lobar veins (Par et al 1965, DeLand 1970, Gupta et al 1980, Sow et al 1991, Liu et al 1996).
The splenic vein most commonly runs posterior to the splenic artery and tail and body of the pancreas (Skandalakis et al 2004) (see Figs 68.4D–G, 69.5, 69.6) (Par et al 1965, Gürleyik et al 2000). It crosses the posterior abdominal wall anterior to the left kidney and abdominal aorta, separated from the left sympathetic trunk and left crus of the respiratory diaphragm by the left renal vessels, and from the abdominal aorta by the superior mesenteric artery and left renal vein (Par et al 1965, Gupta et al 1976, Gupta et al 1980, Liu et al 1996). It ends posterior to the neck of the pancreas, where it joins the superior mesenteric vein to form the hepatic portal vein. Along its course it receives the short gastric veins, left gastro-omental veins, pancreatic veins, posterior gastric vein, left gastric vein and inferior mesenteric veins (the latter two do so in approximately 30% of individuals (Gerber et al 1951)). Anastomoses between the splenic vein and left renal vein have been reported, particularly in cases of portal hypertension (Bowdler 2002). Approximately 60% of hepatic portal vein blood flow is derived from the gut and the remainder from the spleen and pancreas (Par et al 1965, Gupta et al 1980, Dawson et al 1986, Oguro et al 1993, Liu et al 1996, Gürleyik et al 2000). Obstruction of the hepatic portal or splenic veins can lead to reversed venous flow through the splenic vein and its tributaries, resulting in splenomegaly and widespread varices (Re et al 1985, Skandalakis et al 1993, Petroianu 2011).

Lymphatic drainage

The lymphatic drainage of the spleen begins with the peri-arteriolar lymphoid sheaths (PALS) in the white pulp. The lymphatic vessels run toward the splenic hilum along trabecular arteries in the trabeculae, and then flow into the lymph nodes at the hilum. The lymphatic vessels from the subcapsular plexus also flow to the lymph nodes at the splenic hilum (Fig. 69.6). From there lymph drains to superior pancreatic, inferior pancreatic and pancreaticoduodenal nodes, and from there to coeliac nodes and the cisterna chyli (Bowdler 2002). There are no lymphatic vessels in the red pulp.
In malignant neoplasms of the greater curvature of the stomach, pancreas or distal transverse colon, it may be necessary to remove the spleen and tail of the pancreas to achieve a radical resection. In leukaemias and lymphomas, lymphadenopathy of splenic nodes and splenomegaly may be found during staging investigations (Petroianu 2011).

Innervation

The spleen is innervated by both components of the autonomic nervous system; the sympathetic supply is dominant. Postganglionic sympathetic nerves from the coeliac plexus travel with the splenic vessels. They innervate arteries at least to the trabecular level and have the potential to influence blood flow within the human spleen (Kudoh et al 1979). Parasympathetic nerves from the posterior vagal trunk may travel with the splenic vessels to the spleen, but this innervation has been questioned (Skandalakis et al 2004, Martelli et al 2014, Jung et al 2017). In contrast to some animals, the motor innervation of the human splenic fibrous capsule is largely vestigial: the fibrous capsule contains minimal quantities of smooth muscle and therefore does not contract. However, the fibrous capsule and parenchyma are innervated by sensory fibres that convey pain. Mild to moderate splenomegaly is often painless but splenic inflammation from infection, infarction, sickle cell disease, splenic artery embolization or abrupt distension of the fibrous capsule (e.g. haematoma) can cause severe pain, intensified by inflammation of the overlying parietal peritoneum. Referred pain from the splenic parenchyma is poorly localized to the epigastric region.

Microstructure

The spleen contains the largest single mass of lymphoid tissue in the body in direct continuity with the circulation. Splenic parenchyma is divided into two principal regions, white pulp and red pulp, named according to the appearance of the cut surface of a fresh spleen (Fig. 69.7). The microstructure of the spleen has been studied in many animals, most extensively in rats, mice and humans. However, species-specific differences have caused some terminological confusion when describing what are probably functionally equivalent zones: for example, the human spleen does not appear to contain large marginal or perimarginal sinuses or ellipsoids (although macrophage-sheathed capillaries may be equivalent to ellipsoids), and the so-called marginal zones (a B-cell compartment between red and white pulps) are arranged differently in rodent and human spleens. In humans, white pulp contains accumulations of T and B lymphocytes in periarteriolar lymphatic sheaths (PALS) and lymphatic follicles. Red pulp is a loose connective tissue containing many different types of blood cell, arterioles and macrophage sheathed capillaries, the latter consisting mainly of endothelial cells, pericytes, CD271+ stromal capillary sheath cells of varying phenotype (CD271 is the low affinity nerve growth factor receptor p75), CD68+CD163− macrophages and recirculating B lymphocytes (Steiniger 2015).

Fibrous framework

The serosa of the peritoneum covers the entire spleen, except at its hilum and where the peritoneal ligaments are attached. The connective tissue fibrous capsule, deep to the serosa, is approximately 1.5 mm thick and contains abundant type I collagen fibres and some elastin fibres. It consists of an outer and inner lamina in which the directions of the collagen fibres differ, so increasing its strength. Numerous trabeculae extend from the fibrous capsule into the substance of the spleen, where they branch to form a connective tissue scaffold (Faller 1985, Scothorne 1985). The largest trabeculae enter at the splenic hilum and divide into branches in the splenic pulp, providing conduits for the splenic vessels and nerves (see Fig. 69.9). Within the spleen, these branches become continuous with a delicate network of type III (reticular) collagen fibres that pervades both the red and white pulps and is maintained by fibroblasts within its interstices (Gupta et al 1976, Scothorne 1985, Krieken and Velde 1988).

White pulp

The white pulp in an adult human spleen accounts for up to 20% of the total splenic volume. Spherical lymphoid follicles form the major compartment. Branches of the splenic artery radiate out into the parenchyma from the splenic hilum, ramifying within the trabeculae. In their terminal few millimetres, their connective tissue adventitia is replaced by a sheath of T lymphocytes, the peri-arteriolar lymphoid sheath (PALS) (Figs 69.8–69.9). This is expanded in places by aggregations of B lymphocytes, lymphoid follicles measuring 0.25–1 mm in diameter and visible to the naked eye on the freshly cut surface of the spleen as white semi-opaque dots, in contrast to the surrounding deep reddish purple of the red pulp. Follicles are usually situated near the terminal branches of arterioles and typically protrude to one side of a vessel, which consequently appears to be eccentrically placed within the follicle. Arterioles branch laterally within follicles to form a series of parallel terminal arterioles, the penicillar (follicular) arterioles (Scothorne 1985, Krieken and Velde 1988; see Fig. 69.9).
Follicles are centres of lymphocyte aggregation and proliferation. After antigenic stimulation they become sites of intensive B cell proliferation, developing germinal centres similar to those found in lymph nodes; antigen presentation by follicular dendritic cells is involved in this process. Germinal centres regress when the stimulus abates. Follicles tend to atrophy with advancing age and may be absent in the very elderly (Scothorne 1985, Krieken and Velde 1988).

Red pulp

The red pulp constitutes approximately 75% of the total splenic volume and is a unique filtration device that enables the spleen to clear particulate material from the blood as it perfuses the organ (Scothorne 1985, Krieken and Velde 1988, Mishra 2016; see Fig. 69.7). It contains large numbers of venous sinusoids that ultimately drain into tributaries of the splenic vein. The sinusoids are separated from each other by a fibrocellular network of small bundles of collagen fibres, the reticulum, numerous reticular fibroblasts and splenic macrophages. Seen in two dimensions, these intersinusoidal regions appear as strips of tissue, the splenic cords (cords of Billroth; pulpar strands) (see Fig. 69.9), whereas in reality they form a three-dimensional continuum around the venous spaces (Chen 1978, Weiss 1983, Scothorne 1985, Krieken and Velde 1988).
Venous sinusoids are elongated ovoid vessels, approximately 50 μm in diameter, supported externally by circumferential and longitudinal reticular fibres and lined by a characteristic, discontinuous endothelium that is unique to the spleen. The long, narrow endothelial cells are aligned with the long axes of the sinusoids; previously called stave cells because of their resemblance to the planks in a barrel, they are now often called rod- or rib-shaped cells (see Fig. 69.9). These cells are attached to their neighbours at intervals along their length by short stretches of intercellular junctions that alternate with intercellular slits that allow blood cells to squeeze into the lumen of the sinusoid from the surrounding splenic cords (Pivkin et al 2016). A discontinuous basal lamina is present on the abluminal aspect of the sinus.
Large, stellate fibroblasts, reticular cells, lie around the sinusoids. They synthesize the matrix components of the reticulum, including collagen and proteoglycans, and their cytoplasmic processes help to compartmentalize the reticular space. Blood from the open ends of the capillaries that originate from penicillar arterioles percolates through the reticular spaces within the splenic cords. Macrophages in the reticular spaces remove blood-borne particulate material, including ageing and damaged erythrocytes. If the number of damaged erythrocytes increases, the reticular cells proliferate and the red pulp expands, causing the spleen to enlarge (Kudoh et al 1979, Scothorne 1985).

Perifollicular zone

The perifollicular zone lies between the follicular marginal zone and the red pulp: it is the site where blood is delivered into the red pulp and where many lymphocytes leave the circulation to migrate to their respective T and B lymphocyte aggregations in the white pulp (Steiniger et al 2001). These lymphocytes are more loosely arranged than they are in the white pulp, and are held in a dense network of reticular fibres and cells.

Splenic microcirculation

Segmental splenic arteries ramify in the trabeculae as trabecular arteries which taper to become central arterioles that pass through the white pulp and give off penicillar arterioles. These leave the white pulp and traverse the perifollicular zone to enter the red pulp, where the capillaries and sinuses at the end of the arterial tree and the beginning of the venous part of the circulation, respectively, are not connected so that blood flows in spaces delimited by fibroblasts rather than endothelial cells (Gupta et al 1976, Scothorne 1985, García-Porrero and Lemes 1988, Steiniger 2015, Steiniger et al 2018). Evidence supports a predominantly ‘open’ splenic circulation in humans, in which blood empties into and percolates slowly through the reticular tissue of the splenic cords before re-entering the vascular lumen through slits in the walls of the venous sinusoids (Chen 1978, Cavalli et al 1982, Pivkin et al 2016). From the venous sinusoids, blood collects in venules that unite to form small veins that run within the trabeculae and drain into segmental splenic veins (Chen 1978, Cavalli et al 1982, Scothorne 1985, Petroianu 2011).
Whether there is an alternative, ‘closed’ route in which blood enters the venous sinusoids directly from arterioles and capillaries in the perifollicular zone is unclear: it has been suggested that there is a totally open circulation system in the human spleen (Steiniger et al 2015). Either way, the blood is exposed to macrophages and the filtering mechanism of the spleen responsible for removing abnormal and effete cells, blood cell particles, bacteria, parasites and foreign antigens.

Imaging

Imaging modalities used to observe the spleen include ultrasound (US), computed tomography (CT), magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), arteriography, splenoportography and scintigraphy (Buntain et al 1988, Nakamura et al 1989, Merran et al 2007, Xu et al 2009, Omar and Freeman 2016) (Fig. 69.10, see Fig. 69.5). Cross-sectional imaging is particularly useful for assessing tumours and infiltrations of the spleen. Percutaneous drainage of splenic and perisplenic fluid collections is routinely performed under image guidance, and percutaneous splenic biopsy is being increasingly used in diagnosis. Arteriography is essential for selective embolization and the insertion of an arterial stent to treat splenic artery aneurysms. Scintigraphy can be used to investigate splenic phagocytic function by quantifying the uptake of radioactive labelled agents (e.g. 99mTechnetium sulphur colloid or heat-damaged erythrocytes) (Hermann and Winkel 1977, Merran et al 2007, Xu et al 2009, Petroianu 2011).

Splenomegaly

The numerous causes of splenomegaly can be classified broadly into haematological (e.g. lymphoproliferative, myeloproliferative, erythrocyte disorders); vascular (e.g. portal hypertension); infectious (e.g. viral, bacterial, other); autoimmune; and infiltrative (e.g. storage disorders and tumours) (Cavalli et al 1982, Re et al 1985, Petroianu 1988, 2011; Fig. 69.11).

Splenic trauma

The spleen is one of the most frequently injured organs during blunt abdominal trauma (Fig. 69.12). It is particularly prone to injury during rapid deceleration or compression, and its peritoneal ligaments render it vulnerable to shearing injuries of the parenchyma and splenic vessels. Irritation of the respiratory diaphragm from splenic haemorrhage with resultant referred left shoulder pain is known as Kehr’s sign. Fractures of the lower left ribs can be associated with blunt injuries and can lacerate the spleen (Buntain et al 1988). Traction on peritoneal ligaments or adhesions can lead to inadvertent tears of the splenic capsule during surgical procedures such as mobilization of the left colic flexure.
Contrast-enhanced CT imaging is particularly useful for assessing splenic trauma. Most splenic injuries from blunt abdominal trauma are successfully treated by conservative management. Extensive burst injuries with persistent bleeding or major injuries to the hilar vessels may require splenectomy but various repair techniques are available. With hilar injuries, a subtotal splenectomy, preserving the short gastric vessels and posterior extremity only, is one option. Minor intraoperative capsular tears can be controlled by the application of topical haemostatic agents and sutures, although direct suture repair is challenging because the splenic pulp is fragile. Arteriography and embolization are additional techniques that can be used to control bleeding or treat vascular lesions (Petroianu 2011).

Splenectomy

Most surgical procedures performed on the spleen should start with ligation of the splenic artery at the superior border of the tail of the pancreas. This can be difficult in patients with portal hypertension, splenic artery aneurysm, retroperitoneal fibrosis, regional lymphadenopathy or inflammatory conditions. After splenic artery ligation, the spleen becomes softer because most of its contained blood returns to the circulation. Despite the interruption of splenic artery inflow, the posterior extremity of the spleen continues to be perfused by the short gastric arteries, and the anterior extremity may be supplied retrogradely via the left gastro-omental artery. Ligation and division of the splenic vein should be the last step before removal of the spleen to maximize autotransfusion of blood from the spleen (Petroianu et al 1989, Sow et al 1991, Skandalakis et al 1993, Petroianu and Petroianu 1994, Liu et al 1996, Petroianu 2011).
In partial or subtotal splenectomy, the vessels that supply the part of the spleen to be removed are ligated and divided close to the organ. The devascularized region turns dark blue, demarcating it from the residual viable splenic tissue; the organ is then divided in this plane (Sow et al 1991, Petroianu and Petroianu 1994, Liu et al 1996, Christo and DiDio 1997, Gürleyik et al 2000). Wedge excision leaves two flaps of splenic fibrous capsule that can be sutured or stapled to cover the remaining raw parenchyma, after suture of any visible bleeding vessels (Petroianu 1988, 1993, 2011; Fig. 69.12B).
If total splenectomy is required to treat splenic trauma, autotransplantation of splenic tissue into the greater omentum or mesentery of the intestines can preserve useful splenic function. Approximately 20% of the original splenic mass needs to be transplanted and venous drainage of this tissue must be to the hepatic portal venous system to maintain the metabolic and immunological functions of the splenic tissue (Oguro et al 1993, Petroianu 2011).
Total splenectomy has numerous potential consequences. Intraoperatively, bleeding or injury to the pancreas, stomach or colon can occur, while early postoperative complications include left-sided basal pulmonary atelectasis and pleural effusion; abscess in the subphrenic space; transient leukocytosis and thrombocytosis with the risk of thrombotic complications. As mentioned earlier, there is an increased risk of infection (especially from encapsulated bacteria), which can be fatal (Petroianu 2011).
Conservative management of splenic trauma and partial splenectomy now enables splenic function to be preserved in situations where splenectomy would once have been routinely performed. Furthermore, most surgical procedures on the spleen can now be performed laparoscopically, which readily affords inspection of the spleen from multiple perspectives (Sow et al 1991, Poulin and Thibaut 1993).
