Chapter 14: Development of the nervous system

The entire nervous system and the special sense organs originate from three sources, each derived from specific cell populations of the early epiblast termed neural ectoderm. The first source to be clearly delineated is the neural plate, which gives rise to the central nervous system (CNS), the somatic motor nerves and the preganglionic autonomic nerves. The second source is from cells at the perimeter of the neural plate, neural crest cells, which remove themselves by epithelial to mesenchymal transition from the plate and migrate away just prior to its fusion into a neural tube. These cells give rise to the majority of the neurones and glia of the peripheral nervous system (PNS), i.e. the somatic sensory nerves, the somatic and autonomic ganglia, postganglionic autonomic nerves and suprarenal and chromaffin cells, as well as significant mesenchymal populations in the head. The third source is from ectodermal placodes, focal thickenings of the ectoderm covering the embryonic head, that contribute cells to the cranial sensory neurones and form the olfactory epithelia, the epithelia of the inner ear and, by a non-neuronal contribution, the lens of the eye. Early establishment of a pial capillary network, derived from mesenchymal neural crest cells, provides cell lines for neuroepithelial:endothelial interactions throughout the developing nervous system.

Neurulation

Primary neurulation

Primary neurulation begins at stage 9 and is completed during stage 12 (Figs 14.1–14.2) (embryonic stages 10–23 correlated to postfertilization days of development and postmenstrual weeks of pregnancy are shown in Fig. 23.3). The process is continuous spatially and temporally but has been envisaged as four stages. It begins with local elongation of the ectoderm cells in a midline zone of the embryonic disc and their reorganization into a pseudostratified epithelium, the neural plate. This is followed by reshaping and bending of the neural plate into a neural groove that subsequently closes to form a neural tube bidirectionally from its mid portion to its cranial and caudal ends. A continuous surface ectoderm forms dorsal to the tube. As neurulation proceeds, the neural tube elongates by convergent extension, a process where the apical surfaces of adjacent cells narrow (convergence) and there is rostrocaudal elongation (extension) (Bay and Caspary 2012, Nikolopoulou et al 2017).
Two sites of dorsal fusion of the neural tube appear in succession in the human embryo. The initial fusion site, termed α, or the dorsal lip of the rostral neuropore, is in the rhombencephalic region from which closure extends both rostrally and caudally. A second site, termed β, or the terminal lip of the rostral neuropore, forms in the prosencephalic region and extends caudally (O’Rahilly and Müller 2002). Closure of the rostral neuropore is completed in stage 11 when about 19–20 pairs of somites are present. Continued generation of the neural plate from the primitive node adds caudal regions to the elongating and closing neural tube and generation of mesenchyme from the primitive streak adds paraxial mesenchymal populations that form somites as the neural tube closes (Ch. 18). Primary neurulation ends and the caudal neuropore starts to close in stage 12 (30–32 days postfertilization, see Fig. 23.3), when approximately 29 somites are present: the final site of closure corresponds to the level of the future somite 31 (the level of the future second sacral vertebra) (O’Rahilly and Müller 2004).
Primary neurulation depends on cellular changes within the neural plate; and may also also involve movements of the paraxial mesenchyme in the trunk. Initial fusion of the surface ectoderm is followed by fusion of the neural ectoderm. The neural ectodermal cells become elongated and then wedge-shaped. The forces needed to shape the neural tube are intrinsic to the cells of the neurectoderm: human embryonic stem cells can self-organize into three-dimensional neural structures (Eiraku and Sasai 2012, Sasai et al 2012). Epithelial populations are established in the embryo with the formation of the epiblast. Three main control pathways that maintain epithelial apicobasal polarity have been described (Crumbs, Baz/PAR-3, Scribble) and Wnt/planar cell polarity gene expression is involved with epithelial cell movement. When the neural tube is closing, its walls consist of a pseudostratified layer of columnar neural epithelial cells (see below). The cells increase in length and develop numerous longitudinally disposed microtubules; their apical surfaces are joined by tight junctions supported by claudins and linked to the actin cytoskeleton that forms a dense paraluminal web of microfilaments. The nuclei assume basal positions and this, together with the contraction of apically arranged actin, imparts a slight wedge conformation along the midline of the neural plate, creating a median hinge point that is termed the floor plate. The apical location of Wnt and the planar polarity genes maintain the apicobasal polarity and contribute to the convergent extension movements (Baumholtz et al 2017). Movements of the cell populations on each side of the floor plate promote elevation of the layer and neural tube formation.
The position of hinge points within the neural plate confers different luminal characteristics within the formed neural tube. With a median hinge point the neural folds remain relatively straight and the tube in this position has a slit-shaped lumen, as can be seen from the initial region of fusion rostralwards. If dorsolateral hinge points are added the resulting neural tube lumen becomes rhombic, and if all neuroepithelial cells exhibit some apical narrowing then the tube has a circular lumen. Rostrally, the slit-shaped profile depends more on support from adjacent tissues than at the caudal end of the tube where secondary neurulation produces a neural tube with a circular lumen.

Secondary neurulation

Secondary neurulation is the process by which the caudal portion of the neural tube is formed in the absence of a neural plate. It starts from the closure of the caudal neuropore at stage 12 and ends at about stage 17. At the time of caudal neuropore closure the caudal midline cells are generically termed the caudal eminence (O’Rahilly and Müller 2004). A specific population of mesenchymal cells, the caudoneural hinge or junction, shares the same molecular markers as the primitive node. These cells aggregate at the midline and undergo mesenchymal to epithelial transition, producing a cellular cylinder that is contiguous with the caudal end of the neural tube. Further elongation of the caudal neural tube involves cavitation of the neural cylinder. In a study of human embryos, the caudal end of the forming neural tube showed multiple outpouchings of the lumen at stage 18. The most caudal part was accompanied by a notochord. Immunohistochemical staining was negative for a range of neuronal antigens: N-CAM and NeuN labelling was seen after stage 16 and neurofilament-associated protein (3A10) labelled cells were seen from stages 16 and 18 in dorsal tissues. Apoptosis was noted in the very terminal end of the caudal eminence and the caudal portion of the neural tube showed regression at stage 19 (Yang et al 2014). Neural crest cells delaminate from the dorsal surface of the cylinder in a rostrocaudal direction and the paraxial mesenchyme concurrently undergoes somitogenesis. The range of processes involved in secondary neurulation, the mechanisms of generation of caudal tissues, their further interactions within the developing cloacal region, and the formation of associated somatic and autonomic nerves, are all still as yet unknown. It may be that, as occurs in the head, where special populations of cells are required to form and provide neurological control of the oropharyngeal reflexes associated with the airway and proximal gut, a similar system occurs in the caudal region, where special cell populations provide tissues for the cloacal region and control the reflexes of the enteric, urinary and reproductive sphincters.

Failure of neurulation

Failure of neurulation produces the conditions of craniorachischisis totalis (where the entire neural tube is unfused in the dorsal midline), cranioschisis or anencephaly (where the neural tube is fused dorsally to form the spinal cord but is not fused dorsally in the brain), and spina bifida (where local regions of the spinal neural tube are unfused, or there is failure of formation of the vertebral neural arches) (Fig. 14.3; see Fig. 18.14). Anencephalic fetuses display severe disturbances in the shape, position and ossification of the basichondrocranium and in the course of the intracranial notochord, and so the condition is more complicated than ‘simply’ a failure of neural tube fusion. Spinal neural tube defects commonly correspond to the junctional region between primary and secondary neurulation processes (Dady et al 2014). Heterozygous mutations in the polarity gene signalling pathway have been identified in individuals with neural tube defects. Defects in the structure or function of the primary cilium (see below) are also associated with neural tube defects and with changes in cortical formation thought to underlie cognitive deficits and mental retardation in a range of recessive ciliopathies (Higginbotham et al 2013, Wheway et al 2018).

Early Brain Regions

Prior to the closure of the neural tube, the neural folds become considerably expanded in the head region. The first indication of a brain and its major divisions can be seen at stage 10 (see Fig. 14.2; Fig. 14.4). Regional expansions, the prosencephalon (forebrain), mesencephalon (midbrain) and rhombencephalon (hindbrain), have been called the three primary cerebral vesicles, although the term ‘vesicle’ in this context is probably inappropriate to describe localized accelerations of growth in the wall of the brain. As the neural tube closes, the neural wall appears to form a series of ridges and depressions perpendicular to its long axis. These transient repeating segments are termed primary neuromeres. Initially, six can be identified, namely, the prosencephalon, mesencephalon and four subdivisions of the rhombencephalon, rhombomeres A, B, C and D. The primary neuromeres themselves become subdivided during stages 10, 11 and 12, and a total of 16 secondary neuromeres have been identified. The prosencephalon gives rise to the telencephalon, diencephalon 1 (D1) and diencephalon 2 (D2), which has three subdivisions. The mesencephalon is subdivided into the mesencephalon 1 (M1) and mesencephalon 2 (M2). The rhombencephalon is subdivided into the isthmus rhombencephali and rhombomeres 1–8; the original rhombomere A gives rise to secondary rhombomeres 1, 2 and 3; rhombomere B gives rise to secondary rhombomere 4; rhombomere C gives rise to secondary rhombomeres 5, 6 and 7; and rhombomere D gives rise to secondary rhombomere 8 (O’Rahilly and Müller 1999). As the rhombencephalon grows, the rhombomeric boundaries become less distinct. With the early appearance of the cerebellum from the isthmus rhombencephali and rhombomere 1, the rhombencephalon is traditionally divided into the metencephalon, which extends to about rhombomere 3, and the myelencephalon, which extends to the spinomedullary junction. A summary of the derivatives of the cerebral regions is given in Table 14.1.
Before the closure of the neural tube the elongation of the brain occurs concurrently with the appearance of three flexures, two are concave ventrally and one is concave dorsally. During stages 13 and 14 the brain bends at the mesencephalon (mesencephalic flexure) so that the prosencephalon bends in a ventral direction around the cephalic end of the notochord and foregut until its floor lies almost parallel with that of the rhombencephalon (see Fig. 14.4). A bend (cervical flexure) also appears at the junction of the rhombencephalon and spinal cord, increasing in angulation over stages 14–20, by which time the rhombencephalon forms nearly a right-angle to the spinal cord. Extension of the head takes place after stage 20 and the cervical flexure diminishes and eventually disappears. The third bend, the pontine flexure, is directed ventrally between the metencephalon and myelencephalon and does not substantially affect the outline of the head. In this region, the roof plate thins until it is composed only of a single layer of cells and pia mater, the tela choroidea. The flexure of the neural tube at this point produces a rhombic shape in the roof that later forms the medullary velum.

Neural Crest

The neuronal populations of the early epiblast become arranged in the medial region of the embryonic disc as the neural plate (see Fig 10.5). Laterally, neural folds or crests indicate a transitional region between the neural and surface ectoderm. Cells in the crests undergo epithelial to mesenchymal transition and leave the epithelium just prior to its fusion with the contralateral fold in the rhombencephalic dorsal midline: their new mesenchymal phenotype means that they acquire migratory properties. Two distinct populations of neural crest cells are formed: a neuronal population produced throughout the brain and spinal cord that gives rise to sensory and autonomic neurones and associated glia, and a non-neuronal mesenchymal population that arises only from the brain (Figs 14.5-14.6). As development proceeds, sequential migration of these cells gives rise to four neural crest subpopulations: cranial, vagal, trunk and sacral.

Neural crest cells arising from the diencephalon up to the level of the future pineal gland (epiphysis) contribute mesenchymal populations to the frontonasal process. From stage 10 a number of transverse subdivisions perpendicular to the long axis of the brain can be seen within the rhombencephalon, dividing it into segments termed rhombomeres (Müller and O’Rahilly 1997). Eight main rhombomeres extend from the midbrain–hindbrain boundary rostrally to the developing spinal cord caudally (see Fig. 14.2). Both non-neuronal neural crest mesenchyme and neuronal somatic sensory and autonomic ganglia are formed from rhombencephalic crest cells (see Fig. 12.4). Rhombomeres 1 and 2 produce neural crest cells that invade the first pharyngeal arch; rhombomere 4 crest cells migrate into pharyngeal arch 2; rhombomere 6 crest cells migrate into pharyngeal arch 3; rhombomeres 7 and 8 derived neural crest cells migrate into pharyngeal arches 4 and 6. Rhombomeres 3 and 5 give rise to only a very small number of neural crest cells that migrate rostrally and caudally to enter the adjacent even-numbered rhombomeres. These rostral origins give rise to cranial and vagal neural crest. Vagal neural crest migration is extensive: the cells pass ventrally into the developing heart and appear to have a particular association with organs that arise from endoderm, e.g. the thymus gland, foregut-derived respiratory tubes and the gut, where they give rise to the enteric nervous system (Hutchins et al 2018).
Trunk neural crest cells are patterned by their migration through the somites. As the crest cells move laterally and ventrally they can pass between the somites and within the rostral sclerotomal half of each somite, but they cannot penetrate the caudal portion of the sclerotomal mesenchyme. The segmental distribution of the spinal and sympathetic ganglia is therefore imposed on trunk neural crest cells by a pre-pattern that exists within the somitic paraxial mesenchyme (see Fig. 14.6; see Ch. 18; Figs 18.4, 18.8). Caudally, from approximately somite 29 secondary neurulation processes produce the most caudal sacral neural crest.
Melanocytes develop from lateral migration of a subpopulation of neural crest cells derived from both the head and the trunk (see Fig. 14.6). They form one of the three pigment cell types (the others being retinal pigment epithelium and the pigment cells of the pineal organ, both of which originate from the diencephalon).

Ectodermal Placodes

Before the neural tube closes, the elevating neural folds contain two distinctive neuronal populations. The larger population, of neural crest cells, migrates from the neural epithelium prior to neural tube fusion. The smaller population, of neuroepithelial cells, becomes incorporated into the surface ectoderm after neural tube closure. These areas of neuroepithelium within the surface ectoderm have been termed ectodermal placodes. Although the majority of the ectodermal placodes form nervous tissue, non-neurogenic placodes also occur (Begbie and Graham 2001). After an appropriate inductive stimulus, the placodes thicken and they either generate migratory neuronal cells that will contribute to the cranial sensory ganglia, or the whole placodal region invaginates to form a vesicle beneath the remaining surface ectoderm. Neurogenic placodes undergo both processes. Paired non-neurogenic placodes invaginate to form the lens vesicles under the inductive influence of the optic vesicles (Ch. 15).
The neural folds meet in the rostral midline adjacent to the buccopharyngeal membrane. The rostral neural fold does not generate neural crest but gives rise to the hypophysial placode, i.e. the future Rathke’s pouch, which remains within the surface ectoderm directly rostral to the buccopharyngeal membrane. The rostral neural fold also gives rise to the olfactory placodes that remain as paired, laterally placed placodes, and to the epithelium of the nasal cavity (Fig. 14.7).
Further caudally, similar neurogenic placodes, the epibranchial, otic and trigeminal placodes, can be identified (Fig. 14.8). The epibranchial placodes appear in the surface ectoderm immediately dorsal to the area of pharyngeal (branchial) cleft formation. The first epibranchial placode is located at the level of the first pharyngeal groove and contributes cells to the distal (geniculate) ganglion of the facial nerve. The second and third epibranchial placodes contribute cells to the petrosal and nodose ganglia of the glossopharyngeal and vagus nerves, respectively. These placodes thicken and cells begin to detach from their epithelium soon after the pharyngeal pouches have contacted the overlying ectoderm. Concurrently, the neural crest cells reach and move beyond these lateral extensions of the pharynx. Neurones migrate from the epibranchial placodes internally to the sites of ganglion formation, where they show signs of early differentiation into neurones, including the formation of neurites.
The otic placodes, located lateral to the myelencephalon, invaginate to form otic vesicles from which the membranous labyrinth of the inner ear develops (Ch. 16). Neurones of the vestibulocochlear ganglia arise from neurones that bud off the ventromedial aspect of the otic cup, after which they can be distinguished in the acoustic and vestibular ganglia.
The profundal and maxillomandibular trigeminal placodes, which fuse in humans to form a single entity, lie rostral to the otic placodes, alongside the mesencephalic–rhombencephalic junction. Prospective neuroblasts migrate from foci dispersed throughout the surface ectoderm lateral and ventrolateral to the caudal mesencephalon and metencephalon to contribute to the distal portions of the trigeminal ganglia.
A small endodermal diverticulum, Seessel’s pouch, projects towards the brain from the cranial end of the foregut, immediately caudal to the buccopharyngeal membrane. In some marsupials, this pouch forms a part of the hypophysis, but it is not well defined and disappears entirely in humans.

Pituitary gland (hypophysis cerebri)

The hypophysis cerebri consists of the adenohypophysis, derived from an ectodermal placode, and the neurohypophysis, derived from the neural plate. Prior to neurulation, these cell populations are adjacent within the rostral portion of the floor of the neural plate and the contiguous midline neural fold (see Fig. 14.7). During primary neurulation the neurohypophysial cells remain within the floor of the prosencephalon but the adenohypophysial cells are displaced into the ectoderm as a placode rostral to the buccopharyngeal membrane. Both components remain in contact: there is no growth or migration of one to meet the other during subsequent development.
The buccopharyngeal membrane ruptures at about stage 12. By stage 13 the placodal thickening of the adenohypophysis can be seen just rostral to the edge of the now ruptured buccopharyngeal membrane and rostral to the tip of the subjacent notochord. The placode invaginates to form the roof and walls of a saccular depression, the adenohypophysial (Rathke’s) pouch (Figs 14.9–14.10) that expands laterally to remain associated with the floor and sides of the overlying diencephalic wall. By stage 15 an infundibular recess in the floor of the diencephalon is present, and it evaginates to form the infundibular process and infundibular stem during stage 16. During stage 18 the adenohypophysis and neurohypophysis are closely apposed with no intervening mesenchyme: the wall of the neurohypophysis has characteristic folds. There is vascular mesenchyme between the adenohypophysis and the hypothalamic floor in stage 18 and between the adenohypophysis and neurohypophysis by stage 20. At this time, the adenohypophysial stem is solid and embedded in the chondrocranium.
The interactions between hypothalamic and oral ectoderm previously identified in cultured mouse embryos can be replicated in human embryonic stem cells grown in serum-free floating embryoid body-like aggregates. The tissues that form will generate hormone-producing cells and differentiated cell lines that respond to corticotrophic hormone and secrete ACTH, and respond to GHRH and secrete growth hormone (Ozone et al 2016). For an overview of the regulatory factors involved in pituitary embryogenesis and the conditions under which pituitary stem cells may be activated in postnatal life see Cox et al (2017).
At birth the hypophysis is about one-sixth of the weight of the adult gland. It increases in weight to become about one-half of the weight of the adult gland at 7 years, and attains adult weight at puberty. Throughout postnatal life, the gland appears larger in females, in both size and weight. Traces of the original invagination point of the adenohypophysial pouch may be present at the junction of the septum of the nose and palate. A craniopharyngeal canal is a defect of the midline of the body of the sphenoid bone that extends from the floor of the sella turcica to the roof of the nasopharynx: three types of craniopharyngeal canals have been identified based on CT and MR imaging (Abele et al 2014).

Early Cellular Arrangement and Histogenesis of the Neural Tube

Formation of the neural tube floor plate is matched by ventral ingression of the notochord from the primitive node. Dorsoventral patterning of the neural tube is dependent on this close contact because the original floor plate and the dorsal site of fusion of the neural tube initially contain non-neural cells. Motor neurones usually develop ventrally, close to the notochord, and sensory neurones develop dorsally. Experimental removal of the notochord results in the elimination of the floor plate and motor neurones, and the differentiation of dorsal cell types in the ventral region of the cord (Dodd et al 1998). Experimental grafting of another notochord dorsally results in the induction of a floor plate and ectopic motor neurones close to the grafted notochord. Grafting an isolated neural floor plate dorsal to the neural tube also results in induction of ectopic motor neurones overlying the graft (Fig. 14.11). Elucidation of the specific transcription factors involved in the early patterning of the neural tube has enabled the in vitro culture of hPSCs and recapitulation of these early processes. Graded Shh signalling originating in the notochord and floor plate establishes discrete individual neuronal precursor domains that later give rise to specific classes of postmitotic motor neurones (Pantani 2016).
Three zones or regions can be identified within the wall of the early neural tube as the pseudostratified epithelium proliferates. Conventionally, these zones have been named ventricular, mantle and marginal, from deep to superficial (Fig. 14.12). The inner, ventricular zone (germinal, primitive ependymal or matrix layer) contains the apical, nucleated parts of the pseudostratified columnar neuroepithelial cells and rounded cells undergoing mitosis. As daughter cells migrate ventriculofugally (radially) from the ventricular zone, a middle, mantle layer (intermediate zone) is formed. Radial glial cells form early with processes extending from the ventricular surface (apical cell surface) to the pial surface (basal cell surface). The lateral connection of adjacent apical or basal surfaces give rise to layers termed the internal and external glia limitans (glial limiting membrane), respectively. The region containing the basal surfaces and cytoplasmic processes of the radial glia is termed the marginal zone; it will be invaded later by vascular endothelium, varieties of glial cells and axonal processes extending from neuroblasts.
The neural tube caudal to the developing brain is initially oval in transverse section and its lumen is narrow and slit-like (Fig. 14.13). Proliferation in the ventricular zone and migration of the resulting progeny means that the lateral walls thicken and the lumen, now the central canal, widens in its dorsal part. A longitudinal sulcus limitans develops on each side, dividing the ventricular and mantle (now also referred to as intermediate) zones in each lateral wall into a ventrolateral lamina or basal plate, and a dorsolateral lamina or alar plate, respectively. The widening central canal becomes somewhat rhombic on cross-section (Fig. 14.14).
Planar cell polarity genes are expressed by the neuroepithelium. Each cell displays an apical non-motile primary cilium (internal 9+0 configuration of microtubules), and a system of intraflagellar transport (Tissir and Goffinet 2010). The membrane of the primary cilium expresses receptors and ion channels and modulates signalling pathways for Shh, Wnt and PDGFα (Lee and Gleeson 2010).
Pseudostratified epithelial proliferation of neuroepithelial progenitor cells at the ventricular zone is recognized by interkinetic nuclear migration. The cells have a relatively small basal surface that generates a basal lamina, a similarly small apical surface (with a primary cilium) joined by an adherence junctional belt, and extensive lateral surfaces that span the neuroepithelial wall. Their nuclei migrate basally during G1 phase of the cell cycle; S-phase (DNA replication) occurs as the nuclei approach the basal lamina. During G2, the nuclei migrate apically (toward the neural tube lumen) where cell division occurs. The basal body of the primary cilium is anchored to the apical surface: the cilium persists through the cell cycle until the onset of mitosis when the basal body and associated second centriole form the centrosomes of the mitotic spindle (Taverna and Huttner 2010). The movement of the nuclei during proliferation is responsible for the pseudostratified appearance of the epithelium, because the nuclei move basally and then apically as they pass through a complete mitotic cycle (Fig. 14.15).
The first populations formed by symmetric division become radial glial cells: through their contact with both the neural tube lumen and the outer pial surface, they provide a scaffold on which future cell progeny can migrate. Radial glial cells express GFAP, distinguishing them from neuroepithelial progenitor cells (Silver et al 2019). The Golgi apparatus in radial glial cells remains apical to the nucleus and perpendicular to the apicobasal axis of the cell and appears to have only a minor role in synthesis of constituents for the basolateral plasma membrane (Taverna et al 2016). Arl13b, a cilia-specific small guanosine triphosphatase (GTPase), is essential for the formation of radial glia (Higginbotham et al 2013). Later, asymmetric divisions produce one neuroepithelial progenitor cell and another cell that migrates radially (ventriculofugally), so producing clones of neuroblasts and glioblasts. With development of the intermediate zone, the nuclei of cells undergoing asymmetric division show interkinetic nuclear migration only within the ventricular and subventricular zones (Taverna and Huttner 2010). The heterogeneity of the radial glial cell population, which can become detached from the basal and apical surfaces as development proceeds, has yet to be elucidated (Silver et al 2019). The stem cells in the ventricular zone finally produce two postmitotic daughter cells that differentiate into ependyma that lines the ventricular system. The ventricular epithelium differentiates into choroid plexus and specialized circumventricular organs in particular sites within the third and fourth ventricles.
The early spinal cord and much of the brainstem retain only the ventricular, intermediate and marginal zones. However, in the telencephalon, cellular proliferation extends deeper than the ventricular zone. A population of cells resulting from the asymmetric division of stem cells at the ventricular zone move to a subventricular zone, where they divide symmetrically. Cells from both layers continue to multiply to provide generations of neurones and glia that subsequently migrate into the intermediate and marginal zones. Those in the subventricular zone are termed intermediate neural progenitor cells or basal progenitors to emphasize the fact that they are no longer attached to the apical (ventricular) surface. They translocate their nuclei to an abventricular location for mitosis. Radially migrating cells from the ventricular zone in the developing cerebral hemispheres form a zone, the cortical plate, outside the intermediate zone. The most recently formed cells migrate to the outermost layers of the cortical plate, so that earlier derived and migrating cells become subjacent to those migrating at a later time. The subplate zone is a crucial additional transient stratum found deep to the early cortical plate in the forebrain (Molnar et al 2019).
In the cerebellar cortex, some mitotic subventricular stem cells migrate across the entire neural wall to form a subpial population and establish a new zone of cell division and differentiation. Many cells formed in this site remain subpial in position, but others migrate back towards the ventricle through the developing nervous tissue, and finish their migrations in various definitive sites where they differentiate into neurones or macroglial cells.

Segmentation in the neural tube

The early neural tube is visibly divided into segments by shallow transverse folds that extend perpendicular to its long axis. Primary neuromeres can be identified at stage 9, with 16 secondary neuromeres present at stage 14. Rhombomeres, the segments within the rhombencephalon, identified from stage 10, are crucial units of pattern formation (Tümpel et al 2009). Other brain regions are not segmented in quite the same way as the hindbrain. Morphological boundaries, domains of cell lineage restriction and of cell mixing, and regions of gene expression that abut sharp boundaries, are found in the diencephalon and telencephalon. Although these segmental units lose their morphological prominence with subsequent development, they represent the fundamental ground plan of the neuraxis, creating a series of semi-autonomous units, within which local variations in patterning can then develop. It is likely that compartmentation of cell groups with some, if not all, of the features of rhombomeres plays an important role in the formation of various brain regions.
Domains of expression of developmental control genes abut rhombomere boundaries. Single-cell labelling experiments have revealed that cells within rhombomeres form segregated non-mixing populations (Fig. 14.16). The neural crest also shows intrinsic segmentation in the rhombencephalon. It is segregated into streams at its point of origin in the dorsal neural tube, which may represent a mechanism whereby morphogenetic specification of the premigratory neural crest cells is conveyed to the pharyngeal arches (see Fig. 12.4). Developmental studies of rhombomeric fate, developmental gene expression, progeny analysis and fate mapping are calling into question the traditional subdivisions of the brainstem into midbrain, pons and medulla oblongata that have been based on the external appearance of the human brainstem (Watson et al 2019). The brainstem boundaries identified by these new analyses are illustrated in Fig. 14.17 and Figs 28.25, 28.26.
The significance of intrinsic segmentation in the hindbrain is underlined by the absence of overt segmentation of the adjacent paraxial mesenchyme. There is no firm evidence for intrinsic segmentation in the spinal cord. Instead, segmentation of the neural crest, motor axons and thus, eventually, the spinal nerves, is dependent on the segmentation of the neighbouring somites. Both neural crest cell migration and motor axon outgrowth occur through only the rostral and not the caudal sclerotome of each somite, so that dorsal root ganglia form only at intervals. The caudal sclerotome possesses inhibitory properties that deter neural crest cells and motor axons from entering.
Genes such as the Hox and Pax gene families, which encode transcription factor proteins, show intriguing expression patterns within the nervous system. Genes of the Hox-b cluster, for example, are expressed throughout the caudal neural tube, and up to discrete limits in the hindbrain that coincide with rhombomere boundaries. The ordering of these genes within a cluster on the chromosome (5′–3′) is the same as the caudal to rostral limits of expression of consecutive genes. This characteristic pattern is surprisingly similar in fish, frogs, birds and mammals. Hox genes play a role in patterning not only of the neural tube but also of much of the head region, consistent with their expression in neural crest cells, and within the pharyngeal arches. Disruption of Hox a-3 gene in mice mimics DiGeorge’s syndrome, a congenital human disorder characterized by the absence (or near absence) of the thymus, parathyroid and thyroid glands, hypotrophy of the walls of the arteries derived from the aortic arches, and subsequent conotruncal cardiac malformations. Some Pax genes are expressed in different dorsoventral domains within the neural tube. Pax-3 is expressed in the alar lamina, including the neural crest, while Pax-6 is expressed in the intermediate plate. The Pax-3 gene has the same chromosomal localization as the mouse mutation Splotch and the affected locus in the human Waardenburg’s syndrome, both of which are characterized by neural crest disturbances with pigmentation disorders and occasional neural tube defects. Both Hox and Pax genes have restricted expression patterns with respect to the rostrocaudal and the dorsoventral axes of the neural tube, consistent with roles in positional specification. The expression pattern of Hox and other genes expressed in rhombomeres, and the transcriptional analysis of individual rhombomeres illustrate the genome-wide complexity of mid- and hindbrain patterning in animal embryos (Krumlauf et al 1993, Chambers et al 2009, Wilson and Chambers 2014).
While craniocaudal positional values are probably conferred on the neuroepithelium at the neural plate or early neural tube stage, dorsoventral positional values may become fixed later. The development of the dorsoventral axis is heavily influenced by the presence of the underlying notochord. The notochord induces the floor plate in the ventral midline of the neural tube. This specialized region consists of a strip of non-neural cells with distinctive adhesive and functional properties. Notochord and floor plate together participate in inducing the differentiation of the motor columns. Motor neurone differentiation occurs early, giving some grounds for the idea of a ventral to dorsal wave of differentiation. The notochord/floor plate complex may also be responsible for allotting the values of more dorsal cell types within the tube (see Fig. 14.11). For example, the dorsal domain of expression of Pax-3 extends more ventrally in chick embryos experimentally deprived of notochord and floor plate, while grafting an extra notochord adjacent to the dorsal neural tube leads to a repression of Pax-3 expression.

Cell populations of the early brain and their growth

Cells in the nervous system are derived from three embryonic sources. The neural plate and tube give rise to CNS neurones, astrocytes and oligodendrocytes, and neuronal neural crest cells and ectodermal placodes give rise to PNS ganglionic neurones, both somatic and autonomic, and associated astrocytes and Schwann cells. Neuronal neural crest cells also form all neurones and glia of the enteric nervous system and the chromaffin cells of the suprarenal (adrenal) cortex. Non-neuronal neural crest cells form angiogenic mesenchyme in the layers around the developing neural tube, and other haematogenic precursors give rise to microglial cells that subsequently enter the nervous system. Human neural development is now being investigated by in vitro studies of human pluripotent stem cells (hPSCs), 3D culture and the development of cerebral organoids from human tissue (Lee et al 2017). The latter have been used to identify cortical cell types infected by Zika virus and the screening of potential pharmacological treatments (Molnar et al 2019).
Proliferation of the ventricular zone of the early neural tube gives rise to neurones before glial cells. The timing of events differs in various parts of the CNS and between species. Most neurones are formed prenatally in mammals but postnatal neurogenesis occurs in the cerebral cortex, olfactory bulb and the small granular cells of the cerebellum. Gliogenesis continues after birth in periventricular and other sites.
Immature neurones are initially rotund or fusiform. Their cytoplasm contains a prominent Golgi apparatus, many lysosomes, glycogen and numerous unattached ribosomes. As maturation proceeds, cells send out fine cytoplasmic processes that contain neurofilaments, microtubules and other structures, often including centrioles at their bases where microtubules form. Internally, cisternae of the endoplasmic reticulum appear and attached ribosomes and mitochondria proliferate, while the glycogen content progressively diminishes.
Astrocytes differentiate into protoplasmic and fibrous phenotypes. Human cortical protoplasmic astrocytes first appear around postmenstrual week 20 and their density continues to increase into postnatal life. During early adulthood gliogenic progenitors from the subventricular zone can contribute to the generation of new astrocytes in response to injury (Krencik et al 2017).
Oligodendrocytes arise later in development and myelinate developing CNS axons over a protracted period. (For details of myelin formed by oligodendrocytes see Chapter 3.) Oligodendrocyte progenitor cells are prevalent initially in the brainstem at about postmenstrual week 22 and cortical myelination continues into postnatal life. The oligodendrocyte precursor cell population is heterogeneous; maturation appears to be both spatially and temporally controlled (Dimou and Simons 2017). Research in this area is frustrated by methodological difficulties (differentiated oligodendrocytes are very difficult to isolate): single cell transcriptome techniques have been used to study oligodendrocyte lineage (Van Bruggen et al 2017). Synapses between oligodendrocyte precusor cells and neurones have been observed. Vesicular neurotransmitter release and adenosine signalling via adenosine receptors regulate the migration of oligodendrocyte progenitor cells at the motor exit point transition zone in zebra fish embryos (Fontenas et al 2019; see p. 241).
Schwann cells are derived from neural crest cells of the head and trunk. They associate with most PNS axons and many produce myelin, responding to neuronal signals. Schwann cell precursors are multipotent embryonic progenitors that may migrate into the peripheral tissues and signpost nerve fibre migration routes; they also cling to extending axons and, in this way, spread throughout the body with the developing peripheral nervous system. Factors secreted by Schwann cell precursors may pattern the development of neurovascular bundles (Furlan and Adameyko 2018).
Microglial cells are derived from haemopoietic cells found in the yolk sac and precursor blood stem cells become seeded in various body regions (Tay et al 2017). Microglial cells enter the brain by two routes, initially via pial vessels and later also via the early cerebral circulation that supplies both the ventricular zone and the developing choroid plexuses in the lateral ventricle (Reemst et al 2016, Michell-Robinson et al 2017, Menassa and Gomez-Nicola 2018). Within the developing nervous system they have roles during late neurogenesis and gliogenesis. They remain within the CNS after the blood–brain barrier matures and are able to proliferate and respond to homeostatic disequilibrium throughout pre and postnatal life. For the time line of human microglial development see Menassa and Gomez-Nicola (2018).

Early neuronal growth

Early neuroblasts form one main process, the axon, and other processes that establish a dendritic tree. Studied in tissue culture, axons may grow as much as 1 mm per day, navigating with precision over complex courses and considerable distances to reach their targets. Eventually, axons make functional contact with their appropriate end organs (neuromuscular endings, secretomotor terminals, sensory corpuscles or synapses with other neurones). There are reciprocal interactions with the target tissues: reciprocal signalling occurs during development, postnatally and in adult life (Harris 2008, da Silva and Wang 2011).
Ramón y Cajal was the first to describe the expanded growing tips of axons, naming them growth cones. These tips are constantly active, changing shape and extending and withdrawing filopodia and lamellipodia, small protuberances that apparently ‘explore’ the local environment for a suitable surface over which to extend. This incessant palpatory activity depends on the organization and dynamics of actin filaments that fill the leading margin (peripheral (P−) domain) of the growth cone. It is regulated by actin-binding proteins that control all aspects of filament assembly, turnover, interactions with other filaments and cytoplasmic components, and the production of mechanical forces. Adhesive ligands and extrinsic guidance cues bind growth cone receptors to their substratum and trigger signalling activities involving Rho GTPases, kinases, phosphatases, cyclic nucleotides and Ca2+ fluxes. Adhesion to a substratum appears to be important for transient consolidation (stabilization) of the growth cone, elaboration of the axonal cytoskeleton in the direction of growth, and transduction of mechanical forces into traction in order to pull the axon towards its next target (Gomez and Letourneau 2014). After consolidation, the exploratory behaviour begins again, resembling the behaviour at the leading edge of migratory cells such as fibroblasts and neutrophils.
Axon extension occurs when growth cones encounter a permissive physical substrate containing short-range cues such as appropriate extracellular matrix components, transmembrane phosphatases and cadherins. Axonal repulsion requires negative cues that inhibit the progress of the growth cone. Long-range cues diffuse from distant targets down gradients of specific factors, inducing neurones to turn their axons either towards the source of an attractive signal or away from a repulsive signal. Topographic projections, such as the retinotectal pathway, are established as specific populations of growth cones follow spatial gradients of guidance cues (Kalil et al 2011).
Several families of signalling molecules are known to be instructive guidance cues. The original canonical group of netrins, semaphorins, slits and ephrins has now been amplified by members of three families of developmental morphogens (bone morphogenetic protein (BMP), Hedgehog and Wnt families), and a number of growth factors (hepatocyte growth factor (HGF), fibroblast growth factors (FGFs) and glial cell-derived neurotrophic factor (GDNF)) for subsets of motor axons, e.g. neurotrophins (brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) and neurotrophin 3 (NT3)) for sensory axons, neuregulins for thalamocortical axons, and vascular endothelial growth factor A (VEGFA) for commissural axons (Omotade et al 2017).
Once growth cones have arrived in their appropriate target area, they form terminals and synapses. Extensive dendritic trees are generated and subsequently shaped by dendrite retraction and pruning. Signalling mechanisms underlying these processes are poorly understood: centrosomal CaMKIIβ signalling has been proposed as a mechanism restricting dendrite elaboration while promoting dendrite pruning (Puram et al 2011). The final shape of dendritic trees is influenced by patterns of afferent connections and their activity and is necessary for the accurate formation of neuronal circuitry. Dendritic spine density in childhood exceeds adult values by two- to three-fold and begins to decrease during puberty. However, the overproduction and later pruning of supernumerary synapses continues well into the third decade of life before stabilizing (Petanjek et al 2011, Kolb et al 2013). Successful neuronal connectivity is the product of a series of tightly regulated programmed events that include axon initiation, growth cone formation and navigation, and the formation and pruning of dendritic trees and synapses (Chiu et al 2013).
Axonal and dendritic outgrowth is initiated and supported by neurotrophins. Differential responses reflect the distribution of receptors or components of the relevant signal transduction pathways on neuronal, axonal and dendritic surfaces. Structurally and functionally related neurotrophic molecules include the nerve growth factor (NGF) superfamily and the GDNF family (Cirulli and Alleva 2009, Park and Poo 2013, West et al 2014). The NGF superfamily includes BDNF, NT-3 and NT-4/5, which exert their survival effects selectively on particular subsets of neurones. NGF is specific to sensory ganglion cells from the neural crest, sympathetic postganglionic neurones and basal forebrain cholinergic neurones. BDNF promotes the survival of retinal ganglion cells, motor neurones and some placode-derived neurones, such as those of the nodose ganglion, that are unresponsive to NGF. NT-3 has effects on motor neurones, limb proprioceptive neurones and both placode- and neural crest-derived sensory neurones. Other growth factors that influence the growth and survival of neural cells include the FGFs and ciliary neurotrophic factor (CNTF), which are unrelated in sequence to the NGF family. Members of the FGF family support the survival of embryonic neurones from many regions of the CNS. CNTF may control the proliferation and differentiation of sympathetic ganglion cells and astrocytes.
Each of the neurotrophins binds specifically to certain receptors on the cell surface. The receptor p75NTR binds all the neurotrophins with similar affinity, whereas members of the family of receptor tyrosine kinases (Trks) bind with higher affinity and display binding preferences for particular neurotrophins; the presence of a Trk receptor seems to be required for p75NTR function.

CNS/PNS zone patterning

Relatively little is known about tissue interactions at the CNS/PNS junctional zone between neural plate-derived motor neurones (with axons extending into the periphery) and neural crest-derived somatic sensory neurones (with processes entering the neuraxis from the periphery). Transition zones between the central and peripheral nervous systems show segregation of neurones and their axons and also of central and peripheral glia. Different processes appear to operate in the spinal cord at the basal plate motor exit points and alar plate dorsal root entry zones. The experimental evidence for these findings has come largely from zebrafish and mouse embryos, indicating some conservation of developmental processes.
Early in development peripheral nervous cells are apparently able to migrate in and out of the developing spinal cord before the radial glial end feet establish an external glia limitans (Smith et al 2016). Once a continuous glia limitans is in place, neural crest derivatives, termed boundary cap cells, migrate along the spinal cord and transiently cluster at transition zones that match the sites of axonal exit and entry points. In mouse embryos, where Krox-20 is expressed early in rhombomeres 3 and 5, the expression persists in boundary cap cells and later in Schwann cells (Fontenas and Kucenas 2017). Loss of boundary cells adjacent to the basal plate results in the presence of ectopic cell bodies of motor neurones in the ventral rootlets; inactivation of Krox-20 results in ectopic cell bodies of motor neurones within the motor ventral rootlets and ectopic oligodendrocytes, rather than Schwann cells, within the proximal parts of both motor and sensory nerve rootlets. Perineurial glia are nkx2.2a-expressing cells that originate from the basal plate of the spinal cord and migrate apparently exclusively into the motor exit rootlets along the motor axons, ensheathing them, forming tight junctions and differentiating into perineurium. In zebrafish, loss of nkx2.2a leads to motor axons exiting the CNS ectopically, cell bodies of motor neurones moving out of the spinal cord and an absence of perineurial ensheathment of motor axons peripherally (Kucenas 2015, Fontenas and Kucenas 2017). The origin of sensory perineurial glia has yet to be established.
At the alar plate a different mechanism is seen. Axons extending centrally from sensory neurones in the dorsal root ganglia form actin-rich filopodia that extend toward the dorsal root entry zone. Pioneer axons migrate along the radial glial end-feet, then cross the CNS/PNS radial glial boundary to form rostral and caudal projections within the CNS (Nichols and Smith 2019). The growth cones swell during entry and are thought to rearrange their actin filaments dynamically as they cross the glia limitans. Whether or not boundary cap cells are associated with sensory axon entry is still unclear; the temporal dynamics indicate entry of rostral growth cones before more caudal ones, and the later coalescence of peripheral glial populations (Fontenas and Kucenas 2017, Nichols and Smith 2019).

Peripheral Nervous System

Somatic nerves

Spinal nerves

Each spinal nerve is connected to the spinal cord by a ventral root and a dorsal root (Fig. 14.18). The fibres of the ventral rootlets grow out from cell bodies in the anterior and lateral parts of the intermediate zone and pass through the overlying marginal zone and external limiting membrane. Some enter the myotomes of the somites, and some penetrate the somites, reaching the adjacent somatopleure: in both sites, they ultimately form α-, β- or γ-efferents. At appropriate levels these axons are accompanied by the outgrowing axons of preganglionic sympathetic neuroblasts (segments T1–L2) or preganglionic parasympathetic neuroblasts (S2–S4).
The fibres of the dorsal rootlets extend from cell somata in dorsal root ganglia into the spinal cord and into the periphery. Neural crest cells are produced continuously along the length of the spinal cord, but gangliogenic cells migrate only into the rostral part of each somitic sclerotome, where they condense and proliferate to form a bilateral series of oval primordial spinal ganglia (dorsal root ganglia) (see Fig. 14.6; Ch. 18). Negative factors in the caudal sclerotome deter neural crest cells from entering. The rostral sclerotome has a mitogenic effect on the crest cells that settle within it. From the ventral region of each ganglion, a small part separates to form sympathochromaffin cells, while the remainder becomes a definitive spinal ganglion (dorsal root ganglion). The spinal ganglia are arranged symmetrically at the sides of the neural tube and, except in the caudal region, are equal in number to the somites. The cells of the ganglia, like the cells of the intermediate zone of the early neural tube, are glial and neuronal precursors. The glial precursors develop into satellite cells (which become closely applied to the ganglionic nerve cell somata), Schwann cells and, possibly, other cells. The neuroblasts, at first round or oval, soon become fusiform, and their extremities gradually elongate into central and peripheral processes. The central processes grow into the neural tube as the fibres of the dorsal nerve roots, while the peripheral processes grow ventrolaterally to mingle with the fibres of the ventral root, thus forming a mixed spinal nerve. As development proceeds, the original bipolar form of the cells in the spinal ganglia changes: the two processes become closer and ultimately fuse to form a single stem, producing the characteristic morphology of a unipolar neurone. The bipolar form is retained in the ganglion of the vestibulocochlear nerve. For details of the timing of spinal nerve maturation, see Ashwell and Waite (2012).

Cranial nerves

Cranial nerves may contain motor or sensory fibres or both types of fibres. With the exception of the olfactory and optic nerves, cranial nerves develop in a manner that is similar, in some respects, to the development of the components of the spinal nerves. The somata of motor neuroblasts originate within the neuroepithelium, while those of sensory neuroblasts are derived from the neural crest, with the addition in the head of contributions from ectodermal placodes (Fig. 14.19).
The motor fibres of the cranial nerves that project to striated muscle are the axons of cells originating in the basal plate of the midbrain and hindbrain. The functional and morphological distinction between the neurones within these nerves is based on the types of muscle innervated. In the trunk, the motor roots of the spinal nerves all emerge from the spinal cord close to the ventral midline to supply the muscles derived from the somites.
In the head, the motor outflow is traditionally segregated into two pathways (see Figs 14.4B,C, 14.19). General somatic efferent neurones exit ventrally in a similar manner to those of the spinal cord. Thus, the oculomotor, trochlear, abducens and hypoglossal nerves parallel the organization of the somatic motor neurones in the spinal cord. The second motor component, special branchial efferent, consists of the motor components of the trigeminal, facial, glossopharyngeal and vagus nerves that supply the pharyngeal (branchial) arches, and the (spinal) accessory nerve. These nerves all have nerve exit points more dorsally placed than the somatic motor system.
The cranial nerves also contain general visceral efferent (parasympathetic preganglionic) neurones that travel in the oculomotor, facial, glossopharyngeal and vagus nerves and leave the hindbrain via the same exit points as the special branchial efferent fibres. All three categories of motor neurones probably originate from the same region of the basal plate, adjacent to the floor plate. The definitive arrangement of nuclei reflects the differential migration of neuronal somata. It is not known whether all these cell types share a common precursor within the rhombencephalon. In the spinal cord, somatic motor and preganglionic autonomic neurones are linearly related.
Motor neurone types are designated according to the types of muscles or structures they innervate. General somatic efferent nerves supply striated muscle derived from the cranial (occipital) somites and prechordal mesenchyme. Myogenic cells from the ventrolateral edge of the epithelial plate of occipital somites give rise to the intrinsic muscles of the tongue, while the prechordal mesenchyme gives rise to the extraocular muscles. Special branchial efferent nerves supply the striated muscles developing within the pharyngeal (branchial) arches, which are derived from parachordal mesenchyme rostrocaudally between the occipital somites and the prechordal mesenchyme. All the voluntary muscles of the head originate from axial (prechordal) or paraxial mesenchyme, rendering the distinction between somatic efferent supply and branchial efferent supply somewhat artificial. However, the obviously special nature of the arch musculature, its patterning by the neural crest cells, its particularly rich innervation for both voluntary and reflex activity, and the different origins from the basal plate of the branchial efferent nerves compared to the somatic efferent nerves, make the retention of a distinction between the two of some value.
The cranial sensory ganglia are derived in part from the neural crest and in part from cells of the ectodermal placodes (see Figs 14.8, 14.18). Generally, neurones distal to the brain are derived from placodes while proximal ones are derived from the neural crest (see Fig. 12.4). Supporting cells of all sensory ganglia arise from the neural crest. The most rostral sensory ganglion, the trigeminal, contains both neural crest- and placode-derived neurones that mediate general somatic afferent functions. This is also the case for more caudal cranial nerves (the facial, glossopharyngeal and vagus), but the two cell populations form separate ganglia in the case of each nerve. The proximal series of ganglia is neural crest-derived (forming the proximal root of the facial nerve, the superior ganglion of the glossopharyngeal nerve, the jugular ganglion of the vagus nerve, and associated glial precursors) while the distal series is derived from placodal cells (forming the neurones of the geniculate ganglion of the facial nerve, the petrosal ganglion of the glossopharyngeal nerve and the nodose ganglion of the vagus nerve). These ganglia contain neurones that mediate special and general visceral afferent and somatic afferent functions. The vestibular ganglion contains both crest and placodal cells, and the cochlear ganglion contains only placodal neurones; the axons from these cells are special somatic afferents and they all travel in the vestibulocochlear nerve.
The neurones and supporting cells of the cranial autonomic ganglia in the head and the trunk originate from neural crest cells. Caudal to the vagal ganglion, the occipital region of the neural crest is concerned with the ‘ganglia’ of the accessory and hypoglossal nerves. Rudimentary ganglion cells may occur along the hypoglossal nerve in the human embryo but they subsequently regress. Ganglion cells are found on the developing intraspinal root of the accessory nerve and are believed to persist in the adult. The central processes of the cells of these various ganglia, where they persist, enter the alar lamina of the hindbrain. Their peripheral processes join the efferent components of the nerve to be distributed to the various tissues innervated. Some incoming fibres from the facial, glossopharyngeal and vagus nerves collect to form an oval bundle, the tractus solitarius, on the lateral aspect of the myelencephalon. This bundle is the homologue of the oval bundle (of His) of the spinal cord, but in the hindbrain it becomes more deeply placed by the overgrowth, folding and subsequent fusion of tissue derived from the rhombic lip on the external aspect of the bundle. For details of the development of sensory and cranial nerve nuclei see Ashwell and Mai (2012).
Myelination of spinal and cranial nerves
PNS myelination, by Schwann cells, occurs over a protracted period that starts during the second trimester. Motor roots start to myelinate before sensory roots in the PNS, whereas sensory nerves start to myelinate before motor nerves. The cranial nerves of the midbrain, pons and medulla oblongata begin to myelinate from about postmenstrual week 22. For reviews of the molecular mechanisms regulating myelination in the CNS and PNS (including precursor specification, proliferation, migration, differentiation and myelination of appropriate axons), see Elbaz and Popko (2017) and Herbert and Monk (2017), respectively.

Autonomic nervous system

Autonomic nerves, apart from the preganglionic motor axons that arise from the CNS, are formed by the neural crest. For the purposes of the following description, the autonomic nervous system encompasses sympathetic and parasympathetic neurones and their accompanying glia in the peripheral ganglia, the chromaffin cells in the suprarenal medulla and other sites, and the neurones and glia of the enteric nervous system. Within the head, the neural crest cells migrate prior to neural fusion, generating autonomic neurones and a vast mesenchymal population. During caudal neurulation in the trunk, neural crest cells migrate from the neural epithelium to lie transitorily on the fused neural tube, they subsequently migrate laterally and then ventrally to their respective destinations (see Fig. 14.6). Four major neural crest cell populations, cranial, vagal, trunk and lumbosacral, are destined to form autonomic nerves.

Parasympathetic ganglia

Parasympathetic preganglionic (general visceral efferent) neurones innervate the sphincter pupillae and ciliaris muscles, the exocrine glands of the head, and the thoracic and abdominal viscera. From rostral to caudal, three populations of neural crest (cranial neural crest, cardiac neural crest and vagal neural crest) give rise to parasympathetic postganglionic neurones.
Neural crest cells from the caudal third of the mesencephalon and the rostral metencephalon migrate along or close to the ophthalmic branch of the trigeminal nerve and give rise to the ciliary ganglion. Cells migrating from the nucleus of the oculomotor nerve may also contribute to the ganglion; a few scattered cells are often demonstrable in postnatal life along the course of this nerve. Preotic myelencephalic neural crest cells give rise to the pterygopalatine ganglion, which may also receive contributions from the ganglia of the trigeminal and facial nerves. The otic and submandibular ganglia are also derived from myelencephalic neural crest and may receive contributions from the glossopharyngeal and facial cranial nerves, respectively (see Fig. 12.4).
Neural crest from the region located between the otic placode and the caudal limit of somite 3 has been termed cardiac neural crest. Cells derived from these levels migrate through pharyngeal arches 3, 4 and 6, where they provide, inter alia, support for the embryonic aortic arch arteries, cells of the aorticopulmonary septum and truncus arteriosus. Some of these neural crest cells also differentiate into the neural anlage of the parasympathetic ganglia of the heart.
The origin and migration of the sacral neural crest from the caudal eminence and the formation of the caudal parasympathetic ganglia has recently been disputed, and it has been suggested that the sacral autonomic flow is entirely sympathetic and not parasympathetic (Espinosa-Medina et al 2016, Espinosa-Medina et al 2017, Horn 2018). The distinct vasodilator and vasoconstrictor mechanisms of blood vessels in the pelvic viscera, and the complexities of control of the lower urinary tract, lower enteric sphincters and the reproductive reflexes pathways, plus their central connections to circuits in the spinal cord, brainstem, hypothalamus and cortex, are significant. The precise coordination of activity in these pathways and the distinctive functional discharge patterns that define each type of autonomic pathway inform this continuing discussion (Jänig et al 2018). Sacral postganglionic parasympathetic neurones are generated later than sympathetic neurones from sacral glial progenitor-like cells associated with the extending nerves. The analysis of transcription factors expressed by cranial, thoracolumbar and sacral autonomic neurone precursors suggest that, whereas the developmental processes of thoracolumbar and sacral autonomic neurones are similar, both differ from processes seen in the development of cranial parasympathetic precursors (Ernsberger and Rohrer 2018).

Sympathetic ganglia

Neural crest cells migrate ventrally, penetrate the underlying somites and continue to the region of the future paravertebral and prevertebral plexuses, where they form the sympathetic trunks and the major ganglia around the anterior visceral branches of the abdominal aorta (see Figs 14.6, 14.20). Neural crest cells are induced to differentiate into sympathetic neurones by the dorsal aorta through the actions of the signalling molecules Bmp-4 and Bmp-7. The sympathetic trunk appears earlier in the lower cervical and thoracic regions than in the lumbosacral regions; outflow from the lumbosacral sympathetic trunk to the sciatic nerve has been reported about postmenstrual weeks 18–21 (Ashwell and Waite 2012). In mouse embryos, the superior cervical ganglion may arise from vagal neural crest cells (Hutchins et al 2018).
Sympathetic preganglionic neuronal axons leave the spinal cord with the somatic motor rootlets at motor exit points and travel only in the ventral rootlets: the process is presumably controlled by boundary cap cells. When a postganglionic neuroblast is in place it extends axons and dendrites, and synaptogenesis occurs. The earliest axonal outgrowths from the superior cervical ganglion occur at about stage 14. The local environment plays a major role in controlling the appropriate differentiation of the presumptive autonomic ganglion neurones: the factors responsible for subsequent development of an adrenergic, cholinergic or peptidergic phenotype have not been identified. Cholinergic characteristics are acquired relatively early and the appropriate phenotypic expression may be promoted by cholinergic differentiation factor and ciliary neurotrophic factor.

Chromaffin cells

Chromaffin cells are derived from the neural crest. They are found at numerous sites throughout the body, namely the classic chromaffin cells of the suprarenal medulla, bronchial neuroepithelial cells, dispersed epithelial endocrine cells of the gut (formerly known as argentaffin cells), carotid body cells and the paraganglia. The sympathetic ganglia, suprarenal medulla and chromaffin cells are all derived from the cells of the sympathosuprarenal lineage. In the suprarenal medulla, first seen in stage 14, these cells differentiate into small and intermediate-sized neuroblasts or sympathoblasts, and larger, initially rounded phaeochromocytoblasts. Large cells with pale nuclei, thought to be the progenitors of chromaffin cells, can be detected after stage 23, and clusters of small neurones are evident from postmenstrual week 14.
Intermediate-sized neuroblasts differentiate into multipolar postganglionic sympathetic neurones that secrete noradrenaline (norepinephrine) at their terminals. The smaller neuroblasts probably correlate with the small, intensely fluorescent (SIF) cells, types I and II, that store and secrete dopamine type I and are thought to function as true interneurones, synapsing with the principal postganglionic neurones. Type II cells probably operate as local neuroendocrine cells, secreting dopamine into the ganglionic microcirculation. Both types of SIF cells can modulate synaptic transmission in the ganglionic neurones. The large cells differentiate into masses of columnar or polyhedral phaeochromocytes (classic chromaffin cells) that secrete either adrenaline (epinephrine) or noradrenaline. These cell masses are termed paraganglia and may be situated near, on the surface of, or embedded in, the capsules of the ganglia of the sympathetic chain, or in some of the large autonomic plexuses. The largest members of the latter are the para-aortic bodies that lie along the sides of the abdominal aorta in relation to the inferior mesenteric artery. During childhood, the para-aortic bodies and the paraganglia of the sympathetic trunk partly degenerate and can no longer be isolated by gross dissection; even in the adult, chromaffin tissue can still be recognized microscopically in these various sites. Both phaeochromocytes and SIF cells belong to the amine precursor uptake and decarboxylation (APUD) series of cells and are paraneuronal in nature. Suprarenal chromaffin-like cells have been derived in culture from human pluripotential stem cells (Abu-Bonsrah et al 2018). For further reading about the mechanisms of chromaffin cell development see Unsicker et al (2013) and Hockman et al (2018).

Enteric nervous system

The enteric nervous system is different from the other components of the autonomic nervous system because it can mediate reflex activity independently of control by the brain and spinal cord. The number of enteric neurones that develop is believed to be of the same magnitude as the number of neurones in the spinal cord. The mechanisms by which neuronal diversity is generated in the enteric nervous system are unclear: to date, expression patterns of a number of transcription and signalling factors have been mapped in the developing murine and human enteric nervous systems using transcriptome and histochemical analyses (Memic et al 2018).
Neuronal and glial enteric nervous system precursors are derived from vagal and sacral neural crest. The axial levels of crest origin are shown in Fig. 14.20. Rostrally, adjacent to the early rhombencephalon formed by primary neurulation, vagal neural crest cells give rise to both neuroblasts and glial support cells throughout the gut. In experimental studies on chick and mouse embryos, vagal neural crest cells destined for the gut appear to travel through the rostral half of adjacent somites and are thought to gain enteric lineage from exposure to retinoic acid within the somatic mesenchyme. Vagal neural crest cells migrate ventrally to enter the gut, then turn caudally to populate the entire length of the gut progressing at a migratory speed of about 40 μm/hour (Nagy and Goldstein 2017).
Within the gut of chick and mouse embryos, vagal neural crest cells migrate more rapidly when in contact with each other in a wave front of chain migration. The cells proliferate as they migrate, and the end of proliferation promotes the onset of differentiation. Those cells at the leading edge of the wave front move into unpopulated regions of splanchnopleuric mesenchyme that support their migration and subsequent differentiation (Lake and Heuckeroth 2013, Nagy and Goldstein 2017). The submucosal plexus is formed by a secondary radial migration from the myenteric plexus. Enteric neural crest cells differentiate into sensory and motor neurones and interneurones, and into four types of glial cells with a transcriptome that overlaps with astrocytes, oligodendrocytes and Schwann cells (Charrier and Pilon 2017). Almost all enteric glia express myelin proteolipid protein, and have more genes in common with myelinating glia than with astrocytes (Rao and Gershon 2018). Neurospheres of human postnatal enteric neural stem cells transplanted into chick or mouse embryos interact and migrate within their hosts to form chimeric enteric ganglia (Cheng et al 2017). A population of vagal neural crest cells that colonize the foregut secondarily migrate into the developing pancreatic buds and give rise to the resident ganglia and Schwann cells around the islets of Langerhans (Hutchins et al 2018).
Caudally, from somites 28 onwards, neural crest cells and somites arise by secondary neurulation processes within the caudal eminence. Sacral neural crest cells migrate through the adjacent somites and then ventrally within the mesocolon towards the hindgut (which will form the distal part of the colon, rectum and cloaca); some crest cells subsequently contribute to components of the pelvic plexus (see Fig. 14.6). Once within the gut wall, a regionally specific pattern of enteric ganglia formation occurs, possibly controlled by the local splanchnopleuric mesenchyme. In the mouse embryo, sacral neural crest cells do not colonize the distal colon until vagal neural crest cells are present, at which time the sacral neural crest cells migrate in a caudorostral direction (Mundell et al 2012). The cells lie initially within the developing mesentery, then transiently between the layers of the differentiating muscularis externa, and finally form a more substantial intramural plexus that is characteristic of the adult enteric nervous system.
The human gut has a partially established enteric nervous system with a full complement of enteric neurotransmitters by postmenstrual week 24. Maturation continues into the postnatal period: mucosal enteric glial cells that move into the mucosa after birth interact with the colonizing intestinal flora, suggesting a microbiota-driven homeostatic mechanism for gut function (Ashwell and Waite 2012, Kabouridis and Pachnis 2015). For details of the molecular mechanisms of enteric nervous system development see Rao and Gershon (2018).
Hirschsprung’s disease appears to result from a failure of neural crest cells to colonize the gut wall appropriately. The proximal extent of the condition is variable but the rectum is always affected. This distribution suggests that Hirschsprung’s disease is a failure of migration and differentiation of the most distal vagal neural crest and/or failure of migration and differentiation of the sacral neural crest derived from secondary neurulation. Histologically, ganglia and postganglionic neurones in the myenteric plexus of the affected segment of gut are either absent or reduced in number and postganglionic innervation of the muscle layers is also often defective. The condition is characterized by a dilated segment of colon proximally (or small bowel in cases of total colonic Hirschsprung’s disease) and a lack of peristalsis in the segment distal to the dilation. The condition is also characterized by a transition zone with reduced innervation that may follow an asymmetric course around the circumference of the bowel, which means that a single biopsy may be unreliable. Neonates with Hirschsprung’s disease show delay in the passage of meconium, constipation, vomiting and abdominal distension. The condition is often associated with other defects of neural crest development, e.g. Waardenburg type II syndrome, which includes deafness and facial clefts with megacolon.

Central Nervous System

Spinal cord

In the future spinal cord, the median roof plate (dorsal lamina) and floor plate (ventral lamina) of the neural tube do not participate in the cellular proliferation that occurs in the lateral walls and so remain thin. Their cells contribute largely to the formation of the ependyma.
The neuroblasts of the lateral walls of the tube are large and, at first, round or oval (apolar). They soon develop processes at opposite poles and become bipolar neurones. One process is then withdrawn so that the neuroblast becomes unipolar, although this is not invariable in the spinal cord. On further differentiation, dendritic processes develop and the cells become typical multipolar neurones lying in clusters of neuronal clones. The development of a longitudinal sulcus limitans on each side of the central canal of the cord divides the ventricular and intermediate zones in each lateral wall into a basal (ventrolateral) plate or lamina, and an alar (dorsolateral) plate or lamina (see Fig. 14.18). This separation signifies a fundamental functional difference. Neural precursors in the basal plate include the motor cells of the anterior (ventral) and lateral grey columns, while those of the alar plate exclusively form ‘interneurones’, some of which will synapse with the terminals of primary sensory neurones. Caudally, the central canal of the cord ends as a fusiform dilation, the terminal ventricle.

Anterior (ventral) grey column

The cells of the ventricular zone are closely packed at this stage and arranged in radial columns (see Fig. 14.14). Their disposition may be determined in part by contact guidance along the earliest radial array of glial fibres that cross the full thickness of the early neuroepithelium. The cells of the intermediate zone are more loosely packed. They increase in number initially in the region of the basal plate. This enlargement outlines the anterior (ventral) column of the grey matter and causes a ventral projection on each side of the median plane; the floor plate remains at the bottom of the shallow groove so produced. As growth proceeds, these enlargements are further increased by the development of the anterior funiculi (tracts of axons passing to and from the brain) and encroach on the groove until it becomes converted into the slit-like anterior median fissure of the adult spinal cord. The axons of some of the neuroblasts in the anterior grey column cross the marginal zone and emerge as bundles of ventral spinal nerve rootlets on the anterolateral aspect of the spinal cord. These will eventually constitute both the α-efferents that establish motor end-plates on extrafusal striated muscle fibres and the γ-efferents that innervate the contractile polar regions of the intrafusal muscle fibres of the muscle spindles. As the ventral grey column matures the motor neurones become grouped into motor pools containing functionally coherent subsets of motor neurones that innervate individual muscles in the limb (Dewitz et al 2018). Motor neurone progenitors initially produce medial groups of neurons, later born neurones migrate radially through the medial group to settle in the lateral ventral horn. N-cadherin is required for this migration but does not specify the dorsoventral positioning of these cells (Dewitz et al 2018).
Neurones in different motor pools can be distinguished by their surface protein expression and transcription factor profiles. Motor pools that innervate limb muscles with related functions are grouped into minicolumns or columels, in a 3D organization that reflects the position of the muscles along the three main axes of the limb. Thus, for example, a dorsoventral motor columel and its resident pools displays a linear correlation with the distal-to-proximal position of its synergistic target muscles (Jessell et al 2011, Sürmeli et al 2011). In vitro culture of hPSCs along neuronal pathways has elucidated the developmental steps for patterning the rostrocaudal (Hox gene expression) and dorsoventral (Shh expression) axes of the neural tube and producing postmitotic neural precursor subtypes. Factors promoting further development of motor neurone progenitors have been established (Pantani 2016).

Lateral grey column

Some intermediate zone neuroblasts in the thoracic and upper lumbar regions in the dorsal part of the basal plate outline a lateral column. Their axons join the emerging ventral nerve roots and pass as preganglionic fibres either to the ganglia of the sympathetic trunk or related ganglia, the majority eventually myelinating to form white rami communicantes. The axons within the rami synapse on autonomic ganglionic neurones; postganglionic axons innervate smooth muscle cells, adipose tissue or exocrine glandular cells. Other preganglionic sympathetic efferent axons pass to the suprarenal medulla. An autonomic lateral column is also laid down in the mid-sacral region and gives origin to the preganglionic parasympathetic fibres that run in the pelvic splanchnic nerves, although this origin has recently been disputed (Espinosa-Medina et al 2016, Espinosa-Medina et al 2017, Horn 2018).
The ventral region of each basal plate initially forms a continuous column of cells throughout the length of the developing cord. This soon develops into two columns on each side: one is medially placed and concerned with innervation of the axial musculature, and the other is laterally placed and concerned with the innervation of the limb musculature. At limb levels, the lateral column enlarges enormously, but regresses at other levels.
Axons arising from ventral horn neurones, i.e. α-, β- and γ-efferent fibres, are accompanied at thoracic, upper lumbar and midsacral levels by preganglionic autonomic efferents from neuroblasts of the developing lateral horn. Numerous interneurones develop in these sites (including Renshaw cells); it is uncertain how many of these differentiate directly from ventrolateral lamina (basal plate) neuroblasts and how many migrate to their final positions from the dorsolateral lamina (alar plate).
In the human embryo the definitive grouping of the ventral column cells that characterizes the mature cord occurs early and by postmenstrual week 14 all the major groups can be recognized. As the anterior and lateral grey columns assume their final form, the germinal cells in the ventral part of the ventricular zone gradually stop dividing. The layer becomes reduced in thickness until it forms the single-layered ependyma that lines the ventral part of the central canal of the spinal cord.

Posterior (dorsal) grey column

The posterior (dorsal) column develops later, which means that the ventricular zone is transiently much thicker in the dorsolateral lamina (alar plate) than it is in the ventrolateral lamina (basal plate) (see Figs 14.14, 14.18). While the columns of grey matter are being defined, the dorsal region of the central canal becomes narrow and slit-like, and its walls come into apposition and fuse with each so that the central canal becomes relatively reduced in size and somewhat triangular in outline.
From about stage 13 advancing axonal sprouts invade the marginal zone. The first to develop are those destined to become short intersegmental fibres (from the neuroblasts in the intermediate zone) and fibres in the dorsal roots of spinal nerves that invade the dorsal marginal zone (from small dorsal root ganglionic neuroblasts). By about stage 16 the latter form a well-defined oval bundle near the peripheral part of the dorsolateral lamina (see Fig. 14.14). This bundle of small calibre axons increases in size and spreads towards the median plane, forming the primitive posterior funiculus. Later, fibres derived from new populations of large dorsal root ganglionic neuroblasts join the dorsal root and are destined to become larger calibre fibres. As the posterior funiculi increase in thickness, their medial surfaces come into contact, separated only by the posterior medial septum, which is ependymal in origin and neuroglial in nature. It is thought that the displaced primitive posterior funiculus may form the basis of the dorsolateral tract or fasciculus (of Lissauer). Experimental studies have shown that the connection of first order 1A afferent neurones from muscle spindles to the appropriate dorsoventral motor neurone pool is independent of the target motor neurones: these sensory afferents avoid synapsing on motor neurones that innervate antagonistic muscles at the same joint (Sürmeli et al 2011).

Maturation of the spinal cord

Long intersegmental axons begin to appear early in the second trimester; corticospinal axons are seen somewhat later. All nerve fibres initially lack myelin sheaths. Myelination starts in different groups at different times, e.g. along the ventral and dorsal nerve roots during the second trimester, and persists until overall growth of the CNS and PNS has finished. In many sites, slow growth continues for long periods, even into the postpubertal years. In the CNS, myelin is formed by oligodendrocytes derived from the ventricular zone of the neural tube, and in the PNS myelin is formed by Schwann cells derived from neural crest cells. A boundary zone controlled by neural crest-derived boundary cap cells separates these two myelin sources (see p. 241). The cervical and lumbar enlargements appear at the time of the development of their respective limb buds.
Ascent of conus medullaris
The spinal cord occupies the entire length of the vertebral canal in early embryonic life; the spinal nerves pass at right-angles to the cord. From the second trimester, the vertebral column grows more rapidly than the spinal cord, which means that the relationship between the conus medullaris and the vertebral column changes as the conus gradually ascends to lie at higher vertebral levels (Barson 1970). Most of this relative rostral migration occurs during the first half of intrauterine life. By postmenstrual week 25 the terminal ventricle of the spinal cord has altered in level from the second coccygeal vertebra to the third lumbar, a distance of nine segments (Ch. 18, p. 300). As the change in level begins rostrally, the caudal end of the terminal ventricle, which is adherent to the overlying ectoderm, remains in situ, and the walls of the intermediate part of the ventricle and its covering pia mater become drawn out to form a delicate filament, the filum terminale. The separated portion of the terminal ventricle persists for a time; it usually disappears before birth, although it does occasionally give rise to congenital cysts near the coccyx. In the definitive state, the upper cervical spinal nerves retain their position roughly at right-angles to the cord: proceeding caudally, the nerve roots lengthen and become progressively more oblique. By postmenstrual week 40 the conus medullaris typically lies between the first and third lumbar vertebrae.
Determining the prenatal position of the conus medullaris relative to specific vertebrae is a widely used method of identifying the termination of the spinal cord. Studies of formalin-fixed fetuses, usually of preterm stillbirths that may be small for dates, have provided the main timescales. More recently, values have been derived from antenatal ultrasound and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) examinations. The often wide discrepancies between published values attest to the fact that ageing preterm infants remains an inexact science. Measurements may be influenced by factors such as placental health, maternal diet, ethnic biometrical variation, fetal sex and the interpretation and correlation of results obtained using differing methodologies (cadaveric dissection, ultrasound, MRI).

Brain

A summary of the derivatives of the cerebral regions from caudal to rostral is given in Table 14.1.

Rhombencephalon

By the time the midbrain flexure appears, the length of the rhombencephalon is greater than that of the combined extent of the mesencephalon and prosencephalon. Rostrally, it exhibits a constriction, the isthmus rhombencephali (see Fig. 14.4A), best viewed from the dorsal aspect. Ventrally, the hindbrain is separated from the dorsal wall of the primitive pharynx only by the notochord, the two dorsal aortae and a small amount of mesenchyme; on each side, it is closely related to the dorsal ends of the pharyngeal arches.
The pontine flexure appears to ‘stretch’ the thin, epithelial roof plate, which becomes widened. The greatest increase in width corresponds to the region of maximum convexity, so that the outline of the roof plate becomes rhomboidal. By the same change, the lateral walls become separated, particularly dorsally, and the cavity of the hindbrain, (presumptive fourth ventricle), becomes flattened and somewhat triangular in cross-section. The pontine flexure becomes increasingly acute until, at the end of the first trimester, the laminae of its cranial (metencephalic) and caudal (myelencephalic) slopes are opposed to each other (see Fig. 14.22); at the same time, the lateral angles of the cavity extend to form the lateral recesses of the fourth ventricle.
At about stage 14 when the pontine flexure is first discernible, the association between the rhombomeres and the underlying motor nuclei of certain cranial nerves can be seen. The general pattern of distribution of motor nuclei is as follows: rhombomere 1 contains the trochlear nucleus, rhombomeres 2 and 3 contain the trigeminal nucleus, rhombomeres 4 and 5 contain the facial nucleus, rhombomere 5 contains the abducens nucleus, rhombomeres 6 and 7 contain the glossopharyngeal nucleus, and rhombomeres 7 and 8 contain the vagal, accessory and hypoglossal nuclei. Rhombomeric segmentation represents the ground plan of development in this region of the brainstem and is pivotal for the development of regional identity (see Figs 14.2, 12.4). With further morphogenesis, the obvious constrictions of the rhombomere boundaries disappear and the medulla once again assumes a smooth contour. The differentiation of the lateral walls of the hindbrain into basal (ventrolateral) and alar (dorsolateral) plates has a similar significance to the corresponding differentiation in the lateral wall of the spinal cord: ventricular, intermediate and marginal zones are formed in the same way.
Cell columns of the basal plate (ventrolateral lamina)
Cells of the basal plate form three elongated, discontinuous, columns: ventral, intermediate and dorsal (Fig. 14.21). The most ventral column is continuous with the anterior grey column of the spinal cord and will supply muscles considered ‘myotomic’ in origin. It is represented in the caudal part of the hindbrain by the hypoglossal nucleus, and it reappears at a higher level as the nuclei of the abducens, trochlear and oculomotor nerves (somatic efferent nuclei). The intermediate column is represented in the upper part of the spinal cord and caudal brainstem (medulla oblongata and pons), and its neurones supply branchial (pharyngeal) and postbranchial musculature. It is discontinuous, forming the elongated nucleus ambiguus in the caudal brainstem, which gives fibres to the glossopharyngeal, vagal and (cranial) accessory nerves, and continues into the cervical spinal cord as the origin of the (spinal) accessory nerve. At higher levels, parts of this column give origin to the motor nuclei of the facial and trigeminal nerves. The nucleus ambiguus and the facial and trigeminal motor nuclei are termed branchial (special visceral) efferent nuclei. Neurones in the most dorsal column of the basal plate (represented in the spinal cord by the lateral grey column) innervate viscera. This column is discontinuous in that its large caudal part forms some of the dorsal nucleus of the vagus and its cranial part forms the salivatory nucleus. These nuclei are termed general visceral (general splanchnic) efferent nuclei, and their neurones give rise to preganglionic, parasympathetic nerve fibres. The neurones of the basal plate and their three columnar derivatives are only motor in the sense that some of their number form either motor neurones or preganglionic parasympathetic neurones. The remainder greatly outnumber the former and differentiate into functionally related interneurones and, in some loci, into neuroendocrine cells.
Cell columns of the alar plate (dorsolateral lamina)
Cell columns of the alar plate are discontinuous and give rise to general visceral (general splanchnic) afferent, special visceral (special splanchnic) afferent, general somatic afferent and special somatic afferent nuclei (their relative positions, in simplified transverse section, are shown in Fig. 14.21). The general visceral afferent column is represented by a part of the dorsal nucleus of the vagus, the special visceral afferent column by the nucleus of the tractus solitarius, the general somatic afferent column by the afferent nuclei of the trigeminal nerve, and the special somatic afferent column by the nuclei of the vestibulocochlear nerve. (The relatively simple functional independence of these afferent columns implied by the foregoing classification is, in the main, an aid to elementary learning. The emergent neurobiological mechanisms are much more complex and less well understood.) Although they tend to retain their primitive positions, some of these nuclei are later displaced by differential growth patterns, by the appearance and growth of neighbouring fibre tracts, and possibly by active migration.
It has been suggested that a neurone tends to remain as near as possible to its predominant source of stimulation, and that, to achieve this aim, it will migrate around intervening structures, towards the greatest density of stimuli. The curious paths of the axons arising from the facial nucleus and the nucleus ambiguus have been regarded as exemplars of this phenomenon of so called neurobiotaxis. At stage 16 the facial nucleus lies in the floor of the fourth ventricle, occupying the position of the special visceral efferent column, and it is placed at a higher level than the abducens nucleus. As growth proceeds, the facial nucleus migrates at first caudally and dorsally, relative to the abducens nucleus, and then ventrally to reach its adult position. As it migrates, the axons to which its somata give rise elongate and their subsequent course is assumed to map out the pathway along which the facial nucleus has travelled. Similarly, the nucleus ambiguus initially arises immediately deep to the ventricular floor but, in the adult, it is more deeply placed and its efferent fibres pass first dorsally and medially before curving laterally to emerge at the surface of the medulla oblongata.

Myelencephalon

The caudal slope of the embryonic hindbrain constitutes the myelencephalon, which develops into the medulla oblongata (see Figs 14.4, 14.19, 14.22). The nuclei of the glossopharyngeal, vagal, accessory and hypoglossal cranial nerves develop in the positions already indicated: afferent fibres from the ganglia of the glossopharyngeal and vagus nerves form an oval marginal bundle in the region overlying the alar (dorsolateral) lamina. Throughout the rhombencephalon, the dorsal edge of this lamina is attached to the thin, expanded roof plate and is termed the rhombic lip. (The inferior rhombic lip is confined to the myelencephalon, the superior rhombic lip to the metencephalon.) As the walls of the rhombencephalon spread outwards, the rhombic lip protrudes as a lateral edge that becomes folded over the adjoining area. The rhombic lip may later become adherent to this area, and its cells migrate actively into the marginal zone of the basal plate. In this way, the oval bundle that forms the tractus solitarius becomes buried. Alar plate cells that migrate from the rhombic lip are believed to give rise to the olivary and arcuate nuclei and to the scattered grey matter of the nuclei pontis. While this migration is in progress, the floor plate is invaded by fibres that cross the median plane (accompanied by neurones that cluster in and near this plane), and it becomes thickened to form the median raphe. Some of the migrating cells from the rhombic lip in this region do not reach the basal plate and form an oblique ridge, the corpus pontobulbare (nucleus of the circumolivary bundle), across the dorsolateral aspect of the inferior cerebellar peduncle.
The lower part (caudal half) of the myelencephalon takes no part in the formation of the fourth ventricle and closely resembles the spinal cord in its development. The gracile and cuneate nuclei and some reticular nuclei are derived from the alar plate, and their efferent arcuate fibres and interspersed neurones play a large part in the formation of the median raphe.
The descending corticospinal fibres invade the ventral part of the medulla oblongata to initiate formation of the pyramids. Contemporaneously, the inferior cerebellar peduncle is formed dorsally by ascending fibres from the spinal cord, and by olivocerebellar, parolivocerebellar and external arcuate fibres, and two-way reticulocerebellar and vestibulocerebellar interconnections. (The reticular nuclei of the lower medulla probably have a dual origin from both basal and alar plates.) In a full-term neonate, the brainstem is more oblique and has a distinct bend as it passes through the foramen magnum to become the spinal cord.

Metencephalon

The rostral slope of the embryonic hindbrain is the metencephalon, from which both the cerebellum and the pons develop. Before formation of the pontine flexure, the dorsolateral laminae of the metencephalon are parallel with one another. After its formation, the roof plate of the hindbrain becomes rhomboidal and the dorsal laminae of the metencephalon lie obliquely. They are close at the cranial end of the fourth ventricle, but widely separated at the level of its lateral angles (see Figs 14.19, 14.22). Accentuation of the flexure approximates the cranial angle of the ventricle to the caudal, and the alar plates of the metencephalon now lie almost horizontally.
The basal plate of the metencephalon becomes the pons. Ventricular, intermediate and marginal zones are formed in the usual way, and the nuclei of the trigeminal, abducens and facial nerves develop in the intermediate layer. It is possible that the grey matter of the reticular formation is derived from the basal plate and that of the nuclei pontis from the alar plate by the active migration of cells from the rhombic lip. At about postmenstrual weeks 15–16 the pons is invaded by corticopontine, corticonuclear and corticospinal fibres, becomes proportionately thicker, and takes on its adult appearance; it is relatively smaller in the full-term neonate.
The region of the isthmus rhombencephali undergoes a series of changes that are notoriously difficult to interpret. They result in the incorporation of the greater part of the region into the caudal end of the midbrain. Only the roof plate, in which the superior medullary velum is formed, and the dorsal part of the alar plate, which becomes invaded by converging fibres of the superior cerebellar peduncles, remain as recognizable derivatives in the adult. Early in development, the decussation of the trochlear nerves is caudal to the isthmus, but it is gradually displaced rostrally until it reaches its adult position.
Fourth ventricle and choroid plexus
Caudal to the developing cerebellum, the roof of the fourth ventricle remains epithelial, and covers an approximately triangular zone from the lateral angles of the rhomboid fossa to the median obex (see Figs 14.19, 14.22). Nervous tissue fails to develop over this region and vascular pia mater is closely applied to the subjacent ependyma. At each lateral angle and in the midline caudally, the membranes break through, forming the lateral (Luschka) and median (Magendie) apertures of the roof of the fourth ventricle: they become the principal routes by which cerebrospinal fluid, produced in the ventricles, escapes into the subarachnoid space. The vascular pia mater (tela choroidea), in an inverted V formation cranial to the apertures, invaginates the ependyma to form vascular fringes that become the vertical and horizontal parts of the choroid plexuses of the fourth ventricle.
Cerebellum
The cerebellum develops from the rhombic lip (dorsal rhombomere 1 (r1)), the dorsal part of the alar plate of the metencephalon, which constitutes the rostral margin of the diamond-shaped fourth ventricle. This region is specified as the isthmus organizer (Yu et al 2013). Two rounded swellings develop and initially project partly into the ventricle, forming the rudimentary cerebellar hemispheres (Fig. 14.22). The most rostral part of the roof of the metencephalon originally separates the two swellings, but it becomes invaded by cells derived from the alar plate, which form the rudiments of the vermis. At a later stage, extroversion of the cerebellum occurs, its intraventricular projection is reduced and the dorsal extraventricular prominence increases. The cerebellum now consists of a bilobar (dumbbell-shaped) swelling stretched across the rostral part of the fourth ventricle (see Fig. 14.22). It is continuous rostrally with the superior medullary velum, formed from the isthmus rhombencephali, and caudally with the epithelial roof of the myelencephalon. With growth, a number of transverse grooves appear on the dorsal aspects of the cerebellar rudiment: they are the precursors of the numerous fissures that characterize the surface of the mature cerebellum (Fig. 14.23).
The first fissure to appear on the cerebellar surface is the lateral part of the posterolateral fissure, which forms the border of a caudal region corresponding to the flocculi of the adult. The right and left parts of this fissure subsequently meet in the midline, where they form the boundary between the most caudal vermian lobule, the nodule, and the rest of the vermis. The flocculonodular lobe can now be recognized as the most caudal cerebellar subdivision at this stage and it serves as the attachment of the epithelial roof of the fourth ventricle. Because of the expansion of the other divisions of the cerebellum, the flocculonodular lobe comes to occupy an anteroinferior position in adults. At the end of the first trimester, a transverse sulcus appears on the rostral slope of the cerebellar rudiment and deepens to form the fissura prima that cuts into the vermis and both hemispheres, and forms the border between the anterior and posterior lobes. Contemporaneously, two short transverse grooves appear in the caudal vermis. The first is the fissura secunda (postpyramidal fissure), which forms the rostral border of the uvula and the second is the pre-pyramidal fissure, which demarcates the pyramid. The cerebellum now grows dorsally, rostrally, caudally and laterally, and the hemispheres expand much more than the inferior vermis, which therefore becomes buried at the bottom of a deep hollow, the vallecula. Numerous other transverse grooves develop, the most extensive being the horizontal fissure.
Cellular development of the cerebellum
The cerebellum consists of a cortex, beneath which are buried a series of deep nuclei. The organization of the cerebellar cortex is similar to that of the cerebral cortex, except that the latter typically has six layers while the former has only three. However, whereas in the cerebral cortex neuroblasts originate from the ventricular zone and migrate ventriculofugally towards the pial surface (in an ‘inside-out’ fashion), early in cerebellar development a layer of cells derived exclusively from the metencephalic rhombic lip initially migrates ventriculofugally to form a layer beneath the glia limitans over the surface of the developing cerebellum. These cells form the external germinative layer; later in development their progeny will migrate ventriculopetally (in an ‘outside-in’ manner), into the cerebellum. The cerebellum therefore has an intraventricular portion (cells proliferating from the ventricular zone) and an extraventricular portion (cells proliferating from the external germinative layer) during development. The extraventricular portion becomes larger at the expense of the intraventricular portion, the so-called extroversion of the cerebellum. Before the end of the first trimester, the main mass of the cerebellum is extraventricular.
The developed cerebellar cortex contains three layers, namely the molecular, Purkinje and granular layers. The early bilateral expansion of the ventricular surface reflects the production, by the metencephalic alar plate ventricular epithelium, of neuroblasts that will give rise to the radial glia, cerebellar nuclei and efferent neurones of the cerebellar cortex (the Purkinje cells) (Fig. 14.24). The radial glia play a role in guiding the Purkinje cells to the meningeal surface of the cerebellar anlage. During this early stage of cerebellar development, which is dominated by the production and migration of efferent cerebellar neurones, the surface of the cerebellar anlage remains smooth. The extroversion of the cerebellum begins later when cells of the external germinative layer, also termed the superficial matrix, begin proliferation and migration. These cells produce the granule cells which migrate inwards along the radial glia, through the layers of Purkinje cells, settling deep to them in the granular layer. This stage coincides with the emergences of the transverse folial pattern. Proliferation and migration of granule cells lead to a great rostrocaudal expansion of the meningeal surface of the cerebellum, forming the transverse fissures and transforming the multicellular layer of Purkinje cells into a monolayer. The development of the efferent neurones of the cerebellar cortex and nuclei precedes the development of its afferent organization: Purkinje cells and nuclear cells are formed prior to the granule cells, which are the recipients of the main afferent (mossy fibre) system of the cerebellum.
The early bilateral cerebellar anlage is changed into a unitary structure by fusion of the bilateral intraventricular bulges and the disappearance of the ependyma at this site; the merging of the left and right primitive cerebellar cortices over the midline; and the development of the cerebellar commissure by ingrowth of afferent fibres and outgrowth of efferent axons from the medial cerebellar nuclei.
The distinction between the efferent projections from the vermis and hemispheres is established at an early age. When the external germinative layer is initially formed, the multicellular Purkinje cell layer beneath is not uniform, but subdivided into clusters that form columns that extend rostrocaudally (Fig. 14.25). These clusters will give rise to Purkinje cell zones in the adult cerebellum that project to a single vestibular or cerebellar nucleus. The medial Purkinje cell clusters develop into the future vermis and their axons will synapse on neurones in the vestibular and fastigial nuclei. The lateral clusters belong to the future hemispheres and will grow axons that terminate in the interposed and dentate nuclei.
In the developing human brain, only the external germinative layer can be seen at postmenstrual weeks 17–18; the Purkinje cells become apparent between postmenstrual weeks 20 and 23. After postmenstrual week 30 four layers can be recognized: the external germinative layer (external granular layer), formed by 6–8 rows of densely packed small round cells; the Purkinje cell layer, formed by 5–6 layers of larger, round, immature neurones external to the internal granular layer; and the molecular layer, containing cells that resemble external granular layer cells in migration (Lavezzi et al 2006). The external granular layer involutes between postnatal months 5–7; it is only a discontinuous layer at postnatal month 10 and is totally absent by postnatal month 12, after which time the cerebellar cortex shows a three-layered structure. From postnatal months 5–7 the Purkinje cells are reduced in number and more widely spaced, displaying mature polygonal somata with evident axons and dendrites.
Relatively little is known about the molecular mechanisms involved in cerebellar development in the human (but see Manto and Jissendi (2012), Yu et al (2013), Martinez et al (2013)). The cerebellum undergoes its major growth during the third trimester and infancy (Leto et al 2016). Very small preterm infants are at particular risk of later cerebellar dysfunction with consequences for cognition and behaviour. Because much of cerebellar maturation occurs postnatally there is interest in therapeutic agents that may improve long-term outcomes for such infants (Manto and Jissendi 2012). A summary of mammalian cerebellar development including human cerebellar anomalies is given by Leto et al (2016).

Mesencephalon

The mesencephalon or midbrain is subdivided early in development into two neuromeres, mesencephalon 1 and mesencephalon 2. It persists for a time as a thin-walled tube enclosing a cavity of some size, separated from that of the prosencephalon by a slight constriction and from the rhombencephalon by the isthmus rhombencephali (see Fig. 14.4; Fig. 14.26). Later, its cavity becomes relatively reduced in diameter and, in the adult brain, it forms the cerebral aqueduct. The basal (ventrolateral) plate of the midbrain increases in thickness to form the cerebral peduncles, which are at first of small size but enlarge rapidly in the early second trimester, when their numerous fibre tracts begin to appear in the marginal zone. The neuroblasts of the basal plate of mesencephalon 2 give rise to the nuclei of the oculomotor nerve and some grey masses of the tegmentum, while the nucleus of the trochlear nerve remains in the region of the isthmus rhombencephali. The cells that give rise to the trigeminal mesencephalic nucleus arise either side of the dorsal midline, from the isthmus rhombencephali rostrally across the roof of the mesencephalon. The progenitors of these cells do not express neural crest cell markers.
The cells of the dorsal part of the alar (dorsolateral) plates proliferate and invade the roof plate, which therefore thickens and is later divided into corpora bigemina by a median groove. Caudally, this groove becomes a median ridge that persists in the adult as the frenulum veli, and the corpora bigemina are later subdivided into the superior and inferior colliculi by a transverse furrow. The red nucleus, substantia nigra and reticular nuclei of the midbrain tegmentum may first be defined at the end of the first trimester. Their origins are probably mixed, from neuroblasts of both basal and alar plates.
The detailed histogenesis of the tectum and its main derivatives, the colliculi, will not be followed here but, in general, the principles outlined for the cerebellar cortex, the paleopallium and neopallium also apply to this region. A high degree of geometric order exists in the developing retinotectal projection (the equivalent of the retinogeniculate projection) and in the tectospinal projection. (For further reading, see Cang and Feldheim (2013).)

Prosencephalon

At an early stage, a transverse section through the forebrain shows the same parts that are displayed in similar sections of the spinal cord and medulla oblongata, i.e. thick lateral walls connected by thin floor and roof plates. Each lateral wall is divided into a dorsal area and a ventral area separated internally by the hypothalamic sulcus (see Fig. 14.26). This sulcus ends rostrally at the medial end of the optic stalk. In the fully developed brain, it persists as a slight groove extending from the interventricular foramen to the cerebral aqueduct. It is analogous to, if not the homologue of, the sulcus limitans. The thin roof plate remains epithelial but invaginated by vascular mesenchyme, the tela choroidea of the choroid plexuses of the third ventricle. Later, the lateral margins of the tela undergo a similar invagination into the medial walls of the cerebral hemispheres. The floor plate thickens as the nuclear masses of the hypothalamus and subthalamus develop.
At a very early period, before the closure of the rostral neuropore, the subdivision of the prosencephalon into the most rostral telencephalon and two subdivisions of the diencephalon, D1 and D2, is heralded (see Fig. 14.2). At this early time, two eye fields are separated by the future neurohypophysis in the floor of the future D1. After head folding, the eye fields expand as two lateral optic evaginations, that will become optic vesicles, one on each side of the early brain. For a time, they communicate with the cavity of the prosencephalon by relatively wide openings. The distal parts of the optic vesicles expand, while the proximal parts become the tubular optic stalks. The optic vesicles (described with the development of the eye in Ch. 15) are thus derived from the lateral walls of the D1 subdivision of the prosencephalon before the telencephalon can be clearly identified. The optic chiasma is often regarded as the boundary between the diencephalon and telencephalon.
As the most rostral portion of the prosencephalon enlarges, it curves ventrally, and two further diverticula expand rapidly from it, one on each side. These diverticula, rostrolateral to the optic stalks, subsequently form the cerebral hemispheres. Their cavities are the rudiments of the lateral ventricles and they communicate with the median part of the forebrain cavity by relatively wide openings that ultimately become the interventricular foramina. The rostral limit of the median part of the forebrain consists of a thin sheet, the lamina terminalis (see Fig. 14.26), which stretches from the interventricular foramina to the recess at the base of the optic stalks. The rostral part of the forebrain, including the rudiments of the cerebral hemispheres, consists of the telencephalon and the caudal part of the diencephalon; both contribute to the formation of the third ventricle, although the latter predominates. The fate of the lamina terminalis is described below.

Diencephalon

The diencephalon, D2, is broadly divided by the hypothalamic sulcus into dorsal (pars dorsalis diencephali) and ventral (pars ventralis diencephali) parts; each contributes to diverse neural structures. The dorsal part develops into the (dorsal) thalamus and metathalamus along the immediate suprasulcal area of its lateral wall, while the highest dorsocaudal lateral wall and roof form the epithalamus. The thalamus is first visible as a thickening that involves the rostral part of the dorsal area (see Fig. 14.26). Caudal to the thalamus, the lateral and medial geniculate bodies, or metathalamus, are recognizable at first as surface depressions on the internal aspect and as elevations on the external aspect of the lateral wall. As the thalami enlarge as smooth ovoid masses, the wide interval between them gradually narrows into a vertically compressed cavity that forms the greater part of the third ventricle. After a time these medial surfaces may come into contact and become adherent over a variable area, the connection (single or multiple) constituting the interthalamic adhesion or massa intermedia. The caudal growth of the thalamus excludes the geniculate bodies from the lateral wall of the third ventricle.
At first the lateral aspect of the developing thalamus is separated from the medial aspect of the cerebral hemisphere by a cleft that becomes obliterated as the thalamus fuses with the part of the hemisphere in which the corpus striatum is developing (Fig. 14.27). The subsequent development of the corticofugal and corticopetal projection fibres of the neocortex means that the thalamus becomes related to the internal capsule, which intervenes between it and the lateral part of the corpus striatum (lentiform nucleus). Ventral to the hypothalamic sulcus the lateral wall of the diencephalon, in addition to median derivatives of its floor plate, forms a large part of the hypothalamus and subthalamus.
The epithalamus, which includes the pineal gland, the posterior and habenular commissures and the trigonum habenulae, develops in association with the caudal part of the roof plate and the adjoining regions of the lateral walls of the diencephalon. Between stages 17–20 the epithalamus in the lateral wall projects into the third ventricle as a smooth ellipsoid mass, larger than the adjacent mass of the (dorsal) thalamus and separated from it by a well-defined epithalamic sulcus. Subsequent growth of the thalamus rapidly overtakes that of the epithalamus and the intervening sulcus is obliterated: structures of epithalamic origin become relatively diminutive topographically.
The pineal gland arises as a hollow outgrowth from the roof plate, immediately adjoining the mesencephalon. Its distal part becomes solid by cellular proliferation but its proximal stalk remains hollow and contains the pineal recess of the third ventricle. In many reptiles the pineal outgrowth consists of a rostral outgrowth (parapineal organ) that develops into the pineal or parietal eye, and a glandular caudal outgrowth that is homologous with the human pineal gland. The rostral outgrowth also develops in the human embryo but soon disappears entirely.
The nucleus habenulae, the most important constituent of the trigonum habenulae, develops in the lateral wall of the diencephalon. It is initially a close relation of the geniculate bodies, from which it is later separated by the dorsal growth of the thalamus. The habenular commissure develops in the cranial wall of the pineal recess. The posterior commissure is formed by fibres that invade the caudal wall of the pineal recess from both sides.
The ventral part of the diencephalon forms the subsulcal lateral walls of the third ventricle and takes part in the formation of the hypothalamus, including the mammillary bodies, the tuber cinereum and infundibulum of the hypophysis. The mammillary bodies arise as a single thickening that becomes divided by a median furrow during the end of the first trimester. The tuber cinereum develops rostral to the mammillary bodies as a cellular proliferation that extends forwards as far as the infundibulum. A wide-mouthed diverticulum forms in the floor of the diencephalon in front of the tuber cinereum and grows towards the stomodeal roof, coming into contact with the posterior aspect of a dorsally directed ingrowth from the stomodeum (Rathke’s pouch). These two diverticula together form the hypophysis cerebri (see Fig. 14.10). An extension of the third ventricle persists in the base of the neural outgrowth as the infundibular recess. The remaining caudolateral walls and floor of the ventral diencephalon are an extension of the midbrain tegmentum, the subthalamus. This forms the rostral limits of the red nucleus, the substantia nigra, numerous reticular nuclei and a mass of interweaving, ascending, descending and oblique nerve fibre bundles.
Third ventricle and choroid plexus
The roof plate of the diencephalon rostral to the pineal gland continues over the median telencephalon, remaining thin and epithelial in character and is invaginated by the choroid plexuses of the third ventricle (see Fig. 14.27; Fig. 14.28). Before the development of the corpus callosum and the fornix, it lies at the bottom of the longitudinal fissure between and reaching the two cerebral hemispheres. It extends as far rostrally as the interventricular foramina and lamina terminalis. Here, and elsewhere, choroid plexuses develop by the close apposition of vascular pia mater and ependyma without intervening nervous tissue. With development, the vascular layer is infolded into the ventricular cavity and develops a series of small villous projections, each covered by a cuboidal epithelium derived from the ependyma. The cuboidal cells display numerous microvilli on their ventricular surfaces and complex folding of their basal plasma membranes. The early choroid plexuses secrete a protein-rich cerebrospinal fluid into the ventricular system, which may provide a nutritive medium for the developing epithelial neural tissues. As the latter become increasingly vascularized, the histochemical reactions of the cuboidal cells and the character of the fluid change to the adult type. Many regions of the lining of the third ventricle become highly specialized, and develop concentrations of tanycytes or other modified cells that are collectively termed the circumventricular organs, e.g. the subfornical organ, the organum vasculosum (intercolumnar tubercle) of the lamina terminalis, the subcommissural organ, and the linings of the pineal, suprapineal and infundibular recesses.

Telencephalon

The telencephalon consists of two lateral diverticula connected by a median region, the telencephalon impar. The rostral part of the third ventricle develops from the impar and is closed below and in front by the lamina terminalis. The lateral diverticula are outpouchings of the lateral walls of the telencephalon that may correspond to the alar lamina, although this is uncertain. Their cavities are the future lateral ventricles and their walls are formed by the presumptive nervous tissue of the cerebral hemispheres. The roof plate of the median part of the telencephalon remains thin and is continuous behind with the roof plate of the diencephalon (see Figs 14.26–14.27). The rostral parts of the hypothalamus, which include the optic chiasma, optic recess and related nuclei, develop in the floor plate and lateral walls of the prosencephalon, ventral to the primitive interventricular foramina. The chiasma is formed by the meeting, and partial decussation, of the optic nerves in the ventral part of the lamina terminalis. The optic tracts subsequently grow backwards from the chiasma to end in the diencephalon and midbrain.

Cerebral hemispheres

The cerebral hemispheres arise as diverticula of the lateral walls of the telencephalon, with which they remain in continuity around the margins of initially relatively large interventricular foramina, except caudally, where they are continuous with the rostral part of the lateral wall of the diencephalon (see Figs 14.4, 14.26). As growth proceeds each hemisphere enlarges forwards, upwards and backwards and acquires an oval outline, medial and superolateral walls, and a floor. As a result the medial surfaces approach, but are separated by, a vascularized mesenchyme and pia mater that fills the median longitudinal fissure (see Fig. 14.28). At this stage the floor of the fissure is the epithelial roof plate of the telencephalon, which is directly continuous caudally with the epithelial roof plate of the diencephalon.
At the early oval stage of hemispheric development, regions are named according to their future principal derivatives. The rostromedial and ventral floor becomes linked with the forming olfactory apparatus and is termed the primitive olfactory lobe. The floor (ventral wall, or base) of the remainder of each hemisphere forms the anlage of the primitive corpus striatum and amygdaloid complex, including its associated rim of lateral and medial walls (the striate part of the hemisphere). The rest of the hemisphere, i.e. the medial, lateral, dorsal and caudal regions, is the suprastriate part of the hemisphere. Although it is the largest in terms of surface area, initially it possesses comparatively thin walls. The rostral end of each oval hemisphere becomes the definitive frontal pole. As the hemisphere expands its original posterior pole moves relatively in a caudoventral and lateral direction, following a curve like a ram’s horn. It curves towards the orbit in association with the growth of the caudate nucleus and other structures to form the definitive temporal pole. A new posterior part persists as the definitive occipital pole of the mature brain (Figs 14.29-14.30).
The migration and differentiation of neural progenitors to form nuclei is either minimal or limited throughout the brainstem and spinal cord. The progeny of these progenitors remain immediately extra-ependymal or partially displaced towards the pial exterior, and are arrested deeply embedded in the myelinating ‘white matter’ of the region. In marked contrast, proliferation and migration of neuroblasts in the cerebral hemispheres produce a superficial layer of grey matter in both the striate and the suprastriate regions, but not in the central areas of the original medial wall, where secondary fusion with the diencephalon occurs. The superficial layer of grey matter consists of neuronal somata, dendrites, the terminations of incoming (afferent) axons, extending outgrowing (efferent) axons, and glial and endothelial cells. Successive generations of neuroblasts migrate through the layers of earlier generations to attain subpial positions (see below and Fig. 14.33), which means that the outer, pial surface of the cerebral hemispheres expands at a rate greater than that of the hemispheres as a whole. The subpial region of grey matter is termed the cortex or pallium; a transient cortical subplate is seen. The growing hemispheres subsequently overlap, successively, the diencephalon and the mesencephalon, and then meet the rostral surface of the cerebellum. The temporal lobes embrace the flanks of the brainstem.
The terminology used to describe regions of the cortex is based on evolutionary concepts. The oldest portions of cortex receive information concerned with olfaction and are termed archicortex (archipallium) and paleocortex (paleopallium): both are subdivisions of an overall allocortex. The archicortex is the forerunner of the hippocampal lobe, and the paleocortex gives rise to the piriform area. The remaining cortical surface expands greatly in mammals, forming the neocortex that displaces the earlier cortices so that they come to lie partially internally in each hemisphere. The typically six-layered mammalian neocortex is also described as isocortex.
Lateral ventricles and choroid plexus
The early diverticulum or anlage of the cerebral hemisphere initially contains a simple spheroidal lateral ventricle continuous with the third ventricle via the interventricular foramen. The rim of the foramen is the site of the original evagination. The expanding ventricle develops the ram’s horn shape of the surrounding hemisphere, becoming first roughly ellipsoid and then a curved cylinder that is convex dorsally (see Fig. 14.29). The ends of the cylinder expand towards, but do not reach, the frontal and (temporary) occipital poles; differentiating and thickening neural tissues separate the ventricular cavities and pial surfaces at all points, except along the line of the choroidal fissure. Pronounced changes in ventricular form accompany the emergence of a temporal pole. The original caudal end of the curved cylinder expands within its substance and the temporal extensions in each hemisphere pass ventrolaterally to encircle both sides of the upper brainstem (see Fig. 14.27). Another extension may develop from the root of the temporal extension in the substance of the definitive occipital pole and pass caudomedially; it is quite variable in size, often asymmetric on the two sides, and one or both may be absent. Although the lateral ventricle is a continuous system of cavities, specific parts are now given regional names. The central part (body) extends from the interventricular foramen to the level of the posterior edge (splenium) of the corpus callosum. Three cornua (horns) diverge from the body: anterior towards the frontal pole, posterior towards the occipital pole, and inferior towards the temporal pole. At these early stages of hemispheric development, in most instances the term pole is preferred to lobe: lobes are defined by specific surface topographical features that will appear over several postmenstrual weeks, and differential growth patterns persist for a considerable period.
The pia mater covers the epithelial roof of the third ventricle at this stage and is itself covered with loosely arranged mesenchyme and developing blood vessels. These vessels subsequently invaginate the roof of the third ventricle on each side of the median plane to form its choroid plexuses (see Fig. 14.27). The lower part of the medial wall of the cerebral hemisphere, which immediately adjoins the epithelial roof of the interventricular foramen and the rostral extremity of the diencephalon, also remains epithelial and consists of ependyma and pia mater. Elsewhere, the walls of the hemispheres are thickening to form the pallium. The thin part of the medial wall of the hemisphere is invaginated by vascular tissue that is continuous in front with the choroid plexus of the third ventricle and constitutes the choroid plexus of the lateral ventricle. This invagination occurs along a line that arches upwards and backwards, parallel with and initially limited to, the rostral and upper boundaries of the interventricular foramen. This curved indentation of the ventricular wall, where no nervous tissue develops between ependyma and pia mater, is termed the choroidal fissure (see Figs 14.26C, 14.27). The subsequent assumption of the definitive form of the choroidal fissure depends on related growth patterns in neighbouring structures. Of particular importance are the relatively slow growth of the interventricular foramen; the secondary ‘fusion’ between the lateral diencephalon and medial hemisphere walls; the encompassing of the upper brainstem by the forward growth of the temporal lobe and its pole towards the apex of the orbit; and the massive expansion of two great cerebral commissures (the fornix and corpus callosum). The choroidal fissure is now clearly a caudal extension of the much-reduced interventricular foramen, which arches above the thalamus and is here only a few millimetres from the median plane. It diverges ventrolaterally near the caudal end of the thalamus, its curve reaching and continuing in the medial wall of the temporal lobe over much of its length (i.e. to the tip of the inferior horn of the lateral ventricle). The upper part of the arch will be overhung by the corpus callosum and it is bordered by the fornix and its derivatives throughout its convexity.
Basal nuclei
At first, growth proceeds more actively in the floor and the adjoining part of the lateral wall of the developing hemisphere, and thickenings of the ventricular zone formed by the rudimentary corpus striatum, often termed medial and lateral ganglionic (ventricular) eminences, develop from stage 18 (see Figs 14.26–14.29). The head of the caudate nucleus appears as three successive parts, medial, lateral and intermediate. Caudally, these merge to form the tail of the caudate nucleus and the amygdaloid complex, which both remain close to the temporal pole of the hemisphere. When the occipital pole grows backwards and the general enlargement of the hemisphere carries the temporal pole downwards and forwards, the tail of the caudate is continued from the floor of the central part (body) of the lateral ventricle into the roof of its temporal extension, the future inferior horn (see Fig. 14.29). The amygdaloid complex encapsulates its tip. Rostrally, the head of the caudate nucleus extends forwards to the floor of the interventricular foramen, where it is separated from the developing rostral end of the thalamus by a groove; the head subsequently expands in the floor of the anterior horn of the lateral ventricle. The lentiform nucleus develops from two cellular laminae, medial and lateral, that are continuous with both the medial and lateral parts of the caudate nucleus. The internal capsule appears first in the medial lamina and extends laterally through the lateral lamina to the cortex. It divides the laminae into two, the internal parts join the caudate nucleus and the external parts form the lentiform nucleus (see Figs 14.27–14.28): the remaining medial lamina cells give rise mainly to the globus pallidus and the lateral lamina cells to the putamen. The putamen expands concurrently with the intermediate part of the caudate nucleus.
Fusion of diencephalic and telencephalic walls
As the hemisphere enlarges, the caudal part of its medial surface overlaps and hides the lateral surface of the diencephalon (thalamic part), from which it is separated by a narrow cleft occupied by vascular connective tissue. A transverse section made caudal to the interventricular foramen would pass from the third ventricular cavity successively through the developing thalamus, the narrow cleft just mentioned, the thin medial wall of the hemisphere, and the cavity of the lateral ventricle, with the corpus striatum in its floor and lateral wall (see Fig. 14.27).
As the thalamus increases in extent it acquires a superior surface in addition to medial and lateral surfaces. The lateral part of its superior surface fuses with the thin medial wall of the hemisphere so that this part of the thalamus is finally covered with the ependyma of the lateral ventricle immediately ventral to the choroidal fissure. As a result the corpus striatum is approximated to the thalamus and is separated from it only by a deep groove that becomes obliterated by increased growth along the line of contact. The lateral aspect of the thalamus is now in continuity with the medial aspect of the corpus striatum so that a secondary union between the diencephalon and the telencephalon is effected over a wide area, providing a route for the subsequent passage of projection fibres to and from the cortex (see Fig. 14.27).
Formation of the insula
At the end of the first trimester, while the corpus striatum is developing, growth between the frontal and temporal lobes is relatively restricted. The region lateral to the striatum becomes depressed to form a lateral cerebral fossa with a portion of cortex, the insula, at its base (see Figs 14.29–14.30). As the temporal lobe continues to protrude towards the orbit, and with more rapid growth of the temporal and frontal cortices, the surface of the hemisphere expands at a rate greater than that of the hemisphere as a whole, and the cortical areas become folded, forming gyri and sulci. The insula is gradually overgrown by adjacent cortical regions that overlap it to form the opercula: the free margins of the opercula form the anterior part of the lateral fissure. This process is not completed until after birth. The lentiform nucleus remains deep to and coextensive with the insula.
Olfactory bulb
A longitudinal groove appears in the anteromedial part of the floor of each developing lateral ventricle at about the stage 17. It deepens and forms a hollow diverticulum that is continuous with the hemisphere by a short stalk. The diverticulum becomes connected on its ventral or inferior surface to the olfactory placode. Placodal cells give rise to afferent axons that terminate in the walls of the diverticulum. As the head increases in size, the diverticulum grows forwards, loses its cavity and becomes converted into the solid olfactory bulb. The forward growth of the bulb is accompanied by elongation of its stalk to form the olfactory tract. The part of the floor of the hemisphere to which the tract is attached constitutes the piriform area.
Olfactory nerve, limbic lobe and hippocampus
The growth changes in the temporal lobe that help to submerge the insula produce important changes in the olfactory and neighbouring limbic areas. The olfactory tract diverges into lateral, medial and (variable) intermediate striae as it approaches the hemispheric floor. The medial stria is clothed with a thin archicortical medial olfactory gyrus. This curves up into further archicortical areas rostral to the lamina terminalis (paraterminal gyrus, prehippocampal rudiment, parolfactory gyrus, septal nuclei) and these continue into the indusium griseum. The lateral stria, clothed by the lateral olfactory gyrus, and the intermediate stria (when present) terminate in the rostral parts of the piriform area, including the olfactory trigone and tubercle, anterior perforated substance, uncus and entorhinal area of the anterior part of the future parahippocampal gyrus. The lateral limit of the lateral stria is indicated by the rhinal sulcus. The forward growth of the temporal pole and the general expansion of the neocortex cause the lateral olfactory gyrus to bend laterally, the summit of the convexity lying at the anteroinferior corner of the developing insula. During the second trimester, much of the piriform area becomes submerged by the adjoining neocortex and, in the adult, only a part of it remains visible on the inferior aspect of the cerebrum.
The limbic lobe is the first part of the cortex to differentiate. At first it forms a continuous, almost circular, strip on the medial and inferior aspects of the hemisphere. Below and in front, where the stalk of the olfactory tract is attached, it constitutes a part of the piriform area. The portion outside the curve of the choroidal fissure constitutes the hippocampal formation (see Fig. 14.27; Fig. 14.31). In this region, the neural progenitors of the developing cortex proliferate and migrate. The wall of the hemisphere thickens, producing an elevation that projects into the medial side of the ventricle. The elevation is the hippocampus; it appears first on the medial wall of the hemisphere, in the area above and in front of the lamina terminalis (paraterminal area), and gradually extends backwards, curving into the region of the temporal pole, where it adjoins the piriform area. The marginal zone in the neighbourhood of the hippocampus is invaded by neurones to form the dentate gyrus. The hippocampus and the dentate gyrus extend from the paraterminal area backwards above the choroidal fissure and follow its curve downwards and forwards towards the temporal pole, where they continue into the piriform area. A shallow groove, the hippocampal sulcus, crosses the medial surface of the hemisphere throughout the hippocampal formation. The efferent fibres from the cells of the hippocampus collect along its medial edge and run forwards immediately above the choroidal fissure. Rostrally, they turn ventrally and enter the lateral part of the lamina terminalis to gain the hypothalamus, where they end in and around the mammillary body and neighbouring nuclei. These efferent hippocampal fibres form the fimbria hippocampi and the fornix.
Projection fibres, internal capsule
The growth of the neocortex, and its enormous expansion during the latter part of the first trimester, is associated with the initial appearance of corticofugal and corticopetal projection fibres and the internal capsule. The fibres follow the route provided by the apposition of the lateral aspect of the thalamus with the medial aspect of the corpus striatum. They divide the latter, almost completely, into a lateral part, the lentiform nucleus, and a medial part, the caudate nucleus; these two nuclei remain confluent only in their anteroinferior regions (see Figs 14.27–14.28). The corticospinal tracts are first identified at the end of stage 23 at the pyramidal decussation. The lateral corticospinal tracts are in the cervical cord by postmenstrual weeks 14–15 and in the anterior corticospinal tract from about postmenstrual week 16 (Jin et al 2016). The fibres destined for the cervical and upper thoracic regions, which innervate the upper limbs, are in advance of those that innervate the lower limbs, and these, in turn, are in advance of fibres that innervate the face; the timing of the appearance of reflexes associated with these three parts of the body is similarly staggered. The proportions of crossed and uncrossed fibres in each corticospinal tract vary; a dominant uncrossed corticospinal tract and a complete absence of pyramidal decussation have been found in posterior fossa anomalies such as occipital encephalocele, Dandy–Walker malformation and Joubert’s syndrome. There is evidence of substantial plastic reorganization of the motor cortex and corticospinal projections following pre- or perinatal lesions to the corticospinal system (Eyre 2007). For further reading about the development of the human pyramidal tract, see ten Donkelaar et al (2004).
The majority of subcortical nuclear masses receive terminals from descending fibres of cortical origin and thalamocortical, hypothalamocortical and other afferent ascending bundles. The internal capsular fibres pass lateral to the head and body of the caudate nucleus, the anterior cornu and central part of the lateral ventricle, the rostroventral extensions and body of the fornix, the dorsal thalamus and dorsal choroidal fissure, and medial to the lentiform nucleus (see Figs 14.27–14.28).
Formation of gyri and sulci
Apart from the shallow hippocampal sulcus and the lateral cerebral fossa, the surfaces of the hemisphere remain relatively smooth and uninterrupted until postmenstrual week 21 (see Fig. 14.30). The parieto-occipital sulcus appears at about that time on the medial aspect of the hemisphere. Its appearance seems associated with an increase in the number of splenial fibres in the corpus callosum. Over the same period, the posterior part of the calcarine sulcus appears as a shallow groove extending forwards from a region near the occipital pole. It is a true infolding of the cortex in the long axis of the striate area and produces an elevation, the calcar avis, on the medial wall of the posterior horn of the ventricle.
The cingulate sulcus appears on the medial aspect of the hemisphere about postmenstrual weeks 18–20, and sulci appear on the inferior and superolateral aspects after postmenstrual week 21. The central, precentral and postcentral sulci appear, each in upper and lower parts that usually coalesce shortly afterwards, although they may remain discontinuous. The superior and inferior frontal, the intraparietal, occipital, superior and inferior temporal, occipitotemporal, collateral and rhinal sulci all make their appearance during the same period. Secondary and tertiary gyri form after neurones attain their final areal and laminar positions (Silver et al 2019). All the important sulci can be recognized by postmenstrual week 32 (see Fig. 14.30). Preterm infants developing ex utero have less gyrification and shallower sulci by their equivalent postmenstrual week 40 than neonates born at full-term (postmenstrual week 40) (Engelhardt et al 2016a, Shimony et al 2016). Mathematical modelling of gyral patterns has identified distinct folding patterns and sex differences in the superior temporal, inferior frontal and cingulate gyri in healthy singleton, twin, preterm and full-term neonates (Duan et al 2019).
Development of commissures
The development of the commissures causes a very profound alteration of the medial wall of the hemisphere. At the time of their appearance, the two hemispheres are connected to each other by the median part of the telencephalon. The roof plate of this area remains epithelial, while its floor becomes invaded by the decussating fibres of the optic nerves and developing hypothalamic nuclei. These two routes are thus not available for the passage of commissural fibres passing from hemisphere to hemisphere across the median plane, and these fibres therefore pass through the rostral wall of the interventricular foramen, i.e. the lamina terminalis. The first commissures to develop are those associated with the paleocortex and archicortex. Fibres of the olfactory tracts cross in the ventral or lower part of the lamina terminalis and, together with fibres from the piriform and pre-piriform areas and the amygdaloid bodies, form the rostral part of the anterior commissure (see Fig. 14.31; Fig. 14.32). The two hippocampi become interconnected by transverse fibres that cross from fornix to fornix in the upper part of the lamina terminalis as the commissure of the fornix (hippocampal commissure). Various other decussating fibre bundles (known as the supraoptic commissures, although they are not true commissures) develop in the lamina terminalis immediately dorsal to the optic chiasma, between it and the anterior commissure.
The commissures of the neocortex develop later and follow the pathways already established by the commissures of the limbic system. Fibres from the tentorial surface of the hemisphere join the anterior commissure and constitute its larger posterior part. All the other commissural fibres of the neocortex associate themselves closely with the commissure of the fornix and lie on its dorsal surface. These fibres increase enormously in number and the bundle rapidly outgrows its neighbours to form the corpus callosum (see Figs 14.31–14.32).
The corpus callosum originates as a thick mass connecting the two cerebral hemispheres around and above the anterior commissure. The upper end of this neocortical commissural area extends backwards to form the trunk of the corpus callosum. The rostrum of the corpus callosum develops later and separates some of the rostral end of the limbic area from the remainder of the cerebral hemisphere. Further backward growth of the trunk of the corpus callosum then results in the entrapped part of the limbic area becoming stretched out to form the bilateral septum pellucidum. As the corpus callosum grows backwards, it extends above the choroidal fissure, carrying the commissure of the fornix on its undersurface. In this way, a new floor is formed for the longitudinal fissure, and additional structures come to lie above the epithelial roof of the third ventricle. In its backward growth, the corpus callosum invades the area hitherto occupied by the upper part of the archicortical hippocampal formation, and the corresponding parts of the dentate gyrus and hippocampus are reduced to vestiges, the indusium griseum and the longitudinal striae. The posteroinferior (temporal) archicortical regions of both dentate gyrus and hippocampus persist and enlarge.
Cellular development of the cerebral cortex
Cortical neurones are not generated within the cortex itself but in proliferative transient embryonic zones such as the ventricular (VZ) and subventricular (SVZ) zones (see Fig. 14.33). Precise patterns of cell division and the subsequent migration of the progeny of these divisions along the shafts of a transient population of radial glial cells transform the neuroepithelium of the embryonic forebrain into the adult cerebral cortex (Noctor et al 2004). Neurones are derived from a lineage of radial glia stem cells and transit amplifying intermediate progenitor cells; expansion in one or both cell populations has been proposed as a potential mechanism for neocortical expansion (Kriegstein and Gotz 2003, Lui et al 2011). The earliest-generated cortical neurones accumulate in an outside-in sequence to form the preplate. Subsequent generations of neurones migrate into the preplate, forming a series of layers called the cortical plate that splits the preplate into a superficial layer at the pial surface, the marginal zone, and a deeper layer, the subplate. Subplate neurones integrate into the intra- and extracortical circuitry and extend axons via the internal capsule towards the thalamus and superior colliculus at times before other cortical neurones have been born (Kanold and Luhmann 2010). In humans, the subplate is maximal at approximately postmenstrual week 25 and recedes after birth by postnatal month 6 (Kostovic et al 2012). The diverse cellular components of the subplate are embedded in an abundant hydrophilic extracellular matrix rich in secreted polymeric glycosaminoglycans, fibronectin and chondroitin sulphate proteoglycans that, for example, provide a major corridor for migrating GABAergic neurones (Hoerder-Suabedissen and Molnar 2015). Subplate neurones are vulnerable to injury during prenatal stages; their premature loss has been implicated in the pathogenesis of chronic deficits such as cognitive delay, behavioural problems and epilepsy associated with preterm birth (Jantzie et al 2015). They have also been implicated in the pathogenesis of cognitive developmental disorders such as autism and schizophrenia (Silver et al 2019, Molnar et al 2019).
Neurones migrate along radial glial processes (see Fig. 14.12). The migration of neuronal precursors from the ventricular and intermediate zones occurs radially towards the pial surface (Kriegstein and Noctor 2004). From the pial surface inwards, the following zones may be defined: marginal, cortical plate, subplate, intermediate, subventricular and ventricular (Fig. 14.33). (For a review of the nomenclature of the developing zones, see Bystron et al (2008).) The marginal zone, the outermost layer of the developing cerebral cortex, will form layer 1. The neurones of the cortical plate and subplate form the neurones of the remaining cortical laminae (2–6), the complexity varies in different locations and with further additions of neurones from the deeper zones. The intermediate zone gradually transforms into the white matter of the hemisphere. Meanwhile, other deep progenitor cells produce generations of glioblasts that also migrate into the more superficial layers. As proliferation wanes and finally ceases in the ventricular and subventricular zones, their remaining cells differentiate into general or specialized ependymal cells, tanycytes or subependymal glial cells.
The timing of proliferation of different cortical neurones varies according to their laminar destination and cell type. The first groups of cells to migrate are destined for the deep cortical laminae and later groups pass through them to more superficial regions. The subplate zone exists until postmenstrual week 42 and is most prominent during mid-gestation: it contains neurones surrounded by a dense neuropil and is the site of the most intense synaptogenesis in the embryonic cortex (Molnar et al 2019). The cumulative effect of this radial and tangential growth is evident in a marked expansion of the surface area of the cortex without a comparable increase in its thickness (Rakic 1988, 2009).
The phylogenetically oldest regions in the pallial walls of the mammalian cerebral hemisphere are the first to differentiate during ontogeny: they border the interventricular foramen and its extension, the choroidal fissure, the lamina terminalis and the piriform lobe. The pallidum contributes interneurones to the formation of the cerebral cortex. A significant portion of GABAergic inhibitory (non-pyramidal) cells are generated not in the cortical ventricular zone, but along the inner curving border of the developing lateral ventricle, in the ganglionic (ventricular) eminences that will later form the lentiform and caudate nuclei (see Figs 14.27–14.28). The ganglionic eminences have three anatomical subdivisions, medial, lateral and caudal, distinguished by the molecular markers and cell types they produce (Fig. 14.34). Cortical interneurones are generated mainly in the caudal and medial parts of the ganglionic eminence and the preoptic area and migrate tangentially through the future striatocortical junction and the subplate to reach the cortex, governed by molecular cues including Semaphorin3a/f, CXCL12, Neuregulin1, Robo, and Ephrin (Métin et al 2006, Wonders and Anderson 2006, Ma et al 2012, Hansen et al 2013, Molnar and Butt 2013). Experimental evidence suggests that the developing cortical areal map is patterned in the same way as the rest of the body, by diffusible proteins (members of the FGF, Wnt, BMP and SHH families) that are released from signalling centres: in the cortex, FGF8 influences the rostrocaudal axis, and Wnts and BMP influence the dorsoventral axis. The extent to which these findings can be extrapolated from experimental animals, especially mice, to the developing human cortex remains speculative; there are indications that some mechanisms may be conserved (Silver et al 2019).
Although the biological significance of the inside-out gradient of neurogenesis is unclear, it is known that if the gradient is disturbed, by genetic or epigenetic factors, neurones display abnormal cortical function. Failure or delay in neuronal migration has been associated with a wide range of disorders, such as lissencephaly and schizophrenia (Wu et al 2014).
After the initial generation of neuroblasts the ventricular and subventricular zones give rise to glioblasts that migrate to the cortical subplate. Oligodendrocyte progenitor cells are prevalent initially in the subventricular zone and after postmenstrual weeks 19–22 in the subplate zone (Jakovcevski et al 2009). The cues that initiate myelination in some axons and not others are still unclear: regional differences in oligodendrocyte-lineage cells, which probably emerge after specification of oligodendrocyte precursors, may explain some differences in myelination between CNS regions (Osso and Chan 2017, Saab and Nave 2017). In human cerebral white matter, the oligodendrocyte lineage appears to involve three successive stages (pre-oligodendrocyte, immature oligodendrocyte and mature oligodendrocyte) from about postmenstrual week 22 (Back et al 2001). Ventrodorsal gradients of oligodendrocyte precursor cell density and of myelination have been described in the telencephalon.
The sequence of myelination of the motor pathways may explain, at least partially, the order of development of muscle tone and posture in the premature infant and full-term neonate. Myelination of the various subcorticospinal pathways (vestibulospinal, reticulospinal, olivospinal and tectospinal) starts at postmenstrualweeks 24–30 for the medial tracts, and at postmenstrual weeks 28–34 for the lateral tracts (Dubois et al 2014) (Fig. 14.35).
The vast species differences between invertebrate, murine, non-human primate and primate nervous systems pose particular, sometimes intractable, problems for neuroscientists exploring the evolution of the human cerebral cortex. Current understanding of the pre- and postnatal events that regulate the numbers of neurones and their associated glia; the dynamics of neuronal migration to proper layers, columns and regions; the enormous expansion of the cortical surface; and the development of connectivity, particularly networks mediating higher cognitive functions and language, remains in its infancy for the human cortex. (For further reading on the evolution of the neocortex, see Rakic (2009), Lui et al (2011), Geschwind and Rakic (2013), Lister et al (2013), Silver et al (2019).)
The Allen Human Brain Atlas (http://human.brain-map.org/) uses DNA microarrays for transcriptional profiling of several hundred locations spanning the adult human neocortex (Hawrylycz et al 2012), while the NIH Blueprint Non-Human Primate Atlas profiles individual cortical layers in the Rhesus monkey (http://www.blueprintnhpatlas.org/). Similarly, the BrainSpan Atlas of the Developing Human Brain (http://www.brainspan.org/) is a resource for studying the transcriptome across human brain development, using RNA sequencing on discrete cortical regions across an entire lifespan, and comparative DNA microarrays on laser-microdissected individual layers of different regions of the developing neocortex.
Culture of hPSCs (ESC and iPSCs) into 3D organoids has provided new ways to study cortical development: cells recapitulate temporal and spatial migration. Optimized culture methods have produced outer radial glial progenitor cells; self-organized cortical tissues show early-born neurones migrating to deep cortical plate layers and later-born cells migrating to the upper layers. Cerebral organoids showing discrete brain regions and tissues, including the hippocampus, midbrain/hindbrain boundary and choroid plexus, have been used to study neurodevelopmental disorders and neurodegenerative diseases (Lee et al 2017, Amin and Paşca 2018, Wang 2018). Spontaneous cellular network formation that exhibits periodic and regular oscillatory events dependent on glutamatergic and GABAergic signalling has been reported in long-term culture of cerebral organoids (Trujillo et al 2019).

Vascular Supply

Cerebral arteries

The cranial extensions of the dorsal aortae that supply the head receive blood entirely through the third aortic arches. The internal carotid artery is formed progressively from the third arch artery (which contributes its proximal part) (see Fig 17.9) and a further forward continuation that differentiates, at the time of regression of the first and second aortic arches, from the capillary plexus extending to the walls of the forebrain and midbrain. At its anterior extremity, this primitive internal carotid artery divides into cranial and caudal divisions. The former terminates as the primitive olfactory artery and supplies the developing regions implied (see Fig. 17.8). The latter sweeps caudally to reach the ventral aspect of the midbrain; its terminal branches are the primitive mesencephalic arteries. Simultaneously, bilateral longitudinal channels differentiate along the ventral surface of the rhombencephalon from a plexus fed by intersegmental and transitory presegmental branches of the dorsal aorta and its forward continuation. The most important of the presegmental branches is closely related to the trigeminal nerve, the primitive trigeminal artery. Otic and hypoglossal presegmental arteries occur and may persist. The longitudinal channels later connect cranially with the caudal divisions of the internal carotid arteries (each of which gives rise to an anterior choroidal artery supplying branches to the diencephalon, including the telae choroideae and midbrain), and caudally with the vertebral arteries through the first cervical intersegmental arteries. Fusion of the longitudinal channels results in the formation of the basilar artery. The caudal division of the internal carotid artery becomes the posterior communicating artery and the stem of the posterior cerebral artery. The remainder of the posterior cerebral artery develops comparatively late, probably from the stem of the posterior choroidal artery, which is annexed by the caudally expanding cerebral hemisphere, its distal portion becoming a choroidal branch of the posterior cerebral artery. The posterior choroidal artery supplies the tela choroidea at the future temporal end of the choroidal fissure; its rami advance through the tela to become confluent with branches of the anterior choroidal artery. The cranial division of the internal carotid artery gives rise to anterior choroidal, middle cerebral and anterior cerebral arteries. The stem of the primitive olfactory artery remains as a small medial striate branch of the anterior cerebral artery. The cerebellar arteries, of which the superior is the first to differentiate, emerge from the capillary plexus on the wall of the rhombencephalon.
The source of the blood supply to the territory of the trigeminal nerve varies at different stages in development. When the first and second aortic arch arteries begin to regress, the supply to the corresponding arches is derived from a transient ventral pharyngeal artery, which grows from the aortic sac and terminates by dividing into mandibular and maxillary branches.

Leptomeningeal arteries

At stages 20–23 the circle of Willis is completed. The anterior communicating arteries develop by stage 23. An anular network of leptomeningeal arteries, originating mainly from each middle cerebral artery, passes over each developing cerebral hemisphere. Caudally, similar meningeal branches arise from the vertebral and basilar arteries and embrace the cerebellum and brainstem. The further development of the telencephalon somewhat obscures this early pattern over the cerebrum.
The meningeal arteries so formed have been classified into three groups: paramedian, short circumferential and long circumferential arteries. They can be described both supratentorially and infratentorially, and all give off fine side branches and end as penetrating arteries. Of the supratentorial vessels, the paramedian arteries have a short course prior to penetrating the cerebral neuropil (e.g. branches of the anterior cerebral artery); the short circumferential arteries have a slightly longer course before becoming penetrating arteries (e.g. the striate artery); and the long circumferential arteries reach the dorsal surface of the hemispheres. Infratentorial meningeal arteries are very variable. The paramedian arteries, after arising from the basilar or vertebral arteries, penetrate the brainstem directly. The short circumferential arteries end at the lateral surface of the brain before penetration and the long circumferential arteries later form the range of cerebellar arteries. These vessels, arranged as a series of loops over the brain, arise from the circle of Willis and brainstem vessels on the base of the brain.
By postmenstrual week 16 the anterior, middle and posterior cerebral arteries that contribute to the formation of the circle of Willis are well established. The leptomeningeal arteries arising from them display a simple pattern with little tortuosity and very few branches. With the increasing age of the fetus and acquisition of the gyral pattern on the surface of the brain, their tortuosity, diameter and number of branches all increase. The branching pattern is completed by postmenstrual week 28 and the number of branches does not increase further. Numerous anastomoses (varying in size from 200 to 760 μm) occur between the arteries in the depths of the developing sulci, nearly always in the cortical boundary zones of the three main cerebral arteries supplying each hemisphere. The number, diameter and location of these anastomoses change as fetal growth progresses, reflecting the regression and simplification of the complex embryonic cerebral vascular system.
Vascularization of the brain
The brain becomes vascularized by angiogenesis rather than by direct invasion by angioblasts. The first indication of pia mater, containing a plexus of blood vessels that forms on the neural surface, is seen at stage 11 around the caudalmost part of the rhombencephalon and extends to the mesencephalic level by stage 12. The pial basement membrane is formed from interaction between neural crest cells, the meningeal extracellular matrix and the basal processes (end feet) of the radial glial cells. Meningeal extracellular matrix-mediated activation of integrins promotes proliferation of basal radial glia progenitors, which in turn sustain the expression of the extracellular matrix genes (Silver et al 2019). Vessels in the pial plexus sprout and form branches that elongate at the junction between the ventricular and marginal zones; the branches project laterally within the inter-rhombomeric boundaries and longitudinally adjacent to the median floorplate. Additional sprouts later penetrate the inter-rhombomeric regions on the walls and floor of the hindbrain. Branches from the hindbrain elongate towards and join the branches in the inter-rhombomeric junctions, forming primary vascular channels between rhombomeres and longitudinally on each side of the median floorplate. Later additional sprouts invade the hindbrain within the rhombomeres, anastomosing in all directions.
Leptomeningeal perforating branches pass into the brain parenchyma as cortical, medullary and striate branches (Fig. 14.36). The cortical vessels supply the cortex via short branches that may form precapillary anastomoses, whereas the medullary branches supply the white matter. The medullary branches converge towards the ventricle but rarely reach it and often follow a tortuous course as they pass around bundles of nerves. The striate branches penetrate the brain through the anterior perforated substance and supply the basal nuclei and internal capsule via a sinuous course; they are larger than the medullary branches and the longest reach close to the ventricle. The periventricular region and basal nuclei are also supplied by branches from the tela choroidea, which develops from the early pial plexus, becoming medially and more deeply placed as the telencephalon enlarges.
The cortical and medullary branches irrigate a series of corticosubcortical cone-shaped areas, each centred around a sulcus containing an artery. They supply a peripheral portion of the cerebrum and are grouped as ventriculopetal arteries. In contrast, striate branches arborize close to the ventricle and supply a more central portion of the cerebrum. Together with branches from the tela choroidea, they give rise to ventriculofugal arteries that supply the ventricular zone (germinal matrix of the brain) and also send branches towards the cortex. The ventriculopetal and ventriculofugal arteries run towards each other but do not anastomose. The ventriculopetal arteries form networks of small arterioles (see Fig. 14.36) and supply relatively more mature regions of the brain compared to the ventriculofugal vessels. The latter are subject to constant remodelling and do not develop tunicae mediae until ventricular zone proliferation is completed. The same pattern of centripetal and centrifugal arteries develops around the fourth ventricle. The ventriculofugal circulation is more extensive in the cerebellum than in the telencephalon. The arteries arise from the various cerebellar arteries and course with the cerebellar peduncles directly to the centre of the cerebellum, bypassing the cortex. The ventriculopetal arteries are derived from the meningeal vessels over the cerebellar surface and most terminate in the white matter.
At postmenstrual week 24 the blood supply to the basal nuclei and internal capsule is relatively well developed, via a prominent Heubner’s artery (arteria recurrens anterior), a branch of the anterior cerebral artery. The cortex and the white matter regions are rather poorly vascularized at this stage. The distribution of arteries and veins on the lateral aspect of the cerebral hemispheres is affected by the formation of the lateral fissure and the development of cerebral sulci and gyri. Between postmenstrual weeks 12–20 the middle cerebral artery and its branches are relatively straight, branching in an open-fan pattern. At the end of postmenstrual week 20 the arteries become more curved as the opercula begin to appear and submerge the insular cortex. The area supplied by the middle cerebral artery becomes dominant when compared to the territories supplied by the anterior and posterior cerebral arteries. Early arterial anastomoses appear around postmenstrual week 16and increase in size with advancing fetal age. The sites of anastomoses between the middle and anterior cerebral arteries move from the convexity of the brain towards the superior sagittal sinus and anastomotic connections between the middle and posterior cerebral arteries shift towards the basal aspect of the brain.
By postmenstrual weeks 32–34 marked involution of the ventricular zone (germinal matrix) has occurred and the cortex acquires its complex gyral pattern and an increased vascular supply. Ventricular zone capillaries are gradually remodelled to blend with the capillaries of the caudate nucleus. Heubner’s artery eventually supplies only a small area at the medial aspect of the head of the caudate nucleus. In the cortex, there is progressive elaboration of cortical blood vessels (see Fig. 14.36). Towards the end of the third trimester, the balance of cerebral circulation shifts from one that is central and basal nuclei-orientated, to one that predominantly serves the cortex and white matter.
Vessels of the ventricular zone (germinal matrix) and formation of the blood–brain barrier
The capillary bed of the ventricular zone is supplied mainly by Heubner’s artery and terminal branches of the lateral striate arteries from the middle cerebral artery. The capillary channels in the ventricular zone open at right-angles directly into the veins. Subependymal veins (septal, choroidal, thalamostriate and posterior terminal) flow towards the interventricular foramen where there is a change of direction and the veins recurve at an acute angle to form the paired internal cerebral veins.
Vessels within the ventricular and subventricular zones have been described as less mature than cortical vessels: their apparent fragility has been implicated in the aetiopathogenesis of intraventricular haemorrhage in premature infants. A rounded contour, relative paucity of pericytes, an immature basal lamina and a deficiency of glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP) in the ensheathing astrocyte end feet have all been cited as indicators of the immaturity of the microvasculature of the germinal matrix (Ballabh 2010). The ventricular and subventricular zones retain an embryonic capillary bed until cell proliferation is complete, which means that the highly cellular structure of the ventricular zone is a temporary feature. The capillary beds have the capacity to remodel after the ventricular cells finally differentiate as ependyma.
The direct mutual support of two epithelial layers and their shared basal laminae, rather than support of an epithelium by a lamina propria is unique, seen elsewhere only in the kidney glomerular basement membrane and alveolar-capillary membrane (see Fig 11.2). In the nervous system the cells of the neurovascular unit, endothelial, astrocyte and pericyte, form a blood–brain barrier that matures at different times in different brain regions during development. The permissive interactions between these epithelial phenotypes via their respective basal laminal secretions may contribute towards the patterning of the brain parenchymal spaces. Endothelial cells have a complex glycocalyx and are joined by tight and gap junctions; the integral membrane protein, occludin, spans the intracellular gaps between adjacent cells and claudins increase the hydraulic and electrical resistance across the endothelium (Pearce 2018). Pericytes, sparse in fetal vessels, are required for endothelial–astrocyte barrier formation and disruption of pericyte–endothelial cell interaction may lead to barrier dysfunction. Cerebral vessels differ during development: their proximity to the great arteries; position in the cortex or white matter; reactions to fluctuating oxygen and carbon dioxide tissue levels; expression of membrane receptors; and recent vasoreactivity to a given stimulus, all contribute to their extensive functional heterogeneity (Pearce 2018). Endothelial–astrocyte interactions can affect both neuronal migration and blood–brain barrier function (Keep et al 2019) (Ch. 3). It has been suggested that interstitial fluid from the neurovascular unit flows along an intramural perivascular pathway (Engelhardt et al 2016b).
The ventricular zone operates a brain–cerebrospinal fluid barrier during development but not once the ventricular zone has ceased as a dividing layer and the cells have differentiated to form ependyma. The choroid plexuses, sites of a blood–cerebrospinal fluid barrier, function during development and control paracellular transport to the cerebrospinal fluid (Neuwelt et al 2011).

Cerebral veins

The developing venous channels in the brain direct blood from the capillary networks along two main routes, superficial and deep, from pial vessels supplying the cortical and subcortical regions, and from the ventricular zone and subependymal regions, respectively. The arterial supply is ventriculopetal in the cortical/subcortical zone and ventriculofugal in the ventricular zone, whereas the venous return is ventriculofugal in pial veins and ventriculopetal in the subependymal region.
The pial capillary plexus supplies the developing cortical plate and subjacent developing white matter. Pial veins deep within the sulci drain this region, receiving superficial medullary veins at the grey/white matter interface and numerous fine short subcortical veins that may join arcuate veins within the cortex before draining into intracortical veins. The latter join cortical veins on the cerebral cortex that may attach to the dura or run within the dural layers towards the closest dural sinus. At the superior sagittal sinus, the cortical veins join at an acute angle, against the dural blood flow: this arrangement is thought to be due to differential growth of the cerebrum in the third trimester (Pearl et al 2011). Venous vessel density in the cortex and subcortical white matter is low between postmenstrual weeks 16 and 28 and increases after postmenstrual week 36. The superior, middle, inferior, anterior and posterior cortical cerebral veins appear more tortuous than the meningeal arteries. Rapid cortical development correlates with the regression of the middle cerebral vein and its tributaries and the development of ascending and descending cortical veins. Cortical cerebral veins and the anastomoses between them can be identified from postmenstrual week 16. The inferior anastomotic vein (of Labbé), an anastomosis between the middle and inferior cerebral veins, becomes recognizable at postmenstrual week 20, and the superior anastomotic vein (of Trolard), connecting the superior and middle cerebral veins, is seen after the end of postmenstrual week 30.
The ventricular zone capillary plexus is drained by a deep venous plexus that extends to the enlarging subcortical region. This plexus forms deep medullary veins within a region filled with migrating neurones and growing axons. Vessel density is high in this region between postmenstrual weeks 16–24, transiently decreases in postmenstrual weeks 28–36 and increases after postmenstrual week 39 (Takashima et al 2009). The deep medullary veins are very long veins; the deepest join arcades of larger veins until all drain into subependymal veins within the ventricular zone that become the internal cerebral veins. Early in development the invagination of pia mater to form the choroid plexuses gives rise to the tela choroideae which are drained by a transitory vessel, the median prosencephalic vein (of Markowski); this vessel gives rise to bilateral veins of the lateral choroid plexuses, bilateral internal cerebral veins and the great cerebral vein that drains into the straight sinus (Pearl et al 2011).
Sympathetic axons are seen in the walls of arteries supplying the rostral brain regions by about postmenstrual weeks 19–23. Unlike adult cerebral arteries, cholinergic nerves appear at the same time: fetal and neonatal cerebral arteries dilate in response to low concentrations of acetylcholine (Pearce 2018). Fetal perivascular innervation includes sensory fibres from the trigeminal ganglion (able to release vasoactive intestinal polypeptide, calcitonin gene-related peptide, substance-P, somatostatin and cholecystokinin) and, particularly around the arteries that form the circle of Willis and in the pial circulation, nerves that release neuropeptide Y (Pearce 2018). Very little is known about the functions of the neurotransmitters that act on the fetal cerebral circulation.
The development of the primary head veins, dural stems and dural sinuses is described in Chapter 17.

Developmental venous anomalies

Developmental venous anomalies include any changes to the previously described developmental pattern such that the usual balance of cortical and medullary venous drainage is not evident. Lack of cortical veins may lead to parts of the cortex draining only into the subependymal network via larger deep medullary veins, in a ventriculopetal rather than ventriculofugal matter. Conversely, a significant connection between the deep medullary veins and an intracortical vein may result in a ventriculofugal rather than ventriculopetal flow. Enlarged deep medullary veins, recognized as caput medusa, is seen in both variants. A vein of Galen aneurysmal malformation is seen in up to 30% of intracranial vascular anomalies; the early median prosencephalic vein does not regress, but remains connected to one or more choroidal arteries and enlarges as a result of the retained arteriovenous anastomosis (Pearl et al 2011, Cao and Cai 2019).
Interventricular haemorrhage
Anatomical variability of the course of the subependymal veins draining the proliferating ventricular zone (germinal matrix) is reported to be a predisposing factor for germinal matrix haemorrhage–interventricular haemorrhage (GMH–IVH), together with the level of developmental maturity of the ventricular zone capillary bed and its drainage. Each subependymal vein exhibits a U-shaped curve at the level of the interventricular foramen as it passes from the caudate–thalamic groove to its confluence with the internal cerebral vein. Increased vessel curvature (a more acute angle) is associated with blood flow alterations, increased luminal shear stress and possible venous thrombosis. Anomalous subependymal vein curvature in premature infants with GMH–IVH has been positively associated with unilateral haemorrhage occurring only in the hemisphere with a subependymal vein variant (Tortora et al 2018).
Periventricular leukomalacia
In fetuses and preterm infants of less than postmenstrual week 30 the blood vessels in the ventricular zone (germinal matrix) contain no smooth muscle, collagen or elastin fibres and have a disproportionately small number of reticulin fibres. In later preterm infants, the basal lamina surrounding the vessels is deficient in fibronectin, and thicker and more irregular than cortical vessels. In the periventricular regions at this time, the widening intermediate zone is filled with neuroblastic and glioblastic progeny from the ventricular and subventricular zones and the medial and lateral eminences migrating to the cortical subplate; axons extending from differentiating cortical neurones to the developing basal nuclei; axons extending from differentiating basal nuclei neurones to the cortex, all with their associated populations of pre-oligodendrocytes. This region represents a boundary zone between cortical and subcortical white matter supplied by superficial cortical arteries and veins, and the ventricular and subventricular zones supplied by the deeper striate arteries and deep medullary veins, and can be regarded as a watershed area between ventriculopetal and ventriculofugal circulations. Perturbation of cerebral blood flow in this watershed area can lead to a spectrum of developing white matter injury (see below).

The Neonatal Brain

Preterm neonate

Those babies born premature and with very low birth weight have been shown to have an altered cortical thickness that is still present in late teenage years (Bjuland et al 2013). It is not clear whether such changes are a consequence of preterm delivery, of perinatal brain injury or of fetal inflammatory response syndrome. Approximately 50% of preterm births are associated with clinical and subclinical chorioamnionitis (Maltepe and Penn 2018). Extremely and very preterm infants are usually stabilized by assisted ventilation, however, ventilation-induced lung injury in preterm infants also causes alveolar injury and release of cytokines that contribute to a systemic inflammatory response affecting the brain in those without prior infection (Barton et al 2015). Very preterm infants are also thought to be at risk of altered postnatal brain growth because of epigenetic regulation (DNA methylation) of the serotonin transported gene, as a consequence of early neonatal intensive care stress (Fumagalli et al 2018).
Preterm infants born before postmenstrual week 27 are at risk during early postnatal life of perturbation of the intermediate zone and the developing white matter (Ment and Sould 2012, Molnar and Rutherford 2013). Serial postnatal MRI scans of a group of infants delivered before postmenstrual week 27 (postnatal age counted sequentially in postmenstrual weeks), found increasing pathological changes with age, with about 20% showing white matter changes by 28 postmenstrual weeks, 50% showing changes during postmenstrual week 29 and about 80% exhibiting diffuse white matter signal anomalies by postmenstrual week 40 (Volpe 2017).
It was thought that in the preterm infant, where autoregulation of cerebral blood flow is limited, hypoperfusion of the intermediate zone was sufficient to cause local ischaemic lesions resulting in periventricular leukomalacia (PVL). PVL can also occur in full-term infants with congenital cyanotic heart anomalies. It is now thought that the disturbance of the differentiation of pre-oligodendrocytes (which are particularly affected by swings in cerebral ischaemia and reperfusion), accompanying astrocytosis and microgliosis, and prenatal systemic infection and inflammation cause hypomyelination (Volpe et al 2011, Back 2017). Histopathological studies now identify a spectrum of white matter injury from macroscopic lesions that are easily recognized on MRI, to microscopic focal necrotic lesions; the terms severe, moderate and mild white matter injury have been recommended to describe the condition (Volpe 2017). Studies on the long-term effects of preterm delivery note that even late preterm birth (postmenstrual weeks 34–37) has long-term effects. The late preterm brain may be unable to cope with postnatal conditions that may add to the problems of immaturity: for example, speech and language acquisition are delayed, as are school readiness and academic performance (Woythaler 2019).

Full-term neonate

Because the head is large at birth, measuring one-quarter of the total body length, the brain is also proportionally larger. The brain of a full-term (postmenstrual week 40) neonate ranges from 300 g to 400 g in weight, with an average of 350 g; the brains of neonatal males are slightly heavier than those of females. The brain occupies 97.5% of the cranial cavity from birth to 6 years of age, after which the space between the brain and skull increases in volume until the adult brain occupies only 92.5% of the cranial cavity. The cerebral ventricles are relatively larger in the neonatal brain than they are in the adult with a total of 10–15 ml of cerebrospinal fluid when delivered vaginally and 30 ml when delivered by caesarean section (as the head moves down the birth canal and is compressed, the cerebrospinal fluid is pushed out into the venous sinuses, whereas this does not happen in a caesarean delivery).
The sulci of the cerebral hemispheres appear after postmenstrual week 24. At full term, the general arrangement of sulci and gyri is present, but the insula is not completely covered, the central sulcus is situated further rostrally, and the lateral sulcus is more oblique than in the adult. The caveat here is that most of the developmental stages of sulci and gyri have been identified in the brains of premature infants where sulci are shallower at their equivalent postmenstrual week 40 than those of full-term neonates (Engelhardt et al 2016a, Shimony et al 2016). Of the cranial nerves, the olfactory nerve and the optic nerve at the chiasma are relatively much larger than they are in the adult, whereas the roots of the other cranial nerves are relatively smaller.

Reflexes present in the perinatal period

At birth mammals exhibit a range of coordinated motor movements that enhance their survival; these are species-specific. In humans, early reflex patterns, often somewhat erroneously termed ‘primitive’ reflexes, indicate the patterns of activity of multiple muscle groups present at birth (Feldman and Chaves-Gnecco 2018). Five tests of neurological development are most useful in determining developmental age. Lower limb flexor tone is detectable from about postmenstrual week 29. The pupillary reflex is consistently absent before postmenstrual week 29 and present after postmenstrual week 31; the glabellar tap, a blink in response to a tap on the glabella, is absent before postmenstrual week 32 and present after postmenstrual week 34; the neck-righting reflex appears between postmenstrual weeks 34–37; the traction response, where flexion of the neck or arms occurs when the baby is pulled up by the wrists from the supine position, appears after postmenstrual weeks 33; head-turning in response to light appears between postmenstrual weeks 32 and 36. The Babinski response, which involves extension of the great toe with spreading of the remaining toes in response to stimulation of the lateral aspect of the sole of the foot, is frequently elicited in neonates and reflects the level of myelination of the pathways. Early reflexes disappear in a predictable sequence reflecting postnatal myelination; their persistence suggests abnormal myelination of higher inhibitory pathways, seen in neurological developmental disorders and perinatal hypoxic–ischaemic brain injury (Futagi et al 2012, Feldman and Chaves-Gnecco 2018).
By full-term the neonate at rest has a strong flexor tone accompanied by adduction of all limbs. Neonates also display a distinct preference for a head position facing to the right, which appears to be independent of handling practices. The usual reflexes that can be elicited in a full-term (postmenstrual week 40) neonate include Moro, asymmetric tonic neck response, rooting–sucking, grasp, placing (contacting the dorsum of the foot with the edge of a table produces a ‘stepping over the edge’ response), stepping, and trunk incurvation (elicited by stroking down the paravertebral area with the infant in the prone position) (Futagi et al 2012). Examination of the motor system and evaluation of these reflexes allow assessment of the nervous system in relation to developmental age. The neonate also exhibits complex reflexes, such as nasal reflexes and sucking and swallowing. Early reflexes are tested during the routine examination of the newborn (Walker 2018). Production of otoacoustic emissions from sensory cell activity in the inner ear in response to sound is now routinely tested in neonates as an assessment of normal hearing. An adult-like auditory reflex (medial olivocochlear reflex) is present postnatally from about postmenstrual week 36 (Abdala and Mishra 2013).
Nasal reflexes produce apnoea via the diving reflex, sneezing, sniffing, and both somatic and autonomic reflexes. Stimulation of the face or nasal cavity with water or local irritants produces apnoea in neonates. Breathing stops in expiration, with laryngeal closure, and neonates exhibit bradycardia and a lowering of cardiac output. Blood flow to the skin, splanchnic areas, muscles and kidneys decreases, whereas flow to the heart and brain is protected. Different fluids produce different effects when introduced into the pharynx of preterm infants. A comparison of the effects of water and saline in the pharynx showed that apnoea, airway obstruction and swallowing occur far more frequently with water than with saline, suggesting the presence of an upper airway chemoreflex. Reflux of gastric content into the oesophagus is a well-recognized cause of apnoea and constitutes an acute life-threatening event in infants. Reflex responses to the temperature of the face and nasopharynx are necessary to start pulmonary ventilation: in the past, midwives often blew on the faces of neonates to induce the first breath.
Sucking and swallowing are a particularly complex set of reflexes, partly conscious and partly unconscious. As a combined reflex, sucking and swallowing require the coordination of several of the 12 pairs of cranial nerves. A neonate can, within the first couple of feeds, suck at the rate of once per second, swallow after five or six sucks, and breathe during every second or third suck. Air moves in and out of the lungs via the nasopharynx, and milk crosses the pharynx en route to the oesophagus without apparent interruption of breathing and swallowing, or significant misdirection of air into the stomach or fluids into the trachea. Swallowing movements are first noted at about postmenstrual week 11; in utero fetuses swallow 450 ml of amniotic fluid per day. Sucking and swallowing in premature infants (1700 g) is not associated with primary peristaltic waves in the intestine; however, in older babies and full-term neonates, at least 90% of swallows will initiate primary peristaltic waves.
Sucking develops, generally, slightly later than swallowing, although mouthing movements have been detected in premature babies as early aspostmenstrual weeks 18–24, and infants delivered at postmenstrual weeks 29–30 make sucking movements a few days after birth. Coordinated activities are not noted before postmenstrual weeks 33–34. The concept of non-nutritive and nutritive sucking has been introduced to account for the different rates of sucking seen in a neonate. Non-nutritive sucking, when rhythmic negative intraoral pressures are initiated that do not result in the delivery of milk, can be spontaneous or stimulated by an object in the mouth. This type of sucking tends to be twice as fast as nutritive sucking; the sucking frequency for non-nutritive sucking is 1.7sucks/second in preterm infants of postmenstrual weeks 37–38, 2sucks/second in term (postmenstrual week 40) neonates, and 2.7sucks/second at postnatal months 7–9. Corresponding times for nutritive sucking are about 1 suck/second in term neonates, increasing to 1.5 sucks/second by postnatal month 7.
The taste of the fluid, as well as nutrient content, affects the efficiency of nutritive sucking in the early neonatal period. There is more sucking with milk than with 5% dextrose: sucking activities increase with solutions that are determined to be sweet by adult appraisal. In full-term neonates, the placing of a spoon or food on to the anterior part of the tongue elicits an extrusion reflex: the lips are pursed and the tongue pushes vigorously against the object. By postnatal months 4–6, the reflex changes and food deposited on the anterior part of the tongue is moved to the back of the tongue, into the pharynx, and swallowed (see Ch. 40 for a description of swallowing in the adult). Rhythmic biting movements occur by postnatal months 7–9, even in the absence of teeth.
Difficulties in sucking and swallowing in infancy may be an early indication of disturbed nervous system function. There is a correlation between feeding styles of neonates and later eating habits. Children who were obese at 1 and 2 years of age, as measured by triceps skin-fold thickness, had a feeding pattern in the first month of life that was characterized by sucking more rapidly, producing higher pressures during prolonged bursts of sucking, and having shorter periods between bursts of sucking. Fewer feeds and higher sucking pressure seem to be associated with greater adiposity (Agras et al 1987). It has been noted that the levels of appetite-regulation hormones in fully breastfed infant serum are higher than in formula-fed infants at 4 postnatal months (Vásquez-Garibay et al 2019). Infants who are unable to feed orally in the first few weeks of life as a result of a congenital anomaly such as oesophageal atresia may develop food aversion.

Postnatal brain maturation

Postnatal brain growth velocity is illustrated by the change in brain volume from 25% of adult volume at full-term delivery to 75% of adult volume by the end of the first postnatal year. This growth can be accounted for partly by increase in the size of neuronal cell bodies, the profusion and dimensions of their dendritic trees, axons and collaterals, and by the growth of the neuroglial cells and cerebral blood vessels, but mostly it reflects the myelination of many of the axons. During the second and subsequent years, growth proceeds much more slowly. The brain reaches 90% of its adult size by year 5, 95% by year 10, and adult size between years 17–18, largely as a result of the continuing myelination of various groups of nerve fibres.
Maturational progression of neonatal and postnatal brains can be evaluated using diffusion tensor magnetic resonance imaging (DTMRI). A number of studies have assessed the growth status of white matter tracts from prenatal ages to early childhood (Huang et al 2006, Huang et al 2009, Lodygensky et al 2010, Dubois et al 2014, Huang and Vasung 2014, Dubois et al 2016) (see Fig. 14.35). Evidence from diffusion tensor imaging (DTI) suggests that myelination is a dominant process during the first year of life, starting centrally and spreading peripherally, and spreading rostrally from the occipital to the frontal lobe (Dubois et al 2016). Myelination of the corticospinal tracts starts in full-term neonates at about postnatal days 10–14 in the internal capsule and cerebral peduncles, and then proceeds simultaneously in both regions. Myelin appears to start first around longer axons, which presumably explains why, in the preterm infant, axial extension precedes flexion, whereas finger flexion precedes extension.
Comparison of postnatal rates of myelination in human and primate brains shows that humans achieve approximately 60% of adult myelination during adolescence (compared to 96% in chimpanzees at comparable stages of growth). Myelin growth continues in humans to the end of the third decade. Miller et al (2012) suggest this schedule of neural connectivity in humans might contribute to the development of functional circuitry with greater plasticity and capacity to be shaped by postnatal environmental and social interactions. The greatest delay in myelination is seen in the prefrontal cortex, an area where many neural circuits concerned with learning and memory develop only after sexual maturity has been attained.
Prior to preterm or full-term birth, the mother and fetus share circadian and other synchronized homeostatic physiological rhythms. After delivery, via processes similar to those seen in the postnatal synchronization of the optic pathways to ensure the development of the lateral geniculate nuclei and optic cortex, and of the auditory pathways to ensure postnatal maturation of hearing, the establishment of close mutual interactions between carer and neonate forms the basis for continuing infant cortical maturation and epigenetic upregulation of postnatal developmental genes. The continuing close reciprocal bond between carer and infant, with synchronization of a range of physiological rhythms, is described as the mother–infant dyad (Provenzi et al 2018).
Establishment of the mother–infant dyad initially promotes infant motor activity, skin tactile input and via establishment of oral feeding routines, autonomic sensory experiences. Synchrony of interactions, mutual eye contact, and mutual responsiveness of the mother–infant dyad appears to be set by postnatal month 4 (Navarez 2014). Later, the mother and family members encourage mirroring of behaviour (smiling and talking to the infant) and the infant becomes aware of its own sensory somatic states induced by the result of its motor actions. In this way the culturally driven rules for play and emotional display are transmitted to the infant. From psychological and sociological perspectives (i.e. through concepts of constructivist interactionism), the multiple ongoing interactions with the infant by family members (playing with the infant), and the resultant homeostatic interactions ongoing within the infant (feelings generated by their own movement), promote the development of cognitive pathways (Cantor et al 2018). The development of these ‘salience’ networks and pathways (anterior insula to anterior cingulate) in the neonate are important for normal postnatal adaptive behaviour responses (Molnar et al 2019). Failure of these postnatal processes is recognized in stress responses and withdrawn behaviours in infants; infants do not cry if they learn no one will come, inhibiting their emotional expression even when their cortisol levels are high. In later life such individuals may have difficulties regulating intense feelings. Mothers who get less support during these times are reported to demonstrate more restraint, avoidant and anxious attachment to their offspring, which subsequently affects the infant. Establishment of the mother–preterm infant dyad is difficult, with immature salience network development involved in neuropsychiatric disorders seen in adults born preterm (Molnar et al 2019). The preterm infant is less alert, hypersensitive to stimulation, inefficient at oral feeding, with lower capacity for self-regulation. Mothers of preterm infants have heightened stress and anxiety and may respond by either overstimulation or subsequent decreased stimulation; maternal anxiety is associated with preterm infants being less facially responsive and this can lead to difficulty over time with responsive maternal facial interactions (White-Traut et al 2013). For details of the mother–infant dyad and the neurobiology of postnatal development see Navarez (2014).
Autism spectrum disorders are at present viewed as a range of neurodevelopmental conditions with increasing prevalence. In China the prevalence increased from 10.3/10,000 in 2009 to 26.6/10,000 in 2013, and in the United States, it affected 1 in 152 children in 2002 and 1 in 68 in 2012 (Wang et al 2017). Prevalence in the UK showed a five-fold increase in incidence rates during the 1990s, with rates in 8-year-old children then reaching a plateau (Taylor et al 2013). The condition is thought to arise from increased proliferation of neuroprogenitor cells in the subventricular zone that leads to hyperexpansion of cortical surface areas. Early deficits in visual receptive and attention abilities are reported. At 2–4 postnatal years infants and children have increased grey matter volume, although this diminishes in adolescence and adulthood (Piven et al 2017, Wang et al 2017).
