Commentary 3.1: Comparative anatomy of the corticospinal system

Roger Lemon
The comparative anatomy of the long, corticofugal pathways from cortex to subcortical structures is important because we can interpret the key features of these pathways in humans on the basis of their similarities and differences when compared with other animals. This is particularly true of the corticospinal system. All mammals have a corticospinal tract, which originates from a number of parietal and frontal cortical areas, and which projects to different regions of the spinal cord. However, in functional terms, the different components of the corticospinal projection show large species-specific contrasts. There are also marked variations in other anatomical features, such as range of fibre sizes, spinal trajectory, pattern of termination within the spinal grey matter, and extent of the tract along the length of the spinal cord.

Cortical Origin

There is a basic pattern across all mammals for the cortical areas that give rise to the corticospinal tract. There are more areas in primates than in non-primates. In the former they include primary motor (M1) and somatosensory (S1) cortex, parietal area 5, S2, and frontal areas including dorsal and ventral premotor cortex and the supplementary motor area (all area 6) and a family of cingulate motor areas in the cingulate cortex on the medial surface of the hemisphere. In all of these areas, corticospinal fibres arise only from lamina V, which of course is very thick in motor cortex. In humans, all of these areas probably give rise to corticospinal fibres. Classical studies suggested that 60% of these fibres come from the frontal areas and 40% from parietal.

Number and size of axons

The number of fibres in the mammalian corticospinal (pyramidal) tract varies from around 30,000 from each cortical hemisphere in the mouse, up to around 1 million fibres in humans. There is a close relationship between fibre number, overall body weight and brain size. In all mammals the great majority of the fibres are of small diameter (<3 μm). In some large-bodied primates, there are larger fibres (>8 μm) and the largest are in humans (22 μm), but large fibres make up only a few per cent of all fibres in the tract.

Spinal trajectory

The course taken by corticospinal fibres within the spinal cord shows great variation. In rodents, rabbits and opossums most of the fibres are located at the base of the dorsal columns, whereas in elephants and hedgehogs, they are exclusively in the ventral funiculus. In carnivores and primates, most of the fibres are in the dorsolateral funiculus, with a smaller number of uncrossed fibres travelling in the ventral funiculus. A few fibres may also be scattered in the dorsal columns. In humans, fibres making up the lateral corticospinal tract are found throughout the dorsolateral funiculus (Fig. 3.1.1), although they are considered to lie slightly medial to the ascending dorsocerebellar tract fibres, which occupy the most lateral part of the dorsolateral funiculus. There is no obvious somatotopic organization within the various corticospinal tracts: fibres from different subdivisions (arm, leg) of M1, for example, are intermingled, as are projections from different cortical areas.

Extent of termination

In primates, including humans, the corticospinal tract extends for the full length of the spinal cord to the most caudal segments of the sacral cord. This is also the case in carnivores and rodents. Thus the tract can influence spinal mechanisms concerned with all four limbs (and tail when present). In some species, such as elephants, and ungulates such as goats, the tract does not extend beyond the upper cervical cord. Indeed, in the elephant the heaviest motor projection is to the facial nucleus, supplying the muscles of its trunk. In rodents, the heaviest motor projection from the cortex is again to the facial nucleus, with direct projections involved in the whisker movements, so important for the rodent’s exploration of space.

Pattern of spinal termination

Kuypers (1981) reviewed the pattern of corticospinal termination in the spinal grey matter across different species (see Fig. 3.1.1). He recognized that from rodents to carnivores and New World monkeys and from Old World monkeys to chimpanzees and humans there was a distinctive ventral shift in the pattern of terminations.
Projections to the dorsal horn
Kuypers stressed that in rodents and more primitive mammals, the major termination of the tract was in the dorsal horn, and this has been confirmed repeatedly for mouse and rat (Fig. 3.1.2). This suggests that at least one function of the tract, probably shared across all mammals and including humans, is the descending control of afferent input, including nociceptive input. This descending control may be very important for sensorimotor control, and in particular for the rapid recognition of predictable patterns of somatosensory input resulting from planned movements. Descending corticospinal signals can alter pain thresholds. In primates, most of the projections to the dorsal horn probably come from S1 (see Fig. 3.1.2), and there is only a weak projection to the dorsal horn from frontal motor areas.
Intermediate zone
In primates, and some non-primates such as cat, racoon, etc., the motor areas project to the intermediate zone, influencing spinal reflex pathways. In macaques the greatest number of corticospinal boutons are found in lamina VII (Fig. 3.1.3), which is the source short propriospinal pathways to motor neurones innervating the distal extremity.
Ventral horn
Direct projections (cortico-motoneuronal (CM) projections) to motor neurones of lamina IX are found only in some primate species, and these are best developed in great apes, such as chimpanzee and in humans (see Fig. 3.1.1). In macaques, quantitative analysis of terminal boutons, labelled after injections of tracer material in the M1 hand/arm region, shows that the number of boutons within lamina IX is second only to those in lamina VII (see Fig. 3.1.3).
Retrograde transneuronal virus labelling from different upper limb muscles in macaques suggests that only two areas give rise to significant numbers of CM projections: the caudal part of M1 (‘new’ M1) and area 3a.
The functional implications of this conspicuous ventral shift in termination pattern are still being researched, but there are some important clues in terms of the comparative anatomy. The long-standing idea that the presence of direct CM projections contributes to the capacity for skilled control of the hand is supported by the fact that CM projections are only well developed in those primates that are tool users/tool manufacturers. These include the New World capuchin monkey and Old World macaque monkey, baboon and the great apes. There is a well-established correlation between the strength of this projection, the size of postsynaptic excitation in hand and digit motor neurones and the measure of digital dexterity (Fig. 3.1.4).
Laterality of projections
It is estimated that in humans around 90% of the fibres in the corticospinal tract decussate to the opposite side and most descend in the lateral corticospinal tract. The remaining 10% descend ipsilaterally, mostly in the ventral corticospinal tract. The functions of these two components of the tract is still being investigated. The classical story is that the crossed fibres are mostly involved in control of the contralateral extremity, via their terminations in lamina VII and the lateral motor neurone pools, while ipsilateral fibres make bilateral terminations in lamina VIII and the medial motor neurone pools, innervating axial and girdle muscles.
This account will need to be revised in the light of research in macaques which points to the fact that the balance between contralateral versus ipsilateral terminations (i.e. where the fibres exert their synaptic actions, as opposed to their trajectory within the cord) may vary substantially between cortical areas and for cervical versus lumbosacral enlargement. Projections from the arm/hand region of macaque M1 terminate almost entirely in the contralateral grey matter of C5–T1 (see Fig. 3.1.3A), suggesting that even those fibres that descend ipsilaterally from the M1 arm/hand area cross at the segmental level and terminate contralaterally. In contrast to the arm/hand region, projections from M1 leg area are bilateral and exert bilateral effects on the lumbosacral cord, and this may reflect the involvement of leg motor cortex in skilled forms of locomotion such as skiing, dancing, etc. Secondary motor areas such as the SMA and the dorsal and ventral premotor areas generally give to a greater degree of bilateral projections to the cervical cord.
