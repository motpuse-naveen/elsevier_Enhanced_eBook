Isocortex

The isocortex and its parcellation into cortical areas will be separately described for each lobe in terms of its external sulci and gyri, internal cortical structure, connectivity and function. The subdivisions of the cerebral cortex as described in the different lobes is mainly based on the cytoarchitectonic parcellation schemes of Brodmann (1909) and von Economo and Koskinas (1925), and recent observer-independent cytoarchitectonic and multimodal parcellation methods (Schleicher et al 1999, Amunts et al 2007, 2014, Zilles and Amunts 2010, 2012, Amunts and Zilles 2015, Palomero-Gallagher et al 2015, Zilles et al 2015c, Palomero-Gallagher and Zilles 2018). Myeloarchitectonic findings will be included as appropriate (for review, see Zilles et al 2015a).

Frontal lobe

The frontal lobe contains numerous cortical areas for motor and cognitive functions, and is the largest lobe of the human forebrain. Its prefrontal cortex developed during the evolution of the brain to an absolute and relative size that is not attained by other primates.
The frontal lobe is delimited posteriorly by the central sulcus, medially by the cingulate sulcus and inferolaterally by the lateral fissure. The precentral gyrus is delimited by the central sulcus posteriorly and the precentral sulcus anteriorly. Anterior to the precentral gyrus the superior, middle and inferior frontal gyri converge anteriorly where they form the frontal pole. Its dorsal surface is covered by the frontal bone, and its ventral surface lies over the orbital part of the frontal bone and the cribriform plate of the ethmoid bone, and displays the orbital and rectus gyri. Its medial surface faces the falx cerebri.
The frontal lobe contains the primary motor area (BA4) in the anterior wall of the central sulcus; the supplementary motor area (SMA) and pre-supplementary motor area (pre-SMA) on its dorsomedial surface (medial part of BA6); premotor areas (lateral part of BA6) on its dorsolateral surface; the medial prefrontal (BA32) and lateral prefrontal (BA8–BA10 and BA44–BA47) cortex rostral to the precentral gyrus; and the fronto-orbital region (BA11–BA12) on its baso-rostral surface (see Fig. 32.13).
Frontal lobe sulci and gyri
The central sulcus demarcates the primary motor (BA4) and somatosensory (BA1–BA3) cortical areas located in the precentral and postcentral gyri, respectively. The central sulcus starts in or near the interhemispheric fissure, a little behind the midpoint between the frontal and occipital poles, and runs in a letter S shape ventrally and rostrally, to end usually shortly above the lateral sulcus: it is mostly continuous in both hemispheres (Fig. 32.47).
The precentral gyrus lies over the dorsolateral and lateral surface of the cerebral hemisphere, its upper aspect extending onto the medial surface. It is continuous medially and inferiorly with the postcentral gyrus along connections that encircle both extremities of the central sulcus. The inferior connection corresponds to the subcentral gyrus, delineated anteriorly and posteriorly by the anterior and posterior subcentral rami of the lateral fissure. They can be situated either completely over the lateral fissure, or in part internal to the fissure. The superior connection corresponds to the paracentral lobule that is located on the medial surface of the hemisphere inside the interhemispheric fissure, and delineated anteriorly by the paracentral sulcus and posteriorly by the ascending marginal ramus of the cingulate sulcus. Some brains show a middle connection between the pre- and postcentral gyri (pli de passage moyen of Broca), which is usually hidden within the central sulcus: when this middle connection reaches the brain surface, it interrupts the central sulcus. The motor hand area has been localized by fMRI to an Ω-like protrusion of the precentral gyrus directed to the postcentral gyrus (Fig. 32.48) that is found at the level of the middle genu of the central sulcus (Yousry et al 1997). The precentral gyrus is delimited anteriorly by the precentral sulcus, itself divided into superior and inferior precentral sulci by a connection between the middle frontal gyrus and the precentral gyrus. The superior part of the precentral sulcus is very often interrupted superiorly by a connection between the superior frontal and precentral gyri, producing a more medial segment, the medial precentral sulcus. More dorsally, within the precentral region, the marginal precentral sulcus (see Fig. 32.47) may merge with the superior precentral or central sulci.
The superior frontal gyrus is continuous anteriorly and inferiorly with the rectus gyrus, and may also be connected to the orbital gyri and the middle frontal gyrus. Posteriorly, it is connected to the precentral gyrus (see Fig. 32.47). Usually the superior frontal gyrus is subdivided by an intermediate frontal sulcus into a lateral and a medial portion. The middle frontal gyrus is usually the largest of the frontal gyri, and is frequently connected posteriorly to the precentral gyrus. It harbours a complex of multiple shallow sulcal segments known collectively as the middle or intermediate frontal sulcus (Petrides and Pandya 2012).
The inferior frontal gyrus is subdivided by small sulci into a posterior pars opercularis, an intermediate pars triangularis and an anterior pars orbitalis (see Fig. 32.47). The opercular part is rostrally bordered by the ascending ramus of the lateral fissure, and posteriorly by the precentral sulcus. A diagonal sulcus is occasionally visible in the pars triangularis. The triangular part extends between the ascending and horizontal rami of the lateral fissure, and continues with the lateral orbital part. The frontomarginal sulcus is often a short branching sulcus that separates the frontal pole into different portions.
The olfactory sulcus lies longitudinally in a paramedian position on the frontobasal (orbital) surface of the frontal lobe (see Fig. 32.47). It underlies the olfactory tract and bulb. Posteriorly, the olfactory tract is divided into medial and lateral striae that delineate the most anterior aspect of the anterior perforated substance. The narrow gyrus rectus, medial to the olfactory sulcus, is considered to be the most anatomically constant of the cerebral gyri. The orbital gyri, lateral to the olfactory sulcus, account for the greatest proportion of the frontobasal region. The orbital gyri are delineated by the lateral, medial and transverse orbital sulci that together form a characteristic H shape. The posterior orbital gyrus lies anterior to the anterior perforated substance and typically presents a configuration similar to a tricorn hat, a feature that may facilitate its identification in anatomical specimens where the H-shaped orbital sulcus is less obvious.
On its medial surface, the frontal lobe is limited inferiorly by the cingulate sulcus, which starts within the subcallosal region and extends over the cingulate gyrus. The cingulate sulcus may occasionally be double in its anterior portion, when the more rostrally and dorsally situated sulcus is called the paracingulate sulcus (see Fig. 32.2).
The paracentral lobule contains the distal part of the central sulcus and, inferior to it, the paracentral fossa (see Fig. 32.2). The gyrus rectus is bounded superiorly by the superior rostral sulcus and accommodates the shallower inferior rostral sulcus along its medial surface.
Frontal lobe internal structure and connectivity
The primary motor cortex (BA4) corresponds to a largely intrasulcal part of the precentral gyrus and is visible on the cortical surface only at its most superior level. BA4 has the lowest threshold for eliciting contralateral muscle contraction by electrical stimulation. It contains a topographically organized map (‘motor homunculus’) of the opposite body half, where the head is represented most laterally and inferiorly, the hand is represented in the central region, and the leg and foot are represented on the medial surface of the hemisphere in the paracentral lobule (Fig. 32.49). A striking feature of this map is the disproportionate representation of body parts in relation to their physical size: large areas represent the muscles of the face and hand, which are capable of finely controlled or fractionated movements (Penfield and Boldrey 1937; for a recent review of the homunculus, see Catani 2017).
In the adult stage, layer IV of BA4 and BA6 is difficult to identify in Nissl-stained sections (Fig. 32.50A), and both areas were traditionally classified as agranular cortex. A distinct inner granular layer IV is visible in fetal brains, but disappears during the first year of life (Amunts et al 1995). The typical small cells of layer IV have been demonstrated at the border region between layers III and V in the adult stage (Barbas and Garcia-Cabezas 2015). The most characteristic feature of BA4 are the somata of extremely large pyramidal cells (Betz cells) in lamina V (Fig. 32.50). These cells are 80–100 μm in diameter, and their axons form the corticospinal and corticonuclear tracts. BA4 contains a very high density of myelinated axons in the lower half of its cortical depth. It also has the lowest concentration of most transmitter receptor types across all cortical areas (Fig. 32.51), presumably because most receptors are found pre- and post-synaptically and myelin sheaths prevent the building of synapses.
The major thalamic connections of BA4 are with the posterior part of the ventral lateral nucleus, which receives afferents from the deep cerebellar nuclei and contains a topographic representation of the contralateral half of the body that is preserved in its projection to BA4. The anterior part of the ventral lateral nucleus projects to the premotor and supplementary motor areas of the cortex. Other thalamic connections of BA4 are with the centromedian and parafascicular thalamic nuclei. Apart from the corticospinal tract and thalamic projections, the motor cortex has extensive subcortical connections with the striatum, subthalamic nucleus, pontine nuclei and all the somatomotor nuclei in the brainstem (oculogyric, facial and hypoglossal nuclei).
BA4 is the origin of polysynaptic descending pathways to the spinal cord that relay in the reticular formation, red nucleus, superior colliculus, vestibular nuclei and inferior olivary nucleus. BA4 is also connected with the ipsilateral somatosensory cortex through short U-fibres, which are topographically organized according to the homuncular principle, i.e. the largest projections are observed for the hand and there are progressively smaller projections for the tongue/mouth and foot areas (Catani et al 2012). These projections to the motor cortex arise mainly in BA1 and BA2, with minor contribution from area 3b. Fibres from the somatosensory cortex terminate in layers II and III of BA4: neurones in the motor cortex that can be activated from the somatosensory cortex tend to have a late onset of activity, mainly during the execution of movement. Connections between motor and somatosensory homunculi may play a role in precision grasping and motor learning. Additional ipsilateral corticocortical fibres to BA4 posterior to the central sulcus come from the opercular part of the somatosensory area (SII). Neurones in BA4 are responsive to peripheral stimuli and have receptive fields similar to those in the primary somatosensory cortex. Cells located posteriorly in the motor cortex (area 4p) have cutaneous receptive fields, whereas more anteriorly situated neurones (in area 4a) respond to stimulation of deeper tissues (Geyer et al 1996).
The motor cortex receives inputs from the lateral premotor cortex, supplementary motor area and insula. Most of these connections are short U-shapes that modulate motor cortical activity in relation to the preparation, guidance and temporal organization of movements. BA4 sends fibres to, and receives fibres from, its contralateral counterpart, and also projects to the contralateral supplementary motor cortex.
Brodmann’s ‘agranular’ area 6 (see Fig. 32.13) lies immediately in front of the primary motor cortex on the precentral gyrus and extends superiorly into the lateral and medial portions of the superior frontal gyrus (Geyer et al 2012). BA6 is subdivided into the lateral premotor cortex and the supplementary motor (SMA proper) and presupplementary motor (pre-SMA) cortices on the medial hemispheric surface (Fig. 32.52). The lateral premotor cortex is segregated into a dorsal and a ventral area on cytoarchitectonical, connectional and functional grounds; both dorsal and ventral areas contribute to the corticospinal tract. Further anterior and posterior subdivisions of the dorsal and ventral lateral premotor cortex can be identified (see Fig. 32.50B–C).
The major thalamic connections of the premotor cortex are with the anterior division of the ventrolateral nucleus and with the centromedian, parafascicular and centrolateral components of the intralaminar thalamic nuclei. Subcortical projections to the striatum and pontine nuclei are prominent, and it projects to the superior colliculus and the reticular formation. Commissural connections are with the contralateral premotor, primary motor and superior parietal (BA5) cortices. Ipsilateral corticocortical connections with BA5 are common to both dorsal and ventral subdivisions of the premotor cortex, and both send a major projection to the primary motor cortex. The dorsal premotor area receives fibres from the posterior superior temporal cortex and projects to the supplementary motor cortex. The dorsal subdivision of the lateral premotor area receives projections from the frontal eye field. Perhaps the greatest functionally significant difference in connectivity between the dorsal and ventral subdivisions of the lateral premotor area is that the dorsal premotor area receives fibres from the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, whereas the ventral subdivision receives fibres from the ventrolateral prefrontal cortex. All of these association connections are reciprocal.
Direction selectivity for movement is a common feature of many premotor neurones. Neurones in the dorsal premotor cortex show anticipatory activity and task-related discharge as well as direction selectivity, but little or no direct stimulus-related activity. Together with the posterior parietal cortex, the dorsal premotor cortex is important in establishing a motor programme or intention, thus contributing to motor preparation in relation to internally guided movement. In contrast, the ventral premotor cortex is related more to the execution of externally guided movements in relation to a specific stimulus, especially visually guided movements.
The supplementary motor area (SMA proper) is found within the medial portion of BA6 (see Fig. 32.52A). An additional functional subdivision, the pre-supplementary motor area (pre-SMA), lies anterior to the supplementary motor area on the medial surface of the cortex. The portion of area 24 buried in the cingulate sulcus and adjacent to BA6 contains two motor areas, the anterior and posterior cingulate motor areas (see Fig. 32.52). SMA proper and pre-SMA are ‘agranular’ cortices, because a layer IV is not clearly definable in Nissl stained sections of this area (see Fig. 32.52B).
SMA receives its major thalamic input from the anterior part of the ventral lateral thalamic nucleus, which in turn is the major recipient of input from the internal segment of the globus pallidus. Additional thalamic afferents are from the ventral anterior nucleus, intralaminar nuclei (notably the centrolateral and centromedial nuclei) and the mediodorsal nucleus. The connections with the thalamus are reciprocal. SMA makes a substantial contribution to the corticospinal tract. It contains a homuncular representation of the body in which the leg is posterior, the face anterior and the upper limb is represented between them. Its role in the control of movement is primarily in complex tasks that require temporal organization of sequential movements and in the retrieval of motor memory. Stimulation of SMA elicits the sensation of an urge to move, or of anticipation that a movement is about to occur.
Pre-SMA receives connections from widespread regions of the ipsilateral frontal lobe, including the primary motor cortex, dorsal premotor area, dorsolateral and ventrolateral prefrontal, medial prefrontal, fronto-orbital cortices and the frontal eye field. These connections are reciprocal, but the major ipsilateral efferent pathway is to the motor cortex. Parietal lobe connections of pre-SMA are with the superior parietal area BA5 and inferior parietal lobule. Contralateral connections are with SMA and contralateral premotor cortices. Further subcortical connections pass to the striatum, subthalamic and pontine nuclei, the brainstem reticular formation and the inferior olivary nucleus.
The frontal eye field (FEF) is an important area for the control of eye movements. It is located near the junction of the middle frontal and precentral sulci: activation studies in humans indicate a more posterior localization (BA6) compared to monkeys (BA8). It receives its major thalamic projection from the parvocellular mediodorsal nucleus, with additional afferents from the medial pulvinar, ventral anterior nucleus, suprageniculate–limitans complex and the paracentral nucleus of the intralaminar group. The thalamocortical pathways to FEF are part of pathways from the superior colliculus, substantia nigra and the dentate nucleus of the cerebellum. The FEF has extensive ipsilateral corticocortical connections, and receives input from several visual areas in the occipital, parietal and temporal lobes, including the middle temporal area (V5). There are also projections from the auditory superior temporal gyrus and the ventrolateral and dorsolateral prefrontal cortices. The FEF projects to the dorsal and ventral premotor cortices and to a medial motor area, probably the supplementary eye field adjacent to the supplementary motor area proper. The FEF projects prominently to the superior colliculus, the pontine gaze centre within the pontine reticular formation, and oculogyric-related nuclei in the brainstem.
The prefrontal cortex on the dorsolateral surface of the hemisphere consists predominantly of Brodmann’s areas 8, 9, 10, 46, 47 and language areas 44 and 45. All prefrontal areas except area 44 have a layer IV, and thus are granular cortices; area 44 is dysgranular (Fig. 32.53C–D). These areas receive their major thalamic afferents from the mediodorsal nucleus, with additional contributions from the medial pulvinar and ventral anterior and paracentral nuclei of the anterior intralaminar group. The dorsolateral region receives long association fibres from the posterior and middle superior temporal gyri (including auditory association areas) and from the posterior parietal and much of the middle temporal cortices. It also receives projections from the frontal pole (BA10), and from the medial prefrontal cortex (BA32). It projects to SMA, the dorsal premotor cortex and the frontal eye field. All these thalamic and corticocortical connections are reciprocal. Commissural connections are with the homologous areas and with the contralateral inferior parietal cortex. The ventrolateral prefrontal region receives long association fibres from the inferior parietal lobule, auditory association areas of the temporal operculum, the insula and the anterior part of the ventral bank of the superior temporal sulcus. It also receives input from the anterior fronto-orbital cortex and projects to the frontal eye field and the ventral premotor cortex. It is reciprocally connected with the contralateral homologous area via the corpus callosum.
The granular cortex of the frontal pole (BA10) has greatly expanded in humans compared to other species. It receives thalamic inputs from the mediodorsal nucleus, medial pulvinar and paracentral nucleus and is reciprocally connected with the cortex of the temporal pole, the anterior fronto-orbital cortex and the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex. Within the frontal lobe, BA10 has connections with BA32, the ventrolateral prefrontal cortex, and premotor areas on the medial surface of the hemisphere. Commissural connections to the homologous area of the contralateral hemisphere are extensive. BA10 is segregated into lateral (Fp1) and medial (Fp2) areas on cytoarchitectonic, genetic and functional grounds (see Figs 32.13, 32.53C–D) (Bludau et al 2014, 2018). The lateral area is involved in working memory, episodic memory retrieval and attending environmental stimuli, whereas the medial frontal pole region is engaged in self-generated stimuli (i.e. the ‘thoughts in our head’). The interaction between the two areas mediated by the fronto-marginal tract determine whether signals from the internal (mental) or external (sensory) world dominate ongoing behaviour and cognition (Burgess et al 2011, Catani et al 2012).
The fronto-orbital cortex is connected with the mediodorsal, anteromedial, ventral anterior, medial pulvinar, paracentral and midline nuclei of the thalamus. Three areas are cytoarchitectonically identified in the fronto-orbital cortex (Fig. 32.54A): the granular area Fo1, mainly on the rostral gyrus rectus and medial of the olfactory sulcus; the granular to dysgranular area Fo2, mainly on the posterior part of the ventromedial gyrus rectus and the medial and lateral banks of the olfactory sulcus; and the granular area Fo3 between the olfactory and medial or intermediate orbital sulci. Fo3 is bordered medially by Fo1 and Fo2. The posterior part of the fronto-orbital cortex extends to the olfactory and insular cortices and is partly covered by the temporal lobe. The parolfactory sulcus often marks the border between the medial part of the fronto-orbital cortex and BA25. The medial fronto-orbital cortex has reciprocal connections with various limbic structures including the anterior cingulate, insula, amygdala and entorhinal cortices, hippocampus, diagonal band of Broca, midline thalamic nuclei and the magnocellular sector of the mediodorsal thalamic nucleus. The fronto-orbital cortex is involved in a variety of functions associated with pleasant as well as painful perceptions and reward processing (see limbic lobe). Olfactory and gustatory stimuli elicit activations in the fronto-orbital cortex that are often associated with hedonistic aspects, and is also involved in touch and pain processing.
The language area of Broca consists of BA44 and BA45 in the opercular and triangular parts of the inferior frontal gyrus, respectively. BA44 is found immediately anterior to the most inferior aspect of the precentral gyrus (Fig. 32.54B). Both areas are involved in speech production and show considerable inter-individual variability in size and relationship to gyri. Volumes of left BA44 are always larger than those of the right hemisphere. Thus, interhemispheric differences in the volume of BA44 may be interpreted as anatomical correlates of language dominance. Volumes of BA45 do not differ significantly between the hemispheres. BA44 is involved more in syntactic processes, and BA45 more in semantic processes. The borders between these areas are not consistently defined by sulcal landmarks (Amunts et al 1999). BA47, or at least its most posterior part, should also be considered as part of the Broca region for functional reasons. The cytoarchitecture of BA44 and BA45 is characterized by very large pyramidal cells, sometimes clustered, in deeper layer III, and large pyramidal cells in upper layer V (Fig. 32.53A–B). The size of cells in layer III increases from its border with layer II to the layer III/IV border. The most important difference between BA44 and BA45 is found in layer IV, which is dysgranular and invaded by pyramidal cells of layers III and V in BA44. In contrast, layer IV of BA45 is seldom invaded by layer III or layer V pyramidal cells, i.e. BA45 is a granular cortical area. Both areas can be further subdivided on the basis of regional and laminar densities of multiple receptors (Fig. 32.53E–F).

Parietal lobe

The parietal lobe lies posterior to the central sulcus on the superolateral and medial surfaces of the cerebral hemisphere. The anterior part of the human parietal cortex comprises somatosensory areas 3a, 3b, BA1 and BA2 in the postcentral gyrus, and the parietal operculum (OP1–OP4). The posterior parietal cortex contains multimodal areas in both the superior (BA5, BA7) and inferior (BA40, BA39) parietal lobules, as well as areas in the intraparietal sulcus that were not described by Brodmann (see Fig. 32.13). The posterior parietal cortex is a key node in networks connecting visual occipital and auditory temporal areas with premotor and prefrontal cortices. The superior parietal lobule (SPL) encompasses parts of the lateral as well as the medial surface, the latter including the precuneus.
Parietal lobe sulci and gyri
Anteriorily, the parietal lobe is delimited by the central sulcus. Posteriorly, it is well delineated on the medial aspect by the parieto-occipital sulcus, and on the dorso-lateral aspect by a line that can be drawn running from the point where the parieto-occipital sulcus reaches its most superior extremity at the edge of the hemisphere to the occipito-temporal incisure. The inferior boundary is defined by the lateral fissure and its imaginary prolongation to the line connecting the parieto-occipital sulcus and the occipito-temporal incisure.
The lateral aspect of the parietal lobe is divided into three regions by the postcentral and intraparietal sulci. The intraparietal sulcus, which originates from around the midpoint of the postcentral sulcus, is prominent on the superolateral surface of the parietal lobe, running parallel with the superior margin of the hemisphere (see Figs 32.2, 32.3). Anteriorly, the intraparietal sulcus is usually continuous with the inferior portion of the postcentral sulcus and posteriorly it passes into the occipital lobe as the superior occipital sulcus, which continues posteriorly into the transverse occipital sulcus. The intraparietal sulcus delineates the superior parietal lobule, continuous medially with the precuneus, and the inferior parietal lobule, made up of the supramarginal and angular gyri and a more posterior convolution continuous with the occipital lobe. The superior vertical branch of the intraparietal sulcus (transverse parietal sulcus) divides the SPL into an anterior and posterior portion, whereas the inferior vertical branch (intermediate sulcus of Jensen) separates the supramarginal gyrus anteriorly from the angular gyrus posteriorly. The inferior aspect of the supramarginal gyrus within the inferior parietal lobule of the dominant hemisphere corresponds to Wernicke’s area, which extends along the posterior aspect of the superior temporal gyrus.
The postcentral gyrus is usually narrower than the precentral gyrus. Both gyri are located obliquely on the superolateral surface of the hemisphere, just superior to the lateral fissure. The superior portions of the pre- and postcentral gyri, which constitute the paracentral lobule on the medial surface of the cerebral hemisphere, are topographically related to the ventricular atrium, situated posterior to the thalamus. The inferior portions of both gyri cover the posterior half of the insula and are topographically related to the body of the lateral ventricle, situated superior to the thalamus. The portion of the subcentral gyrus corresponding to the base of the postcentral gyrus consistently lies over the transverse gyri of Heschl, situated on the opercular surface of the temporal lobe.
The supramarginal gyrus surrounds the distal portion of the lateral fissure (its posterior ascending branch) and becomes continuous with the posterior portion of the superior temporal gyrus. Above the distal end of the lateral fissure, the supramarginal gyrus is connected anteriorly to the postcentral gyrus through a fold that runs underneath the inferior aspect of the postcentral sulcus. Posteriorly, it occasionally rounds the inferior extremity of the intermediate sulcus and connects to the angular gyrus. The angular gyrus is a curved gyrus and always surrounds one of the distal segments of the superior temporal sulcus, usually the middle one (angular sulcus or t2 in Fig. 32.2H); its most inferior portion is continuous with the middle temporal gyrus. The configuration of the angular gyrus is defined by the distal branching of the superior temporal sulcus, which typically ends as three continuous or interrupted caudal branches.
The most superior distal branch of the superior temporal sulcus has an ascending course; it may either penetrate the supramarginal gyrus or coincide with the intermediate sulcus of Jensen separating the supramarginal and angular gyri. The second branch (t2) is usually more horizontal and enters the angular gyrus as the angular sulcus. The most inferior caudal branch (t3) of the superior temporal sulcus is less evident and less constant. It courses underneath a posterior fold that frequently connects the angular gyrus and the most lateral aspect of the occipital lobe, and is usually continuous with the anterior occipital sulcus, lying predominantly vertically along the anterior edge of the middle occipital gyrus.
On the medial surface of each hemisphere, the precuneus lies posterior to the paracentral lobule as a medial extension of the superior parietal lobule. The precuneus is quadrangular, delineated anteriorly by the marginal branch of the cingulate sulcus, posteriorly by the parieto-occipital sulcus and inferiorly by the subparietal sulcus. Inferior to the subparietal sulcus, the precuneus is connected to the isthmus of the cingulate gyrus, which is continuous with the parahippocampal gyrus. The parieto-occipital sulcus separating the precuneus from the cuneus is deep and contains many small sulci and gyri.
Parietal lobe internal structure and connectivity
All parietal areas have a six-layered eulaminate cortex, with the exception of areas 3a and 3b of the primary somatosensory cortex. The molecular layer (layer I) does not differ between the various areas, but differences in layers II to VI help to parcellate the parietal cortex. The basic myeloarchitectonic organization of the isocortex, with radiate fibre bundles reaching the superficial parts of cortical layer III, can be found in all parietal areas, although the preponderance and extent of the inner and outer stripes of Baillarger differ.
The primary somatosensory cortex (SI) located in the postcentral gyrus includes areas 3a, 3b, BA1 and BA2. Area 3a lies in the depth of the central sulcus and is contiguous to area 4p whereas area 3b is in the posterior wall of the central sulcus. BA1 is on the crown of the postcentral gyrus, and BA2 occupies its posterior cortex (see Fig. 32.13). Area 3a has a low cell packing density, with a few larger pyramidal cells in deep layer III, and an incipient layer IV (see Fig. 32.50D). In contrast, area 3b has a very high cell packing density in the supragranular layers, particularly layer IV, and the cells in layer III are homogeneously distributed: this led to the classification of area 3b as koniocortical (see Fig. 32.50E). BA1 is characterized by an obvious layer IV, and a pronounced columnar arrangement of typically large and numerous pyramidal cells in layers III and V, with a marked superficial-to-deep increase in cell size across layer III (see Fig. 32.50F). BA2 also has an obvious layer IV, fewer pyramidal cells in layer III, large pyramidal cells only appearing deep in layer III and a sharp layer VI–white matter border (see Fig. 32.50G).
The primary somatosensory cortex contains a topographical map of the contralateral half of the body. The face, tongue and lips are represented inferiorly, the trunk and upper limb are represented on the superolateral aspect, and the lower limb is represented on the medial aspect of the hemisphere. The function of SI depends on its thalamic input from the ventral posterior nucleus. This nucleus is divided into a ventral posterolateral part, which receives information from the trunk and limbs, and a ventral posteromedial part, in which the head is represented. Within the ventral posterior nucleus, neurones in the central core respond to cutaneous stimuli and those in the most dorsal anterior and posterior parts, which arch as a ‘shell’ over the central core, respond to deep stimuli. This is reflected in their differential projections to the cortex: the cutaneous central core projects to 3b, the deep tissue-responsive neurones send fibres to areas 3a and 2, and an intervening zone projects to area 1. Within the ventral posterior nucleus, anteroposterior rods of cells respond with similar modality and somatotopic properties. They appear to project to restricted focal patches in the primary somatosensory cortex of approximately 0.5 mm width. The laminar termination of thalamocortical axons from the ventral posterior nucleus is different for the separate cytoarchitectonic subdivisions of the primary somatosensory cortex. In 3a and 3b, these axons terminate mainly in layer IV and the adjacent deep part of layer III, whereas in BA1 and BA2 they end in the deeper half of layer III, avoiding layer IV. Additional thalamocortical fibres to primary somatosensory cortex arise from the intralaminar system, notably the centrolateral nucleus.
There is a complex internal local connectivity among the primary somatosensory areas with a stepwise hierarchical progression of information from area 3b through BA1 to BA2. Short U-fibres connect the primary somatosensory areas with ipsilateral BA4 and parietal areas in the superior and inferior parietal lobule, and the parietal operculum. The primary somatosensory cortex has reciprocal commissural connections with its contralateral homologue, except that the cortices containing the representation of the distal extremities are relatively devoid of such connections. Callosal fibres in the primary somatosensory areas arise mainly from the deep part of layer III and terminate in layers I–IV. Pyramidal cells contributing callosal projections receive monosynaptic thalamic and commissural connections.
The primary somatosensory area has reciprocal connections with subcortical structures that include the claustrum (which in turn receives afferents from the nucleus basalis of Meynert), the locus coeruleus and the midbrain raphe. Corticostriatal fibres originating in layer V project mainly to the ipsilateral putamen. Other projections from layer V of the primary somatosensory cortex terminate in the main pontine nuclei, the nucleus reticularis tegmenti pontis (pontine tegmental reticular nucleus) and the spinal cord. The topographical representation in the cortex is preserved in terms of the spinal segments to which different parts of the postcentral gyrus project. Thus, the arm representation projects to the cervical enlargement, the leg representation to the lumbosacral enlargement, and so on. Within the grey matter of the spinal cord, fibres from the somatosensory cortex terminate in the dorsal horn, in Rexed’s laminae 3–5; fibres from 3b and BA1 end more dorsally, and those from BA2 more ventrally.
The rostral part of the parietal operculum can be subdivided into four areas, i.e. areas OP1–OP4 (Eickhoff, Schleicher et al 2006), which reflect the subdivision of this region into a secondary somatosensory region (SII; OP1, 3, 4) and a putative vestibular cortex (OP2). The parietal opercular areas are characterized by a columnar arrangement. While OP1 has a pronounced superficial-to-deep increase in cell size in layer III, area OP2 appears more homogeneous, but with additional very large pyramidal cells in deep layer III. In area OP3, layer III, V and VI have considerably lower cell packing densities. Area OP4 is characterized by smaller cells throughout, with only medium-sized pyramids in deep layer III, and layer IV is difficult to visualize. The secondary somatosensory region lies along the upper bank of the lateral fissure, posterior to the central sulcus. It contains a somatotopic representation of the body, with the head and face most anterior, adjacent to SI, and the sacral regions most posterior. The secondary somatosensory region is reciprocally connected with the ventral posterior nucleus of the thalamus in a topographically organized fashion. Some thalamic neurones probably project to both primary and secondary somatosensory regions via axon collaterals. Other thalamic connections of the secondary somatosensory region are with the posterior group of nuclei and with the intralaminar central lateral nucleus. The secondary somatosensory region also projects to laminae IV–VII of the dorsal horn of the cervical and thoracic spinal cord, the dorsal column nuclei, the principal trigeminal nucleus and the periaqueductal grey matter of the midbrain. Within the cortex, SII is reciprocally connected with SI in a topographically organized manner and projects to the primary motor cortex. SII also projects in a topographically organized way to the lateral part of PPC, and makes connections with PCC. Both right and left SII areas are interconnected across the corpus callosum, although distal limb representations are probably excluded. Neurones in SII respond particularly to transient cutaneous stimuli, e.g. brush strokes or tapping, which are characteristic of the responses of Pacinian corpuscles in the periphery: they show little response to maintained stimuli.
The superior and inferior parietal lobules are each composed of seven cytoarchitectonically distinct areas (Fig. 32.55). The major difference in cytoarchitecture between the areas of the two lobules is that the pyramidal cells in layers IIIc and V are larger in the superior parietal lobule. Using modern quantitative cyto- and receptorarchitectonic methods, BA5 and BA7 in the superior lobule have been subdivided into areas 5L, 5M and 5Ci, and areas 7A, 7P, 7M and 7PC, respectively (Scheperjans et al 2005, Scheperjans, Hermann et al 2008). Using the same methodological approach for the inferior parietal lobule, areas PFop, PFcm, PFt, PF and PFm were identified within BA40, and areas PGa and PGp within BA39 (Caspers and Zilles 2018) (Fig. 32.56).
BA5 receives a dense feed-forward projection from all cytoarchitectonic areas of primary somatosensory cortex in a topographically organized manner. The thalamic afferents to this area come from the lateral posterior nucleus and from the central lateral nucleus of the intralaminar group. Ipsilateral corticocortical fibres from BA5 go to BA7, the premotor and supplementary motor cortices, the posterior cingulate gyrus and the insular granular cortex. Commissural connections between BA5 on both sides tend to avoid the areas of representation of the distal limbs. The response properties of cells in BA5 are more complex than in SI, with larger receptive fields and evidence of submodality convergence. BA5 also contributes to the corticospinal tract.
The areas of the inferior parietal lobule are reciprocally connected to adjacent areas in the intraparietal sulcus, the superior parietal lobule, the ventrolateral premotor and prefrontal cortex as well as posterior temporal and occipital areas. These connections are mediated by short U-fibres (Catani et al 2017) and long association fibres of the arcuate fasciculus, and superior and middle longitudinal fasciculi.
Functional anatomy of the superior and inferior parietal lobules
Studies of the regional and laminar distribution patterns of multiple neurotransmitter receptors supported the parcellations found by quantitative cytoarchitectonic observations. In SI, area 3b had the highest densities of GABAergic GABAA, cholinergic muscarinic M2, noradrenergic α2, and serotoninergic 5-HT2 receptors than BA1 and BA2 (see Fig. 32.51). Additionally, areas 3b and BA2 had higher concentrations of cholinergic muscarinic M1 receptors than areas 3a and BA1. In both the IPL and SPL, decreasing concentrations of glutamatergic AMPA receptors and increasing concentrations of serotonergic 5-HT1A and 5-HT2 receptors were observed along caudal-to-rostral gradients, respectively. Caudal as compared to rostral and middle areas of IPL were additionally characterized by higher concentrations of GABAergic GABAA and GABAB receptors and lower concentrations of benzodiazepine binding sites of the GABAA receptor, while middle areas as compared to rostral and caudal areas had higher concentrations of glutamatergic kainate and cholinergic nicotinic receptors (Caspers et al 2013a). In the SPL, rostral as compared to caudal areas additionally expressed higher concentrations of adrenergic α2 receptors and lower concentrations of cholinergic nicotinic receptors (Scheperjans et al 2005).

Occipital lobe

On the superolateral cerebral surface, the occipital lobe is situated posterior to a virtual line connecting the point of emergence of the parieto-occipital fissure on the superomedial border of the cerebral hemisphere with the incisura occipito-temporalis. On the medial surface, the occipital lobe is limited anteriorly by the parieto-occipital sulcus and by its prolongation towards the tentorium cerebelli. Along the inferior cerebral surface, the base of the occipital lobe is continuous with the base of the temporal lobe. The sulci and gyri of the occipital lobe, especially of its superolateral surface, exhibit a greater degree of anatomical variation than is seen in the other lobes. Regarding the major named sulci, there appears to be reasonable consensus that the parieto-occipital, calcarine, paracalcarine, lingual, transverse occipital, inferior lateral occipital and anterior occipital sulci, together with the occipital parts of the collateral and lateral occipitotemporal sulci, are constant features of the surface of the occipital lobe, whereas the superior occipital, superior lateral occipital, inferior occipital, lunate and occipitopolar sulci are less constant.
Occipital lobe sulci and gyri
There are two or three gyri (superior, middle and inferior) on the superolateral cerebral surface of the occipital lobe that converge posteriorly to form the occipital pole (see Figs 32.2–32.3). The superior occipital gyrus is always well defined, and is continuous along the superomedial margin of the hemisphere with the cuneus. Superiorly, it is delimited by the depth of the parieto-occipital fissure on the superolateral hemispheric surface. It is continuous with the SPL through the parieto-occipital ‘pli de passage’ of Gratiolet. Laterally, the superior occipital gyrus may be delimited by either the intra-occipital, transverse occipital or superior occipital sulci. The inferior occipital gyrus lies horizontally along the inferolateral margin of the hemisphere. Anteriorly, it is usually continuous with the inferior temporal gyrus; posteriorly, it can extend medially around the occipital pole, becoming continuous with the lingual gyrus on the medial surface of the hemisphere; superiorly, it is delimited by the lateral or inferior occipital sulcus. The superior lateral occipital sulcus is a horizontal sulcus. Anteriorly, it is frequently connected to the inferior temporal sulcus; inferiorly, it may be accompanied by a shorter inferior lateral occipital sulcus. Both of these sulci may be connected with a sulcal complex known as the anterior occipital sulcus, which, when present, lies along the anterior aspect of the middle occipital gyrus.
The intraparietal sulcus extends longitudinally and inferiorly into the occipital lobe, where it becomes the intra-occipital sulcus. The latter may occasionally descend to the occipital pole but it usually terminates on reaching the transverse occipital sulcus, dividing it into lateral and medial parts that penetrate the superior occipital gyrus. The lunate sulcus, although conspicuous in monkeys and apes, is only sometimes identifiable in human brains, when it appears as a well-defined vertical and backward-curved sulcus anterior to the occipital pole.
Despite significant anatomical variation, the superolateral occipital convolutions are connected to the parietal and temporal convolutions by consistent cortical folds. Four folds connect the parietal and temporal lobes with the occipital lobe: the superior parieto-occipital fold (parieto-occipital arcus) connects the SPL with the superior occipital gyrus; the inferior parieto-occipital fold, a posterior extension of the angular gyrus, connects with the middle occipital gyrus and occasionally also with the superior occipital gyrus; the first temporo-occipital fold is the continuation of the middle temporal gyrus with the inferior occipital gyrus; and the second temporo-occipital fold is the continuation of the inferior temporal gyrus with the inferior occipital gyrus.
The medial surface of the occipital lobe is separated from the parietal lobe by the parieto-occipital sulcus and dominated by the calcarine sulcus. The dorsal part of this region, above the calcarine sulcus and posterior to the parieto-occipital sulcus, is the cuneus. The ventral part of this region, lying below the calcarine sulcus and extending as far as the occipital extension of the collateral fissure, is the lingual gyrus. The calcarine sulcus starts anteriorly beneath the splenium of the corpus callosum, delineating the inferior aspect of the isthmus of the cingulate gyrus, and runs posteriorly just above the inferomedial margin of the hemisphere. The parieto-occipital sulcus separates the cuneus from the precuneus, and divides the calcarine sulcus into an anterior and a posterior part. The parieto-occipital and calcarine sulci appear continuous on the surface, but when their borders are retracted it becomes obvious that they are separated by one or more small gyri. The anterior part of the calcarine sulcus is classified as a complete sulcus because its depth creates an elevation (calcar avis) in the medial wall of the occipital horn of the lateral ventricle. The posterior part of the calcarine sulcus is considered an axial sulcus, given that its axis runs along the visual cortex. Only the posterior part includes the primary visual cortical areas, which are located on its superior (cuneal) and inferior (lingual) surfaces: this part of the calcarine sulcus frequently harbours the cuneolingual gyrus that links both gyri.
At the level of the occipital pole, the calcarine sulcus usually branches in a T or Y shape as the retrocalcarine sulcus. The gyrus descendens of Ecker lies posterior to and along the retrocalcarine sulcus and is occasionally bounded posteriorly by the occipitopolar sulcus. The retrocalcarine sulcus and its variations are sometimes referred to as external calcarine sulci.
Given the anatomical constancy of the calcarine and parieto-occipital sulci on the medial occipital surface, the cuneus is always a well-defined wedge-like convolution. The real anterior border of the cuneus is the cuneal limiting sulcus within the parieto-occipital fissure. Posteriorly, the cuneus rests over the posterior part of the calcarine sulcus and over the posterior aspect of the lingual gyrus. Superior to the posterior part of the calcarine sulcus, the cuneus harbours the paracalcarine or cuneal sulcus (the inferior sagittal sulcus of the cuneus of Retzius) and, further dorsally, the occipital paramedial sulcus (the paramesial sulcus of Elliot Smith or superior sagittal sulcus of Retzius). The basal or inferior surface of the occipital lobe is continuous with the basal surface of the temporal lobe. It is formed, from medial to lateral, by the lingual (also known as the medial occipito-temporal gyrus), fusiform (also known as the lateral occipito-temporal gyrus), and inferior occipital gyri respectively. The lingual gyrus lies inferiorly along the entire length of the calcarine sulcus, forming the mediobasal portion of the occipital lobe. It is continuous anteriorly with the parahippocampal gyrus and its basal surface rests on the tentorium cerebelli. Posteriorly, it is frequently divided into a superior and inferior part by an intralingual sulcus, which may be a posteromedial ramus of the collateral sulcus.
The fusiform gyrus lies along the temporo-occipital transition. Its posterior or occipital part is bounded medially by the collateral sulcus and laterally by the occipito-temporal sulcus; hence it lies between the lingual gyrus medially and the inferior occipital gyrus laterally. The occipital part of the fusiform gyrus lies over the tentorium cerebelli just posterior to the petrous part of the temporal bone. Topographically, it corresponds to the floor of the ventricular atrium; the temporal part of the gyrus lies beneath the temporal horn of the lateral ventricle. The posterior portion of the fusiform gyrus contains the midfusiform sulcus lying between the collateral and occipito-temporal sulci (Weiner and Zilles 2016). The midfusiform sulcus is of functional importance, because it points to cortical areas involved in object recognition, face perception and reading.
The occipitotemporal sulcus rarely extends posteriorly as far as the occipital pole; both the collateral and occipitotemporal sulci frequently have secondary side branches and merge anteriorly. The inferior or basal aspect of the inferior occipital gyrus lies lateral to the fusiform gyrus and constitutes the most inferior portion of the lateral aspect of the occipital lobe. Along the inferolateral border of the hemisphere, the inferior temporal gyrus is continuous with the inferior occipital gyrus over the occipito-temporal incisure (preoccipital notch), and the inferior occipital gyrus is continuous with the lingual gyrus along the occipital pole. Along the parietal and occipital aspects of the superomedial border of the hemisphere, the superior parietal lobule is continuous with the precuneus, and the superior occipital gyrus is continuous with the cuneus above the calcarine sulcus and with the lingual gyrus below the calcarine sulcus.
Occipital lobe internal structure and connectivity
The occipital lobe is composed of Brodmann’s areas BA17, BA18 and BA19 and part of BA37. However, Brodmann’s delineation of BA19 and BA37 has not been replicated using modern quantitative approaches and the whole region covered by these two areas has been re-defined (see Fig. 32.13).
BA17, the striate cortex, is the primary visual cortex (V1) and is located mostly on the medial aspect of the occipital lobe: it occupies the upper and lower lips and depths of the calcarine sulcus and extends onto the cuneus and lingual gyrus. V1 receives visual afferent fibres from the lateral geniculate nucleus via the optic radiations. The retina of each eye can be divided into a nasal (medial) and temporal (lateral) half, and the left and right visual fields of each eye are composed of a lateral and medial half, the latter partially overlapping in the midline. Each lateral visual field is perceived by the ipsilateral nasal retina and each medial visual field is perceived by the ipsilateral temporal retina. The fibres from the nasal retinae form the medial half of the optic nerve and decussate in the optic chiasma to form the optic tract with the temporal fibres of the contralateral retina. Projections of the ipsilateral and contralateral retinae reach the lateral geniculate nucleus where representations of the visual hemifields remain segregated in the magnocellular (layers 1–2) and parvocellular layers (layers 3–6). The fibres of the optic radiations originating from the lateral geniculate nucleus terminate in strict retinotopic fashion in the ipsilateral striate area. The cortex of each hemisphere receives impulses from two hemi-retinae, which represent the contralateral half of the binocular visual field. Superior and inferior retinal quadrants are connected with corresponding areas of the striate cortex. According to Elze (1928), Flechsig, in 1896, called attention to the peculiar course of the ventral fibres of the optic radiations, and named the sharp turn made by these fibres the ‘temporal knee’ (later referred to as Meyer’s loop). The superior retinal quadrants (representing the inferior half of the visual field) are connected with V1 above the calcarine sulcus, and the inferior retinal quadrants (representing the upper half of the visual field) are connected through the Meyer’s loop with the V1 below the calcarine sulcus. The peripheral nasal parts of the retinae (monocular periphery of the visual field) projects on the most anterior parts in the visual cortex near to the splenium of the corpus callosum: this is the small monocular part of V1. The fovea of the retina concerned with central vision impinges on a disproportionately large posterior part (binocular part of V1) around the occipital pole.
Area V1 is koniocortical (Fig. 32.57): in contrast to most of the isocortical areas where layer III is dominated by pyramidal cells, layers II/III of V1 is populated by small, densely packed granule cells. Layers II/III consist of excitatory pyramidal cells and receive massive inputs from layer IVc neurones but only a few thalamic inputs. They are also connected through reciprocal projections with layer V neurones. The neurones of layers II/III have cortico-cortical projections to the extrastriate regions V2, V3, V4 and V5. About 20% of the cells in layers II/III are GABAergic inhibitory interneurones. Layer IV of V1 is divided into three sublayers: IVa, IVb (which harbours the heavily myelinated stria of Gennari and contains a few cells, mainly interneurones), and IVc (Fig. 32.57B). Layer IV plays a major role in the M-pathway that originates from magnocellular (M) cells of the lateral geniculate nucleus. After receiving information through the M-pathway in layer IVcα, IVB projects to cytochrome oxidase-positive blobs (see below) in layers II/III, to areas V3 and V5/MT and the superior colliculus. The densely cellular IVc is further subdivided into a superficial IVcα and a deep IVcβ. The input to area 17 from the lateral geniculate nucleus terminates predominantly in layers IVa and IVc. Other thalamic afferents, from the inferior pulvinar nucleus and the intralaminar group, terminate in layers I and VI. Layer V neurones project primarily to III, provide strong feedback to layer VI neurones, and receive feedback via projections from II/III neurones. Very large pyramidal cells, Meynert cells, found at the V/VI border and within layer VI, project to both area MT and the superior colliculus.
The projections of the right and left retinae remain segregated in the lateral geniculate nucleus, where layers 2, 3 and 5 receive information from the ipsilateral eye, and layers 1, 4 and 6 from the contralateral eye. The magnocellular layers 1 and 2 of the lateral geniculate nucleus are important relay stations of the M-pathway from the retina to the visual cortex, whereas the parvocellular layers 3–6 are a relay station in the parallel P-pathway. The M-pathway mainly conveys motion information, the P-pathway information on shape and colour. The optic radiation consists of the geniculo-cortical projections to layer IV of V1, which terminate in alternating cell strips for each eye separately. Thus, the neurones within layer IV are monocularly driven. This horizontal, strip-like segregation is the anatomical basis of ocular dominance columns (Hubel and Wiesel 1977) (Fig. 32.58). Neurones encountered in vertical strips from pia to white matter exhibit a preference for stimulation of one or other eye, although binocularly driven neurones are found in the other layers of V1. In the human V1, ocular dominance columns are orientated vertical to the V1/V2 boundary and are about 0.5 mm wide with quite distinct functional borders.
Orientation columns in V1 are based on the finding that all neurones orientated in a column extending from pia to white matter and vertical to the cortical surface, respond preferentially to either a stationary or a moving line of a given orientation (see Fig. 32.58). Cells with simple, complex and hypercomplex receptive fields are found in V1. Simple cells are mainly in layer IV and respond optimally to lines in a narrowly defined position. Complex cells occur in layers II and III or layers V and VI, and respond to stimuli within a receptive field, but with a specific orientation. Hypercomplex cells are also found in layers II and III or layers V and VI: their response is similar to that of complex cells, but the length of a line is critical. The borders of orientation and ocular dominance columns intersect at right-angles.
Cytochrome oxidase (CO) blobs are found in V1 (see Fig. 32.58). They are regions of high staining for the metabolic enzyme cytochrome oxidase (Wong-Riley 1989) in layers II/III and V/VI. Interleaved ‘interblob’ regions form discrete structures between CO blobs. Layer II/III neurones in the CO blob region receive direct affrents from the interlaminar cells of LGN (I-pathway). Although CO blobs have often been associated with colour vision, their function remains elusive. Direct connections from the blob areas of V1 pass to V4. Output from V1 terminates in the claustrum, lateral geniculate nucleus, superior colliculus, pretectum and parts of the brainstem reticular formation; efferents to the striatum and pontine nuclei are sparse.
The human extrastriate visual cortex covers the remainder of the occipital lobe and extends into the temporal and parietal lobes, which means that about 20% of the entire cerebral cortex is covered by the visual system.
Area V2, which overlaps in part with BA18, contains a complete retinotopic representation of the visual hemifield, which is a mirror image of that in V1, with the vertical meridian along the border between areas V1 and V2. Within area V2 (Fig. 32.57H–N), functional modules called thin stripes, thick stripes, and interstripes are present. The blob areas of V1 project to the colour-sensitive neurones of the thin stripes, and those of the interblob regions of V1 terminate in the interstripe regions of V2. The thin stripes of V2 project to V4. The thick stripes of V2 receive input from layer IVb of V1. The neurones in the thick stripes are orientation- and direction-selective, and are also sensitive to binocular disparity, which allows them to code depth information. Reciprocal projections from V2 pass to several other visual areas, including V3 and its subdivisions (V3/V3d, VP/V3v, V3a; see Fig. 32.57O–Q), V4, V5 and areas in the temporal and parietal association cortices and frontal eye field. Afferents to V2 arrive from the lateral geniculate nucleus, inferior and lateral pulvinar nuclei and parts of the intralaminar thalamic nuclei. Subcortical efferents of V2 arise in layers V and VI and pass to the striatum, thalamus, claustrum, superior colliculus, pretectum, brainstem reticular formation, and pons. The reciprocal callosal connections of V2 are restricted to regions with the representation of the vertical meridian.
Area V3 (see Fig. 32.57O–Q) is a narrow strip adjoining the anterior margin of V2 and has been subdivided into two dorsal (V3/V3d; V3A) and a ventral (VP/V3v) regions on the basis of its afferents from area V1, myelo- and receptor architecture, and callosal and receptive field properties. V3d receives input from V1, whereas V3v does not. V3d shows less colour selectivity and greater directional selectivity than V3v. Both areas receive projections from V2 and are interconnected. A further visual area, area V3a, lies anterior to V3d and receives afferents from V1, V2, V3d and V3v. All V3 subdivisions project to diverse visual areas in the parietal, occipital and temporal cortices, and to the frontal eye field.
Area V4 lies within BA19 anterior to V3 (see Fig. 32.57O–Q). It receives afferents from V2, and is colour- and orientation-selective. Bilateral damage, e.g. caused by localized ischaemia, leads to achromatopsia. V4 is also involved in orientation, form and movement selectivity. It projects reciprocally to the inferior temporal cortex and also connects with other visual areas in the temporal and parietal lobes and with the pulvinar and intralaminar thalamic nuclei. Visual processing in the inferior temporal and temporoparietal lobes is realized via the dorsal and ventral visual streams that emanate from the occipital lobe. The dorsal pathway, concerned primarily with visuospatial discrimination, projects from V1 and V2 to the temporal and parietal cortices and enables sensory-motor transformations for visually guided actions directed at an object. V4 is an important relay station for the ventral stream, which is related to the perceptual identification of various classes of objects (see below). It is connected along the inferior temporal gyrus with the posterior, intermediate and anterior parts of the inferior temporal cortex, which feed into the temporal polar and medial temporal areas of the language and limbic networks.
The motion-selective area V5 (also called hMT+) (see Fig. 32.57O–Q) is located close to the junction of the occipital, temporal and parietal lobes and can be identified by its intense myelination. It receives input from layer IVb of V1 and the thick stripes of V2, forming a further M-pathway. Its cells are direction- and motion-sensitive. It is surrounded by the areas MST and FST that process higher-order aspects of stimulus motion. Bilateral lesions of V5 cause akinetopsia, a severe impairment in detecting the movements of objects.
Higher-order visual areas, selective for specific objects such as faces, words or buildings, are localized lateral to the collateral sulcus in the fusiform gyrus around the midfusiform sulcus. Best known is the posterior face recognition area. Lesions of this area cause prosopagnosia, the inability to recognize faces. Recognition of faces, numbers and words are associated with a central visual field bias, whereas the recognition of buildings depends on peripheral vision. Several cyto- and receptor architectonic areas (FG1–FG4) of the fusiform gyrus have been identified (Caspers et al 2013b, Lorenz et al 2017). FG1 and FG3 are medial to the midfusiform sulcus, and FG2 and FG4 are lateral to this sulcus on the fusiform gyrus (Fig. 32.59). Combined functional and cytoarchitectonic studies have shown that these areas subserve face, word form (characters), place and object (body) recognition (Weiner et al 2017). FG1 corresponds to the face-selective area in the posterior fusiform gyrus (area pFus-faces); FG2 to the posterior visual word form area (VWFA-1); FG3 to the parahippocampal place area (PPA); and FG4 to the medial face-selective area (mFus-faces), fusiform body area (FBA) and anterior visual word form area (VWFA-2).

Temporal lobe

The lateral surface of the temporal lobe is inferior to the lateral fissure and lies beneath the squamous portion of the temporal bone. The basal surface lies over the floor of the middle cranial fossa posterior to the greater wing of the sphenoid bone. The opercular surface lies inside the lateral fissure. (For the definition of the posterior limit of the temporal lobe, see the occipital lobe). The isocortical part of the temporal lobe is related with auditory functions and, in the dominant hemisphere, mostly with language. It also comprises parts of the visual system in the inferior temporal region (higher visual areas). The medial side of the temporal lobe belongs to the limbic system (see archicortex and periarchicortex).
Temporal lobe sulci and gyri
The lateral surface is composed of the superior, middle and inferior temporal gyri, separated by the superior and inferior temporal sulci, all lying parallel to the lateral fissure. Anteriorly, the middle temporal gyrus is generally shorter, and the superior and inferior gyri fuse to form the temporal pole (see Figs 32.2–32.3).
The superior temporal sulcus terminates within the inferior parietal lobule, posterior to the end of the lateral fissure, by branching into an ascending sulcal segment and an inferior branch that runs towards the occipital lobe. The superior temporal gyrus continues posteriorly to the supramarginal gyrus encircling the terminal portion of the lateral fissure. The middle temporal gyrus is connected to the angular gyrus beneath the distal and horizontal portion of the superior temporal sulcus. The inferior temporal gyrus is continuous posteriorly with the inferior occipital gyrus over the preoccipital notch (occipito-temporal incisure). Both superior and inferior temporal sulci start at the most anterior aspect of the temporal pole and end posterior to the arbitrary border of the temporal lobe, and both have their depths directed towards the temporal horn of the lateral ventricle. Topographically, the depth of the posterior part of the superior temporal sulcus is particularly related to the ventricular atrium.
The basal surface of the temporal lobe is composed laterally by the inferior surface of the inferior temporal gyrus and medially by the anterior or temporal portion of the fusiform or lateral temporo-occipital gyrus; the gyri are separated by the temporo-occipital sulcus. Medially, the fusiform gyrus is delimited by the collateral sulcus, which separates it from the parahippocampal gyrus. Although not extensive, the fusiform gyrus has an anterior or temporal part between the inferior temporal and parahippocampal gyri, and a posterior or occipital part between the inferior occipital and lingual gyri. The anterior part of the fusiform gyrus is typically curved or pointed, resembling an arrow; its anterior border usually lies close to the level of the cerebral peduncle. The temporal portion of the fusiform gyrus lies over the posterior aspect of the floor of the middle fossa and the upper surface of the petrous part of the temporal bone. The occipital part of the fusiform gyrus lies underneath the ventricular atrium.
Anterior to the fusiform gyrus, the collateral sulcus may be continuous with the rhinal sulcus. More frequently, these sulci are separated by the so-called temporolimbic passage. The rhinal sulcus separates the entorhinal cortex of the uncus medially from the isocortex of the temporal pole laterally.
The superior or opercular surface of the temporal lobe is formed by the superior surface of the superior temporal gyrus, which lies within the lateral fissure and is composed of multiple transverse gyri, one or two of which are the transverse gyrus/gyri of Heschl. The opercular surface originates around the midpoint of the superior temporal gyrus and is orientated diagonally towards the posterior vertex of the floor of the lateral fissure, with its longest axis orientated towards the ventricular atrium. Heschl’s gyrus/gyri is/are bounded anteriorly by the first transverse sulcus and posteriorly by the second transverse sulcus (sulcus of Heschl), and divide the temporal opercular surface into an anterior polar plane, and a posterior temporal plane.
The surface of the polar plane shows several small transverse gyri directed obliquely towards the inferior part of the insular circular sulcus (inferior limiting sulcus) (see Fig. 32.2). The temporal plane is flat and triangular in shape and is usually larger in the dominant hemisphere, supposedly reflecting its association with language functions. Heschl’s gyrus underlies the opercular surface of the postcentral gyrus, and the flat surface of the temporal plane underlies the opercular surface of the supramarginal gyrus.
Temporal lobe internal structure and connectivity
The superior, middle and inferior temporal gyri correspond to BA22, BA21 and BA20, respectively, and the temporal pole corresponds to BA38 (see Fig. 32.13).
The primary auditory cortex (BA41 or A1) covers a major part of Heschl’s gyrus/gyri, and is surrounded by the secondary auditory cortex (BA42). BA41 corresponds to the functionally and connectionally defined core region of the primary auditory cortex, and BA42 to the belt region. BA41 is reciprocally connected with the medial geniculate nucleus via the acoustic radiation. The ventral division of the medial geniculate nucleus mainly projects to BA41, which receives additional projections from the medial pulvinar. The architectonically defined primary auditory cortex does not extend medially into the insular cortex, and does not reach the lateral bulge of the superior temporal gyrus. Posteriorly, it never extends beyond the second transverse temporal gyrus although Heschl’s sulcus does not indicate precisely the border between BA41 and BA42.
The primary auditory cortex is koniocortical, like parietal area 3b and occipital area V1 (Fig. 32.60). Layer III mainly contains small to medium-sized pyramidal cells arranged in radial columns that extend partially into the neighbouring layers: this arrangement is known as the ‘rain shower formation’ of the primary auditory cortex (von Economo and Koskinas 1925). The geniculocortical fibres from the medial geniculate nucleus terminate densely in layer IV (Fig. 32.61). Te1 is also characterized by a very high density of cholinergic muscarinic M2, GABAergic GABAA, and noradrenergic α2 receptors. The serotoninergic 5-HT2 receptor has a slightly higher density in Te1. A high density of these receptors also occurs in the primary somatosensory (area 3b) and primary visual (BA17) areas, whereas the noradrenergic α1 receptor has a distinctly low density in the deeper layers of Te1 (see Fig. 32.61E–H). Dense myelination and strong expression of acetylcholine esterase and cytochrome oxidase are further characteristics of the primary auditory cortex. The neurones within the primary auditory cortex are spatially organized according to their response to sounds of different frequency (tonotopy) or intensity (ampliotopy) (Talavage et al 2004, Schreiner and Malone 2015). Using observer-independent cytoarchitectonic and multimodal parcellation methods, the primary auditory cortex (indicated as Te1) can be further subdivided into areas Te1.0, Te1.1 and Te1.2. Whether and how tonotopic fields relate to the architectonic subdivisions of Te1 is not known.

The secondary auditory area Te2 (BA42) is found anteriorly, laterally and caudally to Te1, and occupies the largest portion (>90%) of the temporal plane. Te2 does not extend onto the insular cortex medially nor does it extend on the convexity of the superior temporal gyrus laterally. The medial and the lateral borders are not precisely associated with sulcal landmarks. The koniocortical structure of Te1 disappears in Te2. The packing density of neurones in layer V increases from Heschl’s gyrus through the planum temporale to the superior temporal gyrus. The pyramidal cells in layer III of Te2 are also arranged in vertical columns, referred to by von Economo and Koskinas (1925) as the ‘organ pipe formation’. GABAB, α1 and 5-HT1A receptors show higher densities in Te2 than in Te1.
The temporo-insular area TI differs from Te2: it has prominent and cell-dense infragranular layers, and there are differences in the regional and laminar receptor densities (see Fig. 32.61E–H). The border between the transitional area TI and the insular cortex can be defined on the basis of cytoarchitectonic differences and the distribution of cholinergic muscarinic M2 receptor.
The auditory area Te3 occupies approximately the posterior two-thirds of the free surface of the superior temporal gyrus. Thus, Te3 corresponds to large parts of BA22, and is part of the auditory belt. It has a high density of IIIc pyramidal cells of prominent size and a rather cell-dense layer V. The layer VI/white matter boundary is less sharp than in Te2. Compared to Te2, Te3 has lower densities of M2, and also slightly lower densities of M1, NMDA and GABAA receptors. Compared to the areas (Te4 and Te5; see Fig. 32.61) located ventrally in the upper bank of the superior temporal sulcus, Te3 has lower densities of α1, 5-HT1A, and AMPA receptors, particularly in the infragranular layers. The secondary and higher areas of the auditory cortex are involved in the perception of complex sounds, including frequency-modulated tones, harmonic complexes, amplitude modulations, spectrally dynamic sounds and sound sequences.
Direct corticofugal projections link the auditory cortex with the medial geniculate nucleus and the inferior colliculus. The auditory cortex interconnects with the prefrontal cortex, and posterior parts of the operculum project to areas 8 and 9. Central parts project to areas 8, 9 and 46. More anterior regions project to areas 9 and 46, to area 12 on the latero-orbital surface of the hemisphere, and to the anterior cingulate gyrus on the medial surface. Callosal connections are with the same and adjacent regions in the other hemisphere.
The functionally defined Wernicke’s area is not clearly delineated anatomically, but corresponds to BA22 in the middle and posterior third of the superior temporal gyrus. It has a prominent role in comprehension of language, in particular sentences, syntactic processing and auditory working memory.
Temporal lobe areas connect via a ‘dorsal’ or a ‘ventral’ pathway with separate regions of the frontal lobe. The ‘dorsal’ pathway connects posterior temporal areas with the posterior dorsolateral prefrontal cortex; the ‘ventral’ pathway terminates in the anterior prefrontal cortex. In this context, the arcuate fascicle and the extreme capsule are particularly important for the impairment in Wernicke’s aphasia. Considering that the dorsal and ventral prefrontal regions are characterized by spatial and non-spatial functional domains, it has been proposed that auditory information is channelled into at least two parallel processing streams: a dorsal or ‘where’ stream that subserves spatial localization, and a ventral or ‘what’ stream that subserves auditory object or pattern recognition. It has been shown that the anterior belt is responsive to the spectrotemporal features of a sound that code object identity, whereas neurones in the caudal belt are more sensitive to the spatial properties of a sound. It thus appears that object and spatial information is transmitted in different pathways beyond the auditory cortex.
BA21 on the middle temporal cortex is multimodal. It establishes reciprocal connections with early auditory, somatosensory and visual areas and connects to frontal areas according to an anterior–posterior gradient: posterior BA21 with BA8 and BA9, intermediate BA21 with BA9 and BA46, and anterior BA21 with BA10, BA46 and fronto-orbital areas. BA21 also projects to the temporal pole and the entorhinal cortex. Thalamic connections are with the pulvinar nuclei and the intralaminar group. Other subcortical connections with the ventral tegmental area, locus coeruleus and raphe nuclei follow the general pattern for all cortical areas.
The inferior temporal cortex, area 20, is, to a large extent, a higher visual association area. The posterior inferior temporal cortex receives major ipsilateral corticocortical fibres from V4. It contains a coarse retinotopic representation of the contralateral visual field and sends a major feed-forward pathway to the anterior part of the inferior temporal cortex. The anterior part of BA20 projects onto the temporal pole and the medial surface of the temporal lobe. Additional ipsilateral connections are with the parieto-temporal region. BA20 is also connected to BA46, the fronto-orbital cortex and the frontal eye field. Reciprocal thalamic connections are with the pulvinar and the intralaminar nuclei. Callosal connections are between corresponding areas and the adjacent higher visual areas of each hemisphere.
The cortex of the temporal pole receives feed-forward projections from widespread areas of temporal association cortex that are immediately posterior to it. The dorsal part receives predominantly auditory input from the anterior part of the superior temporal gyrus. The inferior part receives visual input from the anterior area of the inferior temporal cortex. Other ipsilateral connections are with the anterior insular, posterior and medial orbitofrontal, and medial prefrontal cortices. The temporal pole projects onwards into limbic and paralimbic areas. Thalamic connections are mainly with the medial pulvinar and with intralaminar and midline nuclei. Physiological responses of cells in this and more medial temporal cortex correspond particularly to behavioural performance and to the recognition of high-level aspects of social stimuli. Bilateral lesions of the temporopolar cortex cause retrograde amnesia (loss of all remote memories, but sparing learning and recent memory). Patients with these lesions suffer from a loss of autobiographical memories and the ability to recognize faces and names. The awareness of consolidated recognition of objects thus seems to occur in the temporo-polar cortex.

Insular lobe

The roughly triangular-shaped insular lobe (island of Reil) forms the base of the Sylvian cistern and lies between the frontal and temporal lobes (see Fig. 32.1). It is hidden in the depths of the lateral sulcus and covered by the opercular parts of the frontal, parietal and temporal lobes in the adult human brain.
The insular cortex subserves an extremely wide array of functions. It comprises such diverse functions such as olfaction, gustation, interoception, vestibular functions, general somatosensation, pain, cognition, attention, working memory, emotion including empathy, willed action, addictive behaviour and language. The insula seems to play a major role as a convergence zone and is implicated in the coordination between internal and external information through emotional subjective awareness. If the various functions are classified as larger functional domains, the sensorimotor domain is in the midposterior insula and forms the region posterior to the most posterior gyri breves; the cognitive domain is in the anterior-dorsal part of anterior and middle short gyri; the olfacto-gustatory domain is on the right middle insular gyrus; and the emotional domain is in the anterior-ventral part around the insular pole (Fig. 32.62).
Insular lobe sulci and gyri
The insular surface is delineated peripherally by the circular sulcus of Reil, which is interrupted by the transverse insular gyrus running across the limen insulae. The anterior surface of the insula is covered by the fronto-orbital operculum (the posterior portion of the posterior orbital gyrus and the orbital part of the inferior frontal gyrus). Its lateral surface is covered superiorly by the frontoparietal operculum (triangular and opercular parts of the inferior frontal gyrus, subcentral gyrus, and anterior and basal part of the supramarginal gyrus) and inferiorly by the temporal operculum (polar plane of the superior temporal gyrus). Its anteroinferior vertex is the limen insulae, a narrow strip of olfactory cortex that extends along the lateral aspect of the lateral olfactory stria, conjoining the insular cortex and anterior perforate substance. The surface of the insula is divided into anterior and posterior parts by a deep central insular sulcus that courses obliquely from the limen insulae towards the central sulcus between frontal and parietal lobes. The anterior part of the insular surface is composed of short insular gyri (anterior, middle and posterior short insular gyri). The transverse insular gyrus runs along the limen insulae, connecting the anterior insula with the posteromedial part of the fronto-polar cortex. The latter is the connection between the posterior portion of the medial orbital gyrus and the posterior orbital gyrus, and is located anterior to and along the lateral olfactory stria. The accessory gyrus extends from the anterior portion of the anterior short gyrus superiorly to the transverse insular gyrus, beneath the fronto-orbital operculum. Both gyri constitute the insular pole within the anterior aspect of the insula. The posterior part of the insula is located behind its central sulcus and is composed of anterior and posterior insular long gyri. Both of these gyri are separated by the postcentral insular sulcus; the anterior long gyrus is usually larger, and may be single or divided at its upper end (see Fig. 32.62).
Insular lobe internal structure and connectivity
Cytoarchitectonically, four major zones are recognized within the insula. Starting from an allocortical small centre, a peri- to pro-isocortical semicircular zone is surrounded by a dysgranular to eulaminate isocortical zone. Most posteriorly, a relatively small granular isocortical zone follows to the caudal limit of the insula (see Fig. 32.62).
Thalamic afferents to the insula come from subdivisions of the ventral posterior nucleus and of the medial geniculate nucleus, from the oral and medial parts of the pulvinar, the suprageniculate/nucleus limitans complex, the mediodorsal nucleus and the nuclei of the intralaminar and midline groups. It appears that the anterior (agranular) cortex is connected predominantly with the mediodorsal and ventroposterior nuclei, while the posterior (granular) cortex is connected predominantly with the pulvinar and the ventral posterior nuclei.
Ipsilateral cortical connections of the insula are diverse. There are somatosensory connections with S1, S2 and surrounding areas, and the anterior part of the superior parietal lobule. The insular cortex also has connections with the orbitofrontal cortex, and several auditory regions in the temporal lobe interconnect with the posterior granular and dysgranular insular cortex. Connections with visual areas are virtually absent. The periallo- and allocortical insula has connections primarily with olfactory, limbic and paralimbic structures, including, most prominently, the amygdala.

White Matter of the Cerebral Hemispheres

The white matter of the cerebral hemispheres is composed of long myelinated axons grouped into larger white matter tracts (fasciculi). According to their origin, course and termination, white matter tracts can be classified into association, commissural or projection pathways (Fig. 32.63). The course of the large white matter tracts of the human brain can be studied using postmortem blunt dissections of brains prepared using Klingler’s technique or in vivo with diffusion imaging tractography (Video 32.1).

Association tracts

Association tracts connect cortical areas in the same hemisphere and can be divided into short U-fibres linking adjacent gyri and long association tracts connecting distant gyri (Fig. 32.64). Most of the U-shaped fibres connect gyri within the same lobe (intralobar) whereas most of the long association fibres connect gyri of different lobes (interlobar). There is a relationship between the length of the fibres and the depth of their course within the white matter of the cerebral hemispheres: short U-fibres run more superficially, closer to the cortex of the gyri and sulci, whereas longer fibres group together deeper in the white matter of each gyrus and in the centrum semiovale. Association tracts are composed of both feed-forward and feed-back fibres that enable rapid feed-forward propagation of sensory input and top-down modulation of sensory processing.
Most of the intralobar tracts have been recently described and classified for the first time in humans with diffusion tractography.
In the frontal lobe, the frontal aslant tract is a short intralobar connection between the regions of the superior medial frontal lobe (supplementary and presupplementary motor areas, medial prefrontal cortex) and Broca’s region (see Fig. 32.64; Fig. 32.65). The fronto-orbitopolar tract connects the posterior orbitofrontal, olfactory cortex and nucleus accumbens with the anterior orbitofrontal cortex and inferior frontal pole regions (see Fig. 32.64; Fig. 32.66). The superior and inferior frontal longitudinal fasciculi run underneath the superior and inferior frontal sulci, respectively and connect the posterior premotor cortex to the anterior prefrontal cortex (Fig. 32.67) (Catani et al 2012). The hand-knob region in the precentral gyrus is particularly rich in short U-fibres that are concentrically arranged around the origin of the corticospinal projections that are directed to the cervical spinal cord controlling finger movements (Fig. 32.68). The frontomarginal tract runs underneath the frontomarginal sulcus and connects medial and lateral frontal pole regions (see Fig. 32.66). The fronto-insular tracts are U-fibres connecting different areas of the inferior frontal and precentral gyri to the anterior gyri of the insula (Fig. 32.69). In the parietal lobe, short intralobar tracts connect the superior and inferior parietal lobules (intraparietal tract) and the post-central gyrus to the inferior parietal lobule (Fig. 32.70) (Catani et al 2017). In the temporal lobe, the temporal longitudinal fasciculus runs along the white matter of the superior, middle and inferior temporal gyri and connects anterior to middle and posterior temporal regions (Fig. 32.71). In the occipital lobe, the vertical occipital bundle of Wernicke links the regions of the superior, middle and inferior occipital gyri whereas the medial occipital longitudinal tract connects early visual areas to the parahippocampal place area (Figs 32.72–32.73).
Long interlobar association tracts have been originally described using postmortem blunt dissections in humans and axonal tracing studies in non-human primates. They include the arcuate fasciculus, superior, middle and inferior longitudinal fasciculi, inferior fronto-occipital fasciculus, uncinate fasciculus and cingulum. Individual variability in both the three-dimensional topography and size of these tracts has been reported in the healthy population, especially between hemispheres (interhemispheric asymmetry) or between male and female brains. The terminology used to indicate the association tracts often refers to their shape (e.g. the uncinate from the Latin uncinatus meaning ‘hook-like’), their origin and termination (e.g. inferior fronto-occipital fasciculus), or their course, location and orientation within the cerebral hemispheres (e.g. inferior longitudinal fasciculus).

Arcuate fasciculus

The arcuate fasciculus is a dorsal association tract connecting perisylvian regions of the frontal, parietal and temporal lobe (see Fig. 32.64). In humans, two parallel pathways have been distinguished within the arcuate fasciculus (Catani et al 2005) (Fig. 32.74). The medial direct pathway (i.e. the arcuate fasciculus sensu strictu or direct long segment) connects Wernicke’s region in the temporal lobe (BA41, 42, 22, 37) with Broca’s region in the frontal lobe (BA6, 44, 45). The indirect pathway runs more laterally compared to the direct pathway and is composed of two separate segments: the anterior segment links Broca’s region in the frontal lobe to Geschwind’s region in the inferior parietal lobule (BA39, 40); the posterior segment links Geschwind’s to Wernicke’s region (Catani et al 2005). The direct long segment of the arcuate fasciculus is larger on the left hemisphere compared to the right in about 80% of the population; the remaining 20% shows a bilateral distribution (Catani et al 2007) that is more prevalent in the female population and it has been associated with better recovery of language after a left hemisphere stroke (Fig. 32.75) (Forkel et al 2014). The arcuate fasciculus is important for language, social communication, working memory and complex limb motor coordination (praxis).

Superior longitudinal fasciculus

The superior longitudinal fasciculus (SLF) has three distinct branches as originally described in the monkey brain using axonal tracing methods (Petrides and Pandya 1984). In humans (see Fig. 32.64) (Thiebaut de Schotten et al 2011), the first branch of the superior longitudinal fasciculus (SLF I) connects the superior parietal lobule and precuneus (BA5 and 7) to the superior frontal (BA8, 9, 32) and perhaps to some anterior cingulate areas (BA24). The SLF I processes the spatial coordinates of trunk and lower limbs and contributes to the preparatory stages of movement planning (e.g. anticipation) (Leiguarda and Marsden 2000), oculomotor coordination (Anderson et al 2012), visual reaching (Johnson et al 1996), and voluntary orientation of attention (Corbetta and Shulman 2002). The SLF I is bilateral and there are lateralization differences between left- and right-handers (rightward lateralized in left-handers) (Howells et al 2018).
The second branch of the superior longitudinal fasciculus (SLF II) projects to the posterior region of the inferior parietal lobule (including the cortex buried in the intraparietal sulcus) and the lateral aspect of the superior (i.e. frontal eye field) and middle frontal gyri (BA6, 8, 9). The SLF II is involved in processing the spatial coordinates of the upper limbs, including the hand for reaching and grasping movements (Leiguarda and Marsden 2000, Goldenberg and Spatt 2009), and contributes to tasks requiring attention, visuospatial and spatial working memory (Levy and Goldman-Rakic 2000, Corbetta and Shulman 2002, Thiebaut de Schotten et al 2005). In humans the SLF II is right lateralized in most, but not all, subjects (about 70%) and the degree of lateralization is correlated with asymmetry in behavioural performances for visuospatial tasks (Thiebaut de Schotten et al 2011). This finding indicates that a right hemispheric specialization for visuospatial attention could be related to an unbalanced speed of signal processing along the SLF II of the two hemispheres.
The most ventral branch of the superior longitudinal fasciculus (SLF III) projects to the inferior parietal lobule (supramarginal and anterior angular gyri) and the posterior region of the inferior frontal gyrus and corresponds to the anterior segment of the arcuate fasciculus. In humans, this branch is almost always right lateralized.

Inferior longitudinal fasciculus

The inferior longitudinal fasciculus (ILF) is an occipito-temporal tract that connects early extrastriate visual cortex with the amygdala, parahippocampal gyrus and temporal pole (see Fig. 32.64). Its fibres run lateral to the optic radiations and inferior to the temporal horn. The inferior longitudinal fasciculus carries visual information from occipital areas to the temporal lobe and plays an important role in visual object and face recognition, language, semantic processing and reading (Epelbaum et al 2008, Fox et al 2008, Ffytche et al 2010).

Inferior fronto-occipital fasciculus

The inferior fronto-occipital fasciculus is a bow-tie-shaped bundle connecting medial occipital regions to the prefrontal regions and orbitofrontal cortex. The fibres of the inferior fronto-occipital fasciculus compact in the anterior floor of the external/extreme capsule where they run parallel to the more inferior fibres of the uncinate fasciculus (see Fig. 32.64) (Forkel et al 2014). Within the temporal lobe, the inferior fronto-occipital fasciculus contributes to the temporal stem and covers the temporal horn of the lateral ventricle and the ventricular atrium superiorly and laterally (Türe et al 2000). The functions of the inferior fronto-occipital fasciculus remain largely unknown. The occipital areas connected by the inferior fronto-occipital fasciculus include the lingual gyrus and cuneus, which engage in tasks requiring retrieving information from visual memory, spatial attention and recognition of colours, letters and faces. The anterior projections of the inferior fronto-occipital fasciculus terminate in the medial and lateral orbitofrontal cortex, which activates in tasks requiring top-down modulation (either activation or inhibition) of visual perception and short-term memory. These frontal regions seem especially important in guiding decision-making and social behaviour in relation to the emotional value of external sensory stimuli (Rudrauf et al 2008).

Uncinate fasciculus

The uncinate fasciculus is a hook-shaped bundle that connects the anterior temporal lobe, including the amygdala, with the orbitofrontal region and inferior frontal gyrus (see Fig. 32.64). From the temporal lobe, the uncinate fasciculus enters the anterior floor of the external/extreme capsule between the insula and the putamen. Here, it runs inferiorly to the fronto-occipital fasciculus before splitting into a lateral branch which terminates in the lateral orbito-frontal cortex and inferior frontal gyrus (BA11 and 47), and a medial branch which projects to the medial orbitofrontal cortex, gyrus rectus and subcallosal area (Crosby et al 1962). The uncinate fasciculus plays an important role in lexical retrieval, semantic association, naming and social cognition (Craig et al 2009, Papagno et al 2011, Wilson et al 2011, Catani et al 2013). In addition to language and memory, it is also involved in several aspects of behaviour, including response inhibition, outcome monitoring, object–reward association learning and multimodal sensory integration, especially for olfactory–gustatory–visceral functions (Catani et al 2013). Damage to this tract has been correlated with the severity of antisocial traits in psychopaths (Craig et al 2009) and behavioural symptoms in frontotemporal dementia (D’Anna et al 2016) and traumatic brain injury (Zappalà et al 2012).

Middle longitudinal fasciculus

The middle longitudinal fasciculus runs in the white matter within the superior temporal gyrus, connecting the angular gyrus (area 39) with the superior temporal gyrus (area 22) up to the temporal pole (area 38) (see Fig. 32.71) (Makris and Pandya 2009). It lies medial and ventral to branch II of the superior longitudinal fasciculus and the arcuate fasciculus; lateral and superior to the inferior fronto-occipital fasciculus; and dorsal to the inferior longitudinal fasciculus. Its function is still unknown.

Cingulum

The cingulum lies within the depth of the cingulate and parahippocampal gyri, starting below the rostrum of the corpus callosum within the paraolfactory gyri (see Fig. 32.64). It contains fibres of different length; the longest run from the amygdala, uncus (BA35) and parahippocampal cortex (BA36 and 30) to sub-genual areas of the orbito-frontal lobe (BA25 and 11) (Crosby et al 1962, Nieuwenhuys et al 2008, Catani, Dell’acqua et al 2013). Shorter fibres, that join and leave the cingulum along its length, connect to adjacent areas of the cingulate cortex (BA23 and 24), superior medial frontal gyrus (BA32, 6, 8 and 9), paracentral lobule (BA4), precuneus (BA7), cuneus (BA19), lingual (BA18 and 19), and fusiform gyri (BA19 and 37). The cingulum can be divided into a dorsal and a ventral component (Catani and Thiebaut de Schotten 2012, Jones et al 2013). The dorsal component connects those areas in the anterior cingulate/medial frontal cortex and posterior cingulate/precuneus that form the dorsomedial default-mode network (Raichle et al 2001, Raichle and Snyder 2007, Raichle 2015). The activity of these regions decreases in the transition between a ‘resting state’ and the execution of goal-directed tasks, irrespective of the nature of the task. The default-mode network has been linked to a number of functions including working memory, focusing attention to sensory-driven activities, understanding other people’s intention (mentalizing or theory of mind), prospective thinking (envisioning the future) and memory for personal events (autobiographic memory) (see cingulate cortex). The ventral cingulum connects the amygdala and anterior parahippocampal cortex to retrosplenial regions and posterior parahippocampal areas and forms a network dedicated to spatial orientation and memory for navigation (Aggleton 2008, Vann et al 2009).

Commissural fibres

Commissural fibres originate in one hemisphere, cross the midline and project to the contralateral hemisphere. Homotopic fibres interconnect corresponding cortical areas in the two hemispheres and represent the most prevalent type of commissural connections. Heterotopic commissural fibres connect a cortical area with non-corresponding areas in the contralateral hemisphere. The largest commissural tracts of the human brain are the corpus callosum and the anterior commissure.

Corpus callosum

The corpus callosum contains 150–200 million fibres and is the largest fibre pathway of the human brain. On a midsagittal section, it forms an anteroposterior midline arch approximately 10 cm long that early anatomists subdivided into rostrum, genu, trunk (or body), isthmus and splenium. Each division of the corpus callosum contains fibres that project to specific cortical regions (Fig. 32.76).
The rostrum corresponds to the floor of the lateral ventricle and its fibres connect the orbitofrontal cortex. The genu is the most anterior portion of the corpus callosum and forms the anterior wall of the frontal horn of the lateral ventricle. Fibres in the genu curve forwards, as the forceps minor, to connect the areas of the prefrontal cortex. The body forms the roof and lateral wall of the lateral ventricles. Its fibres pass laterally, intersecting with the projection fibres of the corona radiata (see Fig. 32.76B, Video 32.1) to connect premotor and motor areas of the frontal lobe and most areas of the parietal lobe. The isthmus corresponds to a narrow portion of the corpus callosum between the body and the splenium. The fibres of the isthmus connect auditory temporal cortices.
The splenium of the corpus callosum overhangs the posterior ends of the thalami, the fornix, the pineal gland and tectum. A superior and an inferior layer of tela choroidea advance below the splenium through the transverse fissure, forming the velum interpositum cistern within the roof of the third ventricle and between the thalami, just below the bodies of the fornices; it contains the distal branches of the medial posterior choroidal arteries and the internal cerebral veins. The majority of the fibres of the splenium curve back into the occipital lobes as the forceps major. Some fibres of the splenium and isthmus arch forwards along the lateral wall of the inferior horn of the lateral ventricle, and constitute the tapetum. Acquired callosal lesions may manifest with disconnection syndromes characterized by the inability to transfer information or competences from one hemisphere to the other. The technique of severing commissural fibres in epileptic patients (split-brain) was introduced in the 1930s to control the spread of ictal discharge from one hemisphere to the other. Split-brain patients provided an opportunity to understand the functions of the corpus callosum and lateralization of cerebral functions (i.e. left cerebral dominance for language and right dominance for visuospatial functions).

Anterior commissure

The anterior commissure runs within the basal forebrain and fans out laterally within the temporal lobe (Fig. 32.77). It is believed to connect areas that include the olfactory bulb and anterior olfactory nucleus; the anterior perforated substance, olfactory tubercle and diagonal band of Broca; the prepiriform cortex; the entorhinal area and adjacent parts of the parahippocampal gyrus; part of the amygdaloid complex (especially the nucleus of the lateral olfactory stria); the bed nucleus of the stria terminalis and the nucleus accumbens; and inferior regions of the temporal and occipital lobes.
The anterior commissure has the shape of a classic bicycle handlebar. It crosses the midline just ventral to the supraoptic recess of the third ventricle, immediately anterior to the major component of the column of the fornix that projects to the mammillary body, and posterior to the smaller components of the fornix that project to the septal nuclei (see Fig. 32.77B). At this point, the anterior commissure bulges inside the third ventricle, just underneath the interventricular foramen. The lamina terminalis is attached superiorly to the midline segment of the anterior commissure and inferiorly to the upper surface of the optic chiasma; the optic recess of the third ventricle is the cleft between the lamina terminalis and the midportion of the superior surface of the chiasma.
Each side of the anterior commissure is composed of a well-defined posterolateral bundle (the hemispheric part) and a smaller, anterior component (the olfactory part) (Déjérine and Déjerine-Klumke 1895). The anterior component curves forwards and vertically through the anterior perforated substance towards each olfactory tubercle. The hemispheric component curves posterolaterally, passing through a deep groove on the anteroinferior aspect of the globus pallidus, and fanning out mostly to the anterior part of the temporal lobe, including the parahippocampal gyrus, but also reaching the occipital lobe posteriorly. This component of the anterior commissure runs parallel to the amygdolofugal peduncles which carry information from the amygdala to the hypothalamic nuclei (Fig. 32.77B).
When passing under the inferior limiting sulcus of the insula, superior to the temporal horn of the lateral ventricle, the fibres of the anterior commissure join the temporal stem, merging with the fibres of the uncinate and inferior fronto-occipital fasciculi. They pass posteriorly as the sagittal stratum, together with the inferior fronto-occipital fasciculus and the fibres of the optic radiation, over the lateral aspect of the temporal horn and ventricular atrium. Within the temporal stem, the fibres of the anterior commissure are mostly medial to the uncinate fasciculus; both are inferior to the inferior fronto-occipital fasciculus. Within the sagittal stratum, they intermingle but run predominantly below the inferior fronto-occipital fasciculus, superior to the fibres of the optic radiation. The line between the anterior and posterior commissures, the AC–PC line, is a very important landmark in stereotactic atlases, used when localizing neuroanatomical targets for stereotactic neurosurgical procedures.

Hippocampal commissure

The hippocampal commissure (commissure of the fornix) is a thin triangular sheet of fibres that lies between the crura of the fornices, connecting the hippocampi (Fig. 32.78). It is situated underneath and attached to the splenium, overhanging the pineal region.

Projection fibres

Projection fibres connect the cerebral cortex with the subcortical grey matter of the cerebral hemispheres, brainstem and spinal cord. They include large numbers of both corticofugal (descending) and corticopetal (ascending) fibres. Most of the projection fibres originating in the frontal, parietal and occipital lobes converge in the corona radiata and from here pass through the internal capsule to reach their subcortical targets. The projections forming the internal capsule are termed capsular projections and are separated from the non-capsular projections. The extracapsular fibres include the cortico-striatal projections directed to the most external putamen and projections to the claustrum course through the external capsule. The largest limbic projection tracts (fornix, ansa peduncularis, extracapsular thalamic peduncle, ansa and stria terminalis) also have an extracapsular course and do not contribute to the corona radiata. Except for the fornix and cingulum, most of the temporal projection tracts pass through the temporal stem which can be considered as the posterior extension of the external/extreme capsule. The term temporal stem is derived from its appearance in coronal sections of the temporal lobe, where the region resembles a small cauliflower composed of a single short stem and small florets that correspond to the five longitudinal temporal gyri. The stem originates from the floor of the external/extreme capsule; in its narrowest portion it is contained within a space delimited medially by the lateral ventricle and laterally by the inferior branch of the peri-insular sulcus. Within the posterior temporal stem, these fibres run parallel to the lateral ventricle before spreading outward into the temporal gyri to reach their cortical targets.

Internal capsule

The internal capsule has the shape of a V in horizontal section (axial images), with its vertex (genu) located medially between the head of the caudate nucleus and the thalamus (Fig. 32.79). For descriptive purposes, the internal capsule is arbitrarily divided into five parts: anterior limb, located between the putamen and the head of the caudate nucleus; genu, lying laterally and adjacent to the interventricular foramen (of Monro); posterior limb, located between the putamen and the thalamus; retrolenticular or retrolentiform portion, located posterior to the putamen; and sublenticular (or sublentiform) portion, located inferior to the putamen (see Fig. 32.79). The internal capsule contains thalamo-cortical fibres that form the thalamic peduncles, the majority of the cortico-striatal fibres, cortico-nuclear and cortico-spinal tracts that course through the cerebral peduncles, and projections to other nuclei of the brainstem.
Corticospinal and corticonuclear tracts
The corticospinal tract provides direct control by the cerebral cortex over motor centres in the contralateral spinal cord. The corticonuclear tract fulfils a similar function in relation to the motor nuclei of the brainstem that give origin to cranial nerves.
About 20–30% of corticospinal fibres arise from the pyramidal cells in layer V of BA4. The remaining corticospinal fibres originate from the premotor cortex and parietal areas of the postcentral gyrus, superior parietal lobule and operculum. Many axons from BA4 terminate in the ventral horn of the spinal cord: a small percentage establish direct monosynaptic connections with α-motoneurones. Between 40% and 60% of pyramidal tract axons arise from parietal areas, including area 3a, area 5 of the superior part of the parietal lobe and secondary somatosensory areas in the parietal operculum. The majority of parietal fibres to the spinal cord terminate in the deeper layers of the dorsal horn. Motor cortical neurones are active in relation to the force of contraction of agonist muscles; their relation to the amplitude of movement is less clear. Their activity precedes the onset of electromyographic activity by 50–100 milliseconds, suggesting a role for cortical activation in generating rather than monitoring movement.
The corticospinal and corticonuclear fibres that arise within the precentral gyrus are disposed as a fan on a coronal plane throughout the corona radiata. They cross the corpus callosum before entering the genu and posterior limb of the internal capsule (see Fig. 32.76, Video 32.1). To join the internal capsule, the corticospinal and corticonuclear fibres not only converge but also undergo an internal rotation of approximately 90 degrees, because the main axes of the precentral gyrus and of the internal capsule genu and posterior limb are almost perpendicular. Throughout their course the fibres retain their somatotopical motor arrangement, organized according to the homuncular cortical representation, adopting an anterior–cranial to a posterior–caudal disposal along the genu (corticonuclear tract) and the posterior limb (corticospinal tract) of the internal capsule. Within the posterior limb of the internal capsule the fibres concerned with the upper limb are anterior to the fibres representing the trunk and lower limbs. From the internal capsule the fibres converge further into the cerebral peduncles, where the corticospinal and corticonuclear fibres are located in the middle half of the crus between frontopontine fibres (medially) and corticopontine fibres from temporal, parietal and occipital cortices (laterally).
Other descending axons include frontopontine fibres (Arnold’s bundle), particularly from areas 4 and 6, and corticorubral fibres, coursing through the anterior limb of the internal capsule. The retrolenticular part of the internal capsule contains parietopontine, occipitopontine and occipitotectal fibres and the sublenticular part contains temporopontine and some parietopontine fibres. All corticopontine fibres that originate from parietal, occipital and temporal lobes collectively form the bundle of Türk.
Thalamic radiations
The thalamic radiations originate from the thalamic nuclei. After a short course through the internal capsule they enter the corona radiata and terminate in the cortex of the ipsilateral hemisphere (Fig. 32.80). The thalamic radiations from the anterior and medio-dorsal thalamic nuclei enter the anterior limb of the internal capsule and form the anterior thalamic peduncle that projects to all prefrontal and orbitofrontal cortices. The thalamic nuclei of the ventral group and in part those of the lateral group give origin to the superior thalamic peduncle, which courses through both anterior and posterior limbs of the internal capsule, terminating in the posterior frontal cortex (from ventral anterior and ventral lateral thalamic nuclei) and parietal cortex (from ventral posterior and lateral posterior thalamic nuclei), respectively.
The retrolenticular part of the internal capsule includes the optic radiation and the posterior thalamic radiation originating from the pulvinar and directed to the posterior cortex of the parietal and temporal lobes and the occipital cortex.
The fibres of the optic radiation arise from the lateral geniculate body and the pulvinar, join the retrolenticular and the sublenticular parts of the internal capsule, run within the sagittal stratum over the temporal horn and ventricular atrium, and project posteriorly, passing superiorly and inferiorly to the occipital horn as part of the posterior thalamic peduncle to reach both the superior and inferior lips of the calcarine sulcus. Within the temporal lobe, the fibres of the optic radiation are located along the depths of the superior and middle temporal gyri about 2 cm from the brain surface, medial to the arcuate and inferior fronto-occipital fasciculi and inferior to the auditory radiations. Its fibres are divided into anterior (Flechsig’s temporal knee or Meyer’s loop), central and posterior bundles. On sagittal sections, the occipital fibres are concentrically distributed around the occipital horn of the lateral ventricle in the following order: callosal fibres (the most internal layer); optic radiations and extrastriate thalamocortical projections (internal sagittal stratum of Sachs); association fibres of the inferior longitudinal fasciculus and inferior fronto-occipital fasciculus (external sagittal stratum of Sachs).
The sublenticular part of the internal capsule contains the auditory radiation from the medial geniculate body and direct fibres from the inferior colliculus, that together project to the superior temporal and transverse temporal gyri (areas 41 and 42). Thalamic fibres from the pulvinar to the temporal cortex and insula are also contained within the sublenticular part of the internal capsule. Fibres of the auditory radiation sweep anterolaterally below and behind the lentiform complex and superiorly to the temporal horn, ventricular atrium and optic radiation. As they enter the temporal stem, the auditory radiations fan out and split into two branches. The superior branch continues laterally towards Heschl’s gyrus and the surrounding cortex, where it finally projects to early auditory areas. The inferior branch continues anteriorly along the temporal stem in a space delimited by the inferior border of the putamen, the lateral ventricle and the inferior insula. It is directed towards the anterior temporal lobe and sends many projections to the amygdala and most anterior temporal cortex of the superior, middle and inferior temporal gyri. The acoustic radiations also contain fibres that run in the reverse direction from the temporal cortex and amygdala to the medial geniculate nucleus and inferior colliculus (Pickles 2015). This centrifugal system mediates the top-down modulatory effect by which the temporal cortex and amygdala enhance the response of subcortical nuclei to stimuli of significance.

Fornix

The fornix is the fibre bundle of the hippocampus. It wraps round the thalamus, from which it is separated by the choroidal fissure and the choroid plexus. The fornix consists of the fimbria, crus, body and columns or pillars. The fibres of the alveus converge to form the fimbria fornicis (see Fig. 32.78) along the medial portion of the floor of the inferior horn of the lateral ventricle. At a point beneath the splenium of the corpus callosum, the white matter of the fimbria separates from the hippocampus to become the crus fornicis. The two crura pass upwards and forwards beneath the corpus callosum, joined via fibres that cross in the commissure of the fornix (hippocampal commissure). Beyond this point, the crura merge to become the body of the fornix, which continues anteriorly within the roof of the third ventricle, below the lower border of the septum pellucidum and near the midline. At the level of the interventricular foramen (foramen of Monro) and the anterior commissure, the body splits into two anterior (precommissural) and two posterior (postcommissural) columns: the smaller anterior columns terminate in the hypothalamic nuclei whereas the larger posterior columns project to the mammillary bodies. As they separate from the underlying thalamus, the posterior columns of the fornix form the anterior margins of the interventricular foramen.
A smaller number of fornical fibres project to the anterior thalamic nuclei (anterior medial, anterior ventral and anterior dorsal thalamic nuclei), ventral striatum, medial septum and nucleus reuniens; some are exclusively afferent to the hippocampus, e.g. from the supramammillary and raphe nuclei and the locus coeruleus (Aggleton et al 2010, Jang and Kwon 2013, Jang and Kwon 2014). As a midline white matter tract, the fornix is particularly susceptible to involvement by tumours that favour this route of spread, e.g. glioblastoma multiforme, and to rotational shearing injuries responsible for diffuse axonal injury (Thomas et al 2011).
