Commentary 1.4: Merkel cells

Ellen A. Lumpkin
The epidermis, our outermost layer of skin, serves as a protective barrier and sensory interface with the world. In vertebrates, these functions are accomplished through the work of only four conserved cell types. Keratinocytes form a water-tight barrier, melanocytes protect against ultraviolet damage and Langerhans cells perform immune surveillance. Merkel cells, which constitute less than 1% of the epidermis, are an enigmatic cell type whose function and developmental origin have long been debated; however, recent advances show that mammalian Merkel cells are epithelial derivatives that signal to sensory neurones to initiate touch sensation.
As early as the nineteenth century, Merkel cells were proposed to contribute to skin’s sensory function. These cells were first described by Friedrich Sigmund Merkel, who named them touch cells (Tastzellen), based on contacts with nerve terminals (Merkel 1875). Merkel cells cluster in highly touch-sensitive skin areas, including glabrous (hairless) skin of fingertips and lips, some hair follicles and touch domes on the trunk (Fig. 1.4.1). Most Merkel cells are innervated by a particular class of myelinated (Aβ) sensory neurones called slowly adapting type I (SAI) afferents (Iggo and Muir 1969). These touch receptors are unusual because they produce biphasic discharges of electrical impulses that send two types of information to the brain (see Fig. 1.4.1): dynamic responses to moving stimuli represent an object’s spatial features (e.g. edges and curvature), whereas sustained volleys of electrical activity signify steady pressure (e.g. contact with clothing; Johnson 2001).
Merkel cells were initially proposed to serve as sensory receptor cells based on their synapse-like contacts with nerve terminals and dense-core vesicles filled with neurotransmitters (Halata et al 2003). Sensory receptor cells, such as taste-bud cells or hair cells of the inner ear, transduce the energy of a sensory stimulus into electrical signals, which then trigger neurotransmitter release to excite sensory afferents. The hypothesis that Merkel cells are mechanosensory receptors was challenged by neurophysiological studies that reported touch sensitivity after Merkel cell loss, which suggested that Merkel cells act in a non-sensory capacity. Studies in rodent models have reconciled these views by demonstrating that Merkel cells and their sensory afferents are mechanosensory cells. Three lines of evidence support this two-receptor-site model, which was proposed almost three decades ago (Yamashita and Ogawa 1991).
First, Merkel cells are touch-sensitive. Like hair cells in the inner ear, Merkel cells display fast mechanotransduction currents that convert cellular movement into electrical signals (Ikeda et al 2014, Maksimovic et al 2014, Woo et al 2014). These currents are mediated by Piezo2 mechanosensitive ion channels, which are essential for touch and proprioception (Ranade et al 2014, Woo et al 2015, Chesler et al 2016; see Fig. 1.4.1).
Second, Merkel cells are excitatory cells. This was established with optogenetics, a technique that uses light-activated molecules derived from Archea to stimulate or inhibit electrical signalling in specific cells. Transgenic mice were engineered to selectively express light-activated membrane proteins in Merkel cells (Maksimovic et al 2014): when their skin is illuminated, Merkel cells are activated and their sensory afferents produce sustained trains of electrical impulses that mimic the response to pressure (see Fig. 1.4.1). Conversely, neuronal firing is inhibited when Merkel cells are optogenetically silenced. These results confirm that Merkel cells are sensory cells capable of exciting afferents.
Third, Merkel cells are necessary for sustained, but not for dynamic, firing in SAI afferents. Prototypical SAI responses are abolished in transgenic mouse strains that lack either Merkel cells or epidermal PIEZO2 expression (Maricich et al 2009, Maksimovic et al 2014, Woo et al 2014). Inhibiting the release of neurotransmitter-containing vesicles from Merkel cells also attenuated touch-evoked neural activity (Chang et al 2016, Hoffman et al 2018). In all cases, sustained touch produced only transient bursts of neuronal impulses (see Fig. 1.4.1). These findings indicate that mechanosensory signalling from Merkel cells is essential for conveying information about pressure to the nervous system. Touch-driven behaviours are compromised in rodents lacking functional Merkel cells, again indicating that Merkel cells are important for the perception of touch (Maricich et al 2012, Ikeda et al 2014, Woo et al 2014). Taken together, these studies demonstrate that the Merkel cell–neurite complex is a compound touch receptor with two mechanosensory cell types arranged in series (see Fig. 1.4.1).
Which neurotransmitters convey information between Merkel cells and sensory afferents remains a key open question. In rodents, it has been proposed that whisker follicle Merkel cells signal through serotoninergic receptors (Chang et al 2016), whereas touch-dome Merkel cells have been reported to employ adrenergic synapses (Hoffman et al 2018). Both findings support the model that Merkel cells use vesicular release of monoamine neuromodulators rather than classical excitatory neurotransmitters to excite the nervous system (see Fig. 1.4.1). Since drugs that target monoamine signalling are widely used for treatment of depression (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors) and cardiovascular disease (beta-blockers), these basic science findings may have clinical implications by suggesting new treatment avenues for peripheral sensory disturbances such as numbness and tingling.
Transgenic mouse studies have also clarified the ontogeny of the Merkel cell, which expresses an unusual assortment of epithelial and neuronal markers (Moll et al 1984, Halata et al 2003, Haeberle et al 2004). For example, although embedded in a stratified epithelium, Merkel cells express simple epithelial keratins. It was therefore unclear whether Merkel cells are derived from epithelial precursors or neural crest. These competing models were tested in transgenic mice by genetically marking specific cell lineages based on selective expression of Cre recombinase. Wnt1Cre, which marks all neural crest cells, failed to label Merkel cells, whereas Krt14Cre, a marker of keratinocyte-derived cells, labelled Merkel cells in all skin areas (Morrison et al 2009, Van Keymeulen et al 2009). Moreover, Merkel cell specification is disrupted by epithelial-specific deletion of developmental transcription factors (Morrison et al 2009, Van Keymeulen et al 2009, Bardot et al 2013, Lesko et al 2013, Perdigoto et al 2014), and Merkel cells are replenished from epidermal progenitors in mature skin (Van Keymeulen et al 2009, Woo et al 2010, Doucet et al 2013, Wright et al 2017).
The embryonic progenitor of the Merkel cell remains a matter of debate, although recent studies have identified developmental pathways that bestow this neurosensory fate in epidermis. It has been proposed that Merkel cells in mouse hair-bearing skin are derived from hair-follicle placodes, but genetic lineage tracing of hair follicle progenitors has called this model into question (Xiao et al 2016, Jenkins and Lumpkin 2017). Merkel cell progenitors have distinct molecular phenotypes in mouse hairy and glabrous skin (Nguyen et al 2019).
Despite remarkable progress in Merkel cell biology, key questions about human Merkel cells remain unanswered. Do Merkel cells serve additional, non-sensory functions? This is particularly important in human skin, where many Merkel cells lack innervation. Are human Merkel cells derived from epidermis and renewed throughout life? If so, how are these processes disrupted during ageing, which is accompanied by loss of touch receptors in extremities? This loss is thought to contribute to the decline of tactile acuity, grip strength and postural stability in the elderly. Intriguingly, a recent study linked age-related loss of Merkel cells to chronic itch, which is a prevalent clinical problem in the elderly (Feng et al 2018).
Mechanisms of Merkel cell specification may also be important in Merkel cell carcinoma (MCC). This highly aggressive, non-melanoma skin cancer has been linked to Merkel cells on the basis of ultrastructure and molecular markers (Haeberle et al 2004, Bhatia et al 2011, Harms et al 2013). An important unresolved question is whether Merkel cells or their progenitors act as cells of origin for MCC. Distinct molecular pathways appear to give rise to MCC downstream of two risk factors, namely ultraviolet (UV) irradiation or infection with Merkel cell polyoma virus (MCPyV; Feng et al 2008, Harms et al 2018). In most cases of MCC, integration of MCPyV and subsequent inhibition of retinoblastoma tumour suppressor protein give rise to MCPyV-positive tumours with a low mutational burden. By contrast, virus-negative tumours display UV mutational signatures and inactivation of tumour suppressor genes. Despite different aetiologies, virus-positive and virus-negative tumours share similar clinical presentations and treatment guidelines (Harms et al 2018).
Immune checkpoint inhibitors targeting the PD-1 pathway have been a game changer in the clinical care of MCC (Harms et al 2018, Nghiem and Park 2019). These agents are beneficial in almost half of patients with advanced MCC and overall survival at three years is dramatically improved in patients receiving immune checkpoint inhibitors compared with conventional chemotherapies (Nghiem et al 2019). Such promising clinical results have led to rapid changes in treatment guidelines, with immune checkpoint inhibitors now the recommended first-line therapy for advanced MCC in appropriate patients. Nonetheless, new therapies are needed for patients who are immunosuppressed or otherwise resistant to immune checkpoint inhibitors.
