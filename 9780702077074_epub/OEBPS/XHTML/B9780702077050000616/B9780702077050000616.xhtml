Chapter 61: Posterior abdominal wall and retroperitoneum

Definitions, Boundaries and Contents

The posterior abdominal wall has no agreed uniform definition. It is the posterior boundary of the abdominal cavity, and in common with the anterior and lateral abdominal walls, it is composed of several layers (skin, superficial fascia, muscle, extraperitoneal fat/fascia and parietal peritoneum). The vertebral column and paravertebral muscles are part of the posterior abdominal wall, but are usually discussed as part of the back. The posterior abdominal wall is continuous with the posterior thoracic wall at the posterior attachment of the respiratory diaphragm, with the posterior wall of the pelvis inferiorly, and with the anterolateral abdominal wall laterally.
The retroperitoneum is nestled between the posterior abdominal wall and its lining layer of posterior parietal peritoneum. Inferiorly and laterally, it is continuous with the extraperitoneal connective tissues lining the pelvis and anterolateral abdominal wall, respectively. The posterior border of the retroperitoneum is variably defined, depending on whether psoas major and quadratus lumborum are regarded as its contents or boundaries. Advances in cross-sectional imaging techniques have improved our understanding of the anatomy of the retroperitoneum but terminology is not standardized with regard to its components. Terms such as spaces, compartments, subcompartments, recesses and domains have been used to describe the whole and parts of this region.
The retroperitoneum houses parts of the gut tube in whole or in part, namely the duodenum, and the ascending and descending colons, and their vessels and nerves; and the pancreas, kidneys, ureters and suprarenal glands and their vessels and nerves. The mesentery of the small intestine, transverse and sigmoid colons and the ligaments of the liver and spleen all arise from the posterior parietal peritoneum as it covers the posterior abdominal wall. The retroperitoneum also contains the abdominal aorta and its branches; the inferior vena cava and its tributaries; the origins of the azygos and hemiazygos veins; the pre- and lateral aortic nodes, the cisterna chyli and the origin of the thoracic duct; the diaphragmatic crura; the lumbar plexus and lumbosacral trunk; and the autonomic plexuses of the abdomen.

Skin and Soft Tissues

The skin of the posterior abdominal wall is supplied by musculocutaneous branches of the lumbar arteries and veins, and is innervated by the dorsal rami of the lower thoracic and lumbar spinal nerves. The cutaneous distribution of the dorsal rami of the fourth and fifth lumbar spinal nerves is variable and controversial (Lee et al 2008).
Various fascial and connective tissues within the retroperitoneum have been defined and used to divide this region into smaller parts: the boundaries of these smaller parts are not universally agreed and clinical observations often do not support the described anatomical margins (Dodds et al 1986).

Thoracolumbar fascia

The thoracolumbar fascia is a complex arrangement of multilayered fascial planes and aponeurotic sheets that separates the paraspinal muscles from the muscles of the posterior abdominal wall (quadratus lumborum and psoas major) (Willard et al 2012) (Figs 61.1–61.3; see Fig. 46.6). It is most prominent at the caudal end of the lumbar spine where layers of aponeurotic tissue unite and blend to form a thickened brace between the two posterior superior iliac spines and extend caudally to reach the ischial tuberosities. The thoracolumbar fascia is described in detail in Chapter 46.

Iliopsoas fascia

Psoas fascia

The anterior surface of psoas major is covered by a relatively dense layer of fascia. Medially, this fascia is continuous with the attachments of the muscle to the transverse processes and bodies of the lumbar vertebrae. Laterally, it blends with the fascia over quadratus lumborum superiorly and is continuous with the iliac fascia inferiorly. Superiorly, it merges with the medial arcuate ligament; inferiorly, it extends into the thigh around the tendon of iliopsoas. The psoas fascia separates the anterior surface of the muscle and the lumbar plexus within it from the retroperitoneal structures lying anteriorly. An abscess within psoas major, which can arise by haematogenous seeding or by direct extension of infection from adjacent structures, e.g. the spine, kidneys or colon, often tracks along the muscle within the fascia.

Iliac fascia

The iliac fascia overlies iliacus. Superiorly and laterally it is attached to the inner aspect of the iliac crest, and medially it blends with the anterior layer of the thoracolumbar fascia over quadratus lumborum and with the psoas fascia. Inferiorly and laterally it continues into the thigh to fuse with the femoral sheath, while medially it is attached to the periosteum of the ilium and iliopubic eminence near the linea terminalis. Both the femoral nerve and lateral femoral cutaneous nerve lie under the iliac fascia in the pelvis, where they can be targeted for image-guided anaesthetic nerve blockade (Hebbard et al 2011).

Visceral fasciae

The organization of the retroperitoneum is most easily conceptualized in terms of subsections rather than delineation by fasciae (Dodds et al 1986). The fasciae bounding these parts are variable and difficult to see on cross-sectional imaging. Moreover, clinical observations of the spread of pathological fluids and air often do not match anatomically defined fascial boundaries.
The fascial layers located between the parietal peritoneum lining the posterior abdominal wall and quadratus lumborum have been described in confusing eponymous terms in the literature. Different authors have used the same eponym for different fasciae; different eponyms have been used to refer to the same fascia. For example, ‘Gerota’s fascia’ has been used to denote the entire renal fascia or to its anterior or posterior layers (Chesbrough et al 1989). ‘Toldt’s fascia’ has been used to denote the fusion fascia behind the ascending or descending colon (Culligan et al 2013), or the posterior layer of renal fascia, or the fusion fascia posterior to the tail of the pancreas (Kimura et al 2010). ‘Zuckerkandl’s fascia’ has been used to refer to the posterior or anterior layers of renal fascia (Chesbrough et al 1989). ‘Treitz’s fascia’ has been used to describe the fusion fascia behind the head of the pancreas (Kimura et al 2010). The concept of ‘fusion fascia’ describes a retroperitoneal fascia formed by the fusion of an embryonic mesentery with the anterior wall of the embryonic retroperitoneum (Hikspoors et al 2019). It is an avascular layer that allows a dissection plane to be developed and also limits the spread of disease. Many of these fasciae and the subsections they create are not recognized in Terminologia Anatomica but are well established in the clinical literature and are discussed below.
Traditional teaching of the retroperitoneum divides it into the anterior and posterior pararenal spaces and the perirenal space. The anterior pararenal space lies between the parietal peritoneum lining the posterior abdominal wall and the anterior layer of renal fascia. This oversimplification fails to explain all patterns of disease containment or spread (Dodds et al 1986). The smallest set of divisions within the retroperitoneum sufficient to explain most such phenomena is as follows. Some of the spaces are illustrated in the context of clinical disease in Figs 61.4-61.6.

Perirenal space

The paired perirenal spaces are bounded by the renal fascia, which envelops the kidney and suprarenal gland on each side. The renal fascia is traditionally described as having anterior and posterior layers which are continuous with each other laterally (see Figs 61.1–61.3 and Chapter 72). It can usually be identified on cross-sectional radiological imaging as a thin layer surrounding the kidney and suprarenal gland, separated from the fibrous capsule of the kidney by a variable amount of perirenal fat. Inferiorly, the perirenal space continues distally around the ureter but becomes progressively narrower and may or may not extend into the pelvis posterior to the posterior parietal peritoneum lining. Medially, the two perirenal spaces may interconnect anterior to the abdominal aorta and inferior vena cava (Kneeland et al 1987). The existence of a fascial partition separating the kidney and ipsilateral suprarenal gland within the envelope of renal fascia is controversial (Amin et al 1976).

Anterior and posterior pararenal spaces

The anterior pararenal space (an oversimplification) refers to the region between the anterior layer of renal fascia and the parietal peritoneum lining the posterior abdominal wall. On the right, it contains the descending (second) part of the duodenum and the ascending colon, and on the left, the ascending (fourth) part of the duodenum and the descending colon.
The posterior pararenal space lies between the posterior layer of renal fascia anteriorly and the thoracolumbar and psoas fasciae posteriorly. It contains a variable amount of fat. Laterally it is continuous with the extraperitoneal fat of the anterolateral abdominal wall, and inferiorly with the retroperitoneal fat overlying iliacus and the pelvic wall, and the fat around the urinary bladder (Coffin et al 2015).
When the ascending or descending colon is retrorenal (an anatomical variant), these structures and pericolic space lie in a groove between the posterior layer of renal fascia and the posterior pararenal space.

Peripancreatic space

The peripancreatic space contains the duodenum and pancreas, the origins of the superior mesenteric vessels and the retroperitoneal segments of the bile duct, hepatic portal vein and hepatic artery. The posterior boundary is a fusion fascia formed by fusion of the embryonic duodenal mesentery to the anterior wall of the embryonic retroperitoneum (Dodds et al 1986). This fascia is termed the ‘retropancreatic’ or ‘retropancreaticoduodenal’ fascia in the imaging literature, and ‘fusion fascia of Treitz’ in the surgical literature (Kimura et al 2010). It forms a relatively avascular dissection plane that is used to mobilize the duodenum and head of the pancreas.
The lateral extent of the peripancreatic space is variable; it lies posterior to the ascending and descending colons and anterior to the perirenal space (see Fig. 61.1). At the tail of the pancreas, the peripancreatic space lies in continuity with the splenorenal ligament. Anteriorly, it is in continuity with the transverse mesocolon and mesentery of the small intestine. Consequently, peripancreatic fluid collections can extend into both mesenteries and extend posterior to the ascending and descending colons but do not usually cross into the perirenal and pericolic spaces.

Pericolic spaces

The ascending and descending pericolic spaces are the narrow regions surrounding the respective parts of the colon and are continuous medially with the mesocolon (see Figs 61.1–61.3). The pericolic spaces contain a variable amount of fat and are limited anteriorly, superiorly and laterally by the serosa of the colon. Inferiorly, they are continuous with the retroperitoneum of the iliac fossae. Posteriorly, each pericolic space is limited by a fusion fascia formed by the right leaf of the embryonic mesocolon fusing to the left leaf of the embryonic duodenal mesentery and anterior wall of the embryonic retroperitoneum further laterally (Dodds et al 1986). This retrocolic fascia (of Toldt) (Culligan et al 2013, Culligan et al 2014) forms the classic avascular plane of dissection when a hemicolectomy is performed. Its plane is entered by incising the junction of the serosa of the colon with the parietal peritoneum at the white line (of Toldt) in the paracolic gutter. Radiologically, the retrocolic fascia may be indistinguishable from the anterior layer of renal fascia when lateral to the duodenum and pancreas. Variation in colonic retroperitonealization can produce a free mesocolic pedicle (most commonly seen at the caecum).
Lateroconal fascia
The lateroconal fascia was originally defined as the fascial layer extending from the junction of the anterior and posterior layers of renal fascia to the parietal peritoneum in the paracolic gutter (Congdon and Edson 1941). It is better understood as the lateral extension of the retrocolic fusion fascia that blends with the parietal peritoneum (see Figs 61.1–61.3).

Bones

The posterior abdominal wall is supported by the vertebral column and pelvic girdle. The individual bones are the last two ribs, the twelfth thoracic and five lumbar vertebrae, and the sacrum and ilium, together with their interconnecting ligaments.

Muscles

Most of the muscles of the posterior abdominal wall are functionally part of the pelvic girdle/lower limb or vertebral column. They provide the surface against which the neurovascular and visceral structures of the retroperitoneum lie (Fig. 61.7; see Fig. 61.15).

Quadratus lumborum

Quadratus lumborum is an irregularly shaped rectangular muscle, broader at its inferior attachment than superiorly.

Attachments

The inferior attachment is by aponeurotic fibres to the iliac crest over an area 5–7 cm lateral to the distal end of the transverse process of the fourth lumbar vertebra and/or the iliolumbar ligament. Fascicles pass from the iliac crest to be attached superiorly to either the anteroinferior surface of the twelfth rib (iliocostal), or the distal ends of the transverse processes of the upper four lumbar vertebrae (iliolumbar), or the lateral surface of the twelfth thoracic vertebra (iliothoracic). Some fascicles may pass from the lumbar transverse processes to the twelfth rib (lumbocostal). Fascicles vary in number and size but typically are arranged in three layers. The anterior layer consists primarily of iliocostal and iliothoracic fascicles, the middle layer consists primarily of lumbocostal fascicles, and the posterior layer consists primarily of iliocostal fascicles laterally and iliolumbar fascicles medially (Phillips et al 2008).

Relations

Anteriorly are the colon (ascending on the right, descending on the left), kidney, psoas major and minor (when present), and diaphragm. The subcostal, iliohypogastric and ilio-inguinal nerves lie on the fascia anterior to the muscle, bound down to it by the medial continuation of the transversalis fascia.

Vascular supply

Quadratus lumborum is supplied by branches of the lumbar arteries, the arteriae lumbales imae from the median sacral artery, the lumbar branch of the iliolumbar artery, and branches of the subcostal artery.

Innervation

Quadratus lumborum is innervated by the ventral rami of the twelfth thoracic and upper three or four lumbar spinal nerves.

Actions

The iliocostal fascicles of quadratus lumborum fix the twelfth rib, helping to stabilize the lower attachments of the respiratory diaphragm during inspiration. The actions of the iliolumbar fascicles are less clear: their contribution to lateral flexion or extension are reported to be weak (Phillips et al 2008, Park et al 2012). Quadratus lumborum is reported to undergo considerable hypertrophy in some sporting activities (Ranson et al 2008).

Psoas major, psoas minor and iliacus

Psoas major and iliacus are functionally important in the lower limb and are described together with psoas minor in Chapter 77.
Posterior abdominal wall hernias
In the absence of a previous surgical incision, visceral herniation through the posterior abdominal wall is rare because the muscular and fascial layers usually protect against protrusion. However, the posterior free border of external abdominal oblique, the inferior free border of latissimus dorsi and the iliac crest delimit the inferior lumbar triangle (of Petit), an area of potential weakness through which a lumbar hernia may develop (see Fig 60.13) (Stamatiou et al 2009).

Vascular Supply and Lymphatic Drainage

Abdominal aorta

The abdominal aorta begins at the aortic hiatus of the respiratory diaphragm anterior to the twelfth thoracic vertebra. It descends anterior to the lumbar vertebrae and bifurcates into two common iliac arteries anterior to the fourth lumbar vertebra or the intervertebral disc between the fourth and fifth lumbar vertebrae, slightly to the left of the midline (Mirjalili et al 2012a, 2012b, Moussallem et al 2012) (Figs 61.8-61.11). The angle of bifurcation is very variable (Moussallem et al 2012). The mean adult diameter of the aorta inferior to the origin of the renal arteries measured by computed tomography is 19–21 mm (males) and 16–18 mm (females) (Rogers et al 2013), but there are ethnic variations (Jasper et al 2014). Measured by ultrasound, equivalent values are 20±2.5 mm (males) and 17±1.5 mm (females) (Needleman 2006). The mean calibre of the abdominal aorta decreases slightly from proximal to distal. With advancing age, there is a progressive increase in abdominal aortic diameter in both sexes and the tapering becomes more pronounced (Fleischmann et al 2001). In the elderly, the abdominal aorta frequently becomes ectatic and tortuous, changing the angle and position of the bifurcation and rotating the origins of the major branches.
Approximately 80% of abdominal aortic aneurysms occur in the aorta inferior to the origin of the renal arteries. Males with atherosclerosis are most at risk of developing the condition, particularly from the sixth decade onwards (Takayama and Yamanouchi 2013). The high mortality risk from spontaneous rupture has prompted the development of ultrasound screening programmes to detect aneurysms and repair them electively. Overall, surgery is performed in patients with an aneurysm greater than 5.0 cm (females) and 5.5 cm (males) in diameter (Groeneveld et al 2018). Repair is most commonly performed by open surgery or endovascular stenting (endovascular aneurysm repair).

Relations

The proximal abdominal aorta is directly related anteriorly to the coeliac trunk and its branches, autonomic nerve plexuses and lymphatics. Inferior to the coeliac trunk, the superior mesenteric artery leaves the aorta and descends anterior to the left renal vein. The body of the pancreas is anterior to these vessels; the splenic vein is on its posterior surface and extends obliquely up and to the left. Further anteriorly, the omental bursa separates the proximal abdominal aorta from the lesser omentum, stomach and left lobe of the liver. Inferior to the pancreas, the horizontal part of the duodenum crosses the aorta anteriorly.
The twelfth thoracic vertebra, the upper four lumbar vertebrae, intervening intervertebral discs and the anterior longitudinal ligament lie posterior to the abdominal aorta. Lumbar arteries arise from its posterior aspect, and the third and fourth (and sometimes the second) left lumbar veins cross posterior to it to reach the inferior vena cava. The abdominal aorta can overlap the medial border of psoas major on the left.
On the right, the abdominal aorta is related superiorly to the cisterna chyli and thoracic duct, the azygos vein and the right crus of the respiratory diaphragm, which overlaps and separates it from the inferior vena cava and right coeliac ganglion. Below the second lumbar vertebra it is closely applied to the left side of the inferior vena cava. This close relationship allows a fistula to develop between the aorta and adjacent inferior vena cava, a rare complication of aneurysmal disease or trauma. On the left, the aorta is related superiorly to the left crus of the respiratory diaphragm and left coeliac ganglion. Level with the second lumbar vertebra, it is related to the ascending part of the duodenum, the left sympathetic trunk and the inferior mesenteric vein.

Branches

The branches of the abdominal aorta are described as anterior, lateral and dorsal (see Fig. 61.9). The anterior (unpaired) and lateral (paired) branches are distributed to the viscera, while the dorsal branches supply the body wall, vertebral column and the vertebral canal and its contents. The aorta terminates by dividing into the right and left common iliac arteries.
Anterior group
Coeliac trunk
The coeliac trunk is the first anterior branch and arises just inferior to the aortic hiatus, usually at the level of the vertebral body of the twelfth thoracic vertebra. It is 1–3 cm long and passes almost horizontally anteriorly and slightly to the right superior to the body of the pancreas and splenic vein. In most individuals it trifurcates into the left gastric, common hepatic and splenic arteries. Variations occur and include a separate origin of the left gastric artery from the abdominal aorta, one or both inferior phrenic arteries arising from the coeliac trunk, and the superior mesenteric artery or one or more of its branches arising in common with the coeliac trunk (Panagouli et al 2013). Anterior to the coeliac trunk lies the omental bursa. The coeliac plexus surrounds the coeliac trunk, sending extensions along its branches. On the right lie the right coeliac ganglion, right crus of the respiratory diaphragm and the caudate lobe of the liver. To the left lie the left coeliac ganglion, left crus of the respiratory diaphragm and the cardia of the stomach. Rarely, the coeliac trunk can be compressed by the median arcuate ligament (Dunbar syndrome), resulting in visceral ischaemia and abdominal pain (Ho et al 2017).
Superior mesenteric artery
The superior mesenteric artery originates from the aorta approximately 1–2 cm below the coeliac trunk, at the level of the body of the first lumbar vertebra (Mirjalili et al 2012b) (Chs 64, 65). It lies posterior to the body of the pancreas and splenic vein and is separated from the aorta by the left renal vein. It passes anteriorly and inferiorly, anterior to the uncinate process of the pancreas and the horizontal (third) part of the duodenum, to enter the root of the mesentery of the small intestines and supply derivatives of the midgut. Numerous anatomical variants have been described (Tubbs et al 2016), the most common being an accessory or replaced right hepatic artery arising near the origin of the superior mesenteric artery. Present in about 15% of individuals, the accessory or replaced right hepatic artery courses posterior to the hepatic portal vein and ascends posterolateral to the bile duct (Winston et al 2007).
Inferior mesenteric artery
The inferior mesenteric artery is smaller than the superior mesenteric artery. It arises from the anterior or left anterolateral aspect of the abdominal aorta at about the level of the body of the third lumbar vertebra, 3-4 cm proximal to the aortic bifurcation and posterior to the inferior border of the horizontal part of the duodenum (Ch. 65).
Lateral group
Suprarenal artery
The right and left middle suprarenal arteries arise from each side of the abdominal aorta, level with the superior mesenteric artery. Each passes laterally over the crus of the respiratory diaphragm to the suprarenal gland, where it anastomoses with the suprarenal branches of the ipsilateral inferior phrenic and renal arteries (Toni et al 1988) (Ch. 70). The right middle suprarenal artery passes posterior to the inferior vena cava near the right coeliac ganglion. The left middle suprarenal artery passes close to the left coeliac ganglion, splenic artery and the superior border of the pancreas.
Renal artery
The renal arteries are two of the largest branches of the abdominal aorta and arise laterally just inferior to the origin of the superior mesenteric artery at about the level of the body of the first lumbar vertebra (Mirjalili et al 2012b) (Ch. 72). When the arteries arise at different cranio-caudal levels, the right ostium is usually superior to the left. The right renal artery is longer and passes posterior to the inferior vena cava, right renal vein, head of the pancreas and descending part of the duodenum. The left renal artery passes behind the left renal vein, the body of the pancreas and the splenic vein. Variations in the number, origin, course and branching patterns of the renal arteries are common.
Gonadal artery
The gonadal arteries (ovarian or testicular) are two long, slender vessels that arise from the aorta a little inferior to the renal arteries. Each passes inferolaterally deep to the posterior parietal peritoneum covering psoas major to supply the ipsilateral gonad (Chs 74, 75). One or both gonadal arteries can arise from a renal artery or be double.
Dorsal group
Inferior phrenic arteries
The inferior phrenic arteries usually arise either from the aorta, just superior to the level of the coeliac trunk, or directly from the coeliac trunk; occasionally, they originate from the renal artery (Loukas et al 2005a, Gwon et al 2007). They contribute to the arterial supply of the respiratory diaphragm. Each artery ascends laterally, anterior to the crus of the respiratory diaphragm, near the medial border of the ipsilateral suprarenal gland and then divides into an ascending and a descending branch. The left ascending branch passes behind the oesophagus and runs anteriorly on the left side of the oesophageal hiatus, where it bifurcates; one branch curves anteriorly to anastomose with its counterpart anterior to the central tendon of the respiratory diaphragm and the other approaches the thoracic wall to anastomose with the musculophrenic and pericardiacophrenic arteries. The right ascending branch passes posterior to the inferior vena cava and then bifurcates; one branch runs anteriorly on the right side of the caval foramen before anastomosing with its counterpart anterior to the central tendon of the respiratory diaphragm, and the other passes laterally on the inferior surface of the respiratory diaphragm. The descending branches on each side supply the respiratory diaphragm and anastomose with the lower posterior intercostal and musculophrenic arteries. Each inferior phrenic artery gives off two or three small superior suprarenal arteries. The abdominal part of the oesophagus, fibrous capsule of the liver and the superior border of the spleen may also receive small arterial twigs from this vessel.
The inferior phrenic artery may be a source of significant collateral blood flow to large hepatocellular cancers and is sometimes specifically occluded, along with the relevant hepatic artery, when such tumours are treated by arterial embolization.
Lumbar arteries
There are usually four lumbar arteries on each side, in series with the posterior intercostal arteries. They arise from the posterolateral aspect of the abdominal aorta, opposite the lumbar vertebrae. A fifth, smaller, pair occasionally arises from the median sacral artery, but lumbar branches of the iliolumbar arteries often take their place. The lumbar arteries run posterolaterally on the first to the fourth lumbar vertebral bodies, passing behind the sympathetic trunk and tendinous arches formed by the attachments of psoas major to the vertebral bodies. The right lumbar arteries pass posterior to the inferior vena cava. The superior two right lumbar arteries and the first left lumbar artery lie behind the corresponding crus of the respiratory diaphragm. Just beyond the intervertebral foramina, each lumbar artery divides into a medial branch, which gives off spinal and ganglionic branches; a middle branch, from which dorsal and anastomotic branches arise; and a lateral branch, which supplies the abdominal wall (Arslan et al 2011). Of particular importance is the spinal branch known as the great radicular artery (artery of Adamkiewicz), which frequently originates from an upper lumbar artery, particularly on the left side (Biglioli et al 2004) (Ch. 47). Injury to this vessel, e.g. during thoracoabdominal aortic surgery, can cause spinal cord infarction.
The lateral branch of each lumbar artery runs posterior to psoas major and the lumbar plexus, then across the anterior surface of quadratus lumborum, before piercing the posterior aspect of transversus abdominis to run forwards between it and internal abdominal oblique. Perforating branches pass posteriorly to supply the muscles and skin of the posterior abdominal wall (Kiil et al 2009). The lumbar arteries anastomose with one another and the lower posterior intercostal, subcostal, iliolumbar, deep circumflex iliac and inferior epigastric arteries.
The dorsal branch of each lumbar artery passes backwards between the adjacent transverse processes to supply the back muscles, vertebrae and their joints and the skin of the back.
Median sacral artery
The median sacral artery is a small branch that usually arises from the posterior aspect of the aorta a little above its bifurcation. It descends close to the midline, anterior to the fourth and fifth lumbar vertebrae, sacrum and coccyx. At the level of the fifth lumbar vertebra it is crossed by the left common iliac vein and often gives off a small lumbar artery (arteriae lumbales imae), whose small branches reach the anus and rectum via the anococcygeal ligament. Anterior to the fifth lumbar vertebra, the median sacral artery anastomoses with a lumbar branch of the iliolumbar artery. Anterior to the sacrum, it anastomoses with the lateral sacral arteries and sends branches into the anterior sacral foramina.

Inferior vena cava

The inferior vena cava (Figs 61.12–61.13) conveys blood to the right atrium from almost all of the structures inferior to the respiratory diaphragm. Most of its course is within the abdomen, but a small segment lies within the pericardium in the thoracic cavity.
The inferior vena cava is formed by the junction of the left and right common iliac veins anterior to the fifth lumbar vertebral body, about 1 cm to the right of the midline. It ascends anterior to the vertebral column, to the right of the aorta, and lies in a deep groove on the posterior surface of the liver, sometimes completely surrounded by liver tissue. It traverses the central tendon of the respiratory diaphragm and passes through the fibrous pericardium and a posterior inflection of the serous pericardium to open into the posteroinferior part of the right atrium. Its abdominal portion is devoid of valves. The inferior vena cava can be encircled and controlled between the renal veins inferiorly and the hepatic veins superiorly, e.g. during operations on a renal cell carcinoma extending into the inferior vena cava.

Relations of the abdominal part of the inferior vena cava

The inferior vena cava lies behind the parietal peritoneum lining the posterior abdominal wall. At its origin it is related anteriorly to the right common iliac artery. From below upwards, its anterior surface is crossed obliquely by the root of the mesentery of the small intestine and its contained vessels and nerves, the right gonadal artery and the horizontal part of the duodenum. Further cranially, it lies posterior to the head of the pancreas and superior (first) part of the duodenum, separated from those structures by the bile duct and hepatic portal vein. Superior to the duodenum, its anterior surface is covered by the parietal peritoneum lining the posterior abdominal wall, which forms the posterior wall of the omental foramen and separates the inferior vena cava from the right free border of the lesser omentum, i.e. the hepatoduodenal ligament and its contents. Superior to this, it is intimately related to the liver anteriorly.
The posterior relations of the inferior vena cava include the lower three lumbar vertebral bodies and their intervertebral discs, the anterior longitudinal ligament, sympathetic trunk, right third and fourth lumbar arteries, and psoas major on the right. Superior to these structures, it is related posteriorly to the right renal and middle suprarenal arteries, the medial part of the right suprarenal gland, the right coeliac ganglion and the right inferior phrenic arteries.
The right ureter, medial border of the right kidney, descending part of the duodenum and the right lobe of the liver are all lateral to the right side of the inferior vena cava. The abdominal aorta, right crus of the respiratory diaphragm and the caudate lobe of the liver are on its left side.
The normal diameter of an adult inferior vena cava is up to 30 mm (Moeller and Reif 2000); its cross-sectional shape and calibre reflect the degree of venous filling. Anatomical variants, related to its complex embryogenesis (Ch. 13), are well described. Among these are a double inferior vena cava (the left-sided vessel usually joins the left renal vein); azygos continuation of the inferior vena cava; a left-sided inferior vena cava (which may exist in isolation or as part of situs inversus) (Ang et al 2013, Spentzouris et al 2014).

Tributaries

The inferior vena cava usually receives the common iliac veins at its origin and the lumbar, right gonadal, renal, right suprarenal, hepatic and inferior phrenic veins throughout its course (see Fig. 61.12).
Lumbar veins
Four pairs of lumbar veins collect blood from the territories supplied by the corresponding lumbar arteries, including the posterior, lateral and anterior abdominal walls. These branches anastomose posteriorly with tributaries of the azygos and hemiazygos veins, and anteriorly with branches of the epigastric, circumflex iliac and lateral thoracic veins. These superficial anastomoses provide alternative routes of venous drainage from the pelvis and lower limbs to the heart when the inferior vena cava is obstructed. The lumbar veins communicate with the external and internal vertebral venous plexuses, providing an additional collateral pathway for venous return.
The paired longitudinal ascending lumbar veins connect the individual ipsilateral lumbar veins. The third and fourth lumbar veins usually pass anteriorly on the sides of the corresponding vertebral bodies to enter the posterior aspect of the inferior vena cava; the left lumbar veins pass posterior to the abdominal aorta and are therefore longer. The first and second lumbar veins are much more variable and may drain into the inferior vena cava or the ascending lumbar, lumbar azygos or renal veins (on the left); they are often connected to each other. The first lumbar vein often passes inferiorly to join the second lumbar vein or, less commonly, drains either directly into the ascending lumbar vein or passes anteriorly over the body of the first lumbar vertebra to join the lumbar azygos vein. The second lumbar vein may join the inferior vena cava at or near the level of the renal veins or, less commonly, join the third lumbar or ascending lumbar vein.
Ascending lumbar veins
The paired ascending lumbar veins run medial to psoas major and connect the ipsilateral common iliac, iliolumbar and lumbar veins. The ascending lumbar vein is variable in its course and connections; rarely, the whole vein or a segment is absent on one side (Lolis et al 2011). It commonly joins the subcostal vein to form the azygos vein on the right and the hemiazygos on the left. The azygos and hemiazygos veins run anteriorly over the body of the twelfth thoracic vertebra and pass deep to or through the right and left crura of the respiratory diaphragm, respectively, into the thoracic cavity (Ch. 56). The ascending lumbar vein is usually joined by a small vein, the lumbar azygos vein, from the back of the inferior vena cava or left renal vein. Sometimes, the ascending lumbar vein ends in the first lumbar vein, which then joins the lumbar azygos vein at the level of the first lumbar vertebra. Blood can flow in either direction in the ascending lumbar veins (Morita et al 2007).
Gonadal veins
The right gonadal vein enters the inferior vena cava directly on its right anterolateral aspect at an acute angle about 2 cm inferior to the left renal vein in adults; occasionally, it drains into the right renal vein (Tubbs et al 2016). The left gonadal vein terminates in the left renal vein. Both veins may be replaced by multiple vessels in the lower abdomen and sometimes remain double as far as their termination. Of the two gonadal veins, the left is more prone to develop venous incompetence.
Renal veins
The renal veins are large-calibre vessels that lie anterior to the renal arteries and open into the inferior vena cava almost at right angles (Ch. 72). The left is three times longer than the right (approximately 7.5 cm and 2.5 cm, respectively), and lies on the posterior abdominal wall posterior to the splenic vein and body of the pancreas. It passes to the right in the angle between the abdominal aorta posteriorly and the superior mesenteric artery anteriorly and empties into the inferior vena cava. Occasionally, the left renal vein or an accessory left renal vein is retro-aortic. The right renal vein lies posterior to the descending part of the duodenum and sometimes to the lateral part of the head of the pancreas.
Suprarenal vein
Most commonly, a single vein drains each suprarenal gland (Ch. 70). The short right suprarenal vein drains directly into the inferior vena cava at the level of the twelfth thoracic vertebra; the longer left vein usually joins the left renal vein and may receive the left inferior phrenic vein. Numerous anatomical variations of this vein have been described (Cesmebasi et al 2014).
Inferior phrenic veins
The inferior phrenic veins usually originate on the superior surface of the respiratory diaphragm but run mostly on its inferior surface to drain into the posterolateral aspect of the inferior vena cava. Both veins receive several diaphragmatic tributaries. The left vein runs to the left of the oesophageal hiatus, whereas the right courses to the right of the caval foramen; each receives a venous tributary from the oesophagus (Loukas et al 2005b). The left inferior phrenic vein frequently communicates with the left gastric vein and may therefore become prominent in portal hypertension. Both inferior phrenic veins usually drain directly into the inferior vena cava just inferior to the respiratory diaphragm, sometimes joining anteriorly to form a short common trunk. The right occasionally drains into the right hepatic vein or inferior vena cava superior to the respiratory diaphragm, whereas the left frequently drains into the left suprarenal, left renal or left hepatic vein.
Collaterals developing as a result of occlusion of the inferior vena cava
Obstruction of the inferior vena cava from thrombosis, embolism, extrinsic compression (e.g. tumour or pregnancy) or intrinsic disease results in the development of an extensive venous collateral circulation via tributaries that ultimately connect to the superior vena cava. These include the azygos–hemiazygos venous system, the vertebral venous plexuses and the superficial veins of the thoracic and abdominal walls. The lumbar veins contribute significantly to these pathways (Golub et al 1992).

Lymphatic drainage

Lymph from the skin and subcutaneous tissues of the abdominal wall drains via small calibre superficial lymphatics to axillary and inguinal nodes (Tourani et al 2013). The midline and the level of the umbilicus form inconstant and variable watershed boundaries for these drainage territories. Lymphatics from the deeper layers of the body wall and the abdominal and pelvic viscera drain almost exclusively to the cisterna chyli and thoracic duct. The former drains via ipsilateral nodes in the retroperitoneum concentrated around the external iliac and common iliac vessels and along the lateral aspects of the aorta and inferior vena cava. There is considerable overlap between the lymphatic drainage of individual viscera. At certain sites there is a cranio-caudal sequence of lymph nodes, e.g. lateral aortic nodes; and parasternal and inferior epigastric nodes. Some lymphatic drainage from the bare area of the liver occurs directly across the respiratory diaphragm to the thoracic cavity.
The paired viscera of the retroperitoneum drain to lateral aortic nodes around the origin of their arterial supply. Thus, the kidneys and suprarenal glands drain to nodes around the hilum of the kidney and to lateral aortic nodes around the origin of the renal arteries (first and second lumbar vertebral levels). The testes drain to lateral aortic and lateral, precaval and retrocaval nodes (approximately second lumbar vertebral level); the pattern of lymphatic spread from a testicular malignancy suggests that lymph from the left testis drains to left lateral aortic nodes en route to the cisterna chyli, whereas lymph from the right testis drains to right and then left lateral aortic nodes before reaching the cisterna chyli. Lymph from the ovary may drain via lymphatics in the broad ligament of the uterus and round ligament of the uterus to internal iliac and inguinal nodes, respectively. Lymphatic drainage of the ureters is less well defined but follows a regional pattern to nearby nodes.
The lymphatic drainage of the retroperitoneal colon and rectum is described in Chapter 65. The pancreas drains to numerous pancreatic nodes (Ch. 68) and to nodes between the hepatic portal vein and inferior vena cava (clinically referred to as portacaval nodes), splenic, superior mesenteric and coeliac nodes. The lymphatic drainage of the duodenum is not well described but the lymphatics generally drain to nodes related to local arteries (Ch. 64). Whilst the clinical pattern of nodal metastases usually reflects normal lymphatic drainage pathways, lymphatic obstruction may lead to metastases at unusual nodal sites.

Cisterna chyli and abdominal lymph trunks

The abdominal origin of the thoracic duct usually lies to the right of the midline at the level of the body of the twelfth thoracic vertebra or the intervertebral disc between the twelfth thoracic and first lumbar vertebrae. It receives almost all the lymph from below the respiratory diaphragm via the cisterna chyli, a localized saccular or fusiform lymphatic dilation formed most commonly by the union of the intestinal trunk and the left lumbar trunk (Phang et al 2014). The cisterna chyli is approxinately 1 cm wide and 2 cm long in the cadaver (Loukas et al 2007): studies using magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) in vivo have shown that it expands considerably during movement from supine to sitting and again from sitting to standing (Niggemann et al 2010).
The formation of the cisterna chyli is variable and it may be absent. When present, it usually lies anterior to the first and second lumbar vertebrae, posterior to the right crus of the respiratory diaphragm and to the right of the abdominal aorta, but it may be located at a more proximal vertebral level (Fig. 61.14). The most common configuration, found in approximately two-thirds of individuals, is a single structure formed from the union of the intestinal trunk and the left (or, less commonly, the right) lumbar trunk (Loukas et al 2007). In most other individuals it is formed by the confluence of the intestinal and both lumbar trunks, with additional small branches from intercostal lymphatics (intercostal trunks) and retro-aortic nodes (Skandalakis et al 2004). The proximal two right lumbar arteries and the right lumbar azygos vein lie between the cisterna chyli and the vertebral column.
The paired lumbar trunks are formed by lymphatic vessels draining lateral aortic nodes. The intestinal trunk (which may be double) receives vessels draining from coeliac, superior and inferior mesenteric nodes that collectively drain the entire abdominal gastrointestinal tract down to the anal canal. The thoracic duct leaves the superior end of the cisterna chyli and passes through the aortic hiatus of the respiratory diaphragm posterolateral to the right side of the aorta. Its thoracic continuation is described in Chapter 56.
The major lymphatic vessels adjacent to the abdominal aorta are at risk of injury during surgical procedures that involve peri-aortic dissection and/or lymphadenectomy. Injury to these lymphatic vessels or their obstruction by malignant disease can cause leakage of lymph manifesting as chylous ascites or chylothorax.

Retroperitoneal lymph node groups

The lymphatic drainage of the rectum, colon, stomach, pancreas, oesophagus and other organs is often described in terms of lymph node stations and levels of dissection that relate to the management of malignant disease. The terminology and classification of retroperitoneal lymph nodes are based on their location. In classic anatomical descriptions, the lymph nodes of the retroperitoneum around the abdominal aorta are grouped into pre-aortic, lateral and retro-aortic nodes, but adjacent nodal groups merge into one another with no clear demarcating boundaries. Normal lymph node dimensions are very variable. Cross-sectional imaging frequently uses 10 mm as an approximate measurement for the upper limit of normal lymph node dimensions in the adult (Moeller and Reif 2000): some normal retroperitoneal nodes such as the portacaval nodes are typically larger.
Pre-aortic nodes
Pre-aortic nodes drain the gastrointestinal viscera including the pancreas, liver and spleen. They lie anterior to the aorta clustered around its anterior unpaired visceral branches and can be subdivided into coeliac, superior mesenteric and inferior mesenteric nodes. Their efferent lymphatics contribute to the formation of the intestinal lymph trunk.
Coeliac nodes
Coeliac nodes drain lymph from nodes around the stomach, splenic hilum, porta hepatis, cystic duct, lesser omentum, portacaval nodes, pancreatic nodes and pancreaticoduodenal nodes. They also receive lymph from superior and inferior mesenteric nodes and their efferent lymphatics drain to the intestinal trunks.
Superior mesenteric nodes
Superior mesenteric nodes share an extensive overlap with the drainage territory of coeliac nodes, with subsidiary nodal groups that include pancreatic, pancreaticoduodenal, portacaval, ileocolic, mesenteric and inferior mesenteric nodes. Efferent lymphatics drain to the intestinal trunks either directly or via coeliac nodes.
Inferior mesenteric nodes
Inferior mesenteric nodes such as the superior rectal nodes drain lymph from derivatives of the hindgut, including the distal transverse, descending and sigmoid colons, and the rectum.
Lateral aortic groups
Lateral aortic (para-aortic) nodes lie on either side of the abdominal aorta and inferior vena cava anterior to the medial margins of psoas major, the crura of the respiratory diaphragm and the sympathetic trunks. Constituent nodal groups that are recognized clinically include the retrocrural (posterior to the diaphragmatic crura at the aortic hiatus); left and right hilar (of the kidney); aortocaval, lateral caval, retrocaval and precaval groups. Retro-aortic nodes are sometimes included within the lateral aortic group. The latter drain into the paired intestinal trunks that terminate directly or indirectly in the cisterna chyli and thoracic duct. There are lymphatic connections between lateral aortic, pre-aortic, retro-aortic and contralateral lateral aortic nodes. The lateral aortic nodes drain the deep layers of the body wall, the retroperitoneal paired viscera (including the gonads) and tributaries from the iliac nodes. Lymph from the right ovary or testis drains via lymphatics travelling with the gonadal vessels to the right lateral aortic and pre-aortic nodes at the level of the second lumbar vertebra, whereas lymph from the left ovary or testis drains to the left lateral aortic nodes just inferior to the left renal vein (Morton and Thomas 2008, Paño et al 2011).
Iliac nodes
Paired iliac nodes are distributed around the common, external and internal iliac arteries and veins. Constituent groups include the common iliac, external iliac, internal iliac and obturator nodes. The latter are located near the obturator foramen and, together with the iliac nodes, are a common site of lymph node metastasis in prostate cancer. ‘Pelvic side-wall’ nodes, lying adjacent to the ilium but not associated with a large artery or vein, are also described in the clinical literature. The iliac nodes drain the pelvic viscera and walls, except for the ovaries and those parts of the rectum drained by superior anorectal nodes. They also drain lymph from the inguinal nodes and lower limbs.

Innervation

The posterior abdominal wall contains the lumbar plexus and numerous autonomic plexuses and ganglia that lie close to the abdominal aorta and its branches (Figs 61.15–61.16).

Lumbar plexus

The lumbar ventral rami pass laterally into the posterior part of psoas major, anterior to the transverse processes of the lumbar vertebrae (Moro et al 2003, Benglis et al 2009). The first four lumbar ventral rami, usually together with a contribution from the twelfth thoracic ventral ramus, form the lumbar plexus. Although there are many variations, the most common arrangement of the plexus is described here (Tubbs et al 2018).
The first lumbar ventral ramus is often joined by a branch from the twelfth thoracic ventral ramus, and these contribute to the formation of the iliohypogastric and ilio-inguinal nerves, which run laterally on the posterior abdominal wall. A branch from the first lumbar ventral ramus unites with a branch from the second lumbar ventral ramus to form the genitofemoral nerve. The second, third and most of the fourth lumbar ventral rami divide into ventral and dorsal divisions; the ventral divisions unite to form the obturator nerve, while most of the nerve fibres in the dorsal divisions form the femoral nerve. The remaining fibres from the fourth lumbar ventral ramus join the fifth lumbar ventral ramus to form the lumbosacral trunk, which descends to join the sacral plexus. Branches from the dorsal divisions of the second and third lumbar ventral rami unite to form the lateral femoral cutaneous nerve. The accessory obturator nerve, when present, usually arises from the third and fourth ventral rami. The lumbar plexus is supplied by branches from the lumbar vessels that supply psoas major.
The division of constituent ventral rami into ventral and dorsal branches is not as clear in the lumbar and sacral plexuses as it is in the brachial plexus. Lateral cutaneous branches of the twelfth thoracic and first lumbar ventral rami are drawn into the gluteal skin, but otherwise these nerves are similar to intercostal nerves. The second lumbar ventral ramus is more complex. It contributes substantially to the femoral and obturator nerves, and also has an anterior terminal branch (the genital branch of the genitofemoral) and a lateral cutaneous branch that contributes to the lateral femoral cutaneous nerve and the femoral branch of the genitofemoral nerve. Anterior terminal branches of the third to fifth lumbar and first sacral ventral rami are suppressed, but the corresponding branches of the second and third sacral ventral rami supply the perineal skin.
The furcal nerve is an independent nerve with its own ventral and dorsal rootlets most commonly arising alongside the fourth lumbar spinal nerve. Its branches contribute to the femoral and obturator nerves arising from the lumbar plexus and to the lumbosacral trunk (Harshavardhana and Dabke 2014). The term ‘furcal’ refers to its forked nature, since it links the lumbar and sacral plexuses. Occasionally, the furcal nerve arises at the level of either the third or the fifth lumbar spinal nerves, in which case the sacral plexus is considered prefixed or postfixed, respectively.
Inflammatory processes, such as retrocaecal appendicitis on the right and diverticular abscess on the left, can occur in the posterior abdominal wall in the tissues immediately anterior to psoas major. These may irritate one or more branches of the lumbar plexus, causing pain or sensory disturbance in the distribution of the affected nerves, e.g. the skin of the thigh, hip or buttock.

Muscular branches

Small branches from the lumbar ventral rami supply adjacent muscles such as psoas major and quadratus lumborum (Mahan et al 2017).

Iliohypogastric nerve

Distribution

The iliohypogastric nerve usually originates from the first lumbar ventral ramus but may arise wholly or in part from the twelfth thoracic ventral ramus (Klaassen et al 2011). It emerges from the upper lateral border of psoas major and crosses obliquely posterior to the inferior pole of the kidney on the anterior surface of quadratus lumborum. Above the iliac crest, it enters the posterior part of transversus abdominis and then runs anteriorly between transversus abdominis and internal abdominal oblique, which it supplies. It gives off a lateral cutaneous branch that pierces internal and external abdominal oblique above the iliac crest and supplies the posterolateral gluteal skin. It continues forwards, pierces internal abdominal oblique approximately 3 cm medial and 1 cm inferior to the anterior superior iliac spine (in adults), and then penetrates the external abdominal oblique aponeurosis approximately 3 cm above and medial to the superficial inguinal ring to supply the skin of the hypogastric region. The iliohypogastric nerve frequently connects with the ilio-inguinal nerve and less commonly with the subcostal nerve. Motor branches of the iliohypogastric nerve innervate transversus abdominis and internal abdominal oblique. Cutaneous branches innervate the skin of the posterolateral gluteal and hypogastric regions.

Injury

The iliohypogastric nerve is occasionally injured by a surgical incision in the right inguinal region, e.g. during an inguinal hernia repair, open appendicectomy or abdominal wall trocar placement, but there is rarely any noticeable sensory loss because the skin of the hypogastric region is innervated from several sources. Division of the iliohypogastric nerve above the anterior superior iliac spine may weaken the posterior wall of the inguinal canal and predispose to formation of a direct inguinal hernia.

Ilio-inguinal nerve

Distribution

The ilio-inguinal nerve usually originates from the first lumbar ventral ramus but may receive a contribution from the twelfth thoracic or second lumbar ventral rami (Klaassen et al 2011). It emerges from the lateral border of psoas major, with or just inferior to the iliohypogastric nerve, passes obliquely across the anterior aspect of quadratus lumborum and the superior part of iliacus and enters transversus abdominis about 3 cm medial and 4 cm inferior to the anterior superior iliac spine (in the adult); at this site, it is readily blocked by local anaesthetic. In this region, a branch may connect with the iliohypogastric or lateral femoral cutaneous nerve. It pierces internal abdominal oblique more inferiorly, supplies it, and then traverses the remainder of the inguinal canal superficial to the spermatic cord or round ligament of the uterus. It emerges with the spermatic cord from the superficial inguinal ring and divides into terminal cutaneous branches.
Numerous anatomical variations have been described (Al-dabbagh 2002, Ndiaye et al 2007). The ilio-inguinal and genitofemoral nerves may interconnect within the inguinal canal, so that each innervates the skin of the genitalia to a variable extent (Rab et al 2001, Cesmebasi et al 2015). The ilio-inguinal nerve may pierce the aponeurosis of external abdominal oblique proximal to the superficial inguinal ring or divide into terminal branches within the inguinal canal. It may form a common trunk with the iliohypogastric nerve as far as the midpoint of the inguinal canal or may be very small or absent, in which case the iliohypogastric nerve and genital branch of the genitofemoral nerve supply its territory. Rarely it arises as a branch of the genitofemoral nerve. Motor branches of the ilio-inguinal nerve supply transversus abdominis and internal abdominal oblique; the fibres are given off before the nerve enters the lateral aspect of the inguinal canal. Sensory branches innervate the skin of the proximal medial thigh and the skin over the root of the penis and proximal part of the scrotum in males (anterior scrotal nerve), or the skin covering the mons pubis and the adjoining labium majus in females (anterior labial nerve).

Injury

The ilio-inguinal nerve may be injured or entrapped during inguinal surgery, particularly for inguinal hernia repair, leading to sensory disturbances including pain from the external genitalia and proximal medial thigh (Ndiaye et al 2007).

Genitofemoral nerve

Distribution

The genitofemoral nerve originates from the first and second lumbar ventral rami and is formed within the substance of psoas major. It descends obliquely anteriorly through the muscle to emerge on its anterior surface nearer the medial border, opposite the third or fourth lumbar vertebra (Moro et al 2003). It then descends deep to the posterior parietal peritoneum overlying psoas major, crosses obliquely posterior to the ureter, and divides into genital and femoral branches; it may divide close to its origin such that its branches emerge separately from psoas major. The genital branch crosses the distal part of the external iliac artery, enters the inguinal canal through the deep ring and accompanies the spermatic cord or round ligament of the uterus. It exits the superficial inguinal ring, usually posterior to the spermatic cord or round ligament of the uterus, and supplies cremaster and the skin of the external genitalia. The femoral branch descends lateral to the external iliac artery before crossing the deep circumflex iliac artery to pass deep to the inguinal ligament (occasionally, through it) (Rab et al 2001) and enter the femoral sheath lateral to the femoral artery. It pierces the anterior aspect of the femoral sheath and fascia lata and supplies the skin over the proximal part of the femoral triangle. It may connect with the lateral femoral cutaneous nerve and anterior femoral cutaneous branches of the femoral nerve. Motor branches of the genitofemoral nerve innervate cremaster via its genital branch. Sensory fibres travelling in the genital branch innervate the skin of the scrotum in males and of the mons pubis and labium majus in females; there is considerable variability and overlap with the cutaneous distribution of the ilio-inguinal nerve (Cesmebasi et al 2015).

Injury

The genital branch of the genitofemoral nerve may be injured during inguinal hernia repair (open or laparoscopic), leading to neuralgia (Cesmebasi et al 2015).

Femoral nerve

The femoral nerve descends through psoas major and emerges on or deep to its lateral border, about 4 cm above the inguinal ligament (Moore and Stringer 2011). It passes between psoas major and iliacus deep to the iliac fascia and lies lateral to the femoral artery and sheath as it runs posterior to the inguinal ligament into the thigh. It gives off motor branches that supply iliacus, pectineus, sartorius and quadriceps femoris; articular branches to the hip, knee and ankle joints; branches to the femoral artery; cutaneous branches that supply the majority of the skin covering the anterior thigh, medial leg and medial foot (see Fig. 61.16). Injury to the femoral nerve may result in loss of sensation and paraesthesias within its cutaneous distribution, as well as difficulty ambulating, especially descending stairs. The further course and distribution of the femoral nerve are described in Chapter 77.

Lateral femoral cutaneous nerve

The lateral femoral cutaneous nerve (lateral cutaneous nerve of the thigh) is usually derived from the second and third lumbar ventral rami, but variable contributions from the first to the third lumbar ventral rami have been described (de Ridder et al 1999). The nerve emerges from the posterolateral border of psoas major and crosses iliacus obliquely, running towards the anterior superior iliac spine. It supplies sensory fibres to the parietal peritoneum in the iliac fossa. On the right, the nerve passes posterolateral to the caecum, from which it is separated by the iliac fascia and peritoneum; on the left, it passes posterior to the inferior part of the descending colon. Both nerves usually pass posterior to the inguinal ligament about 1–2 cm medial to the anterior superior iliac spine; occasionally they pass through or, rarely, anterior to the ligament (Tubbs et al 2016). A separate fascial canal for this nerve has been described (Hanna 2017). Occasionally, the nerve lies anterior or superior to the anterior superior iliac spine as it enters the thigh. In the thigh, the lateral femoral cutaneous nerve usually passes anterior or lateral to sartorius but may pierce the muscle. Its further course and distribution are described in (Chapter 77).

Obturator nerve

The obturator nerve is formed within the substance of psoas major to emerge from its posteromedial border near the fifth lumbar vertebra (Kirchmair et al 2008). It passes posterior to the common iliac vessels and lateral to the internal iliac vessels, descends on the lateral wall of the pelvis attached to the obturator fascia, and lies anterosuperior to the obturator vessels before running into the obturator canal to enter the thigh. The obturator nerve is said to have no branches in the abdomen or pelvis, although it has been reported to send articular branches to the anterior aspect of the sacroiliac joint. Its sensory branches innervate the hip and knee joints, and a small, variable patch of skin on the proximal medial thigh, and its motor branches innervate obturator externus, adductor longus, adductor brevis, adductor magnus, gracilis and sometimes pectineus (see Fig. 61.16) (Tubbs et al 2016).
Compression of the obturator nerve, e.g. by an obturator hernia, may result in weakness of the muscles of the adductor compartment of the thigh and pain and paraesthesias along the medial thigh, inferiorly to the knee (Howship-Romberg sign). The further course and distribution are described in Chapter 77.

Accessory obturator nerve

The accessory obturator nerve is sometimes present, more often on the left (Turgut et al 2017), and is usually formed by the third and fourth lumbar ventral rami. It emerges from the medial border of psoas major, lateral to the obturator nerve, and runs over the superior pubic ramus posterior to pectineus, where it gives off branches to the muscle and sometimes to the hip joint; it may join with the anterior branch of the obturator nerve (Ch. 77).

Lumbar sympathetic system

The lumbar part of each sympathetic trunk usually contains four interconnected ganglia lying in extraperitoneal connective tissue on the anterolateral aspects of the lumbar vertebrae along the medial margin of psoas major (see Fig. 61.10). Superiorly, it is continuous with the thoracic part of the sympathetic trunk posterior to the medial arcuate ligament at the level of the first and second lumbar vertebrae (Feigl et al 2011). Inferiorly, it passes posterior to the common iliac vessels and is continuous with the sacral part of the sympathetic trunk. On the right side, it lies posterior to the inferior vena cava; on the left, it is posterior to the lateral aortic nodes. It is anterior to most of the lumbar vessels but may pass behind some lumbar veins.
The first, second, and sometimes the third, lumbar ventral rami are each connected to the lumbar part of the sympathetic trunk by a white ramus communicans. All lumbar ventral rami are joined near their origins by long, slender grey rami communicantes from the four lumbar ganglia. Their arrangement is irregular: one ganglion may give rami to two or three lumbar ventral rami; one lumbar ventral ramus may receive rami from two ganglia; grey rami communicantes may leave the sympathetic trunk between ganglia (Murata et al 2003).
The lumbar sympathetic trunks are vulnerable during retroperitoneal lymph node dissection: their injury can impair emission of seminal fluid and lead to retrograde ejaculation.

Lumbar parasympathetic system

The parasympathetic supply to the abdominal viscera is provided by the vagus nerve to the coeliac and superior mesenteric plexuses, and by pelvic splanchnic nerves that are distributed through the inferior hypogastric and inferior mesenteric plexuses to derivatives of the hindgut (Ch. 59).

Para-aortic bodies

The para-aortic bodies (also known as paraganglia or, collectively, as the organ of Zuckerkandl), are collections of neural crest-derived chromaffin tissue closely related to the aortic autonomic plexuses. They are relatively large in the fetus, where they may have a role in maintaining blood pressure by catecholamine secretion. They reach a maximum size at around 3 years of age and have normally regressed by adulthood. They are usually found as paired structures lying anterolateral to the abdominal aorta in the region of the inferior mesenteric and superior hypogastric plexuses, but multiple smaller collections may be present. Occasionally they are found as far proximally as the coeliac plexus or as far distally as the inferior hypogastric plexus, or they may be closely applied to the ganglia of the lumbar part of the sympathetic trunk. Scattered cells that persist into adulthood may, rarely, be the sites of paraganglioma (Pacak and Tella 2018).
