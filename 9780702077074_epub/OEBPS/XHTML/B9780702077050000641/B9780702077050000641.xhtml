Chapter 64: Small intestine

Overview

The small intestine consists of the duodenum, jejunum and ileum. It extends from the pylorus to the ileocaecal junction and has a mean length of 5 metres (3–8.5 metres) when measured intraoperatively in adults (Teitelbaum et al 2013). The duodenum extends from the stomach to the duodenojejunal junction, marked by the suspensory ligament of the duodenum (ligament of Treitz). There is no clear boundary between the jejunum and ileum, but there is a gradual transition in morphology from the proximal to distal ends of the small intestine. The distal 30 cm or so of the ileum is often referred to as the terminal ileum: it has some specialized physiological functions.
The duodenum is in the proximal abdomen and is mostly retroperitoneal. The jejunum and ileum occupy the central and distal parts of the abdominal cavity and usually lie within the boundary formed by the colon. The small intestine is attached to the anterior wall of the retroperitoneum by a mesentery that allows the intestinal loops to be relatively mobile. In the supine position, loops of jejunum can be found anterior to the transverse colon, stomach and lesser omentum, whereas in the erect position, loops of ileum can descend into the pelvis anterior to the rectum. The greater omentum covers the proximal jejunum and ileum to a variable extent. The jejunum and ileum are covered by visceral peritoneum on all but their mesenteric borders, where the peritoneum is reflected to enclose the adipose tissue of the mesentery. The small intestine mesentery abuts about 20% of the circumference of the muscular wall of the ileum and somewhat less of the jejunum.

Duodenum

The adult duodenum is approximately 25 cm long and is the shortest, widest and most predictably located part of the small intestine. The proximal 2.5 cm is intraperitoneal and the remainder is retroperitoneal. The duodenum forms an elongated ‘C’ shape that lies between the level of the first and third lumbar vertebrae in the supine position (Kellogg 1933). The inferior ‘limb’ of the C extends further to the left of the midline than the superior limb. The head of the pancreas lies within the concavity of the duodenum, which is ‘draped’ over the prominence formed by the lumbar spine; the duodenum therefore curves in an anteroposterior direction as well as forming a ‘C’ shape. The duodenum lies entirely proximal to the level of the umbilicus. It is described as having four parts (Fig. 64.1).

Superior part

The superior (first) part is the most mobile part of the duodenum and is about 5 cm long. It starts at the termination of the pylorus of the stomach and ends at the superior duodenal flexure. The proximal 2.5 cm is intraperitoneal. The distal 2.5 cm is covered by peritoneum on its anterior and superior surfaces and forms the inferior boundary of the omental foramen (of Winslow). The lesser omentum is attached to its superior border and the greater omentum to its inferior border. The first 2–3 cm of the duodenum is lined by relatively smooth mucosa and readily distends on insufflation during endoscopy. This part is frequently referred to as the duodenal bulb (duodenal cap). Beyond the duodenal bulb, the internal appearance is characterized by circular folds of mucosa that remain pronounced, even during endoscopic insufflation (Fig. 64.2). During contrast radiology, the duodenal bulb shows a few longitudinal folds continuous with the pylorus (Mather Cordiner and Calthrop 1936) and appears triangular; it is often visible on plain radiographs of the abdomen as an isolated air shadow to the right of the first or second lumbar vertebra. The superior part of the duodenum passes superiorly, posteriorly and laterally before curving sharply inferiorly at the superior duodenal flexure. This portion transitions to a retroperitoneal position where peritoneum only covers its anterior aspect. The section from the duodenal bulb to the superior duodenal flexure lies posterior and inferior to the quadrate lobe of the liver.
The superior part of the duodenum lies anterior to the gastroduodenal artery, bile duct and hepatic portal vein and anterosuperior to the head and neck of the pancreas. The gastroduodenal artery lies immediately posterior to the posterior wall of the duodenum; a penetrating peptic ulcer on the posterior wall can erode into the gastroduodenal artery or one of its branches and cause dramatic haemorrhage. A penetrating peptic ulcer on the anterior wall can perforate into the peritoneal cavity, causing peritonitis because the anterior surface of the superior part of the duodenum is covered only by peritoneum.
Lymph nodes near the common hepatic and hepatoduodenal arteries lie close to the superior part of the duodenum (see Fig. 63.14B). They can be visualized using endoscopic ultrasound and sampled using a fine needle under endoscopic ultrasound guidance. The nodes are important in the staging of gastric, pancreatic or bile duct tumours, or identifying metastases from other solid tumours of the abdominal cavity. The proximity of the bile duct to the superior part of the duodenum enables endoscopic ultrasound examination of the distal bile duct and surgical anastomosis between bile duct and duodenum (choledochoduodenostomy) when required.

Descending part

The descending (second) part of the duodenum is approximately 8 cm long. It starts at the superior duodenal flexure and runs inferiorly in a gentle curve, convex to the right side of the vertebral column and extending to the inferior border of the third lumbar vertebral body. It then turns sharply medially at the inferior duodenal flexure, which marks its junction with the horizontal (third) part of the duodenum. The descending part of the duodenum is covered by peritoneum only on its proximal anterior surface. It lies posterior to the gallbladder and the right lobe of the liver at its start and is crossed anteriorly by the transverse colon. The right end of the gastrocolic ligament and the origin of the transverse mesocolon are attached to the anterior surface of the duodenum by loose connective tissue. The connective tissue and vessels forming the mesentery of the superior aspect of the ascending colon and right colic flexure are loosely attached to its anterior surface, inferior to the attachment of the transverse mesocolon. This part of the duodenum is at risk of iatrogenic injury during surgical mobilization of the ascending colon and right colic flexure.
The descending part of the duodenum lies anterior to the hilum of the right kidney, the right renal vessels, the lateral edge of the inferior vena cava and right psoas major (see Fig. 64.1C). The head of the pancreas and the bile duct are medial and the right colic flexure is cephalad and lateral. Part of the head of the pancreas is sometimes embedded in the medial duodenal wall, and pancreatic ‘rests’ in the duodenal wall can produce small filling defects on contrast radiology. The internal appearance is similar to that of the distal portion of the superior part of the duodenum, with pronounced mucosal folds (Fig. 64.3). The bile duct and pancreatic duct (of Wirsung) enter the medial wall, where they usually unite to form a common channel, which frequently contains a dilated segment known as the hepatopancreatic ampulla (of Vater) (Ch. 68). The narrow distal end of this channel opens on the summit of the major duodenal papilla, a mucosal elevation situated on the posteromedial wall of the descending part of the duodenum, 8–10 cm distal to the pylorus. A duodenal mucosal fold often partially encircles the major papilla, forming a hood (Horiguchi and Kamisawa 2010) (see Fig. 64.3). A second, accessory pancreatic duct (of Santorini) is sometimes present and usually opens about 2 cm proximal to the major duodenal papilla on the minor duodenal papilla (Kamisawa et al 2010, Suda 2010).

Duodenal diverticula

The duodenum is the most common site for a diverticulum in the small intestine. Diverticula are usually solitary and can be congenital (containing all layers of the duodenal wall) or acquired (protrusion of the mucosa and submucosa through a defect in the muscular coat of the intestinal wall). They are typically located on the medial wall of the descending part of the duodenum, intimately related to the head of the pancreas: the major duodenal papilla is frequently found either on the mucosal fold at the mouth of a diverticulum or arising from the mucosa within it. Diverticula can complicate the interpretation of contrast radiographs of the duodenum or biliary system, and can cause difficulties during attempted endoscopic cannulation of the major duodenal papilla. Most are asymptomatic but they can be complicated by bleeding, inflammation, perforation and, occasionally, pancreatitis or biliary complications (Fotiades et al 2005).
Kocher manoeuvre to expose the posterior aspect of the head of the pancreas
The Kocher manoeuvre is used to expose retroperitoneal structures, such as an injury to the inferior vena cava, or the hilum of the kidney or the head of the pancreas, especially as an early step in a pancreaticoduodenectomy operation. The manoeuvre describes the division of the posterior and lateral attachments of the descending portion of the duodenum to allow medial rotation and reflection of the duodenum and head of the pancreas. The inferior vena cava is identified posterior to the horizontal portion of the duodenum and the attachments posterior to the duodenum, medial to the right ureter and anterior to the inferior vena cava, are divided. This is carried cephalad along the lateral aspect of the descending part of the duodenum. The right renal vein and right gonadal vein are often visualized during this manoeuvre. A complete Kocher manoeuvre is carried to the lateral edge of the abdominal aorta, allowing full inspection of the inferior vena cava and the superior mesenteric artery.

Horizontal part

The horizontal (third) part of the duodenum starts at the inferior duodenal flexure and is approximately 10 cm long. It runs from the right side of the inferior border of the third lumbar vertebra and passes to the left and slightly superiorly, anterior to the inferior vena cava and abdominal aorta, becoming continuous with the ascending part of the duodenum (see Fig. 64.1C). It lies posterior to the transverse mesocolon and is crossed anteriorly by the origin of the mesentery of the small intestine and the superior mesenteric vessels. The inferior portion of its anterior aspect is covered by peritoneum, reflected anteriorly to form the posterior layer of the root of the mesentery of the small intestine. The anterior surface of its left end, close to the junction with the ascending (fourth) part, is also covered by peritoneum.
The horizontal part of the duodenum lies anterior to the right ureter, right psoas major, right gonadal vessels, inferior vena cava and abdominal aorta (at the origin of the inferior mesenteric artery), and inferior to the head of the pancreas. Anteroinferiorly, loops of jejunum lie in the right and left infracolic compartments. The middle of the horizontal part lies in the angle between the superior mesenteric artery anteriorly and the abdominal aorta posteriorly. Loss of perivascular adipose tissue can cause narrowing of this angle and is a rare cause of duodenal obstruction, known as superior mesenteric artery syndrome (Merrett et al 2009).

Ascending part

The ascending (fourth) part of the duodenum is 2.5 cm long. It starts just to the left of the abdominal aorta, runs superiorly and laterally to the level of the superior border of the second lumbar vertebra, then turns sharply anteroinferiorly at the duodenojejunal flexure to become continuous with the jejunum. The inferior mesenteric vein lies either posterior to the duodenojejunal flexure or at its lateral margin, deep to a peritoneal fold: the duodenojejunal flexure is a useful landmark for locating the inferior mesenteric vein radiologically or surgically. The abdominal aorta, left sympathetic trunk, left psoas major, left renal and left gonadal vessels all lie posterior, the left kidney and left ureter lie posterolateral, and the transverse colon and mesocolon are anterior, separating it from the stomach (see Fig. 61.8). The inferior border of the body of the pancreas is superior. The peritoneum of the root of the mesentery of the small intestine descends over its anterior surface.
At its left lateral limit, the ascending part of the duodenum becomes progressively invested in peritoneum such that the duodenojejunal flexure is suspended from the anterior wall of the retroperitoneum by a double fold of peritoneum, the suspensory ligament of the duodenum. This has two parts: the first part may contain skeletal muscle fibres and runs from the right crus of the diaphragm to connective tissue around the coeliac trunk, and the second part contains smooth muscle and descends from connective tissue around the coeliac trunk to the duodenum, passing posterior to the pancreas anterior to the left renal vein. The ligament is often absent or rudimentary in adults and its function is unknown (Kim et al 2008). Its length determines the location of the duodenojejunal junction. A short, narrow suspensory ligament causes the ascending part of the duodenum to take a commonly depicted course cephalad prior to the sharp inferior turn to the jejunum, whereas a broad, flat ligament makes this angle less acute. The suspensory ligament of the duodenum is avascular. The vascular supply to the ascending part of the duodenum enters its wall from the posteromedial aspect.
The duodenojejunal flexure and the suspensory ligament of the duodenum are important landmarks in the radiological and surgical diagnosis of incomplete rotation and malrotation of the small intestine (Ch. 21). For many small intestine resection operations, distance is measured from the suspensory ligament of the duodenum.

Vascular supply and lymphatic drainage

Arteries

The main vessels supplying the duodenum are the superior and inferior pancreaticoduodenal arteries. The superior and descending parts of the duodenum also receive contributions from other sources including the right gastric, supraduodenal, right gastro-omental, common hepatic and gastroduodenal arteries (Fig. 64.4). Branches of the superior pancreaticoduodenal artery may contribute to the supply of the pyloric canal, anastomosing to a minor extent with the gastric arteries within the muscular layer of the junction between the pylorus and duodenum. Because of this robust vascular supply, ischaemia of the duodenum is a rare clinical finding and is often a bellwether of severe and generalized circulatory compromise.
Gastroduodenal artery
The gastroduodenal artery usually arises from the common hepatic artery posterior or superior to the superior part of the duodenum. It descends posterior to the retroperitoneal portion of the superior part of the duodenum to the left of the bile duct. At the inferior border of the superior part of the duodenum it is commonly described as dividing into the right gastro-omental and superior pancreaticoduodenal arteries, but this anatomical arrangement is rare (Bradley 1973, Bertelli et al 1995, Bertelli et al 1996) and its usual branching pattern is as follows. As it descends posterior to the superior part of the duodenum it usually gives off the posterior superior pancreaticoduodenal artery, several retroduodenal branches that supply the superior part and the proximal portion of the descending part of the duodenum, and a supraduodenal artery that supplies the anterosuperior part of the proximal duodenum (Bianchi and Albanèse 1989). As the gastroduodenal artery emerges inferior to the superior part of the duodenum it usually gives off the right gastro-omental artery and several pyloric branches. It then descends on the anterior surface of the pancreas, where it divides into the anterior superior pancreaticoduodenal artery and pancreatic branches. Although the gastroduodenal artery usually branches from the common hepatic artery, it occasionally originates from other sources including the coeliac trunk, the superior mesenteric artery, as a trifurcation with the right and left hepatic arteries, or from the right or left hepatic artery. The gastroduodenal artery or one of its branches can be a source of haemorrhage from a penetrating posterior duodenal ulcer (see above) or a site of aneurysm or pseudoaneurysm formation, and for these reasons, it is an important vessel for interventional radiologists.
Superior pancreaticoduodenal arteries
There are usually two superior pancreaticoduodenal arteries: posterior and anterior. The posterior superior pancreaticoduodenal artery is usually a separate branch of the gastroduodenal artery and is given off posterior to the superior border of the superior part of the duodenum. It descends to the right, anterior to the hepatic portal vein and bile duct, where the latter lies posterior to the superior part of the duodenum. It then spirals around the right side of the bile duct to run posterior to the head of the pancreas, crosses posterior to the pancreatic segment of the bile duct (which is embedded, to a variable degree, in the head of the pancreas), and anastomoses with the posterior inferior pancreaticoduodenal artery (Bertelli et al 1996). The posterior superior pancreaticoduodenal artery supplies branches to the head of the pancreas, the superior and descending parts of the duodenum, and several branches to the distal part of the bile duct.
The anterior superior pancreaticoduodenal artery is usually a terminal branch of the gastroduodenal artery and descends in the anterior groove between the descending part of the duodenum and the head of the pancreas or on the anterior surface of the pancreas parallel to the groove (Bertelli et al 1995). It supplies branches to the superior and descending parts of the duodenum and to the head of the pancreas, and then passes posteriorly to anastomose with the anterior inferior pancreaticoduodenal artery.
Inferior pancreaticoduodenal artery
The inferior pancreaticoduodenal artery usually arises from the superior mesenteric artery or its first jejunal branch near the superior border of the horizontal part of the duodenum (Bertelli et al 1996). It crosses posterior to the superior mesenteric vein and passes posterior to the uncinate process of the pancreas, where it divides into the anterior and posterior pancreaticoduodenal arteries. The anterior pancreaticoduodenal artery passes to the right, immediately inferior and then anterior to the inferior border of the head of the pancreas, and runs superiorly to anastomose with the anterior superior pancreaticoduodenal artery. The posterior branch runs posteriorly to the right posterior to the head of the pancreas and anastomoses with the posterior superior pancreaticoduodenal artery (Bertelli et al 1997). Both branches supply the head of the pancreas, its uncinate process, and the superior and descending parts of the duodenum. Occasionally, the anterior and posterior pancreaticoduodenal arteries arise separately from the superior mesenteric or first jejunal artery.
Jejunal artery branches
Branches from the first jejunal branch of the superior mesenteric artery supply the ascending part of the duodenum and frequently anastomose with a terminal branch of the anterior superior pancreaticoduodenal artery. The ascending part of the duodenum therefore receives a potential collateral supply from the coeliac trunk and superior mesenteric artery, which means that it is not commonly affected by ischaemia.
Veins
Submucosal and intramural veins give rise to small veins that accompany the corresponding named arteries. The venous anatomy of this region is variable and not well characterized. The superior pancreaticoduodenal vein runs superiorly on the posterior surface of the head of the pancreas, posterior to the distal bile duct, and usually drains into the hepatic portal vein. The inferior pancreaticoduodenal vein runs inferiorly and usually drains into the superior mesenteric vein or its first jejunal tributary. Small veins from the superior part and the superior aspect of the descending part of the duodenum drain directly into the hepatic portal vein, and veins from the horizontal and ascending parts can drain directly into the superior mesenteric vein. Numerous small anastomoses are present between veins of the descending and horizontal parts of the duodenum and retroperitoneal veins (Murakami et al 1999).
Lymphatic drainage
Duodenal lymphatics run to nodes associated with the superior and inferior pancreaticoduodenal arteries, and from there to nodes related to the common hepatic and superior mesenteric arteries and coeliac trunk and to nodes in supra- and infra-pyloric positions and within the hepatoduodenal ligament. Lymphatic drainage to lateral aortic nodes has also been described (Hirai et al 2001).

Duodenal feeding

When gastric emptying is functionally impaired – as, for example, in gastroparesis – nutrition can be delivered directly into the duodenum or jejunum via a nasal transpyloric feeding tube.

Innervation

The duodenum is innervated by both parasympathetic and sympathetic neurones.
Preganglionic sympathetic neurones have their cell bodies in the intermediolateral grey matter in the fifth to the twelfth thoracic spinal cord segments. Their fibres travel via the greater and lesser thoracic splanchnic nerves to the coeliac plexus and synapse in the coeliac and superior mesenteric ganglia; postganglionic axons are distributed to the duodenal wall via peri-arterial plexuses on the branches of the coeliac trunk and superior mesenteric artery. The sympathetic nerves are vasoconstrictor to the duodenal vasculature and inhibitory to duodenal musculature.
The preganglionic parasympathetic supply is carried by vagal fibres that travel from the coeliac plexus and synapse on neurones in the duodenal wall. The parasympathetic supply is secretomotor to the duodenal mucosa and motor to the duodenal musculature.

Referred pain

In common with other structures derived from the foregut, pain arising from the proximal duodenum is poorly localized and referred to the epigastric region. It is mediated by afferent fibres that accompany the sympathetic neurones.

Jejunum

The jejunum has an external diameter of about 4 cm and an internal diameter of about 3 cm. It has a thicker wall than the ileum and a rich arterial blood supply. The circular folds (plicae circulares) (see below) are most pronounced in the proximal jejunum, where they are more numerous and deeper than elsewhere in the small intestine (Figs 64.5–64.6). They frequently ‘branch’ around the lumen and can appear to be stacked on top of each other, giving the jejunum a characteristic appearance during single contrast radiography (see Fig. 64.6), computed tomography enterography (Fig. 64.7A) or magnetic resonance (MR) enterography (Fig. 64.7B). The circular folds are also clearly visible by capsule endoscopy, in which images of the small intestine are transmitted wirelessly from a small swallowed camera the size of a pill (Fig. 64.8).
In the supine position, the jejunum usually occupies the proximal left infracolic compartment, extending distally to the umbilical region. The first one or two loops often occupy a recess between the left part of the transverse mesocolon and the left kidney. On supine radiological examination, the jejunal loops are characteristically situated in the proximal abdomen, to the left of the midline, whereas the ileal loops tend to lie in the inferior right part of the abdomen and pelvis. This distribution can be reversed in small intestine obstruction owing to rotation of the dilated intestine around its mesenteric attachment.

Jejunal feeding

In patients with a functioning intestine who cannot tolerate adequate oral or intragastric feeding, jejunal feeding is often the preferred option. Enteral feeding is associated with fewer complications than parenteral (intravenous) nutrition: it maintains the integrity of the gut mucosa (thereby reducing bacterial translocation from the gut lumen), decreases the likelihood of aspiration from gastro-oesophageal reflux, is less of a stimulus for pancreaticobiliary secretion, and mitigates the need for central venous access and its accompanying risks. Jejunal feeds can be delivered via a nasojejunal or gastrojejunal tube or directly into the jejunum; gastrojejunal and jejunal tubes can be inserted endoscopically, radiologically or surgically. The distal end of the feeding tube must lie beyond the duodenojejunal flexure to prevent reflux of feeding into the duodenum and stomach.

Ileum

The ileum has an external diameter of about 3 cm, an internal diameter of about 2.5 cm and tends to have a thinner wall than the jejunum (see Fig. 64.5B). The circular folds become progressively less obvious in the distal ileum; they tend to be single and flatter (see Figs 64.6–64.7; Fig. 64.9). The mucosa of the terminal ileum immediately proximal to the ileocaecal junction can appear almost flat at endoscopy, although the villi can be seen when viewed at near focus (see Fig. 64.9) and at capsule endoscopy (see Fig. 64.8).
In the supine position, the ileum lies mainly in the hypogastric region and right inguinal region. The terminal ileum frequently lies in the pelvis, from where it ascends over the right psoas major and right iliac vessels and ends by opening at the ileocaecal junction in the right inguinal region.

Anatomical differences between the jejunum and ileum

While there is no clear boundary between the jejunum (the proximal two-fifths of the small intestine beyond the duodenum) and the ileum (the distal three-fifths), there are general anatomical differences between those regions. The wall of the jejunum is thicker and more vascular, has a greater number of more prominent circular folds, and contains less lymphoid tissue than the ileum. There are also differences between the mesenteric vessels in the jejunum and ileum (Conley et al 2010). The jejunal mesentery, measured from the superior mesenteric artery to the mesenteric border of the intestine, is shorter than the ileal mesentery, and the jejunal arteries are slightly larger than their ileal counterparts. The jejunum typically contains 1–3 tiers of vascular arcades, whereas there are often 2–6 tiers in the ileum (Fig. 64.10). The straight arteries (vasa recta) in the ileum are more numerous, shorter and narrower than in the jejunum. The jejunal and ileal arteries, arcades and straight arteries are muscular arteries capable of influencing blood flow, which can range between 10% and 35% of cardiac output (Rosenblum et al 1997).
Solitary lymphoid nodules are scattered throughout the small intestinal mucosa but are most numerous in the distal ileum. Aggregated lymphoid nodules, Peyer’s patches, are circular or oval masses containing 5–260 follicles. They vary in size, shape and distribution, most measuring 2–8 cm and visible macroscopically as dome-like elevations, usually along the antimesenteric border of the intestine. They are rarely present in the duodenum; small, circular, few in number and impalpable in the distal jejunum; and larger, more numerous and often palpable in the ileum (particularly in the terminal 25 cm; Van Kruiningen et al 2002). Intestinal villi are small or absent over the larger lymphoid nodules. Aggregated lymphoid nodules are most prominent in early childhood and, when enlarged in viral infections, may form the apex of an intussusception. They become less prominent around puberty and become fewer during adult life (Cornes 1965a, 1965b).

Ileal diverticulum

A rare congenital ileal diverticulum (Meckel’s diverticulum) represents the remnant of the proximal part of the omphalo-enteric duct (vitello-intestinal duct, yolk stalk). It projects from the antimesenteric border of the terminal ileum and is commonly located between 50–100 cm from the ileocaecal junction. It is variable in length (usually 2–5 cm in adults) and often possesses a short ‘mesentery’ of adipose tissue containing an omphalomesenteric artery (vitelline artery), from the termination of the superior mesenteric artery, that extends from the ileal mesentery to its base (Fig. 64.11). The lumen of the diverticulum usually has a calibre similar to that of the ileum. The tip is normally free but occasionally it is connected to the anterior abdominal wall near the umbilicus by a fibrous band. The mucosa is typically ileal, but small heterotopic areas of gastric body type epithelium, pancreatic, colonic or other tissues can also occur in the wall of a diverticulum. Unopposed acid secretion by heterotopic gastric body type epithelium can cause ulceration and bleeding in the adjacent normal ileal mucosa. Diverticular inflammation can mimic acute appendicitis: because the congenital ileal diverticulum and the appendix are both derived from the midgut, pain from either structure is referred to the periumbilical region. Rarer complications of a congenital ileal diverticulum include intestinal obstruction, intussusception, perforation, calculi and tumours (Sagar et al 2006, Uppal et al 2011).

Jejunostomy and ileostomy

In clinical practice, a stoma is a surgically created opening from a hollow viscus to the skin, classified according to its location. An intestinal stoma can be either an end stoma, in which only the proximal end of the divided intestine is anastomosed to the skin, or a loop stoma, in which both the proximal and distal ends of the intestines are exteriorized on the surface (Fig. 64.12). The volume output of a jejunostomy is greater than that of an ileostomy, less solid in character, and more likely to result in excessive fluid, electrolyte and nutrient losses.

Short bowel syndrome

The mean length of the small intestine, measured from the duodenojejunal flexure to the ileocaecal junction in vivo, is about 5 m but can range from 3 to 8.5 m (Teitelbaum et al 2013). Males have a longer small intestine than females, and height is positively associated with the length of the small intestine (Teitelbaum et al 2013). Short bowel syndrome results from surgical resection, congenital deficiency or disease-associated loss of absorption, and is characterized by the inability to maintain protein-energy, fluid, electrolyte or micronutrient balances on a conventionally accepted normal diet (O’Keefe et al 2006). If untreated, short bowel syndrome leads to dehydration, malnutrition and weight loss. Adults with the greatest risk of developing intestinal failure fall into three groups: those with an end-jejunostomy and <100 cm of residual small intestine; those with a jejunocolic anastomosis and <50 cm residual small intestine; and those with a jejunoileal anastomosis and <35 cm of residual small intestine (despite the presence of an ileocaecal junction and colon) (Nightingale and Woodward 2006, Jeppesen 2013).
After surgical resection the remaining small intestine undergoes an adaptive process that involves morphological and functional changes: it dilates and intestinal villus height and intestinal gland depth increase, thereby expanding the absorptive surface area. Adaptation begins soon after intestinal resection and can continue for up to two years (Jeppesen and Mortensen 2002). The ability of the proximal and distal small intestine to adapt differ. The likelihood of regaining intestinal autonomy is greatest in patients with a retained segment of ileum and colon in continuity, in contrast to patients with a residual duodenojejunal segment and an end-jejunostomy (Jeppesen 2013). Many patients with short bowel syndrome are maintained on parenteral nutrition for lengthy periods.

Small intestinal transplantation

Small intestine transplantation has become a standard clinical procedure for selected patients with intestinal failure (Abu-Elmagd et al 2009). Intestinal failure can result from an inadequate surface area for nutrient absorption, as in short bowel syndrome, or dysmotility of the stomach or small intestine. The small intestine can be transplanted in isolation or along with the liver and/or other abdominal organs such as the pancreas (multivisceral transplant). Postoperatively, patients are recommenced on parenteral nutrition, and enteral feeding is started via a feeding jejunostomy once intestinal motility has returned. Protein is delivered as peptides and fats as medium-chain triglycerides, which are absorbed directly into the mesenteric veins and not via the lymphatics (divided when the graft is taken from the donor and not anastomosed to recipient lymphatics). Parenteral nutrition can usually be discontinued 3–6 weeks after small intestine transplantation but may continue to be used to supplement enteral feeding when stoma losses are large.
Intestinal dysmotility is common after intestinal transplantation. There is no extrinsic nerve supply to the graft but intrinsic small intestine motility usually recovers within 48–72 hours after transplantation (Gunning and Friend 1998). Adequate gastric emptying is often delayed for several weeks, despite pyloroplasty and the administration of drugs to enhance gastric motility. Reinnervation of the allograft via its vascular pedicle is slow (Walther et al 2013). Intestinal motility is also affected by any inflammatory response caused by rejection. Denervation and division of the lymphatic drainage of the intestinal allograft do not appear to impair long-term function seriously. In animal models, lymphatic drainage is re-established within 21–28 days, which is consistent with observations in patients (Kocandrle et al 1966).

Vascular supply and lymphatic drainage

Arteries

Branches from the superior mesenteric artery supply the jejunum and ileum. The arteries divide as they approach the mesenteric border of the intestine (see Fig. 64.5), giving off numerous branches that extend between the muscular layers before forming a submucosal arterial plexus that supplies the mucosa. Although there is a rich anastomotic network of arteries within the intestinal mesentery, there are few anastomoses between the terminal branches close to the intestinal wall. The intramural and submucosal arterial networks consist of small-calibre vessels only. Consequently, division or occlusion of several consecutive straight arteries can produce segmental ischaemia of the intestine, while division of more proximal arterial branches in the small intestine mesentery might not cause ischaemia because of collateral flow through vascular arcades.
Superior mesenteric artery
The superior mesenteric artery originates from the abdominal aorta 1 cm inferior to the coeliac trunk, at the level of the inferior border of the first lumbar vertebra in the transpyloric plane (Figs 64.13–64.15). The angle of its origin from the aorta is acute (mean value 45°, range 38–60° and greater in individuals with a greater body mass index; Ozkurt et al 2007). Among the major branches of the descending aorta, the superior mesenteric artery typically has the most acute take-off angle. The result is less turbulent flow, which makes embolic events more likely than in the other visceral arterial branches of the abdominal aorta. The acuteness of the angle between the aorta and superior mesenteric artery can make intravascular cannulation via the transfemoral route somewhat difficult. The artery is usually surrounded by fat, lymphatics and neural tissue at its origin, which helps to increase the angle and the distance between it and the aorta, thereby preventing compression of the duodenum where it is crossed by the artery. The artery descends anterior to the uncinate process of the pancreas and the horizontal part of the duodenum and posterior to the splenic vein and the body of the pancreas. The left renal vein lies posterior to it and separates it from the aorta (see Fig. 64.15B). Within the small intestine mesentery, the superior mesenteric artery crosses anterior to the inferior vena cava, right ureter and right psoas major. Its calibre progressively decreases as successive branches are given off to the jejunum and ileum, and its terminal branch anastomoses with the termination of the ileocolic artery.
The superior mesenteric artery usually gives off the inferior pancreaticoduodenal, middle colic, right colic and ileocolic arteries from its right side, and jejunal and ileal arteries from its left side. Its jejunal and ileal arteries form vascular arcades within the small intestine mesentery. The last of these arcades forms an irregular and incomplete marginal artery (of Drummond) of the small intestine. Straight arteries are given off from the most distal arcades and pass directly to the small intestine.
Anatomical variations in the origin and branching pattern of the superior mesenteric artery are well described (Winston et al 2007, Horton and Fishman 2010) (see also Ch. 65). It can be the source of the common hepatic, gastroduodenal, accessory or replaced right hepatic, accessory pancreatic, splenic or rarely the inferior mesenteric artery. Also rare is a superior mesenteric artery arising from a common coeliacomesenteric trunk (Rountas et al 2013).
The remnant of the omphalomesenteric artery (vitelline artery) (the embryonic artery that originally connected the intestinal circulation to the yolk sac) is usually obliterated; when present, it forms the artery supplying a congenital ileal diverticulum. It is occasionally represented in the mesentery by a fibrous strand from the termination of the superior mesenteric artery to the ileum.
Surgical exposure of the superior mesenteric artery
The superior mesenteric artery can be exposed through transperitoneal or retroperitoneal approaches. For a retroperitoneal approach, a left flank incision is used and the superior mesenteric artery is exposed at the base from the left lateral aspect. From the transperitoneal approach the superior mesenteric artery can be accessed through the omental bursa with medial visceral rotation. These exposures are important for either gaining control of a bleeding distal vessel, or more often to expose the superior mesenteric artery for bypass or embolectomy in the setting of intestinal ischaemia.

Retroperitoneal flank approach to expose the superior mesenteric artery

The patient is typically positioned in a modified right lateral decubitus position for this approach. In this position, the shoulders lie at an angle to the operating table with the left upper limb supported on an arm board, but the pelvis is maintained in a position with as much posterior rotation as possible. An incision can be made in the ninth, tenth or eleventh intercostal space extending posteriorly to the mid-axillary line and anteriorly to the lateral edge of rectus abdominis. The incision can be extended in either direction to provide additional exposure. External and internal intercostal are divided, as well as transversus abdominis. Intercostal muscles are divided over the top of the corresponding rib and the thorax can then be entered. Often the respiratory diaphragm must be divided as well. Anteriorly, blunt dissection is typically used to elevate the peritoneum off posterior muscles and the thoracolumbar fascia. The colon, spleen and tail of the pancreas are reflected anteriorly. The plane can be developed posterior to the kidney. The left lateral edge of the abdominal aorta is identified and the superior mesenteric artery can be visualized from the left lateral aspect as well.

Transperitoneal approaches to expose the superior mesenteric artery

A number of different incisions can be used to gain access to the peritoneal cavity, though a vertical midline incision is probably the most common. There are three approaches, with specific exposure determined by which aspect of the superior mesenteric artery needs to be exposed.

Transperitoneal omental bursa approach

The left lobe of the liver is reflected anteriorly. With leftward retraction on the stomach, the hepatogastric ligament can be divided either superior or inferior to the left gastric artery. Dissection of the muscular fibres of the left crus of the respiratory diaphragm will expose the anterior surface of the abdominal aorta. Typically, the coeliac artery is encountered first, overlaid with decussating muscle fibres and the nerves of the coeliac plexus. Large lymphatic channels can also be encountered. Inferior retraction of the body of the pancreas will expose the first few centimetres of the superior mesenteric artery.

Transperitoneal approach with medial visceral rotation

A full left medial visceral rotation (Mattox manoeuvre) provides exposure to the entire abdominal aorta and proximal visceral vessels. The lateral peritoneal reflection of the descending colon is divided and extended superiorly, curving towards the aortic hiatus. A retroperitoneal plane is developed as the colon, spleen and the tail of the pancreas are rotated to the right. The left crus of the respiratory diaphragm may need to be divided to expose the anterior aspect of the abdominal aorta, depending on its inferior extent. The kidney is generally left in position, which provides broad exposure to the origin of the proximal superior mesenteric artery.

Inframesocolic approach

The transverse colon is reflected superiorly to expose the root of the mesentery, while the jejunum and ileum are reflected inferiorly and to the right. The superior mesenteric artery can typically be palpated. A transverse incision made to the right of the suspensory ligament of the duodenum, and inferior to the pancreas, will typically expose both the superior mesenteric artery and vein. Typically, the middle colic artery and the first jejunal branch of the superior mesenteric artery are also visualized, but access to the origin of the superior mesenteric artery might be limited.

Jejunal branches

Usually, 4–6 jejunal branches arise from the left side of the proximal portion of the superior mesenteric artery (see Figs 64.13–64.14). They are distributed to the jejunum via 1–3 tiers of arterial arcades, the most distal of which gives rise to straight arteries. The latter run almost parallel in the mesentery before being distributed alternately to either side of the small intestine, forming two distinct ‘leaves’ of vessels within the mesentery separated by a relatively avascular plane (see Fig. 64.5A). This vascular arrangement allows a dilated segment of small intestine to be bisected longitudinally and tubularized to double its length, a potentially useful technique for achieving small intestine lengthening in short bowel syndrome (Bianchi 1984). Small twigs from the jejunal arteries supply regional mesenteric nodes.

Ileal branches

Ileal branches are more numerous (around 8–12) and slightly smaller in calibre than jejunal branches. They arise from the left and anterior aspects of the superior mesenteric artery. The length of the mesentery from the superior mesenteric artery to the mesenteric border of the intestine is greater in the ileum, and so the branches form between two and six arcades before giving rise to multiple straight arteries that run directly towards the ileal wall (see Fig. 64.10B). These branches run parallel in the mesentery and are distributed to both aspects of the ileum. They are shorter and thinner than their jejunal counterparts, particularly in the distal ileum, and do not form such distinct parallel ‘leaves’ of vessels. The terminal ileal arcades are supplied by the ileal branch of the ileocolic artery and the last ileal branch of the superior mesenteric artery (see Fig. 64.13). Few other vessels connect the ileocolic and superior mesenteric artery territories, and this makes surgical dissection of the ileocolic artery up to its origin relatively simple.
Veins
Superior mesenteric vein
The superior mesenteric vein drains the small intestine, caecum, ascending and transverse colons, and parts of the stomach and greater omentum (Fig. 64.16; see Fig. 59.7). It is formed in the mesentery of the small intestine by the union of tributaries from the terminal ileum, caecum and appendix. It ascends in the mesentery to the right of the superior mesenteric artery, passing anterior to the right ureter, inferior vena cava, horizontal part of the duodenum and uncinate process of the pancreas. It joins the splenic vein posterior to the neck of the pancreas in the transpyloric plane (inferior border of L1 vertebra) to form the hepatic portal vein.
The superior mesenteric vein receives jejunal, ileal, ileocolic, right colic, middle colic, right gastro-omental and inferior pancreaticoduodenal veins. A major proximal jejunal branch usually runs transversely posterior to the superior mesenteric artery to enter the right posterolateral aspect of the superior mesenteric vein (Kim et al 2007). The right colic vein is highly variable. When present, it can drain directly into the superior mesenteric vein or join the right gastro-omental or inferior pancreaticoduodenal vein to form a ‘gastrocolic trunk’, which then drains into the superior mesenteric vein (Yamaguchi et al 2002). Although the inferior mesenteric vein usually drains into the splenic vein, it can join the superior mesenteric vein directly or its confluence with the splenic vein (Graf et al 1997).
Lymphatic drainage
The lymphatic system of the small intestine regulates tissue fluid homeostasis, participates in immune surveillance, and transports dietary fat and fat-soluble vitamins from the gut lumen. It is organized into two networks. First, lacteals from the villi drain into a plexus of lymphatics in the submucosa and are joined by vessels from lymph spaces at the bases of solitary lymphoid follicles; these lymphatic vessels have few if any valves. Second, a coarse plexus of lymphatics also runs in the muscularis externa between the two muscle layers. The submucosal and muscular networks share few connections but both communicate freely with larger valved collecting lymphatics at the mesenteric border of the small intestine (Miller et al 2010). Mesenteric lymphatics pass between the layers of the mesentery and drain via a series of mesenteric nodes concentrated around the regional mesenteric vessels. Individual segments of small intestine have a relatively wide field of lymphatic drainage, which makes radical surgical resection of draining lymph nodes difficult if the blood supply to the remaining unaffected small intestine is to be preserved. Mesenteric nodes drain into superior mesenteric nodes around the proximal part of the superior mesenteric artery.

Innervation

The jejunum and ileum are innervated by parasympathetic and sympathetic fibres via the superior mesenteric plexus (see Figs 59.2–59.4). Preganglionic sympathetic axons originate from neurones in the intermediolateral grey matter of the mid-thoracic spinal cord segments and travel in the greater and lesser thoracic splanchnic nerves to the coeliac and superior mesenteric ganglia, where they synapse. Postganglionic axons accompany the superior mesenteric artery into the mesentery and are distributed along branches of the artery. The sympathetic nerves are vasoconstrictor to the vasculature and inhibitory to the musculature of the jejunum and ileum. Sympathetic neurotransmitters also have an immunomodulatory role by influencing mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue (Straub et al 2006). Preganglionic parasympathetic axons travel in the vagus nerves and are secretomotor to the mucosa and motor to the smooth muscle of the jejunum and ileum. Visceral afferents from the small intestine, conveying pain and other gut sensations, travel with the splanchnic and vagus nerves.

Referred pain

In common with other structures derived from the midgut, the visceral sensation of pain arising from the jejunum or ileum is poorly localized and is usually referred to the periumbilical region.

Small Intestine Physiology

The principal functions of the small intestine are fluid and electrolyte haemostasis, digestion and absorption of nutrients, immunoregulation and secretion of hormones. The absorptive capacity of the small intestine is greatly enhanced by the circular folds, intestinal villi and microvilli, which increase its surface area by factors of approximately 2, 7 and 13, respectively (Helander and Fändriks 2014), yielding a total estimated surface area of almost 30 m2.

Fluids and electrolytes

The secretion and absorption of electrolytes and fluid are two essential functions of the small intestinal epithelium. In healthy adults, the gastrointestinal tract is capable of secreting 8–10 litres of fluid per day in addition to the 1.5–2 litres ingested each day. To reduce this volume to the typical ileocaecal daily flow of approximately 2 litres per day, the small intestinal epithelium has a large number of transport molecules and regulatory proteins. Net fluid movement across the gastrointestinal epithelium is primarily the result of the active transport of sodium, chloride and bicarbonate ions. The jejunum can only absorb sodium across a small sodium gradient and therefore does not normally absorb salt and water, but secretes it into the lumen. Most fluid and electrolyte absorption occurs in the ileum, which is able to absorb across a larger sodium gradient (Nightingale and Spiller 2001): proximal small intestine fistulae or surgical jejunostomies therefore have a higher volume output with greater electrolyte losses than more distal stomata.

Nutrients

The mucosal brush border has an abundance of enzymes and pumps on its surface. Examples include the sodium-dependent glucose transporter protein (SGLT1) and the fructose transporter protein (GLUT5). Oligosaccharides are broken down to monosaccharides by enzymes at the brush border of the small intestine before being transported across the mucosa. A deficiency of lactase on the mucosal brush border is relatively common and results in lactose intolerance. Proteins are broken down into single amino acids and small peptides. The peptidase gradient across the microvillus brush border increases along the length of the small intestine, suggesting that amino acids are absorbed more distally than carbohydrates. Dietary fats are emulsified by biliary secretions and then broken down by pancreatic lipase into free fatty acids and phospholipids, which form micelles. The lipid components are then absorbed within the distal small intestine and converted into chylomicrons in the enterocytes before being secreted into the lymphatic system. Minerals such as calcium, magnesium, phosphorus and iron, and the water- and fat-soluble vitamins, are predominantly absorbed in the duodenum and proximal jejunum (Farrell 2002, Malik and Westergaard 2002). Vitamin B12 is absorbed in the terminal ileum after binding to intrinsic factor released by gastric parietal cells. Bile is largely composed of bile acid salts conjugated to taurine or glycine. These salts are deconjugated by bacteria in the ileum to either a free acid or a secondary bile acid. The free acids are reabsorbed by passive transport in the jejunum and ileum. The terminal ileum has an active sodium cotransporter that scavenges conjugated bile acids. Reabsorbed bile acids enter the portal circulation and are returned to the liver for re-secretion.

Motility

The motility of the small intestine differs depending on whether the individual is fasting or postprandial. In the fasting state, the smooth muscle of the small intestine exhibits cyclical myoelectrical activity, the so-called migrating motor complex (MMC). The frequency of these slow waves is regulated by interstitial cells of Cajal and is about 11/minute in the jejunum, decreasing along the length of the small intestine to 7–8/minute in the ileum (Husebye 1999). When slow waves are amplified beyond a threshold by neural, endocrine or paracrine stimulation, action potentials are generated, resulting in smooth muscle contraction. This spreads circumferentially at first and then propagates along the entire circumference of the intestine in an aboral direction, causing a ring-like contraction of the circular smooth muscle that progresses distally along the small intestine (about 10% of contractions reaching the ileum).
The MMC consists of three phases: phase I is a period of quiescence, phase II is composed of irregular contractions and phase III is a short burst of phasic contractions at the maximum frequency of the slow waves for that part of the intestine. The velocity of migration is 5–10 cm/minute in the jejunum, decreasing gradually along the small intestine to about 1 cm/minute in the ileum. The MMC requires an intact enteric nervous system and does not depend on extrinsic innervation. Consequently, MMCs are present in transplanted (denervated) small intestine. However, there is evidence that extrinsic innervation modulates the MMC. For example, sleep causes the almost complete disappearance of phase II. Phase III activity normally recovers in the proximal small intestine within a few hours of major abdominal surgery, although the pressure wave amplitude is temporarily reduced. Somatostatin and opioids decrease phase II activity. After a meal, the cyclical fasting activity is abolished for several hours and replaced by irregular contractions that consist of both segmental ‘stationary’ contractions and propulsive contractions (Barnert 2007).
Assessment of small intestine motility can complement detailed microscopic examination of full-thickness biopsy specimens of the intestine when conditions associated with intestinal dysmotility are characterized, helping to distinguish intestinal neuropathy from visceral myopathy. Wireless motility capsules are also finding increasing use. These devices are pill-shaped and can be either swallowed or placed into the stomach with an endoscope. Sensors for pH, pressure and temperature record measurements and send these wirelessly to an external recording device. Based on the patterns of these pH, temperature and pressure curves, gastric emptying, small intestine transit and colonic transit times are easily decipherable.

Microstructure

The small intestinal wall is composed of mucosa, submucosa, muscularis externa and serosa or adventitia (see Fig. 59.8). The mucosa is thick and very vascular in the proximal small intestine but thinner and less vascular distally. In places, it is ridged by the underlying submucosa to form circular folds, which protrude into the lumen; mucosal finger- or leaf-like intestinal villi cover the whole surface (Fig. 64.17). There are numerous simple, tubular intestinal glands between the bases of the villi, and additional duodenal submucosal glands (see Fig. 59.8). The submucosa contains aggregated lymphoid nodules, most numerous in the ileum.

Circular folds

Except in the superior (first) part of the duodenum, large circular folds of mucosa (plicae circulares, valvulae conniventes, valves of Kerckring) project into the lumen of the small intestine, orientated either transversely or slightly obliquely to its long axis (see Figs 64.8, 64.17; Fig. 64.18). Unlike gastric folds, they do not disappear during distension of the intestine. Most extend round half or two-thirds of the luminal circumference, some are complete circles, some bifurcate and join adjacent circular folds, while others are spiral and extend one or more times round the lumen. Larger circular folds are up to 8 mm deep but most are smaller than this, and larger folds often alternate with smaller ones. Circular folds begin to appear 2.5–5 cm beyond the pylorus and are relatively large and close together in the distal duodenum and proximal jejunum. Beyond this point, they diminish in size and disappear almost completely in the terminal ileum, which therefore has a relatively thin wall. The circular folds increase the absorptive surface area and enhance mechanical segmentation in the small intestine. They are visible at endoscopy (see Fig. 64.3), during small intestine contrast studies (see Fig. 64.6), and sometimes on plain radiographs.

Intestinal villi

Intestinal villi are highly vascular projections of the mucosal surface, just visible to the naked eye (see Fig. 64.18; Figs 64.19–64.21). They cover the entire small intestinal mucosa, increase the surface area of the lumen about seven-fold (Helander and Fändriks 2014), and give it a velvety texture. Villi are large and numerous in the duodenum and jejunum but smaller and fewer in the ileum. In the superior part of the duodenum they appear as broad ridges, become tall and foliate in the distal duodenum and proximal jejunum, and then gradually shorten to a finger-like form in the distal jejunum and ileum. Villi range from 10 to 40 per mm2 in density and from 0.5 to 1.0 mm in height (see Fig. 64.19). Infoldings of the mucosa dip down from the bases of the villi for a short distance into the lamina propria: these intestinal glands (crypts of Lieberkühn) are most prominent in the proximal small intestine (see Fig. 64.20).

Mucosa

The mucosa consists of epithelium, lamina propria and muscularis mucosae.
Epithelium
A single-layered epithelium covers the intestinal villi (see Figs 64.20, 2.2C) and also lines the intestinal glands that open between the bases of villi. Two types of cell, enterocytes and goblet cells, cover the surfaces of the villi. Microfold cells (M cells) are restricted to the dome epithelium that covers localized accumulations of lymphoid tissue. All three cell types contact and adhere basally to a basal lamina, and all are derived from a common stem cell in the intestinal glands.

Enterocytes

Enterocytes are columnar absorptive cells, approximately 20 μm tall, and are the most numerous cell type in the small intestinal lining. They are responsible for nutrient absorption: their surfaces bear up to 3000 microvilli, greatly increasing the surface area for absorption (Marsh and Swift 1969). Collectively, microvilli are visible by light microscopy as a 1 μm thick brush border (microvillous border or striated border): individual microvilli can be resolved only by electron microscopy. Enterocyte nuclei are elongated vertically, mainly euchromatic and located just below the centre of the cell.
The apical cell surface is resistant to protease attack because microvilli possess a specialized glycoprotein-rich surface coat (glycocalyx; see Fig. 1.26). Together with an overlying layer of mucus (Johansson et al 2011), this glycocalyx protects the epithelium against pancreatic enzymes in the intestinal lumen. The cell coat also contains a number of digestive enzymes as integral membrane proteins, including enzymes that degrade disaccharides and oligopeptides prior to absorption.
The luminal surface is an important barrier to diffusion. Nutrients generally have to pass through enterocytes (transcellular absorption) before they reach the underlying lamina propria and its blood vessels and lymphatics (central lymphatic vessels or lacteals). Classical epithelial junctional complexes encircle the apical plasma membranes of adjacent enterocytes, their tight junctions forming an effective barrier to non-selective diffusion between the gut lumen and the body as a whole. The lateral plasma membranes of enterocytes are highly folded and interdigitate to form complex intercellular boundaries that are anchored periodically by desmosomes and that make contact at gap junctions. The lateral intercellular space expands during active absorption; by supplementing transport across the basal cell surface it is an additional conduit for the passage of fluids with nutrients and other solutes to the vessels of the lamina propria.
After differentiation, enterocytes have a lifespan of about five days. Their position on the intestinal villus wall reflects their stage in the life cycle. They undergo programmed, apoptotic cell death and are shed from the tips of the villi. They are replaced at the bases of the villi by stem cell mitosis. Enterocytes located in the bases of the intestinal villi have a mainly secretory role, contributing more to absorption as they migrate towards the lumen.

Goblet cells

Goblet cells increase in number from the duodenum to their highest density in the terminal ileum. They have elongated basal nuclei, an apical region containing many membrane-bound mucinogen granules, and apical surfaces that bear a few short microvilli. Goblet cell mucins contribute to protection against microorganisms and toxins in the gut lumen, and also provide lubrication and mechanical protection from the intestinal contents.

Microfold (M) cells

Microfold cells are present where the epithelium covers lymphoid aggregates in the intestinal wall. They are cuboidal or flattened and have long, widely spaced microfolds rather than microvilli on their apical surfaces. They sample luminal antigens by endocytosis and transport antigens to lymphocytes lying within intercellular pockets formed by deep invaginations of the M-cell basolateral plasma membranes. (See Chapter 4 for details of antigen processing and presentation.)

Lymphocytes

Intraepithelial lymphocytes are found in close association with M cells and also between the basolateral regions of enterocytes and goblet cells. They are migratory cells derived from the underlying lymphoid tissue and constitute an important means of immune defence.
Intestinal glands
Intestinal glands (intestinal crypts, crypts of Lieberkühn) are tubular pits that open into the lumen throughout the intestinal mucosa via small circular apertures between the bases of the villi (see Fig. 64.20). Their thin walls are composed of columnar enterocytes supplemented by mucous cells of the duodenal submucosa, Paneth cells, stem cells and neuroendocrine cells. They are separated by a basal lamina from a rich capillary plexus within the lamina propria.

Enterocytes

Enterocytes in the intestinal glands secrete ions and alkaline fluid to dilute chyme and aid absorption by structurally similar cells that cover the villi.

Mucous cells

The mucous cells of the duodenal submucosa in the intestinal glands are similar to the goblet cells of the villi.

Paneth cells

Paneth cells are highly specialized epithelial cells of the small intestine, involved in the coordination of many physiological functions. They were first identified more than a century ago on the basis of their readily discernible secretory acidophilic granules that stain strongly with eosin or phosphotungstic acid-haematoxylin (Fig. 64.22). Paneth cells are numerous in the deeper parts of the intestinal glands. They synthesize and secrete lysozyme, a highly specific antibacterial enzyme, other defensive proteins (alpha-defensins), tumour necrosis factor alpha (TNF-α), and phospholipase A that help to protect the luminal surface. These antimicrobial molecules are key mediators of host–microbe interactions, including maintaining the homeostatic balance with colonizing microbiota and providing innate immune protection from enteric pathogens (Clevers and Bevins 2013). Paneth cells secrete factors that help sustain and modulate epithelial stem and progenitor cells in the intestinal glands.

Stem cells

Stem cells occur in a zone just above the basal region of the intestinal glands. Lineage tracing has demonstrated that Lgr5hi crypt base columnar stem cells generate all cell types of the small intestinal epithelium (Barker et al 2007, Clevers 2013). These cells divide rapidly in the intestinal gland compartment directly above the Paneth cells. Their progeny (transit-amplifying cells) move upwards from this compartment onto the surfaces of the intestinal villi while differentiating into goblet cells, tuft cells, neuroendocrine cells and enterocytes. An individual cell takes only 4–5 days to reach the tip of an intestinal villus and undergo apoptosis. Paneth cells escape this upward flow and migrate downwards to settle at the bases of the intestinal glands, where they can remain for one month or more (Clevers and Bevins 2013). Stem cell daughter cells and young transit-amplifying cells retain their plasticity and can revert back to stem cells if an intestinal gland is injured and existing stem cells are lost.

Neuroendocrine cells

Several types of neuroendocrine cell are scattered among the walls of the intestinal glands and less commonly over the intestinal villi. They can all be identified by their clear cytoplasm and round nucleus. They secrete bioactive peptides such as gastrin, cholecystokinin and secretin basally into the surrounding lamina propria. Eleven distinct subpopulations of neuroendocrine cells have been described, distributed throughout the gastrointestinal tract, each producing unique signalling molecules. Intestinal gland neuroendocrine cells are derived from stem cells, that also give rise to enterocytes and other epithelial elements (Posovszky 2017).
Lamina propria
The lamina propria is composed of connective tissue and provides mechanical support for the epithelium. It has a rich vascular plexus, receives nutrients absorbed by the enterocytes, and forms the cores of the intestinal villi. It contains lymphoid tissue, fibroblasts and connective tissue extracellular matrix fibres, smooth muscle cells, eosinophils, macrophages, mast cells, capillaries, lymphatic vessels and unmyelinated nerves. Plasma cells are numerous. Lymphocytes may be clustered in solitary or aggregated follicles, some of which extend through the muscularis mucosae into the submucosa.
Villus core
Each intestinal villus has a core of delicate connective tissue that contains a large, blind-ending lymphatic vessel or lacteal (so called because of its milky content of suspended chylomicrons, the droplets of apoprotein–lipid complex elaborated by enterocytes from absorbed dietary fats). The core also contains blood vessels, nerves and smooth muscle cells derived from fine extensions of the muscularis mucosae. Each lacteal, usually single but occasionally double, starts in a closed dilated extremity near the tip of an intestinal villus and extends through the core to the base of the villus, where it joins a narrower lymphatic plexus in the deeper lamina propria. Its wall is a single layer of endothelial cells. Smooth muscle cells surround the lacteal throughout each villus, and their contraction propels its contents into the underlying lymphatic plexus. Capillaries within the core are lined by fenestrated endothelium to facilitate the rapid intake of nutrients that diffuse from the covering absorptive epithelium.
Mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue
Mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue (MALT; Ch. 4) consists of lymphoid follicles covered by follicle-associated intestinal epithelium that includes scattered M cells. MALT is found mainly in the lamina propria but sometimes expands into the submucosa; it is the source of B and T lymphocytes and other related cells involved in the immune defence of the gut wall. MALT produces immunoglobulin A (IgA), the most abundant immunoglobulin in the body and the major class of antibody secreted by the gut mucosa. IgA regulates the composition, geographical distribution and function of bacterial communities along the gastrointestinal tract via T cell dependent and independent pathways and has also been implicated in autoimmune and inflammatory conditions of the gut.
Like other masses of MALT (except lymph nodes), solitary and aggregated lymphoid follicles are most prominent around the age of puberty, after which they decrease in number and size, although many persist into old age. For further details of intestinal MALT and its function, including aggregated lymphoid nodules, see Didierlaurent et al (2005).
Muscularis mucosae
The muscularis mucosae forms the base of the mucosa and has external longitudinal and internal circular layers of smooth muscle cells. It follows the surface profiles of the circular folds and sends fine fascicles of smooth muscle cells into the cores of the villi.

Submucosa

The submucosa is composed of loose connective tissue and contains blood vessels, lymphatics and nerves. Its ridged elevations form the cores of the circular folds. The geometry of its collagen and elastin fibres permits the considerable changes in transverse and longitudinal dimensions that accompany peristalsis whilst still providing adequate support, elasticity and strength. The submucosa is an important histological landmark in the pathological staging for some gastrointestinal malignancies.
Submucosal glands
Submucosal glands (Brunner’s glands) are limited to the submucosa of the duodenum (see Fig. 64.19). Their ducts traverse the muscularis mucosae to enter the bases of the intestinal glands. They are largest and most numerous near the pylorus and form an almost complete layer in the proximal half of the descending part of the duodenum; thereafter, they gradually diminish in number and disappear at the duodenojejunal junction. They are small, branched tubuloacinar glands; each has several secretory acini lined by low columnar epithelial cells, which produce an alkaline (pH 9) mucoid secretion that effectively neutralizes acidic chyme from the stomach. Many neuroendocrine cells are present among the acinar cells.

Muscularis externa

The muscularis externa, sometimes known as muscularis propria, consists of a thin external longitudinal layer and a thick internal circular layer of smooth muscle cells. It is thicker in the proximal small intestine and is mainly responsible for the differential appearance of the proximal jejunum and distal ileum on computed tomography.

Interstitial cells of Cajal

Interstitial cells of Cajal (ICC) are found throughout the gastrointestinal tract, lying in close contact with nerve terminals (Farrugia 2008). They originate from mesenchymal cells and can be considered as specialized smooth muscle cells. However, whereas smooth muscle cells develop an extensive array of contractile elements, ICC are fusiform cells with a large oval nucleus, sparse cytoplasm, dendritic-like processes, few contractile elements, an abundance of endoplasmic reticula and mitochondria, and characteristic sets of channels in their membranes, including CD117, a tyrosine kinase receptor (Mostafa et al 2010). ICC contact each other and neighbouring smooth muscle cells via gap junctions. They form distinct networks in the myenteric plexus between the circular and longitudinal muscle layers: looser arrangements occur within the individual muscle layers and the submucosa of the gut. Isolated or small groups of ICC are also found in connective tissue septa and the subserosal layer. ICC are involved in generating pacemaker signals, propagating electrical slow wave activity, neuromuscular transmission, and mechanosensation (Sanders et al 2014): defective ICC function has been implicated in a wide range of intestinal motility disorders (Al-Shboul 2013).

Serosa

The serosa is visceral peritoneum. It consists of a subserosal layer of loose connective tissue covered by mesothelium. Serosa covers most of the muscularis externa, with the exception of the parts adjacent to mesenteric adipose tissue and the retroperitoneal portions of the duodenum, both of which are covered mainly by connective tissue.
