Commentary 7.2: Computed tomography coronary angiography (CTCA) of anomalous coronary vasculature

Julia Sun, and Jonathan Weir-McCall
Invasive coronary angiography has long been used to map coronary arterial anatomy and associated disease. In this procedure, a contrast agent is instilled into a coronary artery via a catheter inserted into the opening (ostium) of that artery within an aortic sinus. The fluoroscopic images that are acquired have a high spatial and temporal resolution and are of benefit in patient diagnosis and treatment, but they are two-dimensional (2D). In marked contrast, computed tomography (CT) is a non-invasive imaging modality that can demonstrate the anatomy and relations of a region of interest in 3D.
Despite the benefits afforded by CT, the cross-sectional imaging community used to regard the heart as an ‘object of mystery’ because of the rapidity of its movements and the limited speed of the available technology. Recent advances in the temporal resolution of CT scanners and associated radiation dose have enabled the development of computed tomography coronary angiography (CTCA), which is able to capture motion-free, essentially ‘freeze-frame’, 3D images of the heart and coronary vasculature. Image quality can still be adversely affected by the presence of a tachycardia: in such cases image quality can be enhanced using a pharmacological intervention to reduce heart rate or by timing (gating) scans to end diastole or end systole, when the coronary arteries and associated cardiac structures show the least movement. The simplicity of image capture and quality improves as technology develops: the latest generation of CT scanners are now fast enough to acquire diagnostic images even in patients with tachycardia (Agliata et al 2019).
After volumetric dataset acquisition using CTCA, post-processing with 3D reconstruction tools such as volume rendering and multiplanar reformatting enables visualization of the course, origin and anatomical relationships of the coronary arteries to neighbouring structures in a level of detail unmatched by other modalities. A relatively new post-processing technique, cinematic rendering, provides striking photorealistic images from CTCA datasets (Dappa et al 2016). While standard volume rendering is able to demonstrate anatomy and anatomical relationships in three dimensions, the overall quality achieved with cinematic rendering is much higher (see Commentary I.4 and Fig. 7.2.7C–D for a comparison of standard 3D volume rendering of the coronary arteries with cinematic rendering).

Classification of coronary artery anomalies

While there is no universal definition for the division between coronary artery variants and anomalies, a simple, robust and commonly used threshold defines variants as differences occurring in >1% of the population, and anomalies as differences occurring in ≤1% of the population (Angelini 2002, 2007). Variants and anomalies may be asymptomatic and only of relevance during cardiac surgery or may cause chest pain, shortness of breath, or serious cardiovascular sequelae (Levin et al 1978, Frescura et al 1998). Coronary artery anomalies can be grouped into three broad categories: anomaly of origin and course; anomaly of intrinsic anatomy (absence, atresia or duplication); and anomaly of termination. Some anomalies are asymptomatic and discovered incidentally, whereas others require treatment for symptomatic relief or to ensure continued cardiac function. CTCA is an important clinical tool for the diagnosis and detailed mapping of anomalies and the addition of photorealistic cinematic rendering further enhances its quality and utility for surgical and procedural planning and research, especially in cases of complex aberrant anatomy.
An exhaustive list of all the documented coronary anomalies is not possible in a single article. The cases in this commentary demonstrate a mixture of both the most common and most serious anomalies and have been selected to highlight the sometimes extreme deviations from normal coronary artery anatomy that can occur. Many permit survival to adulthood, and although extremely rare, the physician should be aware of their existence in clinical practice.

Anomalies of origin and course

Anomalies of coronary artery origin and course are the most commonly encountered of the three categories of anomalous vessels. The left coronary artery, anterior interventricular branch or circumflex branch of the left coronary artery, or the right coronary artery, can all arise from ectopic positions, which include a non-conventional aortic sinus, the ascending aorta, pulmonary trunk or other coronary arteries. The proximal part of an anomalous coronary artery follows one of five paths (pre-pulmonic, retro-aortic, inter-arterial, trans-septal or retro-cardiac) to access its more usual distal course and designated perfusion territory (Fig. 7.2.1). The pre-pulmonic and retro-aortic paths pass anterior to the pulmonary trunk and posterior to the ascending aorta, respectively, whereas the inter-arterial path passes between the ascending aorta and pulmonary trunk. The trans-septal path is subpulmonic, traversing anteriorly and inferiorly through the myocardium of the interventricular septum; the retro-cardiac path passes around the heart within the atrioventricular (coronary) sulcus (Kim et al 2006).
Depending on their location and initial path, a proportion of anomalous coronary arteries are associated with functional ischaemia and are the second most common cause of sudden cardiac death among young athletes (Basso et al 2000). Anomalous vessels taking the inter-arterial path are most frequently associated with sudden cardiac death, perhaps due to the exercise-related expansion of the ascending aorta and pulmonary trunk and the consequent increase in angulation and decrease in luminal diameter of the anomalous vessel (Angelini et al 2006) (Fig. 7.2.2). Sudden cardiac death is more likely with an inter-arterial anomalous left coronary artery or anterior interventricular artery because of the larger volume of subtended myocardium at risk during an associated cardiac event (Moustafa et al 2008). Other paths are not commonly associated with haemodynamic compromise because they are not associated with luminal narrowing (Fig. 7.2.3), but their location may complicate surgery.

Single coronary artery

A single coronary artery is an anomaly in which only one coronary artery arises from an aortic sinus and supplies the entire heart (Fig. 7.2.4). A range of permutations of origin, course and distribution may occur, some of which can be classified into predefined groups (Lipton et al 1979). The left or right coronary artery may be singular and perfuse the entire heart, or the left coronary artery may arise ectopically from a relatively normal right coronary artery or vice versa. The proximal part of the ectopic artery may follow one of the five paths previously described to reach its normal course and territory (see Fig. 7.2.1). A single coronary artery may be associated with other congenital cardiac anomalies such as transposition of the great vessels, coronary artery fistulae, bicuspid aortic valve and the tetralogy of Fallot. The direction of blood flow in cases of a single coronary artery is normally anterograde or centrifugal (from larger central vessels to smaller peripheral vessels), whereas in left coronary artery atresia the direction of blood flow is partially retrograde or centripetal (from the smaller peripheral collateral vessels to one or more of the main left-sided arteries) (Musiani et al 1997). The vascular supply of the left ventricle when there is a single coronary artery is much better developed than it is in left coronary artery atresia, where the collateral vessels supplying the left ventricle are small (see Fig. 7.2.6).
Anomalous origins from the pulmonary trunk
Anomalous coronary artery origin from the pulmonary trunk is one of the more haemodynamically significant coronary artery anomalies (Fig. 7.2.5). Either the left or right coronary artery or, uncommonly, both arteries may be involved. Cases where both coronary arteries arise from the pulmonary trunk are unlikely to survive beyond the first month of life without surgical intervention.
Anomalous origin of the right coronary artery from the pulmonary trunk (ARCAPA) and anomalous origin of left coronary artery from the pulmonary trunk/artery (ALCAPA or Bland–White–Garland syndrome) are rare (Pfannschmidt et al 1992, Williams et al 2006). ARCAPA is usually asymptomatic with no evidence of myocardial ischaemia. ALCAPA is of little relevance in utero where systemic (aortic) and pulmonary arterial pressures are equal, so that anterograde flow to the heart of similarly oxygenated blood occurs through both left and right coronary arteries. After birth, closure of the ductus arteriosus and a fall in pulmonary vascular resistance reduces pulmonary arterial pressure, which results in retrograde flow through the left coronary artery. Adequate perfusion of the territory of the left coronary artery becomes reliant on the development of a collateral circulation from the right coronary artery, which consequently dilates to accommodate the increased blood flow. The majority of patients with ALCAPA die during their first year of life, but occasionally patients with well-established collateral vessels may present later in life and are classified as having ‘adult type’ ALCAPA (Wesselhoeft et al 1968).

Anomalies of intrinsic anatomy

Hypoplasia or atresia of the ostium of a coronary artery within the aortic sinus can obstruct that artery at or near its origin and can result in a collateral circulation from the functional (normal) coronary artery developing to supply the affected territory (Fig. 7.2.6). Coronary ostial atresia is often associated with ischaemic manifestations (Musiani et al 1997). When the left coronary artery is affected, right-to-left collateralization occurs during the later stages of development, and is often insufficient to meet the oxygen requirements of the left ventricular myocardium. Atresia of the ostium or proximal part of a coronary artery may be accompanied or replaced by a fibrous connection spanning the gap between the aortic sinus and a more distal functional part of the affected coronary artery. The fibrous connection can pull on its points of attachment causing a focal distortion that may be visible as a beak-like projection on imaging. Single coronary artery anomalies are not characterized by a focal distortion of adjacent vessel walls because their developmental origin is different and no such fibrous remnant exists.

Anomalies of termination

An anomalous termination occurs when a coronary artery drains directly or via branches into a cardiac chamber, great vessel or other vascular structure, rather than into a capillary bed of the heart. Reported sites of abnormal termination include the right and left atria and ventricles, coronary venous system, pulmonary arteries and pulmonary veins. Fistulae terminating in the right heart chambers, pulmonary trunk or pulmonary arteries are the most common (Fig. 7.2.7). Depending on the position of origin and the vessel or chamber of termination, the affected vessel may become dilated or tortuous in response to increased blood flow and aneurysms may form (Fig. 7.2.8).
While most instances of coronary artery fistulae are congenital, some result from iatrogenic injury, infection or inflammatory disease. Larger coronary artery fistulae with significant intra-cardiac shunts are rare in adults: most are detected and repaired during childhood (Gowda et al 2006). Many are asymptomatic and identified incidentally on coronary angiography (Wilde and Watt 1980). Although the degree of left-to-right shunting of blood is usually very small and of little consequence, the presence of large or multiple left-to-right shunts could result in a right ventricular volume overload (Rittenhouse et al 1975). Multiple fistulae may form a complex network of shunts. A careful interrogation of conventional CTCA images can help identify fistulae within a tangled network of collateral vessels, some of which may initially be hidden from view on the 3D rendered images (Fig. 7.2.9): cinematic rendering can aid this task by depicting a more recognizable model of complex aberrant anatomy. The presence of fistulae can complicate surgical approaches to the heart; exact mapping of their anatomy and relations is required in such cases.
