Chapter 51: Wrist and hand

Skin and Soft Tissues

Skin

Dorsal skin versus palmar skin

The dorsal skin is thin, mobile and frequently hirsute over the proximal phalanges and the ulnar aspect of the dorsum of the hand. The skin of the palm and the palmar surface of the digits is thick and hairless, with a well-defined stratum lucidum and a higher density of nerve endings and eccrine sweat glands, but no sebaceous glands. The skin on the dorsum is visible to others and has aesthetic relevance whereas the palmar skin has more functional relevance.

Skin creases and fingerprints

Flexure lines crease the skin of the flexor surfaces of the wrist and hand (Fig. 51.1) where they are anchored to the deep fascia. Less regular, mainly transverse, creases cross the dorsal aspect of the carpal, metacarpophalangeal and interphalangeal joints. The dorsal creases are more prominent in extension; the loose skin becomes stretched in flexion. (For a general review of ‘skin lines’, see Chapter 7.) Three anterior transverse lines usually cross the wrist. The proximal line marks the proximal limit of the flexor synovial sheaths, the intermediate line overlies the radiocarpal joint, and a distal line marks the proximal border of the flexor retinaculum.
In the palm, a curved radial longitudinal line bounds the thenar eminence, ending close to the distal wrist crease. Proximal and distal transverse lines ascend medially across the palm. The proximal line begins at the distal end of the thenar crease and runs obliquely to the middle of the hypothenar eminence across the shafts of the metacarpals. The distal palmar crease begins at or near the cleft between the index and middle finger, and curves across the palm over the necks of the metacarpals from the middle finger to little finger. In the ring and little finger the crease is just proximal to the underlying metacarpophalangeal joint.
The second to fifth digits have proximal, middle and distal sets of transverse lines. The proximal, often double, lies at the level of the distal margin of the webspace, approximately half way along the underlying proximal phalanx. The middle creases are usually double, the proximal line lying directly over the proximal interphalangeal joint. The distal lines are usually single, and lie proximal to the distal interphalangeal joints. The metacarpophalangeal joint of the thumb is crossed by a crease that starts on the radial side and ends in the web space level with the base of the proximal phalanx. There is a second, shorter, crease, usually 1 cm distal to this line. Two creases cross the interphalangeal joint of the thumb (see Fig. 51.1).
‘Fingerprints’ are striations that are unique to each individual and particularly well developed at sites of grip, namely the pulps of the fingers and thumb together with the base of the hypothenar eminence.

Cutaneous vascular supply

The skin of the palmar aspect of the wrist is supplied by branches from the superficial palmar branch of the radial artery, the ulnar artery and, occasionally, the median artery (Fig. 51.2). The skin over the thenar eminence is supplied by small perforating branches from the superficial palmar branch of the radial artery and the princeps pollicis. The skin over the hypothenar eminence is supplied by perforating branches from the ulnar artery, some of which pass through palmaris brevis. The rest of the palm is supplied by branches from the common palmar digital arteries that pierce the palmar aponeurosis, and branches from the radialis indicis artery. The palmar digital skin is supplied by branches from each digital artery. In the distal phalanx, the two digital arteries typically form an H-shaped anastomosis, from which cutaneous perforators fan out within the pulp. Deep digital veins accompanying the digital arteries are usually very small and frequently absent. Superficial palmar veins tend to pass dorsally and drain into the larger superficial dorsal venous system (Fig. 51.3).
The dorsal skin of the wrist is supplied by branches from an arterial plexus which lies over the extensor retinaculum and is formed by branches of the radial artery, including its dorsal carpal branch, the dorsal carpal branch of the ulnar artery, and the anterior and posterior interosseous arteries. The blood supply to the dorsum of the hand arises from longitudinal rows of four or five branches from each of the dorsal metacarpal arteries, which usually arise either directly from the radial artery or from the dorsal carpal arch. At the level of the neck of the metacarpals, where the second, third and fourth dorsal metacarpal arteries communicate with branches from the corresponding common palmar digital arteries, a large cutaneous perforating branch passes proximally to supply an area of skin as far as the dorsal aspect of the wrist.
The blood supply to the dorsum of the fingers arises proximally from the terminal branches of the dorsal metacarpal arteries, (supplying a region as far distally as the proximal interphalangeal joint) as well as from dorsal branches of the palmar digital arteries that are given off at the level of each phalanx (Fig. 51.4). At the level of the distal phalanx, the cutaneous supply comes from three dorsal arcades: a superficial arcade over the base of the distal phalanx, and two distal subungual arcades (Fig. 51.5). The skin of the dorsum of the thumb is supplied by longitudinal axial branches of the princeps pollicis and dorsal branches from the palmar digital arteries.
Degloving injuries of the hand and wrist
A degloving injury is one in which the skin is avulsed from its underlying structures, often leaving the entire musculoskeletal unit of the hand intact, thereby permitting the patient to mobilize the ‘naked’ finger fully. These injuries occur as the result of a low velocity traction force being applied to the affected part. The plane of cleavage is through the subcutaneous tissues, leaving the underlying structures essentially intact. These injuries may occur if the hand is caught in industrial machinery, typically between rollers or in a conveyor belt.
The most common manifestation is the ‘ring avulsion injury’, when a ring is caught and the finger rapidly pulled away (Bamba et al 2018). The degloving may be complete or partial. Complete avulsion is rare, whereas avulsion of the skin of all or part of the finger is relatively common. Urbaniak et al (1981) grouped ring avulsion injuries into three classes: those in which the blood supply is intact (class I); those in which the blood supply is compromised (class II); and those in which digits are either completely degloved or amputated (class III). While these represent injuries of increasing severity, the avulsion force required to achieve them does not occur in a linear manner (Kupfer et al 1999). The longitudinal force applied to a finger causes a traction injury to the blood vessels, which may extend over a considerable distance. The crushing element of a degloving injury may cause skin necrosis and thrombosis of the underlying vessels (Brooks et al 2007).
Treatment of these injuries depends on their extent (Krishnamoorthy and Karthikeyan 2011). In complete avulsion, restoration of the tissue and microvascular anastomosis is called replantation. Where the skin remains attached but is devascularized, restoration of the blood supply by anastomosis is called revascularization. Because the traction injury may extend over a considerable distance, large sections of vessel may need to be excised: the judicious use of vein grafts is essential. If neither of these procedures is feasible, the avulsed skin may be defatted and reapplied to the affected part, where it effectively acts as a split skin graft. If none of these techniques is practicable, primary amputation may have to be considered. Subsequently, reconstruction may be by split-skin graft, skin/fasciocutaneous flap or free flap. The decision whether to reconstruct or to amputate is multifactorial and depends not just on the injury but on the medical, psychological and social situation of the patient.
Basic skin flaps in reconstruction
The pedicled groin flap used to be regarded as the workhorse flap for reconstruction of the hand. Swelling due to the dependent position of the hand, and the need for a second operation, coupled with the social stigma associated with the position of the hand, has led to it falling out of favour.
Regional and free flaps have gradually replaced the pedicled groin flap. Numerous different types of regional and free flaps have been used to cover soft tissue defects in the hand, based on the principle that the hand has a reliable vascular anatomy. The anatomical basis can be a named artery to which a free flap is attached (e.g. toe to thumb transfer), a naked vessel that can be rotated with a paddle of skin (e.g. the radial forearm and dorsal intermetacarpal artery flaps), or a skin flap based on a reliable subcutaneous blood supply (e.g. the cross-finger flap).
The radial forearm flap was originally described by surgeons from the Shenyang Military Hospital in 1978 and was consequently known as the ‘Chinese flap’. It can be used as a free flap or as a reversed pedicled fasciocutaneous island flap to cover soft tissue defects of the ipsilateral hand. Its blood supply is based on septocutaneous perforators that arise from the distal part of the radial artery and supply the skin on the radial side of the lower forearm; these tend to become more sparse proximally. Venous drainage is by the venae comitantes and superficial subcutaneous veins.
The ulnar forearm flap is a fasciocutaneous flap based on the ulnar artery distal to its common interosseous branch. A variant on this flap based on the ascending branch of the dorsal ulnar artery was described by Becker and Gilbert (1988). It has the disadvantage of a short pedicle but is useful for achieving cover of defects of the proximal and ulnar side of the palm. For each of these flaps, the patency of the other vessel must be confirmed by the clinical ‘Allen occlusive test’ wherein both radial and ulnar arteries are occluded by digital pressure at the wrist then the ulnar or radial artery is released: perfusion of the hand indicates adequate residual flow in the other artery. This test is now supplemented by Doppler ultrasonography and angiography.
The posterior interosseous island flap, distally based on the dorsal carpal arterial arch via the anastomoses between the posterior and anterior interosseous arteries just lateral to the head of the ulna (Costa et al 2007), was introduced by Zancolli and Angrigiani in 1985 (Zancolli and Angrigiani 1988). Its principal advantages are that it spares both of the major arteries of the forearm and is a good match for the skin colour and texture of the dorsum of the hand. Care must be taken to avoid injury to the adjacent posterior interosseous nerve when harvesting the flap. Doppler studies should be carried out preoperatively to ensure the presence and positioning of the anastomoses between the posterior and anterior interosseous arteries. It has been claimed that most defects of the hand can be treated using this flap; its particular advantage is in the treatment of hand injuries with severe accompanying vascular damage.
The anatomical distribution of the first dorsal metacarpal artery allows a flap of skin over the dorsum of the proximal phalanx of the index finger to be raised on the artery and its accompanying venae comitantes. This flap is particularly useful under certain circumstances for reconstruction of the thumb following injury. The distribution of the second, third and fourth dorsal metacarpal arteries allows the surgical elevation of flaps of dorsal skin based either proximally, on the dorsal metacarpal arteries proper, or distally, on their direct cutaneous branches. These flaps may be used for reconstructing areas of missing tissue elsewhere in the hand. The dorsal cutaneous branch of the ulnar artery is constant and can support a flap of skin on the medial aspect of the distal forearm just proximal to the wrist; it is useful in the reconstruction of areas of missing skin in this region.
Anatomical studies that define the cutaneous vascularity of the hand provide the scientific foundations of new skin flap techniques. These can then be added to the surgeon’s armamentarium when faced with soft tissue loss in the upper extremity (Moschella et al 1996, Moschella and Cordova 2006, Hierner et al 2016).

Cutaneous innervation

The skin of the anterior aspect of the wrist is innervated by the terminal branches of the lateral and medial cutaneous nerves of the forearm. The skin of the palm is innervated by the palmar branches of the ulnar and median nerves. The skin of the radial aspect of the dorsum of the wrist and hand, as well as the dorsal aspect of the radial three and a half digits as far distally as the nail beds, is innervated by the dorsal digital nerves (the terminal branches of the radial nerve) (Fig. 51.6). The palmar skin of the thumb, index and middle fingers and the radial aspect of the ring finger is innervated by the median nerve, while that of the little finger and the ulnar side of the ring finger is innervated by the ulnar nerve (Figs 51.7–51.8). Between two and five dorsal digital nerves supply each digit. The skin of the ulnar aspect of the dorsum of the wrist and hand, and the dorsal aspect of the ulnar one and a half digits as far distally as the nail beds, is innervated by dorsal digital branches of the dorsal branch of the ulnar nerve. The skin of the dorsum of the middle and distal phalanges is innervated by dorsal branches of the palmar digital nerves (Fig. 51.9).

Nail apparatus

The nail apparatus is described in Chapter 7 (see Fig. 7.27) (Zook 1981).
It comprises the nail plate beneath which lie the germinal and sterile matrix. The tissue to the side of the nail plate is the paronychium. The nail is enveloped proximally by the nail fold, the roof of which is formed by skin and is known as the nail wall. The thin membrane beyond the roof onto the nail is the eponychium. The skin just beyond the nail plate is the hyponychium.

Soft tissues

Flexor retinaculum

The flexor retinaculum (see Fig. 51.27) is a strong, fibrous band that crosses the front of the carpus and converts its anterior concavity into the carpal tunnel which conveys the flexor tendons of the digits and the median nerve. The retinaculum is short and broad, measuring 2.5–3 cm in both dimensions. It is attached medially to the pisiform and the hook of the hamate. Laterally, it splits into superficial and deep laminae. The superficial lamina is attached to the tubercles of the scaphoid and trapezium. The deep lamina is attached to the medial lip of the groove on the trapezium. Together with this groove, the two laminae form a tunnel, lined by a synovial sheath, that contains the tendon of flexor carpi radialis. The retinaculum is crossed superficially by the ulnar vessels and nerve, immediately radial to the pisiform, and by the palmar cutaneous branches of the median and ulnar nerves (see Fig. 51.27). A slender band of fascia, the superficial part of the flexor retinaculum, bridges the ulnar neurovascular bundle and is attached to the radial side of the pisiform, forming a tunnel (Guyon’s canal), which is the occasional site of ulnar nerve entrapment (Fig. 51.10). The tendons of palmaris longus and flexor carpi ulnaris are partly attached to the anterior surface of the retinaculum. Distally, some of the intrinsic muscles of the thumb and little finger are attached to the retinaculum. The retinaculum mimimizes the flexion torque that the long finger flexors transmit at the radiocarpal joint.

Guyon’s canal

Guyon’s canal is a fibro-osseous canal, approximately 4 cm long in the adult, on the anteromedial aspect of the wrist (see Fig. 51.10). Its roof is formed by the superficial part of the flexor retinaculum and palmaris brevis. Its floor is formed by the transverse carpal and pisohamate ligaments and, more distally, by flexor digiti minimi. The ulnar side is bounded by flexor carpi ulnaris, the pisiform bone and abductor digiti minimi, and the radial side by the extrinsic flexor tendons, the transverse carpal ligament and the hook of the hamate. The fibrous arcade at the origin of the hypothenar muscles lies distally.
The canal transmits the ulnar nerve and artery, together with occasional venae comitantes. The ulnar nerve divides within it at the level of the hook of the hamate into a deep motor branch that passes deeply and radially around the medial and then the distal edge of the hook of hamate, and a superficial sensory branch that passes distally and ulnarwards. Depending on the site, motor, sensory or both components can be affected by compression: if the nerve is compressed proximally, both modalities will be affected, whereas, distal to the bifurcation, either the sensory or motor branch will be compromised.
Variations in the branching of the ulnar nerve may occur. The most common variant is its trifurcation into three branches (deep and two superficial) in about a third of cases, and varying innervation of the abductor digiti minimi.

Extensor retinaculum

The extensor retinaculum is a strong, fibrous band which extends obliquely across the back of the wrist (Fig. 51.11). It prevents ‘bowstringing’ of the long digital extensor tendons across the wrist joint, minimizing the torque that these tendons transmit to the radiocarpal joint as the fingers extend. It is attached laterally to the anterior border of the radius, then passes medially to blend into the fascia overlying the ulnar side of the distal forearm and carpus and into the anteromedial surfaces of the pisiform and triquetrum. As the retinaculum passes across the wrist, it is connected to vertical ridges that are attached to the back of the radius, creating separate compartments for abductor pollicis longus–extensor pollicis brevis, extensor carpi radialis longus–extensor carpi radialis brevis, extensor pollicis longus, extensor digitorum–extensor indicis, and extensor digiti minimi. Extensor carpi ulnaris has a separate sub-sheath attached only to the back of the head of the ulna (there would be no rotation if it were in continuity with the other extensors which are bound to the radius by the retinaculum).

Palmar fascial complex

The palmar fascia is a three-dimensional ligamentous system consisting of longitudinal, transverse and vertical fibres (Fig. 51.12).
Longitudinal fibre system
The longitudinal fibres run distally beyond the tendon of palmaris longus (if present) or the flexor retinaculum of the wrist (if palmaris longus is not present) to fan out across the whole width of the central third of the palm, producing four well-defined longitudinal bundles anterior to the metacarpals of the index, middle, ring and little fingers. This is known as the superficial palmar aponeurosis. A less well-defined bundle passes to the thumb. Distal to the transverse fibres of the palmar aponeurosis, the longitudinal fibres pass in three layers (McGrouther 1982). The most superficial longitudinal fibres (layer 1) are inserted superficially into the skin of the distal palm between the distal palmar crease and the proximal digital crease. Some superficial fibres pass distally into the palmar midline of the digit. Deeper longitudinal fibres (layer 2) pass deep to the natatory ligament and neurovascular bundles into the skin at the apex of the web space and into the fingers themselves, where they are continuous with Cleland’s ligaments and the lateral digital sheet on either side. Deeper still, the longitudinal fibres in layer 3 perforate the deep transverse metacarpal ligament to pass around the sides of the metacarpophalangeal joint and attach to the metacarpal and proximal phalanx, and extensor tendon.
Transverse fibre system
The transverse fibre system consists of the superficial transverse metacarpal (natatory) ligament, the transverse fibres of the palmar aponeurosis and the deep transverse metacarpal ligament.
Superficial transverse metacarpal ligament (natatory ligament)
The fibres of the superficial transverse metacarpal ligament cross the apex of the web skin and extend into each digit to blend with the lateral digital sheet. They limit the separation of adjacent fingers and support the skin in the web space. Fibres on the ulnar side of the little finger surround the insertion of abductor digiti minimi. Fibres on the radial side of the index finger form the distal commissural ligament (of Grapow) which passes proximally to meet the fascia in front of the thumb metacarpal.
Transverse fibres of the palmar aponeurosis
The transverse fibres of the palmar aponeurosis (Skoog fibres) form the deepest layer of the palmar fascia. They lie proximal to the distal palmar crease in a band approximately 2 cm wide, interconnecting the anterior fibres of the flexor tendon sheaths with the fasciae over the thenar and hypothenar muscles. The extension to the first ray forms the proximal commissural ligament.
Deep transverse metacarpal ligament
The strong fibres of the deep transverse metacarpal ligament lie deep to the palmar aponeurosis and flexor sheaths, and are attached to the adjacent edges of the volar plates of the metacarpal heads of the little, ring, middle and index fingers. The ulnar edge of the little finger intermetacarpal ligament is attached to abductor digiti minimi; the radial edge of the index finger intermetacarpal ligament is attached to the first dorsal interosseous.
Vertical fibre system
The vertical fibres (of Grapow) are more delicate. They pass from the dermis, between the longitudinal and transverse fibres, to the fibrous flexor sheaths and the metacarpals. They are concentrated on either side of the palmar skin creases, as well as the thenar and hypothenar eminences. The vertical septa of Legueu and Juvara (Bilderback and Rayan 2004) pass from the transverse fibres of the palmar aponeurosis to the underlying deep transverse metacarpal ligament, forming compartments that contain the flexor tendons and the lumbricals and neurovascular bundles.

Digital fascial complex

The superficial fascia within a finger is fibrofatty in its palmar and dorsal aspects, but becomes condensed laterally into the lateral digital sheet. The flexor sheath is a well-defined thickening of the fascia (discussed in detail on page 975). Cleland’s ligaments extend from the sides of the proximal interphalangeal joints, pass transversely and dorsal to the neurovascular bundles to insert into the lateral digital sheet (see Fig. 51.12D–E) (Zwanenburg et al 2014). Grayson’s ligaments are more delicate and may even be discontinuous: they pass palmarwards from the lateral sides of the phalanges to pass over the midline of the flexor sheath to insert in the skin of the opposite side (see Fig. 51.12D–E). The retrovascular cord (of Thomine) is very flimsy in health. The oblique retinacular ligament (ORL) of Landsmeer (Adkinson et al 2014) is an inconsistent anatomical structure with tenuous fibres that connect the flexor sheath attachment to bone proximal to the proximal interphalangeal joint into the lateral band of the extensor tendon alongside the middle phalanx and into the base of the distal phalanx.

Functions of the fascia of the hand

The fascial continuum of the hand performs a number of different, but interrelated, functions, such as channelling structures in transit between the forearm and the digits transmitting loads; and anchoring, binding and tethering structures. It has a complex nerve supply suggesting a proprioceptive function (Stecco et al 2018).
Channelling of structures in transit between forearm and digits
The vertical septa act as spacers between the tendons and neurovascular bundles of the individual digital rays. Where tendons change direction around a concave surface, the channels are thickened. The flexor tendon sheaths, flexor retinaculum and extensor retinaculum act as sheaths or pulleys to prevent bow-stringing of the tendons.
Transmission of loads
At points where compressive loading is applied to the hand, such as the finger pulp and palm, loculi of fat act as shock absorbers. The loculi are contained within defined fibrous boundaries, which means that the shape, but not the volume, of each loculus can change. The compliance or deformability of the boundaries determines the amount of shock absorption. The palm also contains much larger fibrous compartments between skin and skeleton that transmit muscles, tendons and other structures. The honeycomb pattern of these compartments constitutes the palmar shock absorption system. The soft, padded parts of the hand are able to conform to the contours of objects that are grasped which allows better interpretation of sensation and better grip. The hand must also resist tensile loading. Tendons and ligaments are particularly suitable for resisting such forces but many other parts of the fascial continuum, such as the anchorage system of the palm, also play a major role in resisting ‘pulling’ forces.
Anchorage
Whilst the thin hair-bearing skin on the dorsum has considerable excursion on flexion and extension, the thick palmar skin is retained by fascial ligaments that allow the hand to flex while retaining the skin in position. The palmar creases have been described as skin ‘joints’. Fascial anchors may be vertical (perpendicular to the palm), as in the mid-palm, where scattered vertical fibres run from the dermis down into the depths of the hand; horizontal (in the plane of the palm); or oblique to the skin surface. As the hand grips an object, the skin creases constrain each intervening cushion of fat to provide a secure conforming grip; adhesion is further improved by sweating of the palmar skin.
The insertion of the longitudinal (pretendinous) fibres of the palmar aponeurosis is an example of a well-developed horizontal anchorage system. The most superficial longitudinal fibres insert into the dermis of the distal palm. This arrangement resists horizontal shearing force in gripping tasks, such as holding a hammer, where it prevents distal skin slippage or degloving of the palm during use. The characteristic blisters on the palms of those unaccustomed to such use map out the sites of the skin anchorage points. This anchorage system can be demonstrated by flexing the palm until the skin of the distal palm folds loosely. An attempt to pull the loose skin distally will reveal the anchoring longitudinal fibres of the palmar aponeurosis. Oblique anchors occur in the fingers where Cleland’s ligaments tether the skin of the proximal and middle segments of the digits to the region of the proximal interphalangeal joints.
Binding
Transversely orientated fascial structures help to maintain the transverse arch of the hand by ‘binding’ the underlying skeletal structures or the tendon sheaths.
Limiting or tethering
Joint motion is limited not only by the action of its ligaments but also, in some cases, by skin tightness. Skin in the interdigital webs is reinforced by the natatory ligaments in the fingers and by the commissural ligament in the thumb, which resist stretch. The volar plates of the metacarpophalangeal and interphalangeal joints limit hyperextension. The deep transverse intermetacarpal ligaments prevent splaying (abduction from the axis of the third metacarpal).
Since the oblique retinacular ligament of Landsmeer runs from an attachment anterior to the axis of the proximal interphalangeal joint to an insertion posterior to the axis of the distal interphalangeal joint, it causes obligatory extension of the distal interphalangeal joint when the proximal interphalangeal joint extends – a ‘tenodesis’ effect.
Lubrication
There are many other gliding planes, e.g. between periosteum and the extensor apparatus, and between the extensor apparatus and the skin, on the dorsum of the digits. The flexor tendon sheaths are lined by synovium: the extensor tendons are also invested in a synovial gliding plane. Biomechanical studies of tendons gliding within their sheaths have emphasized the importance of avoiding extreme postures of a joint, which increase the friction between tendon and pulley, as well as the need to establish a smooth, well-lubricated tendon surface after repair. Increased friction within a tendon sheath may, in part, explain the cause of spontaneous rupture of extensor pollicis longus because the gliding resistance of the tendon has been shown to be significantly higher than that of other tendons (An 2007).
Vascular protection and pumping action
The blood vessels of the palm are surrounded by a cuff of tough fascia or by a fatty pad. When the hand is compressed, as in gripping, these relatively incompressible fascial structures protect the predominantly palmar arterial inflow into the hand to some extent. By contrast, large capacitance veins on the dorsum of the hand lie in gliding skin, surrounded by loose areolar tissue which allows the veins to dilate. However, when the hand grips firmly, the skin tightens, so expressing the venous blood.
Framework for muscle attachments
The fascial framework can be visualized as a harness by which muscles can act on the underlying skeleton: many of the small muscles of the hand are attached to it, at least in part. The metacarpophalangeal joint is moved by a ring of fascial and ligamentous structures that surrounds the joint and to which tendons are attached.
Dupuytren’s disease
The cause of Dupuytren’s disease remains unknown. Many theories have been postulated. Geographical variations in incidence suggest a genetic cause, but it is not a monoclonal process (Chansky et al 1999) and at least nine genetic loci have been implicated in its development (Dolmans et al 2011). Environmental risk factors include alcohol consumption, smoking, diabetes, manual work and vibration exposure, and possibly the ingestion of anticonvulsants (Descatha et al 2011). In the proliferative stage of the disease, there is considerable myofibroblast activity, which may be the result of upregulation of the musculo-aponeurotic fibrosarcoma oncogene homologue B (MafB), found in the diseased cord tissue (Lee et al 2006). Mechanical stimuli subsequently cause the alignment of myofibroblasts along the lines of traction, which are later replaced by collagen. These collagenous cords cause the flexion contractures that characterize the disease. Treatment of Dupuytren’s disease includes suppressing myofibroblast turnover with radiotherapy; dividing the cord (fasciotomy) with a percutaneous needle or an enzyme (collagenase); surgically removing the cord (fasciectomy), which can include removal of the overlying involved skin (dermofasciectomy), and replacement with disease-free skin from elsewhere (skin autograft).
The pattern of contracture is determined by the anatomy of the basic constituents of the palmar and digital fasciae. The physiological ‘bands’ formed by the healthy palmar fascia are replaced by pathological ‘cords’ of diseased tissue. The pretendinous bands that constitute the superficial layer (layer 1) of the palmar aponeurosis are most frequently involved. The development of nodules that tether the skin to the deep fascia usually precedes cord formation. Contracture of the pretendinous cords causes flexion of the metacarpophalangeal joints. Shortening of the vertical fibres causes skin pits. Involvement of the natatory ligament restricts abduction of the fingers. The transverse metacarpal fibres are never contracted and are a useful surgical landmark in the palm because the neurovascular bundle runs deep to them. The vertical fibres can become very thickened but do not contract: they form a dense barrier between the flexor tendons and the neurovascular bundle.
The pretendinous cord in the palm extends into a finger as a central cord that lies superficially in the midline. The lateral digital sheet gives rise to a lateral cord that is attached to the skin or flexor tendon sheath near Grayson’s ligament, causing a flexion contracture of the proximal interphalangeal joint and, sometimes, displacing the digital neurovascular bundle medially. Spiral cords arise from the pretendinous band in layer 1 of the palmar fascia and pass deeper through layer 2 to become attached to the middle phalanx by Grayson’s ligament. They are so named because they spiral round the neurovascular bundle, displacing it proximally, palmarwards and towards the midline as contracture increases, rendering it vulnerable to injury during fasciectomy (McFarlane 1974).
The ulnar side of the little finger often develops a dense cord (Barton’s ‘abductor cord’) that runs from the medial edge of the insertion of abductor digiti minimi, through the lateral digital sheet and onto the anteromedial surface of the flexor sheath at the level of the A3 pulley. This causes a pronounced flexion contracture of the proximal interphalangeal joint and must be fully removed to achieve a successful and durable correction.
The distal interphalangeal joint is uncommonly involved because the palmar aponeurosis usually peters out beyond the sides of the middle phalanx. Nevertheless, a thin tight cord on one or other side of the digit can occur, causing a combined contracture of the proximal and distal interphalangeal joints. Distal interphalangeal joint hyperextension can occur in association with a severe prolonged contracture of the proximal interphalangeal joint, with secondary attrition of the central slip, soft tissue contractures and boutonnière deformity. Some authors have suggested that this deformity occurs because Dupuytren’s disease affects the oblique retinacular ligament (Littler 1967).

Digital and palmar spaces

There are many potential spaces within the hand, often with ill-defined margins. The nail fold is a ‘U-shaped’ space made up of the eponychium and the lateral nail fold. The pulp space in the apex and palmar pulp space of the fingertip are formed by the fibrous attachments of the distal phalanx to the digital pulp skin. The synovial flexor tendon sheaths are described on pages 975 and 976. The web space is bounded distally by the skin and natatory ligament, by the deep transverse metacarpal ligament posteriorly, and by the deep attachments of the palmar fascia, together with their lateral attachments to the tendon sheaths, proximally. The lumbrical canal transmits the lumbricals as they pass dorsally, palmar to the deep transverse metacarpal ligament and radial to the metacarpophalangeal joint; the interosseous canal transmits the interossei as they pass dorsal to the deep transverse metacarpal ligament to one side or other of the metacarpophalangeal joint. The deep palmar space is a complex, three-dimensional space, limited proximally by the carpal tunnel. It lies deep to the palmar aponeurosis, between the radial and ulnar condensations of vertical fibres that connect the palmar aponeurosis to the thenar and hypothenar eminences. Partitions that pass deeply from the longitudinal bands of the palmar aponeurosis form eight narrow compartments; four contain the digital flexor tendons and four contain the lumbricals and the neurovascular bundles.
Infections of the hand
The spaces of the hand form predictable sites of infection and also limit its spread. Infections in the digit can occur in the nail fold (paronychia); the distal pulp (a felon); and the flexor sheaths. The flexor synovial sheath of the thumb and the little finger often connect in the palm (see Fig. 51.27C) such that an infection in one can spread to the other causing a ‘horseshoe abscess’. Deep infections in the palm are not usually confined to any particular space; the infection may be inoculated by direct penetration but more often occurs from a primary infection in the web space or flexor sheath.
An abscess in any of the digital or palmar spaces should always be drained. An understanding of the anatomy is crucial to successful surgical treatment. Incisions should, wherever possible, follow the line of the flexion creases and adhere to the principles of extensile exposure. A felon is drained with a midlateral incision or midline incision avoiding the neurovascular bundle and spreading apart the taut vertical septa. A web space infection is drained with a longitudinal incision that allows extensile exposure of the web space and the lumbrical-interosseous canals, also avoiding a web contracture that would follow a transverse incision. A thenar space abscess is drained through a palmar incision: the motor and palmar cutaneous branches of the median nerve are very vulnerable to iatrogenic damage (Birch 2011). The mid-palmar space is best approached through a combination of a transverse distal palmar incision with an oblique longitudinal extension (McDonald et al 2011), although alternative incisions can be employed, based on the location and size of the collection. Hypothenar space infections are very rare and tend to be confined by the hypothenar fascia, resulting in swelling of the ulnar side of the hand with some flexion and adduction of the little finger. These lesions are of less clinical consequence than other palmar collections because there is no direct communication with the two other major palmar spaces and hence the long flexors of the fingers and lumbricals are not involved. They may be drained by an incision along the ulnar border of the space without significant risk of damage to underlying structures. The flexor sheath is drained by a transverse or oblique incision over the flexion crease of the distal interphalangeal joint and another over the distal palmar (metacarpophalangeal) joint crease.
Compartment syndrome of the hand
There are 10 identifiable muscle compartments in the hand: four contain each of the dorsal interossei; three contain the palmar interossei; three contain the thenar, hypothenar and adductor compartments. Any or all of these compartments may be injured by a rise in intracompartmental pressure caused by bleeding, blast, burn, crush, ischaemia–reperfusion and by injection, whether of therapeutic substances at low pressure or of industrial agents at high pressure. This rise in pressure prevents venous inflow then capillary (low pressure) inflow. Arterial (high pressure) inflow persists which causes yet more congestion; this compounds the rise in compartment pressure and the condition deteriorates. The tissues then become ischaemic, causing transient impairment then permanent necrosis. The hand will be extremely painful, usually with tingling and weakness due to nerve ischaemia. It becomes increasingly swollen. On examination, the tissues are tense; passive movement of the fingers is very painful due to stretching of the ischaemic muscles. There will be reduced sensitivity in the territory of the nerves passing through the compartment. Intra-compartmental pressure measurements should be taken; the means by which this is done is not critical (Ortiz and Berger 1998) but it should not delay emergency surgery if the diagnosis is reasonably suspected.

Bones

The skeleton of the hand comprises the carpus, metacarpus and the phalanges.

Carpus

The carpus contains eight bones: four each in proximal and distal rows (Figs 51.13–51.15). In radial to ulnar order, the scaphoid, lunate, triquetrum and pisiform make up the proximal row, and the trapezium, trapezoid, capitate and hamate make up the distal row. The pisiform articulates with the palmar surface of the triquetrum. The other three proximal bones form an arch that is convex proximally, and which articulates with the distal surface of the radius and central articular disc of the triangular fibrocartilage complex. The distal concavity of the arch articulates with the capitate and hamate.
The dorsal carpal surface is convex. The palmar surface forms a deeply concave carpal groove, accentuated by the palmar projection of the radial and ulnar borders. The ulnar projection is formed by the pisiform and the hook (hamulus) of the hamate. The pisiform is at the proximal border of the hypothenar eminence, on the ulnar side of the palm, and it is easily felt in front of the triquetrum. The hamulus is concave in a radial direction; its tip is palpable 2.5 cm distal and 1 cm radial to the pisiform, in line with the radial border of the ring finger. The radial border of the carpal groove is formed by the tubercles of the scaphoid and trapezium. The former is distal on the anterior scaphoid surface and palpable (sometimes also visible) as a small medial knob at the proximal border of the palmar thenar eminence, radial to the tendon of flexor carpi radialis. The tubercle of the trapezium is a vertically rounded ridge on the anterior surface of the bone, slightly hollow medially and just distal and radial to the scaphoid tubercle. The scaphoid can be moved passively into extension by direct backward pressure onto the scaphoid tubercle. The carpal groove is converted into an osseofibrous carpal tunnel by the flexor retinaculum which is attached to its margins: the tunnel transmits flexor tendons and the median nerve into the hand. The retinaculum strengthens the carpus and augments flexor efficiency. Ligaments are attached to the palmar and dorsal surfaces of all of the carpal bones, interconnecting the bones with each other and also to the radius, ulna and metacarpals.

Individual carpal bones

Scaphoid
The scaphoid is the largest element in the proximal carpal row (see Fig. 51.15A). It has a long axis that is distal, radial and palmar in direction. A round tubercle on the distolateral part of its palmar surface is directed anterolaterally, and provides an attachment for the flexor retinaculum and abductor pollicis brevis; it is crossed by the tendon of flexor carpi radialis. The rough dorsal surface is slightly grooved, narrower than the palmar, and pierced by small nutrient foramina, which are often restricted to the distal half. The radial collateral ligament is attached to the lateral surface, which is also narrow and rough. The remaining 42% of the surfaces are all articular. The radial (proximal) surface is convex, proximal and directed proximolaterally; the lunate surface is flat and semilunar, and faces medially; the capitate surface is large, concave, distal and directed distomedially. The surface for the trapezium and trapezoid is continuous, convex and distal.
The blood supply of the scaphoid is derived from two sources (Gelberman and Menon 1980). Its dorsal ridge is penetrated by the dorsal scaphoid artery and by branches of the styloid artery. Its distal pole receives vessels from the palmar carpal artery or, in its absence, from branches of the superficial palmar artery (Oehmke et al 2009) or the first dorsal metacarpal artery. Variation in this pattern is not uncommon. The proximal pole is circumferentially devoid of periosteum and therefore has no arterial supply: it relies on intraosseous flow from the dorsal and distal supply, which is disrupted in a fracture through the waist of the scaphoid.

Scaphoid bone fractures

The scaphoid is the most frequently fractured carpal bone (Kawamura and Chung 2008, Oka and Moritomo 2018), typically as a result of a fall on to the outstretched hand in a young adult male. The fracture most commonly occurs at the waist of the bone, probably because, on impact, the scaphoid folds into flexion over the taut radioscaphocapitate ligament. Distal tubercle fractures occur as a result of avulsions by the attached radial collateral ligament. Proximal fractures are the result of avulsion by the scapholunate ligament. Compson (1998), in a study of the true, rather than the radiological, anatomy of scaphoid fractures, identified three distinct fracture patterns: transverse through the ‘surgical waist’; oblique (45° to the surgical waist) in the plane of the dorsal sulcus; and through the proximal pole. A fractured scaphoid is prone to non-union because of its anatomy. Since it is largely covered by articular cartilage, the surface area for arterial inflow is limited. There is no arterial inflow through the scapholunate ligament proximally; the blood supply enters distally, which means that a fracture through the waist or more proximally will cut off the blood supply to the proximal pole. However, it has been argued that an inadequate blood supply is unlikely to be the cause of scaphoid non-union after fracture (Oehmke et al 2009, Sendher and Ladd 2013).
Lunate
The lunate is approximately semilunar and articulates between the scaphoid and triquetrum in the proximal carpal row (see Fig. 51.15B). Its rough palmar surface, almost triangular, is larger and wider than the rough dorsal surface. Its smooth convex proximal surface articulates with the radius and the articular disc of the distal radio-ulnar joint. Its narrow lateral surface bears a flat semilunar facet for the scaphoid. The medial surface, which is almost square, articulates with the triquetrum and is separated from the distal surface by a curved ridge, usually somewhat concave for articulation with the edge of the hamate in adduction. The distal surface is deeply concave to fit the medial part of the head of the capitate. Its articulation with the hamate varies: in the type 1 lunate (35–55%) there is no articulation with the hamate, whereas in the type II lunate (45–65%), the medial facet articulates with the hamate (Viegas 1990). The blood supply relies on variable palmar (volar) and dorsal arteries that may or may not anastomose within the bone.

Kienböck’s disease

Kienböck’s disease, first described in 1910, is avascular necrosis of the lunate. Its aetiology remains unclear but is thought to be associated with an impaired arterial blood supply. Variations in the vascular anatomy may render an individual at increased risk of osteonecrosis. Up to eight small vessels usually enter both the dorsal and the palmar aspects of the bone, three on the dorsal surface and five on the palmar; they divide into smaller branches within the bone. Obstruction of the venous outflow may also lead to osteonecrosis. Extrinsic, nonvascular aetiological factors include trauma, steroid use and mechanical factors such as negative ulnar variance or variations in carpal osseous morphology that affect loading of the lunate.
While patients generally present with pain and stiffness of the wrist, these symptoms correlate poorly with the radiological findings and, indeed, patients may be asymptomatic. The natural history of the disease is variable; some progress and others do not. In the earliest stage, radiographs are normal and only an MRI can detect the disease. In later stages, the lunate becomes sclerotic and then collapses, the relative height of the lunate to the capitate diminishing from the usual ratio of 0.54. (For further reading, see Cross and Matullo (2014).)
Triquetrum
The triquetrum is somewhat pyramidal. Its distal palmar surface bears an oval isolated facet for articulation with the pisiform. Its medial and dorsal surfaces are confluent (see Fig. 51.15C). The dorsal surface is marked by the strong ligamentous attachments of the ulnocarpal, dorsal intercarpal and radiolunate ligaments and is marked distally by the attachment of the ulnar collateral ligament, but is smooth proximally to match the articular disc of the triangular fibrocartilage complex. The lateral and distal, hamate, surface is concavo-convex, broad proximally and narrow distally. The lateral and proximal, lunate, surface is almost square.
Pisiform
The pisiform is a sesamoid bone within the tendon of flexor carpi ulnaris that increases the flexion torque applied by the muscle. It is shaped like a pea, with a distolateral long axis (see Fig. 51.15D). It bears a dorsal flat articular facet for the triquetrum. The tendon of flexor carpi ulnaris and its distal continuations, the pisometacarpal and pisohamate ligaments, are all attached to the palmar non-articular area. The deep motor branch of the ulnar nerve runs on its radial side.
Trapezium
The trapezium has a tubercle and groove on its rough palmar surface (see Fig. 51.15E). The tendon of flexor carpi radialis passes dorsomedial to the tubercle which acts as a pulley to prevent flexor carpi radialis from bowstringing palmarwards on flexion. Two layers of the flexor retinaculum are attached to its margins. The tubercle is obscured by the thenar muscles attached to it (opponens pollicis, flexor pollicis brevis and abductor pollicis brevis) (see Fig. 51.13B). The elongated, rough dorsal surface is related to the radial artery. The large lateral surface is rough for attachment of the radial collateral ligament and capsular ligament of the carpometacarpal joint of the thumb. A large sellar surface faces distolaterally and articulates with the base of the first metacarpal. Most distally, it projects between the bases of the first and second metacarpals and carries a small, quadrilateral, distomedially directed facet that articulates with the base of the second metacarpal. The large medial surface is gently concave for articulation with the trapezoid. The proximal surface is a small, slightly concave facet for articulation with the scaphoid. Its ridge, or ‘summit’, fits the concavity of the base of the first metacarpal, and extends in a palmar and lateral direction, at an angle of approximately 60° with the plane of the second and third metacarpals. Abduction and adduction occur in the plane of the ridge, which is shorter than the corresponding metacarpal groove. Their contours vary reciprocally: they are more curved near the base of the second metacarpal, whereas the radius of curvature is longer further away from this site. The two surfaces are not completely congruent, and the area of close contact probably moves towards the palm in adduction and dorsally in abduction. While the axis of flexion/extension passes through the trapezium, the axis for adduction/abduction is in the metacarpal base. Flexion is accompanied by medial rotation, and extension by lateral rotation.
Trapezoid
The trapezoid is small and irregular. It has a rough palmar surface that is narrower and smaller than its rough dorsal surface (see Fig. 51.15F). The distal surface, which articulates with the grooved base of the second metacarpal, is triangular, convex transversely and concave at right-angles to this. The medial surface articulates by a concave facet with the distal part of the capitate, the lateral surface articulates with the trapezium, and the proximal surface articulates with the scaphoid.
Capitate
The capitate is the central and largest carpal bone. It articulates with the base of the third metacarpal via its triangular distal concavo-convex surface (see Fig. 51.15G). Its lateral border is a concave strip for articulation with the medial side of the base of the second metacarpal. Its dorsomedial angle usually bears a facet for articulation with the base of the fourth metacarpal. The head projects into the concavity formed by the lunate and scaphoid, the proximal surface articulates with the lunate, and the lateral surface with the scaphoid. The facets for the scaphoid and trapezoid, though usually continuous on the distolateral surface, may be separated by a rough interval. The medial surface bears a large facet for articulation with the hamate, which is deeper proximally where it is partly non-articular. Palmar and dorsal surfaces are roughened for carpal ligaments, the dorsal being the larger.
Hamate
The hamate is cuneiform and bears an unciform hamulus (hook) that projects from the distal part of its rough palmar surface (see Fig. 51.15H). The hamulus is curved with a lateral concavity and its tip inclines laterally, contributing to the medial wall of the carpal tunnel. The tendon of flexor digitorum profundus to the little finger passes directly radial to the hamulus which acts as a pulley because the origin of this tendon is more radial than its insertion. The flexor retinaculum is attached to the apex of the hamulus. Distally, on the base of the hamulus, a slight transverse groove may be in contact with the terminal deep branch of the ulnar nerve. The remaining palmar surface, like the dorsal, is roughened for the attachment of ligaments. A faint ridge divides the distal surface into a smaller lateral facet that articulates with the base of the fourth metacarpal, and a medial facet for articulation with the base of the fifth. The proximal surface, the thin margin of the wedge, sometimes bears a narrow facet that contacts a reciprocal facet on the lunate. The medial surface is a broad strip, convex proximally, concave distally, that articulates with the triquetrum; distally, a narrow medial strip is non-articular. The lateral surface articulates with the capitate by a facet covering all but its distal palmar angle.

Ossification

Each carpal bone is ossified from one centre (Fig. 51.16A–D). Most are cartilaginous at birth, although ossification may have started in the capitate and hamate. The triquetrum begins to ossify in the third year and the lunate, scaphoid, trapezium and trapezoid begin to ossify in either the fourth year (females) or the fifth year (males). The pisiform begins to ossify in the ninth or tenth year (females) and in the twelfth year (males). The order varies according to sex, nutrition and, possibly, race. Occasionally, an os centrale occurs between the scaphoid, trapezoid and capitate bones and usually fuses with the scaphoid. Lunate and triquetral elements may occasionally fuse. Other fusions and accessory ossicles have also been described (Vaz and Trippia 2018).

Metacarpus

The metacarpus consists of five metacarpals, conventionally numbered in radio-ulnar order. These are small long bones, with a distal head, shaft and expanded base. The rounded heads articulate with the proximal phalanges. The articular surfaces of the head are convex, although less so transversely, and extend further on the palmar surfaces, especially at their margins. From a distal perspective the metacarpal head has a trapezoidal shape and is broader on its palmar aspect than its dorsal. The knuckles are produced by the metacarpal heads. The metacarpal bases articulate with the distal carpal row and with each other. The shafts have longitudinally concave palmar surfaces, which form hollows for the palmar muscles. Their dorsal surfaces bear a distal triangular area, which is continued proximally as a round ridge. These surfaces are palpable proximal to the knuckles.
The medial four metacarpals gently diverge proximodistally. The first metacarpal is more anterior and rotated medially on its axis through 90°, so that its morphologically dorsal surface is lateral, its radial border palmar, its palmar surface medial, and its ulnar border dorsal. Hence, the thumb flexes medially across the palm and can be rotated into opposition with each finger. Opposition is important in manual dexterity and depends on pronation of the thumb carpometacarpal joint and flexion-supination of the little finger carpometacarpal joint. When an object is grasped, the fingers and thumb encircle it from opposite sides, greatly increasing the power and precision of the grip.

Individual metacarpals

First metacarpal
The first metacarpal is shorter and thicker than the other metacarpals (Fig. 51.17A). Its dorsal (lateral) surface can be felt to face laterally; its long axis diverges distolaterally from its neighbour. The shaft is flattened, dorsally broad and transversely convex. The palmar (medial) surface is longitudinally concave and divided by a ridge into a larger lateral (anterior) and smaller medial (posterior) part. Opponens pollicis is attached to the radial border and adjoining palmar surface: the first dorsal interosseous muscle (radial head) is attached to its ulnar border and adjacent palmar surface. The base is concavo-convex and articulates with the trapezium. Abductor pollicis longus is attached on its lateral (palmar) side, the first palmar interosseous muscle to its ulnar side. The head is less convex than in other metacarpals and is transversely broad. Sesamoid bones glide on radial and ulnar articular eminences on its palmar aspect.
Second metacarpal
The second metacarpal has the longest shaft and largest base (Fig. 51.17B). The latter is grooved in a dorsopalmar direction for articulation with the trapezoid. Medial to the groove, a deep ridge articulates with the capitate; laterally, nearer the dorsal surface of the base, there is a quadrilateral facet for articulation with the trapezium, and just dorsal to this facet, a rough impression marks the attachment of extensor carpi radialis longus. On the palmar surface, a small tubercle or ridge receives flexor carpi radialis. The medial side of the base articulates with that of the third metacarpal by a long facet, centrally narrowed. The shaft is prismatic in section and longitudinally curved, convex dorsally, concave towards the palm. Its dorsal surface is distally broad but proximally narrows to a ridge that is covered by extensor tendons of the index finger. Its converging borders begin at the tubercles, one on each side of its head for the attachment of collateral ligaments. Proximally, the lateral surface inclines dorsally for the ulnar head of the first dorsal interosseous. The medial surface inclines similarly, and is divided by a faint ridge into a palmar strip for attachment of the second palmar interosseous, and a dorsal strip for attachment of the radial head of the second dorsal interosseous.
Third metacarpal
The third metacarpal has a short styloid process which projects proximally from the radial side of the dorsal surface (Fig. 51.17C). Its base articulates with the capitate by a facet which is anteriorly convex but dorsally concave where it invades the styloid process on the lateral aspect of its base. A strip-like facet, constricted centrally, articulates with the bases of the second metacarpal (laterally) and the fourth metacarpal (medially), the latter by two oval facets. The palmar facet may be absent: less frequently, the facets are connected proximally by a narrow bridge. The palmar surface of the base receives a slip from the tendon of flexor carpi radialis: extensor carpi radialis brevis is attached to its dorsal surface, beyond the styloid process. The shaft resembles that of the second metacarpal. The ulnar head of the second dorsal interosseous muscle is attached to its lateral surface; the radial head of the third dorsal interosseous is attached to its medial surface, and the transverse head of adductor pollicis to the intervening palmar ridge in its distal two-thirds. Its dorsal surface is covered by the extensor tendon.
Fourth metacarpal
The fourth metacarpal is shorter and thinner than the second and third (Fig. 51.17D). On its base, it displays two lateral oval facets for articulation with the base of the third metacarpal; the dorsal is usually larger and proximally in contact with the capitate. A single medial elongated facet is for articulation with the base of the fifth metacarpal. The quadrangular proximal surface articulates with the hamate; it is anteriorly convex and dorsally concave. The shaft is like the second, but a faint ridge on its lateral surface separates the attachments of the third palmar interosseous and the ulnar head of the third dorsal interosseous. The radial head of the fourth dorsal interosseous is attached to the medial surface.
Fifth metacarpal
The fifth metacarpal (Fig. 51.17E) differs in its medial basal surface, which is non-articular and bears a tubercle for extensor carpi ulnaris. The lateral basal surface is a facet, transversely concave, convex from palm to dorsum, for articulation with the hamate. A lateral strip articulates with the base of the fourth metacarpal. The shaft bears a triangular dorsal area that almost reaches the base; the lateral surface inclines dorsally only at its proximal end. Opponens digiti minimi is attached to the medial surface. The lateral surface is divided by a ridge, which is sometimes sharp, into a palmar strip for the attachment of the fourth palmar interosseous and a dorsal strip for the ulnar part of the fourth dorsal interosseous.

Ossification

Each metacarpal ossifies from a primary centre for the shaft and a secondary centre that is in the base of the first metacarpal and in the heads of the other four (Fig. 51.18).
Ossification begins in the midshaft about postmenstrual week 11. Centres for the second to fifth metacarpal heads appear in that order in the second year in females, and between 1½ to 2½ years in males. They unite with the shafts about the fifteenth or sixteenth year in females, and the eighteenth or nineteenth in males. The first metacarpal base begins to ossify late in the second year in females, early in the third year in males, uniting before the fifteenth year in females and seventeenth in males. Sometimes, the styloid process of the third metacarpal is a separate ossicle. The thumb metacarpal ossifies like a phalanx, and some authorities therefore consider that the thumb skeleton consists of three phalanges. Others believe that the distal phalanx represents fused middle and distal phalanges, a condition occasionally observed in the fifth toe. When the thumb has three phalanges, the metacarpal has a distal and a proximal epiphysis. It occasionally bifurcates distally. When it does, the medial branch has no distal epiphysis and bears two phalanges, while the lateral branch shows a distal epiphysis, and three phalanges. The existence of only a distal metacarpal epiphysis may be associated with a greater range of movement at the metacarpophalangeal joint. In the thumb, the carpometacarpal joint has the wider range, and the first metacarpal has a basal epiphysis. A distal epiphysis may appear in the first, and a proximal epiphysis in the second, metacarpal.

Phalanges

There are 14 phalanges, three in each finger and two in the thumb (see Fig. 51.18). Each has a head, shaft and proximal base. The shaft tapers distally, its dorsal surface transversely convex. The palmar surface is transversely flat but gently concave anteriorly in its long axis. The bases of the proximal phalanges carry concave, oval facets adapted to the metacarpal heads. Their own heads have a midline dorsal–palmar groove and encroach more on to the palmar surfaces. The bases of the middle phalanges carry two concave facets separated by a smooth ridge, conforming to the heads of the proximal phalanges and a dorsal lip to receive the central slip of the extensor tendon. The bases of the distal phalanges are adapted to the shallow midline groove of the middle phalangeal heads. A dorsal lip receives the conjoined extensor tendon and a palmar lip receives the flexor digitorum profundus tendon. The heads of the distal phalanges are non-articular and carry a rough, crescentic palmar tuberosity, to which the pulps of the fingertips are attached. The flatter dorsal surface is roughened to attach the nail matrix.
Articular ligaments and numerous muscles are attached to the phalanges. The tendon of flexor digitorum profundus is attached to the palmar aspect of the base of the distal phalanx and the tendon of extensor digitorum to its dorsal surface. The tendon of flexor digitorum superficialis is attached to the anterolateral surface of the middle phalanx, and the central slip of the extensor mechanism into the base dorsally. The fibrous flexor sheath is attached to the sides of the proximal and middle phalanx. The dorsal interosseous is attached to the base of the proximal phalanx laterally or medially.
The phalanges of the little finger and the thumb differ. Abductor digiti minimi and flexor digiti minimi are attached to the medial side of the base of the proximal phalanx of the little finger. The tendon of extensor pollicis brevis is attached to the dorsolateral corner of the base of the proximal phalanx. The oblique and transverse heads of adductor pollicis, sometimes conjoined with the first palmar interosseous (medially), are attached to the anteromedial corner of the base of the proximal phalanx of the thumb.

Ossification

Phalanges are ossified from a primary centre for the shaft and a proximal epiphysial centre (see Fig. 51.18). Ossification begins prenatally in the shafts as follows: distal phalanges after postmenstrual week 10, proximal phalanges after postmenstrual weeks 11–12 and middle phalanges after postmenstrual week 14. Epiphysial centres appear in proximal phalanges early in the second year (females), or later in the same year (males), and in middle and distal phalanges in the second year (females), or third or fourth year (males). All epiphyses unite about the fifteenth to sixteenth year (females), and seventeenth to eighteenth year (males).

Joints

Radiocarpal joint

Articulating surfaces

The radiocarpal joint is a synovial, biaxial and ellipsoid joint formed by articulation of the distal end of the radius and the articular disc of the triangular fibrocartilage (which overlies the ulnar head) with the scaphoid, lunate, triquetrum and their interposed ligaments (Figs 51.19–51.20). In the neutral position of the wrist, only the scaphoid and lunate are in contact with the radius and articular disc; the triquetrum comes into apposition with the disc only in full adduction of the wrist joint. The radial articular surface and distal discal surface form an almost elliptical, concave surface with a transverse long axis. The radial surface is bisected by an anteroposterior low ridge into two concavities to receive the reciprocal convex surface of the scaphoid and lunate. The proximal articular surfaces of the scaphoid, lunate and triquetrum and their interosseous ligaments form a smooth convex surface that is received into the proximal concavity.

Fibrous capsule

The fibrous capsule is an agglomeration of collagenous tissue which includes several more defined and thus named ligaments (see below). It is lined by a synovial membrane which is usually separate from that of the distal radio-ulnar and intercarpal joints. A prestyloid recess (recessus sacciformis) lies anteroradial to the styloid process of the ulna.

Synovial membrane

The synovial membrane lines the fibrous capsule.

Muscles producing movement

Movements accompany those of the intercarpal and midcarpal joints, and are described on page 969.

Carpal joints

The intercarpal joints connect the carpal bones. Their articular surfaces are saddle-shaped, flat, ellipsoid or spheroidal. The carpal bones are connected by an extensive array of ligaments, not all of which are specifically named. The flexor retinaculum is an accessory intercarpal ligament which connects the scaphoid and triquetrum to the hamate and pisiform.

Joints of the proximal carpal row

Joints of the proximal carpal row are between the scaphoid, lunate and triquetrum. In addition, the pisiform articulates with the palmar surface of the triquetrum at a small, oval, almost flat, synovial pisotriquetral joint. A thin capsule surrounds the joint. The synovial cavity is usually separate but may communicate with that of the radiocarpal joint.

Joints of the distal carpal row

Joints of the distal carpal row are between the trapezium, trapezoid, capitate and hamate. There is little movement at these joints.

Midcarpal joint

The midcarpal joint, between the scaphoid, lunate and triquetrum (proximally), and trapezium, trapezoid, capitate and hamate (distally), is a compound articulation that may be divided descriptively into medial and lateral parts. Throughout most of the medial compartment, the convexity formed by the head of the capitate and hamate articulates with a reciprocal concavity formed by the scaphoid, lunate and much of the triquetrum. However, most medially, the curvatures are reversed, forming a compound sellar joint. In the lateral compartment, the trapezium and trapezoid articulate with the scaphoid, forming a second planosellar compound articulation.

Carpal synovial membrane

The extensive carpal synovial membrane lines an irregular articular cavity. Its proximal part is between the distal surfaces of the scaphoid, lunate and triquetrum, and the proximal surfaces of the distal carpal row. It has three distal prolongations between the four bones of the second row. The prolongation between the trapezium and trapezoid and/or between the trapezoid and capitate is often continuous with corresponding carpometacarpal joints, either from the second to the fifth, or from the second and third only. In the latter case, the joint between the hamate and fourth and fifth metacarpals has a separate synovial membrane. Synovial cavities of carpometacarpal joints are prolonged slightly between the metacarpal bases. The synovial joint between the pisiform and triquetrum is usually isolated.

Wrist ligaments

The distal radio-ulnar joint and triangular fibrocartilage complex are described on page 942.
The wrist ligaments can be divided into intra-articular and extra-articular ligaments. The intra-articular ligaments can be further divided into intrinsic and extrinsic ligaments. The extra-articular ligaments comprise the flexor retinaculum, extensor retinaculum and the pisohamate ligament. The intrinsic ligaments connect the bones within the carpus. The extrinsic ligaments are condensations of the joint capsule that connect the carpus to the forearm; they tend to be longer than the intrinsic ligaments and are approximately one-third as strong. The deeper surfaces of some of the extrinsic ligaments and the superficial surfaces of some of the intrinsic ligaments are identifiable at wrist arthroscopy.
Wrist ligaments are conventionally named for the bones to which they attach from proximal to distal and from radial to ulnar, e.g. the radioscaphocapitate ligament passes between the radius, scaphoid and capitate.
Extrinsic ligaments
The extrinsic ligaments are best considered as two distinct groups, palmar extrinsic carpal ligaments and the dorsal extrinsic carpal ligaments. These individual ligaments work together as a ligamentous complex with the intrinsics to stabilize the wrist.
Extrinsic palmar carpal ligaments
There are three palmar extrinsic ligaments which arise from the radius and three from the ulna. From radial to ulnar they are: the radioscaphocapitate (RSC), long radiolunate and short radiolunate (long and short RL), ulnolunate (UL), ulnocapitate (UC) and ulnotriquetral UT (Berger 1997, Feipel and Rooze 1999).
When the synovial lining of the carpal tunnel is dissected away, two V-shaped ligamentous bands are visible with their apices lying distally (see Fig. 51.19A). This V-shaped ligamentous complex is termed the arcuate ligament. It is formed by the twin insertions of the radioscaphocapitate and ulnocapitate ligaments to the capitate in the distal carpal row.

Radioscaphocapitate ligament

The radioscaphocapitate ligament originates from the radial styloid and the palmar lip of the radius, and has three parts. The radial part inserts onto the lateral aspect of the waist of the scaphoid (radioscaphoid ligament). The middle part continues as part of the distal ‘V’ and inserts onto the distal pole of the scaphoid. The ulnar part passes over the proximal pole of the scaphoid towards the mid-carpus to insert into the capitate, meeting with the fibres that originate from the ulnar side of the triangular fibrocartilage complex (ulnocapitate ligament) to form the arcuate ligament over the palmar aspect of the capitate. There is a discrete interval (the space of Poirier) between the inferior margin of the arcuate ligament and the palmar surface of the lunate. It is through this area of relative weakness that lunate dislocations may occur (Jones and Kakar 2012). The radioscaphocapitate ligament forms a fulcrum over which the scaphoid waist rotates in wrist movement.

Long radiolunate ligament

The long radiolunate ligament (long RL) arises adjacent to the radioscaphocapitate ligament on the palmar lip of the radius, passes over and supports the proximal pole of the scaphoid, and inserts into the palmar surface of the lunate. It is discrete from the radioscaphocapitate ligament, from which it is separated by the interligamentous sulcus (see Video 51.1) continuous with the space of Poirier.
The long RL resists distal and ulnar translation of the lunate and is a current area of interest in the study of scapholunate instability.

Ligament of Testut

The radioscapholunate ligament is covered by a thick synovial fold that is frequently referred to as the ligament of Testut. It is not a true ligament, more a conduit for nerves and blood vessels. It is a visible landmark during wrist arthroscopy connecting the anterior lip of the radius and the scapholunate interval.

Short radiolunate ligament

The short radiolunate ligament arises from the palmar lip of the lunate fossa of the radius and passes directly to the radial border of the lunate just radial to the ulnolunate ligament.

Ulnolunate ligament

The ulnolunate ligament originates from the palmar aspect of the triangular fibrocartilage complex (TFC) and inserts on to the palmar distal lip of the lunate adjacent to the short radiolunate ligament.

Ulnotriquetral ligament

The ulnotriquetral ligament arises from the palmar aspect of the ulna, inserts into the medial aspect of the triquetrum, and continues distally to a further attachment to the medial aspect of the hamate.

Ulnocapitate ligament

The ulnocapitate ligament passes from the ulnar fovea and proximal radio-ulnar ligament of the TFC and inserts into the ulnar border of the capitate. This ligament partially overlies the other ulnar carpal ligaments. Together they form the ulnar contributions to the arcuate ligament complex.
Extrinsic dorsal carpal ligaments
The dorsal wrist ligaments are comparatively thin and are reinforced by the floor and septa of the fibrous tunnels for the six dorsal compartments.

Dorsal radiolunotriquetral ligament

The dorsal radiolunotriquetral ligament, also known as the dorsal radiocarpal (DRC) ligament, is the only dorsal ligament that attaches the radius to the carpus. It is formed of superficial and deep components. The superficial part connects the radius and triquetrum, and the deep part connects the radius, lunate and triquetrum. The wide superficial component arises from the dorsal margin of the distal radius and courses ulnarwards to insert on the dorsal edge of the triquetrum. The deep component takes a narrower origin from the ulnar aspect of the distal dorsal radius and passes in an ulnar direction to attach to part of the lunotriquetral articulation and the intrinsic lunotriquetral ligament.
Intrinsic ligaments
The intrinsic ligaments of the wrist are only attached to carpal bones (see Fig. 51.19A–C). They are stronger and shorter than the extrinsic ligaments and are connected with the extrinsic ligament complexes by interdigitating fibres. The intrinsic ligaments are subdivided into ligaments that connect the carpal bones of the proximal and distal rows, respectively, and ligaments that connect the rows by crossing over the midcarpal joint.

Proximal row interosseous ligaments

The scapholunate ligament acts as the primary stabilizer of the scapholunate joint. In the sagittal plane, it is an approximate horseshoe-shape with palmar (volar), proximal and dorsal components.
The scapholunate ligament contains short transverse fibres that connect the dorsal aspect of the scaphoid to the lunate and more obliquely orientated fibres which connect the palmar aspects. The stronger and tighter dorsal component of the ligament acts as a hinge permitting just 20° of motion between the scaphoid and the lunate. The ligament continues in its proximal section as an interosseous membrane and is easily visualized from the radiocarpal joint at arthroscopy.
The lunotriquetral ligament has similar dorsal proximal and palmar (volar) components, but the fibres of the palmar component are the thickest and strongest. The interosseous membranous component of the scapholunate and lunotriquetral ligaments separate the midcarpal from the radiocarpal joint spaces. Leaking of dye from one of these joint spaces into the other during an arthrogram denotes a tear of one of these ligaments.

Distal row interosseous ligaments

The strong interosseous ligaments of the distal row connect and stabilize the capitate, hamate, trapezium and trapezoid. They have superficial and deep components, and, unlike the ligaments of the proximal row, are seldom torn.

Palmar midcarpal ligaments

The scaphotrapezial, the scaphotrapezoidal (STm) and the scaphocapitate ligaments constitute the scaphotrapeziotrapezoid ligament complex (STT). They serve to stabilize the triscaphe joint. The triquetrohamate ligament has both palmar (volar) and dorsal components and functions to resist flexion between the hamate and triquetrum. The triquetrocapitate ligament is a palmar (volar) structure that indirectly stabilizes the lunate through its contribution to the ulnar limb of the arcuate ligament.

Dorsal midcarpal ligaments

The dorsal intercarpal ligament helps to stabilize the proximal carpal row. It consistently arises from the dorsal tubercle of the triquetrum. Its radial insertion is more variable but may include almost all the lunate and scaphoid, and about half of the trapezoid and trapezium. The ligament forms the floor of the fourth and fifth extensor compartments.

Arthroscopy of the wrist and hand

See Video 51.1
Arthroscopy of the wrist joint allows inspection of articular surfaces and ligaments, identification of pathology and altered kinematics, and therapeutic manoeuvres using scissors, rongeurs, sutures and cautery. Wrist arthroscopy may be carried out through various anatomically defined ‘portals’ which allow access between the tendons and neurovascular structures into the midcarpal and radiocarpal joints.
Some portals are, as de Smet (2002) has described, ‘frighteningly’ close to key anatomical structures. Consequently, only the skin should be incised when establishing a portal, and the remaining dissection should be carried out with narrow blunt artery forceps to spread aside the neurovascular structures and tendons with subsequent penetration of the capsule using a blunt probe.

Dorsal radiocarpal portals

The dorsal radiocarpal portals are named according to their relationship to the extensor tendon compartments (1–2, 3–4, 4–5, 6–R, 6–U) with 6–R and –U describing a portal radial or ulnar to the sixth compartment which transmits the extensor carpi ulnaris tendon. The 1–2 portal is usually avoided because of the proximity of the radial artery which lies just 3 mm (range 1–5 mm) radially: the branches of the superficial radial nerve are similarly adjacent (range 1–6 mm) (Abrams et al 1994). The 3–4 portal is normally used as the main viewing portal and the 4–5 or 6–R portal for instrumentation. The dorsal cutaneous branch of the ulnar nerve lies within 2.5 mm of the 6–R portal). The articular surfaces of the scaphoid and lunate are seen distally, linked by the scapholunate ligament. Proximally, the triangular fibrocartilage complex may be viewed.

Dorsal midcarpal portals

The radial midcarpal portal (MCR) is used to view the scaphocapitate joint, scaphotrapezoid joint and scapholunate joint. The ulnar midcarpal portal (MCU) is used for viewing the hamate, triquetrum and lunotriquetral joint.

Volar portals

Volar portals (radial, VR, midcarpal, VMC and ulnar, VU) are not commonly used because of their proximity to nerves and arteries. They allow a view of the dorsal capsule, the dorsal radiocarpal ligament and the palmar subregions of the scapholunate and lunotriquetral interosseous ligaments. Use of the volar radial portal carries with it the potential for damage to flexor carpi radialis, the palmar cutaneous branch of the median nerve and the lateral cutaneous nerve of the forearm, all of which can run close to, but not within 3 mm of, the portal. The volar ulnar portal lies more than 5 mm away from the ulnar neurovascular bundle, provided the entry point in the capsule is deep to the ulnar edge of the profundus tendons (Slutsky 2002).

Movements and loading of the wrist joint

Movements of the wrist (carpal kinematics)

See Video 51.2 .
The wrist joint consists of a series of complex articulations between the eight carpal bones, the two forearm bones and the five metacarpals of the hand. The function of the wrist is to allow the hand to function independent of the position of forearm rotation. In order to do this, it needs to function as a universal joint. Our understanding of the exact mechanism by which it does this is developing as new techniques for imaging and tracking carpal movement become available. Despite the progress in imaging and kinematic measurement techniques, no past or present concept of carpal mechanics adequately explains the complex behaviour of the carpus (Rainbow et al 2015).

Flexion–extension

The movements at the radiocarpal and intercarpal joints are considered together. The range of movement varies between individuals; the approximate ranges of active movement are flexion (85°), extension (85°), adduction (ulnar deviation) (45°), abduction (radial deviation) (15°). The range of flexion is greater at the radiocarpal joint, while in extension there is more movement at the midcarpal joint (Fig. 51.21). Hence, the proximal surfaces extend further posteriorly on the lunate and scaphoid bones. These movements are influenced by antagonistic muscles, and therefore the range of flexion is perceptibly diminished when the fingers are flexed, due to increased tension in the extensors. Only when the joints are forced to the limits of flexion or extension with the digits relaxed are the dorsal or palmar ligaments fully stretched. During extension of the wrist, the distal carpal bones rotate into extension with slight radial deviation.

Adduction and abduction

Most ulnar adduction occurs at the radiocarpal joint. The lunate articulates with both the radius and articular disc when the hand is in the midposition but, in adduction, it articulates solely with the radius, and the triquetrum now comes into contact with the articular disc (Fig. 51.22A). Much of the proximal articular surface of the scaphoid becomes subcapsular beneath the radial collateral ligament and forms a smooth, convex, palpable prominence in the floor of the ‘anatomical snuff-box’. Radial abduction from the neutral position occurs at the midcarpal joint. A radiograph of the radially deviated hand shows that the capitate rotates round an anteroposterior axis so that its head passes medially and the hamate conforms to this; the distance between the lunate and the apex of the hamate is increased (Fig. 51.22B). The scaphoid rotates around a transverse axis, and its proximal articular surface moves ulnarwards so that the entire scaphoid now articulates solely with the radius. Movements are limited by the carpal collateral ligaments.

Dart thrower’s motion

The above description fits with the convenient consideration of wrist motion in the traditional orthogonal anatomical directions (flexion, extension, ulnar). However, in reality few if any functional tasks occur within these planes. Most day-to-day activities require simultaneous coupled movements. Fisk noted that many functional activities such as ‘casting a fly, throwing a dart or conducting an orchestra’ occur along an arc from extension and radial deviation to flexion and ulnar deviation (Fisk 1970). This observation led Palmer to coin the term ‘dart thrower’s motion’ (Palmer 1985). During the ‘dart-throwing’ movement almost no movement occurs within the proximal row and the motion occurs almost entirely at the distal row of the carpus (Werner 2004). It is believed that this coupled wrist motion combined with an upright stance allowed early hominid species to begin using tools and throwing objects purposefully (Rohde et al 2010).
The lack of movement between the bones of the proximal carpal row during the dart thrower’s movement has clinical implications for both surgical treatment of wrist arthritis and rehabilitation following injury or surgery of the proximal carpal row. Preservation of this oblique plane when carrying out a partial wrist fusion for arthritis potentially results in more natural wrist kinematics, as has been shown by the improved functional outcome in 3-corner fusion when compared to 4-corner fusion (Singh et al 2015). Traditionally, prolonged immobilization has been part of the routine rehabilitation of injuries or surgery to the proximal carpal row such as for fractures of the scaphoid and reconstruction of the scapholunate ligament. However, with the knowledge that the proximal row undergoes little movement during the dart thrower’s movement it seems logical to allow early rehabilitation exercises provided they occur in this plane (Rohde et al 2010). Radiolunate fusion, a procedure used for conditions such as radiolunate arthritis and radiocarpal instability, incurs surprisingly little loss of functional wrist movement.

Proximal and distal carpal row

The proximal row (scaphoid, lunate and triquetrum) is an intercalated segment; no tendons insert onto the bones of the row. It is inherently unstable and controlled by specific retaining and gliding ligaments. Its relative position is determined by the dimensions of the space between the radius, triangular fibrocartilage complex and ulna on one side, and the rigid distal carpal row on the other. The proximal carpal row is subject to two opposing moments: the scaphoid straddles the proximal and distal rows and tends to rotate the proximal row into flexion under axial load and radial deviation. At the same time, there is a force tending to extend the proximal row, which is initiated by the distal row and transmitted via the midcarpal ligaments to the triquetrum. Stability of the midcarpal joint is thus ensured during both movement and loading. The distal carpal row (trapezium, trapezoid, capitate and hamate) can be regarded as one rigid structure. The scaphoid bridges the proximal and distal carpal rows and provides a functional couple between the two.
Movement of the carpus was originally quantified by measuring carpal bone length in static radiographs while the wrist was moved in orthogonal anatomical planes. From these observations two models emerged. Destot described a row or rigid body theory wherein the carpus was felt to move simply as proximal and distal rows (Destot 1926). According to this view, during the composite movement of wrist flexion and extension, approximately two-thirds of movement occurs at the radiocarpal joint and one-third at the midcarpal joint. Navarro described a model in which the carpus was believed to move in radial, central and ulnar columns more than it did in rows (Navarro 1921). This theory was modified (Taleisnik 1976) to incorporate the specific stabilizing role of the scaphoid as it bridges the proximal and distal rows. Further studies have suggested that carpal movement may display characteristics of both these models in varying proportions. The degree to which a wrist displays column-like or row-like behaviour depends on the bony architecture and ligamentous laxity (Craigen and Stanley 1995, Garcia-Elias 1995). An attempt to provide a unifying theory of carpal kinematics describes a stable central column with the triquetrum serving as an anchor to prevent ulnar translation, and the scaphoid functioning as a supporting lateral column (Sandow et al 2014). This concept of a central column based on the radiolunate articulation is at variance with other kinematic studies which have shown 60% load transmission through the radio-scaphoid joint (Ulrich et al 1999): the theory remains to be validated through a full range of motion, particularly within the dart thrower’s plane (Rainbow et al 2015). With regards to carpal kinematics, it seems sage to recall the words of the statistician George Box, who said ‘all models are wrong, but some are useful’.

Muscles producing movements

Flexion

Flexor carpi radialis, flexor carpi ulnaris and palmaris longus produce flexion, assisted by flexor digitorum superficialis, flexor digitorum profundus and flexor pollicis longus.

Extension

Extensor carpi radialis longus, extensors carpi radialis brevis and ulnaris, assisted by extensors digitorum, digiti minimi, indicis and pollicis longus. With the forearm supinated, extensor carpi ulnaris is a pure extensor with no ulnar deviation moment.

Adduction (ulnar deviation)

Flexor and extensor carpi ulnaris produce adduction. With the forearm pronated, extensor carpi ulnaris is a pure ulnar deviator with no extension moment.

Abduction (radial deviation)

Flexor carpi radialis, extensors carpi radialis longus and brevis, with abductor pollicis longus and extensor pollicis brevis.
Flexor carpi ulnaris is stronger than extensor carpi ulnaris, which, in turn, is stronger than extensors carpi radialis longus and brevis, and flexor carpi radialis. The other muscles are all weaker. It is for this reason that these muscles tend to be selected for the major tendon transfers around the wrist (Brand 1985). During flexion and extension, flexor carpi radialis, flexor carpi ulnaris and extensor carpi radialis brevis have a greater excursion than extensor carpi radialis longus and extensor carpi ulnaris. During radial and ulnar deviation, extensor carpi radialis longus and extensor carpi ulnaris have a greater excursion, whereas that of flexor carpi radialis is relatively small. The axes of rotation for flexion/extension and radial/ulnar deviation intersect at a point in the proximal part of the capitate bone. Flexor carpi radialis and flexor carpi ulnaris are most efficient in flexion, extensor carpi radialis brevis in extension, extensor carpi radialis longus in radial deviation and extensor carpi ulnaris in ulnar deviation.

Loading of the wrist (carpal kinetics)

Axial loading, i.e. load or force applied along a line parallel to the long bones of the arm, occurs in clenching the fist. In most activities, loading is multiplanar with a combination of vectors of force. Grasping an object whilst lifting it against gravity with the elbow in flexion causes transverse and axial loading (Horii et al 1993).
Force transmission
Radiocarpal and ulnocarpal joints
The radiocarpal and ulnocarpal articulations involve the scaphoid and lunate fossae of the radius and the facet formed by the distal aspect of the triangular fibrocartilage complex. The scaphoid fossa contributes 43%, the lunate fossa 46% and the triangular fibrocartilage complex 11% of the total area of this articulating surface. Under resting conditions, about 20% of the surface area of the combined radiocarpal and ulnocarpal joint abuts the proximal carpal row. Greater contact is seen in forearm supination, radial deviation and dorsiflexion of the wrist. Lesser contact occurs in forearm pronation, ulnar deviation and palmar flexion of the wrist. Axial loading increases the contact area to a maximum of about 40%. Force across the joint also varies with the position of the forearm and the degree of wrist flexion. For a given load in wrist-neutral position, 50% of force passes across the scaphoid fossa, 35% through the lunate fossa and 15% across the triangular fibrocartilage complex. With a power grip, there is slight ulnar deviation and the proportion of force passing across the scaphoid and lunate fossae reverses.
Midcarpal joint
With the wrist in neutral position, 50–60% of a given load is transmitted from the distal row through the capitate to the scaphoid and lunate. Up to 30% of the load is transmitted via the scaphotrapeziotrapezoid joint and up to 20% via the hamate–triquetral joint.
Distal radio-ulnar joint (DRUJ)
See Chapter 50.

Power grip

In power grip, the wrist is positioned in mid-dorsiflexion and slight ulnar tilt. Force is transmitted from the hand and wrist via the radiocarpal joint and triangular fibrocartilage complex to both bones of the forearm, through the elbow joint to the humerus, and from there, via the glenohumeral joint, to the pectoral girdle. Dynamic muscle action accounts for transmission of a large part of the force; a smaller proportion is transmitted through the osseoligamentous structures. The distribution of force between the radius and ulna varies dynamically and depends on the absolute value of load, degree of forearm rotation and position of the carpus relative to the forearm.

Carpal instability

See Video 51.3.
The upper limb has evolved to facilitate the placing of the hand in space for prehension, but mobility of the hand on the forearm has evolved at the expense of stability. A wrist is said to be kinetically unstable when it cannot bear physiological loads without giving way or causing injury, and to be kinematically unstable when it exhibits sudden changes in carpal alignment during movement, manifest as a ‘clunk’ as one or more of the carpal bones move abnormally with respect to the others. Stability of the various joints, so that the bones maintain normal anatomical relations with respect to each other throughout the normal range of motion, is essential for physiological load transmission. Disruption of this complex system leads to pain, loss of function and, in time, degeneration of the articular surfaces.
Some degree of carpal instability may occur as part of inherent hypermobility in affected individuals. More commonly it occurs after acute rupture or chronic attenuation of intrinsic and extrinsic carpal ligaments, which may be detected either clinically or radiologically as malalignment of the carpal bones. In clinical practice, certain patterns of instability predominate.
Garcia-Elias described a kinetic theory of carpal stability that states that the neutral posture of the lunate is preserved by balancing the flexion moment of the scaphoid with the extension moment of the triquetrum (Garcia-Elias 1997). When axially loaded, these rotational forces increase. If the interosseous scapholunate ligament is disrupted then the extension moment of the triquetrum remains unopposed leading to an extended position of the lunate. This is referred to as dorsal intercalated segment instability (DISI). In the absence of the luno-triquetral ligament the scaphoid flexion moment loses its antagonistic extension moment from the triquetrum and the lunate adopts a flexed posture: this is termed volar intercalated segment instability (VISI).

Carpometacarpal joints

Carpometacarpal joint of the thumb

Articulating surfaces

The carpometacarpal joint of the thumb (basal joint of the thumb) is a sellar joint between the first metacarpal base and trapezium. Its extensive articular surfaces and their topology ensure that the joint enjoys a wide mobility. Its curved saddle shape, as if designed for a ‘scoliotic horse’ (Kuczynski 1975), allows the metacarpal base to slide into flexion and pronation as the pulp of the thumb opposes the digits.

Ligaments

The first metacarpal and trapezium are connected by lateral, anterior and posterior ligaments and a fibrous capsule. The broad lateral ligament runs from the lateral surface of the trapezium to the radial side of the metacarpal base. The palmar and dorsal ligaments are oblique bands that converge to the ulnar side of the metacarpal base from the palmar and dorsal surfaces of the trapezium, respectively.

Synovial membrane

The synovial membrane lines the joint capsule and is separate from it.

Joint movements

Except at its initiation, flexion is accompanied by pronation (medial rotation) of the thumb metacarpal, whilst pronation necessitates flexion. This linkage of movements is due largely to the shape of the articular surfaces and to the obliquity of the dorsal ligament (which, when taut, anchors the ulnar side of the metacarpal base while its radial side continues to move). Contraction of flexor pollicis brevis, assisted by opponens pollicis, produces medial rotation with flexion; combined with abduction, this opposes the thumb pulp to the pulps of the slightly flexed fingers. Full extension of the thumb metacarpal entails slight supination (lateral rotation), attributable to the saddle shape of the joint and to the action of the palmar ligament (similar to that of the dorsal ligament in flexion) (Edmunds 2011, Ladd et al 2013).

Muscles producing movements

Flexion

Flexor pollicis brevis and opponens pollicis, aided by flexor pollicis longus which continues the flexion moment when the other joints of the thumb are flexed.

Extension

Abductor pollicis longus and extensors pollicis brevis and longus.

Abduction

Abductors pollicis brevis and longus.

Adduction

Adductor pollicis and extensor pollicis longus.

Opposition

Opposition describes the action of bringing the pulp of the thumb across the palm to appose the pulps of the digits. Abductor pollicis brevis abducts the thumb whilst opponens pollicis and flexor pollicis brevis simultaneously flex and pronate (medially rotate) the abducted thumb. Interpulpal pressure, generated by digital grasping, is increased by adductor pollicis and flexor pollicis longus.

Retroposition

Retroposition describes elevating the pulp of the thumb into extension and ulnar adduction dorsal to the plane of the palm. Extensor pollicis longus uniquely provides this movement.

Circumduction

Extensors, abductors, flexors and adductors acting consecutively in this, or reverse, order.

Second to fifth carpometacarpal joints

The second to fifth carpometacarpal joints are synovial ellipsoid joints between the carpus and second to fifth metacarpals. Although widely classed as plane, they have curved articular surfaces that are often of complex saddle shape. The bones are united by articular capsules and by dorsal, palmar and interosseous ligaments.

Ligaments

The dorsal ligaments are the strongest, and connect the dorsal surfaces of the carpals and metacarpals. The second metacarpal has two ligaments, from the trapezium and trapezoid; the third has two, from the trapezoid and capitate; the fourth has two, from the capitate and hamate; and the fifth has a single band from the hamate, which is continuous with a similar palmar ligament, forming an incomplete capsule. The palmar ligaments are similar, except that the third metacarpal has three: a lateral from the trapezium, superficial to the tendon sheath of flexor carpi radialis, an intermediate from the capitate, and a medial from the hamate. The interosseous ligaments consist of two short, thick, fibrous bands. They are limited to one part of the carpometacarpal articulation and connect contiguous distal margins of the capitate and hamate with adjacent surfaces of the third and fourth metacarpals; they may be united proximally.

Synovial membranes

The synovial membranes are often continuous with those of the intercarpal joints. Occasionally, the joint between the hamate and fourth and fifth metacarpals has a separate synovial cavity, bounded laterally by the medial interosseous ligament and its extensions to the palmar and dorsal parts of the capsule.

Muscles producing movements

The second to fifth carpometacarpal joints exhibit an increasing range of movement, progressing from the radial to the ulnar side. Thus, there is little mobility at the base of the index ray but considerable mobility at the base of the small finger ray, which facilitates ‘cupping’ of the palm of the hand. Compressive forces between the metacarpals and the distal carpal row are estimated to be upwards of ten times the forces at the tips of the fingers during ‘pinch’ or ‘chuck’ grip.
Slight gliding movements are effected by the long flexor and extensor muscles of the digits.

Intermetacarpal joints

Articulating surfaces

The second to fifth metacarpal bases articulate reciprocally by small cartilage-covered facets connected by dorsal, palmar and interosseous ligaments.

Fibrous capsule

The intermetacarpal joints have fibrous capsules.

Ligaments

The dorsal and palmar ligaments pass transversely from bone to bone. The interosseous ligaments connect contiguous surfaces just distal to their articular facets.

Synovial membranes

The synovial membranes are continuous with those of the carpometacarpal articulations.

Movements at the carpometacarpal joints

The fifth metacarpal is the most mobile and the second and third are the least mobile. These variations are easily demonstrated by opposing each digit to the thumb over the centre of the palm. While the majority of movement is in the thumb, the carpometacarpal joint of the little finger flexes, abducts and pronates (laterally rotates). Less movement occurs at the carpometacarpal joint of the ring finger and essentially none occurs at the middle and index (Tucker and Titley 2002).

Movements at the intermetacarpal joints

Movements at the intermetacarpal articulations vary. There is very slight gliding movement in flexion–extension in the index–middle joint and slightly more glide in the middle–ring joint. There is more glide and some rotation during pronation and supination generated at the carpometacarpal joint in the ring–little joint and slight rotation in the little finger.

Muscles producing movements

The extrinsic (i.e. forearm-derived) and intrinsic (i.e. palm-derived) flexors and extensors of the second to fifth digits move the carpometacarpal and intermetacarpal joints.

Metacarpophalangeal joints

The metacarpal heads are incongruous with the shallow concavities on the phalangeal bases; they are not regularly convex but are partially divided on their palmar aspects and, thus, almost bicondylar. The dorsal width of the metacarpal head is about two-thirds of its palmar width, which means that the joint is lax in the coronal plane in extension yet taut in flexion (compared with the proximal interphalangeal joint which is taut throughout its range) (Fig. 51.23).

Fibrous capsule

The metacarpophalangeal joints all have fibrous capsules.

Ligaments

Each metacarpophalangeal joint has a palmar and two collateral ligaments.

Palmar ligaments

The palmar ligaments (volar plates) are thick, dense and fibrocartilaginous. They are attached quite loosely to the metacarpal neck proximally and firmly to the base of the proximal phalanx distally (Fig. 51.24). The deep transverse metacarpal ligaments extend between the palmar plates from the ulnar side of the second metacarpal head to the radial side of the fifth metacarpal head. The collateral ligaments arise from the posterior tubercle on the lateral aspect of the metacarpal heads and insert into the palmar portion of the lateral aspect of the base of the proximal phalanges. The palmar aspects are grooved for the flexor tendons. The A1 component of the flexor tendon sheath is attached to the edge of the ligament and the associated attachment of the collateral ligament, but is not attached to the metacarpal. The deep surface of the volar plate increases the articular contact area for the metacarpal heads.

Deep transverse metacarpal ligaments

The deep transverse metacarpal ligaments are three short, wide, flat bands that connect the palmar ligaments of the second to fifth metacarpophalangeal joints. They are related anteriorly to the lumbricals and digital vessels and nerves, and posteriorly to the interossei. The vertical fibres of the palmar aponeurosis (septa of Legueu and Juvara) join their palmar surfaces. On both sides of the third and fourth metacarpophalangeal joints, but only the ulnar side of the second and radial side of the fifth joints, the transverse (sagittal) bands of the extensor hood join the deep transverse metacarpal ligaments. The lumbricals and the phalangeal attachments of the dorsal interossei lie anterior to this band; the remaining attachments of the dorsal and palmar interossei are posterior to it (Fig. 51.25).

Collateral ligaments

The collateral ligaments are attached proximally to the posterior tubercle and an adjacent pit on the side of the metacarpal neck. Two components pass distally. The more dorsal fibres form a cord-like band that is attached to the side of the palmar corner of the base of the proximal phalanx, blending with the insertion of the volar plate. The more palmar fibres spread distally and anteriorly to form a fan that can fold up like a concertina as the metacarpophalangeal joint flexes (see Fig. 51.23). The radial collateral ligament of the index finger is strongest because it must resist ulnar deviation when the thumb opposes the index finger. Because the metacarpal head is narrow dorsally and wider anteriorly, the collateral ligaments are tight only in full flexion but loose in extension.

Synovial membrane

The metacarpophalangeal joints are lined by a synovial membrane.

Joint movements

Digits

These include flexion, extension, adduction, abduction, circumduction and limited rotation. Rotation accompanies flexion–extension, e.g. when the finger flexes and rotates to place its tip near the centre of the palm. The range of rotation is frequently increased as a result of the resistance of a grasped object. Active flexion is 90° or more and is greater in the little finger than in the index. The range of extension depends on whether it is active or passive: the former is limited by the resistance of the antagonistic muscles, the latter is significantly greater because the volar plate offers little resistance. (This contrasts with the thumb–metacarpophalangeal joint and all the proximal interphalangeal joints, in which a rigid volar plate prevents hyperextension, so that passive and active extension are equal.) Passive extension is greater in the little finger than in the index. The range can be even greater in those with generally lax joints. The trapezoidal shape of the metacarpal heads means that the collateral ligament allows no ulnar or radial deviation of the proximal phalanx when the joint is fully flexed, but allows the fingers to move through about 20° in each direction when the joints are extended.
Slight supination (lateral rotation) accompanies digital flexion of the middle, ring and little fingers. Flexion of the index finger may be accompanied by little or no pronation. These movements allow the digits to cup around an object such that the thumb pulp can appose the middle–ring pulp as the sides of these touch each other whilst the edge of the index and little fingers touch the sides of the thumb pulp.

Thumb

The metacarpophalangeal joint of the thumb has a flexion–extension range of approximately 60°, with minimal extension. The latter is prevented by a stout volar plate. There is minimal rotation and no abduction or adduction.

Accessory movements

Anteroposterior translation of the phalanx on the metacarpal and distraction.

Muscles producing movements

Flexion

In the fingers, flexion is produced by flexors digitorum superficialis and profundus, assisted by the lumbricals, interossei and flexor digiti minimi brevis (in the little finger). In the thumb, flexor pollicis longus flexes the thumb. Flexor pollicis brevis, abductor pollicis brevis and adductor pollicis flex the metacarpophalangeal joint through attachments to their respective tubercles at the anteromedial and anterolateral base of the proximal phalanx, and through the extensor hood overlying the dorsum of the base of the proximal phalanx.

Extension

In the digits, extension is produced by extensor digitorum, assisted in the second and fifth digits by extensor indicis and extensor digiti minimi, respectively; in the thumb, extensors pollicis longus and brevis.

Adduction

In extended fingers, adduction towards the middle finger is produced by the palmar interossei, enabled by a lax collateral ligament. In flexion, adduction is prevented by a taut collateral ligament. In the thumb, very limited metacarpophalangeal adduction is possible because of constraint by the radial collateral ligament.

Abduction

In extended fingers, abduction from the axis of the middle finger is produced by the dorsal interossei and abductor digiti minimi. There is no abduction when the metacarpophalangeal joint is flexed because of tightening of the collateral ligament. When the fingers are flexed at the interphalangeal joints, active abduction at the metacarpophalangeal joints is possible. In the thumb, there is minimal or no abduction at the metacarpophalangeal joint because of the constraint of the ulnar collateral ligament. Abduction, if it occurs, is provided by abductor pollicis brevis.

Gamekeeper’s thumb and skier’s thumb

The ulnar collateral ligament of the metacarpophalangeal joint of the thumb may be avulsed from its distal attachment to the base of the proximal phalanx by a combination of forced extension and radial deviation of the joint. Chronic attritional tears of this ligament are traditionally reported to occur in gamekeepers from wringing the necks of pheasants. Acute injuries typically occur in skiers who fall onto an outstretched hand while having the strap of a ski pole wrapped around the thumb, or if the thumb is caught in the snow (or the grid of an artificial slope). The ligament may be avulsed directly from the bone or with a pyramidal fragment of bone. The fragment also includes the insertion of the volar plate, flexor sheath and part of adductor pollicis. The ulnar collateral ligament is normally covered by the aponeurotic fibres of the tendon of adductor pollicis. Following avulsion, the proximal end can become superficially displaced around the proximal free edge of the aponeurotic insertion of adductor pollicis. This is termed a Stener lesion (Mattox et al 2015). The soft tissue interposition prevents the ends of the avulsed ligament from healing. The presence of a Stener lesion is indicated clinically if there is more than 30° of radial laxity when stressing the metacarpophalangeal joint in 40° of flexion. If the joint is assessed in extension, then the lateral fibres of the volar plate may contribute significantly to the lateral stability of the joint and give a false impression of stability. Surgical repair requires division of the aponeurotic fibres of adductor pollicis to expose the insertion, followed by reattachment of the avulsed ligament to its attachment in the anteromedial corner of the proximal phalanx. Postoperative rehabilitation requires early movement of the interphalangeal joint of the thumb, otherwise the adductor aponeurosis will adhere to the repair and prevent flexion of the tip. The ulnar collateral ligament is isometric, i.e. the same length in flexion and extension. Consequently, early movement of the metacarpophalangeal joint will prevent adherence of the repair to the side of the joint, which would otherwise limit movement.
The comparable injury to the radial collateral ligament is less common. It is caused by sudden, forced adduction of the metacarpophalangeal joint as the result of a fall or during ball games. The ligament may be ruptured in its mid-substance or avulsed from either bony attachment, usually the distal. A Stener lesion does not occur because the aponeurosis of abductor pollicis brevis is closely apposed to the joint and thus does not provide the space.

Interphalangeal joints

The interphalangeal joints are uniaxial hinge joints.

Fibrous capsule

Each interphalangeal joint has a fibrous capsule.

Ligaments

The proximal interphalangeal joint is stable in all positions. Stability is conferred by a palmar ligament (the volar plate) and two collateral ligaments (Fig. 51.26; see Figs 51.23, 51.24) in addition to the flexor sheath, which is inserted not only into the side of the volar plate but also into the base and neck of the proximal phalanx, so conferring additional stability to the volar plate. There is a different arrangement in the metacarpophalangeal joint, where the sheath is inserted into the volar plate and the base of the proximal phalanx but not into the metacarpal.

Palmar ligament (volar plate)

The volar plate of the proximal interphalangeal joint constitutes the floor of the interphalangeal joint. In the joint itself, the distal end of the volar plate is thickened laterally where it is firmly attached to the base of the middle phalanx at the position of the true attachment of the collateral ligament. Centrally, it is more delicate and blends with the volar periosteum of the middle phalanx. Proximally, the volar plate is also very delicate in its central portion, but it is thickened laterally to form the ‘check rein ligaments’ that are attached to the periosteum of the proximal phalanx, beyond the walls of the A2 pulley component of the fibrous flexor sheath. Nutrient branches of the digital arteries pass underneath these check ligaments to reach the vincula (see Fig. 51.26). The A3 pulley is attached to the edge of the volar plate, together with the accessory part of the collateral ligament.

Collateral ligaments

The cord-like proper collateral ligaments pass from the dorsal half of the lateral aspect of the neck of the proximal phalanx to the lateral palmar corner of the base of the adjacent proximal phalanx, where they are incorporated into the insertion of the A3 pulley and volar plate. They remain taut throughout flexion and extension in contrast to the collateral ligaments of the metacarpophalangeal joint, which are taut in flexion but lax in extension. In the proximal interphalangeal joint, a fan-like accessory collateral ligament arises in continuity from the palmar edge of the main ligament and passes in a palmar direction to attach to the volar plate: it folds like a concertina on flexion. The accessory collateral ligament and check rein ligament are not found in the distal interphalangeal joint (Slattery et al 2014).

Synovial membrane

Each interphalangeal joint has a synovial lining.

Factors maintaining stability

Stability is conferred by the articular contours of the joint surfaces and the proper collateral ligaments (Allison 2005). The flexor and extensor tendons provide secondary stabilization. Stability against hyperextension of the proximal interphalangeal joint is enhanced by the three-dimensional box arrangement that is produced by the collateral ligament–volar plate complex.

Joint movements

Flexion and extension are active movements at the interphalangeal joints. Their range is greater at the proximal joints. Flexion is considerable, whereas extension is limited by tension of the digital flexors and terminated by tension in the palmar ligaments. Flexion and extension are accompanied by slight rotation: during flexion, this turns the digital pulps into slight supination to face the opposed thumb: an opposite rotation occurs during extension.

Accessory movements

Limited rotation, abduction, adduction and anteroposterior translation permit the fingers to adapt to the shapes of gripped objects and help to protect against stresses and strains.

Muscles producing movements

Flexion

Flexion is produced by flexors digitorum superficialis and profundus at the proximal interphalangeal joints, flexor digitorum profundus at the distal interphalangeal joints and flexor pollicis longus at the interphalangeal joint of the thumb.

Extension

Extension of the interphalangeal joint of the thumb is produced by extensor pollicis longus and by the combined pull of abductor pollicis brevis, flexor pollicis brevis and abductor pollicis through the extensor expansion (which simultaneously flexes the metacarpophalangeal joint). This supplementary action allows extension of the interphalangeal joint of the thumb even if extensor pollicis longus or the radial nerve are divided. Extension of the interphalangeal joints of the digits is achieved though the complex extensor mechanism with contributions from the central tendon (extensors digitorum, digiti minimi and indicis) and the lateral bands that draw from the lumbricals and interossei. Extension occurs simultaneously in both joints in digits 2–5. Simultaneous flexion at the metacarpophalangeal joints and extension at the interphalangeal joints of a digit are essential for the fine movements of writing, drawing, threading a needle, etc. The lumbricals and interossei are primary agents in flexing the metacarpophalangeal joints and also in extending the interphalangeal joints through their attachments to the dorsal digital expansions.
The oblique retinacular ligament of Landsmeer (see Fig. 51.25) runs from an attachment anterior to the axis of the proximal interphalangeal joint to an insertion posterior to the axis of the distal interphalangeal joint. This ligament is inconsistent (Adkinson et al 2014), but when present causes a dynamic tenodesis with obligatory extension of the distal interphalangeal joint when the proximal interphalangeal joint extends.

Mallet finger

Mallet finger deformity is caused by laceration, traumatic rupture or an avulsion fracture of the distal extensor tendon to the terminal phalanx of the finger. It is usually caused by forced flexion of the distal phalanx of the finger or laceration of the tendon. A closed rupture is usually painless. The patient is unable to extend the distal interphalangeal joint actively, but it can be extended passively. As time passes, the residual extensor mechanism is pulled proximally by the intrinsic tone, which increases the torque across the dorsum of the interphalangeal joint, causing a secondary swan neck deformity.

Swan neck deformity

Swan neck deformity is caused by hyperextension of the proximal interphalangeal joint, together with flexion of the distal interphalangeal joint and occurs as a result of an imbalance between the component parts of the extensor and flexor mechanism: it may be related to problems at the metacarpophalangeal, proximal interphalangeal and distal interphalangeal joints. The deformity can be caused by a number of factors: erosion of the volar plate of the proximal interphalangeal joint by synovitis, so that the joint can hyperextend; rupture of flexor digitorum superficialis from rheumatoid disease or laceration; intrinsic muscle tightness (spasm, ulnar drift, palmar subluxation of the proximal phalanx on the metacarpal head); mallet finger, where the extensor mechanism moves proximally because it is unconstrained by the distal insertion into the distal phalanx, conferring an increased extensor moment to the base of the middle phalanx.

Boutonnière deformity

A boutonnière deformity is a flexion deformity of the proximal interphalangeal joint with hyperextension of the distal interphalangeal joint. It reflects an imbalance between tension in the central slip and the lateral bands of the extensor mechanism following failure of the central slip. This can occur as a result of acute division or chronic attrition. Initially, the proximal interphalangeal joint still extends because the lateral bands are intact, but in time the dorsal triangular ligament becomes attenuated, allowing the lateral bands of the extensor tendon to migrate in a volar direction and the head of the proximal phalanx to migrate dorsally: the extensor mechanism then exerts a flexion force at the proximal interphalangeal joint. The terminal part of the extensor mechanism still runs dorsal to the axis of the distal interphalangeal joint but is relatively tightened due to anterior subluxation of the lateral bands, causing hyperextension at the distal interphalangeal joint. Initially, the deformity is passively correctable, but, with time, the soft tissues around the joint contract and a fixed deformity results.

Vascular supply of the joints of the wrist and hand

The radiocarpal joint is supplied by branches of the anterior interosseous artery, anterior and posterior carpal branches of the radial and ulnar arteries, palmar and dorsal metacarpal arteries, and recurrent rami of the deep palmar arch. The carpal joints are supplied by the posterior carpal branches of the radial and ulnar arteries, and by the anterior interosseous artery. The carpometacarpal joint of the thumb receives its blood supply from the radial artery and its first dorsal metacarpal branch. The second to fifth carpometacarpal joints are supplied by the posterior carpal branches of the radial and ulnar arteries, by twigs from the anterior interosseous artery, and from the palmar digital arteries. The metacarpophalangeal joints receive their blood supply from the dorsal and palmar metacarpal arteries, the arteria princeps pollicis and the arteria radialis indicis. The interphalangeal joints are supplied by branches from the palmar digital arteries (see Fig. 51.2A).

Innervation of the joints of the wrist

The radiocarpal joint is innervated by the anterior interosseous nerve, posterior interosseous nerves and lateral antebrachial cutaneous nerves with contributions from the median, ulnar and radial nerves. The palmar cutaneous branch of the median nerve, the deep branch of the ulnar nerve, the superficial branch of the radial nerve and the dorsal branch of the ulnar nerve also innervate the capsule (Van de Pol et al 2006). The ulnar corner of the wrist and triangular cartilage is innervated by the dorsal cutaneous branch of the ulnar nerve, medial antebrachial cutaneous nerve and ulnar nerve. Occasionally it receives fibres from the anterior and posterior interosseous nerves (LaPorte et al 2014). These appear to be pure afferent nerves innervating mechanoreceptor endings (there are an average of 10 in each dorsal radiocarpal ligament) (Tomita et al 2007). Golgi organs, Pacinian corpuscles, Ruffini endings and free nerve endings are found in the palmar radiocarpal ligaments (Petrie et al 1997). The apparently uneven distribution of these receptors throughout the ligaments suggests as yet unspecified functional adaptations.

Innervation of the joints of each digit

The second to fifth carpometacarpal joints are innervated by branches of the ulnar nerve, the anterior interosseous nerve and the superficial radial and dorsal ulnar nerves. The metacarpophalangeal joints are innervated by twigs from the palmar digital branches of the median and ulnar nerve, the deep terminal branch of the ulnar nerve and the posterior interosseous nerve. The interphalangeal joints of the fingers are innervated by one branch, occasionally two, from the palmar digital branches of the median nerve (to the thumb, index, middle and ring fingers), and the ulnar nerve (to the ring and little fingers). The dorsal cutaneous nerves rarely provide innervation (Pastrana et al 2019).

Innervation of the joints of the thumb

The carpometacarpal joint of the thumb is innervated by branches from the lateral antebrachial cutaneous nerve, the palmar cutaneous branch of the median nerve and, less commonly, the superficial radial nerve (Tuffaha et al 2018). The interphalangeal joint of the thumb is innervated by a palmar capsular branch from the radial and ulnar digital nerve as well as a branch from each of the dorsal radial nerve terminal branches (Zaidenberg et al 2018).

Joint neurectomy

Precise knowledge of the nerve supply to the joints of the wrist and hand allows the surgeon to remove the afferent nerve supply in various conditions. This may effectively remove pain and avoid the alternative of joint excision, fusion or replacement, each of which has substantial disadvantages.
