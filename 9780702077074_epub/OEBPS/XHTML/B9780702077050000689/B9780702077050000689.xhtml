Chapter 68: Pancreas

The pancreas is one of the largest digestive glands. Its primary function is exocrine, secreting enzymes involved in the digestion of lipids, carbohydrates and proteins. It has an additional endocrine function derived from clusters of cells scattered throughout the gland, which take part in glucose homeostasis and the control of upper gastrointestinal tract motility and function.
The healthy pancreas is yellow, with a soft to firm consistency and a lobulated surface. Its texture (hard or soft) has important implications for pancreatic surgery: the harder the gland, the lower the rate of leakage of pancreatic fluid after a pancreatic reconstruction procedure.
The pancreas is divided into a head, neck, body and tail (Figs 68.1–68.3A). The head of the pancreas usually has a distinct uncinate process. In adults, the pancreas is between 12 and 15 cm long and is shaped like a flattened ‘tongue’ of tissue lying in the retroperitoneum, thicker at its medial end (head) and thinner towards the lateral end (tail). The head lies within the curve of the duodenum and the remainder of the gland extends transversely and slightly cranially across the retroperitoneum and posterior to the stomach to the splenic hilum. It is ‘draped’ over the inferior vena cava, the hepatic portal vein/superior mesenteric vein and splenic vessels. In adults it has an average volume of 70–80 cm3, although this varies considerably among individuals (with a range of 40–170 cm3) and tends to be greater in males (Djuric-Stefanovic et al 2012). Pancreatic volume increases with age, reaching a peak during the fourth decade. From about 60 years of age the gland atrophies and adipose connective tissue replaces exocrine tissue (Saisho et al 2007, Caglar et al 2012). Pancreatic diameters tend to be smaller in patients with diabetes mellitus (DeSouza et al 2018).
The anterior (ventral) surface of the pancreas is covered by parietal peritoneum and its inferior border abuts the root of the transverse mesocolon. A loose connective tissue layer immediately posterior to the pancreas (sometimes known as the fusion fascia of Treitz in the region of the head of the pancreas and as the fusion fascia of Toldt in the region of the body and tail) contains vessels that supply the gland (Kimura 2000).

Head

The head of the pancreas lies to the right of the midline, anterior and to the right of the vertebral column, within the curve of the duodenum. This is the thickest and broadest part of the pancreas but it is still flattened in the anteroposterior plane. Superiorly, it lies adjacent to the superior part of the duodenum; however, close to the pylorus, where the duodenum is on a short mesentery, the superior part of the head is overlapped anteriorly by the duodenum. The duodenal border of the head is flattened, slightly concave and adherent to the descending part of the duodenum, particularly around the hepatopancreatic ampulla. The superior and inferior pancreaticoduodenal arcades supply the head of the pancreas. The inferior border of the head of the pancreas lies superior to the horizontal part of the duodenum and is continuous with the uncinate process. The anterior surface of the head is covered by peritoneum and abuts the transverse mesocolon (Fig. 68.3B). Posteriorly, the bile duct is partially embedded within the head of the gland just proximal to its junction with the pancreatic duct near the major duodenal papilla (Burgard et al 1991, Nagai 2003). The posterior surface of the head of the pancreas lies anterior to the inferior vena cava at the level of the termination of the right gonadal vein. Near the midline, the head continues as the neck.

Uncinate process

The uncinate process is a hook-shaped continuation of the inferomedial part of the head of the pancreas. Embryologically, it is separate from the rest of the gland. It extends medially anterior to the abdominal aorta and superior to the horizontal part of the duodenum, which can be obstructed by a pancreatic tumour at this site. On sagittal cross-sectional imaging, the left renal vein, the uncinate process and the horizontal part of duodenum can be seen lying between the superior mesenteric artery anteriorly and the abdominal aorta posteriorly.

Neck

The neck of the pancreas is approximately 2 cm wide and connects the head to the body. It is often the most anterior part of the gland and can be defined as that part of the pancreas lying anterior to the formation of the hepatic portal vein (usually the union of the superior mesenteric and splenic veins) (Fig. 68.4D–E). This relationship is crucial for the evaluation of pancreatic cancer because malignant involvement of the hepatic portal vein may have to be considered when the cancer is resected. Encasement of the superior mesenteric artery running posterior to the head of the pancreas is usually considered a contraindication to resection. The superior mesenteric and hepatic portal veins groove the posterior aspect of the neck. The inferior mesenteric vein joins the confluence of the superior mesenteric and splenic veins in one-third of individuals (Kimura 2000; see Fig. 68.7). The anterior surface of the neck of the pancreas is covered by peritoneum and lies inferior to the pylorus. The anterior superior pancreaticoduodenal branch of the gastroduodenal artery runs through the pancreas at the junction of the head and neck.

Body

The body of the pancreas is the longest part of the gland and runs from the neck to the tail, becoming progressively thinner. It is slightly triangular in cross-section and has anterior and posterior surfaces and superior and inferior borders. It lies to the left of the superior mesenteric vessels.

Anterior surface

The anterior surface is covered by peritoneum, which is reflected anteroinferiorly from the surface of the pancreas to be continuous with the posterior layer of the greater omentum and the transverse mesocolon (see Fig. 62.3). The two layers of the transverse mesocolon diverge along this surface (see Fig. 68.3B). Above the attachment of the transverse mesocolon, the anterior surface of the pancreas is separated from the stomach by the omental bursa.

Posterior surface

The posterior surface of the pancreas is devoid of peritoneum. It lies on the fusion fascia of Toldt anterior to the abdominal aorta and the origin of the superior mesenteric artery, the left crus of the diaphragm, the left suprarenal gland, the superior pole of the left kidney surrounded by renal fascia, and the left renal vein (Kimura 2000; see Figs 68.1, 68.4A–G). The splenic vein runs from left to right directly on this surface of the gland and indents it to a variable extent, ranging from a shallow groove to nearly a tunnel.

Superior border

To the right, the superior border of the body of the pancreas is rounded, but it becomes narrower and sharper to the left. An omental eminence usually projects from the right end of the superior border above the level of the lesser curvature of the stomach. The superior border lies anterior to the coeliac trunk and its branches, the common hepatic and splenic arteries: the common hepatic artery runs to the right just superior to the gland, and the splenic artery passes tortuously to the left, projecting above the superior border at several points.

Inferior border

At the medial end of the inferior border of the neck of the pancreas, the superior mesenteric vessels travel posterior to the gland. Laterally, the inferior mesenteric vein runs posterior to the inferior border to join the splenic vein or the confluence of the splenic and superior mesenteric veins. This is a useful site for identification of the inferior mesenteric vein on computed tomography (CT) and during resection of the descending colon. The superior mesenteric artery lies medial to the superior mesenteric vein and is a critical structure that must be identified when the pancreas is divided between its head and neck during a pancreaticoduodenectomy (Whipple procedure).

Tail

The tail of the pancreas is the narrowest, most left-lateral portion of the gland and is continuous with the body. It is between 1.5 and 3.5 cm long in adults and lies between the splenorenal ligament and the splenic hilum. It either terminates at the base of the splenorenal ligament or extends into the splenic hilum, where it is at risk of injury during a splenectomy when the splenic vessels are divided. The splenic artery and its branches, and the splenic vein and its tributaries, lie posterior to the tail (see Fig. 68.4F–G).

Pancreatic ducts

The exocrine pancreatic tissue drains into multiple small lobular ducts. There are several anatomical variations of the ducts draining the pancreas. The most common variation is a single pancreatic duct and a single accessory duct (Fig. 68.5) that reflects the embryological development of the dorsal and ventral pancreatic ducts (see Figs 21.3–21.4). The pancreatic duct (of Wirsung) usually runs through the gland slightly diagonally from left to right, from a more inferior location in the head to a more superior location. Its size varies, which has significant implications for pancreatic surgery when a reconstruction is planned. The duct is formed by the junction of several lobular (secondary) ducts in the tail. It increases in calibre within the body of the pancreas as it receives further lobular ducts that join almost at right-angles to its long axis, forming a herringbone pattern. It can often be visualized on ultrasonography, measuring approximately 3 mm in diameter in the head, 2 mm in the body and 1 mm in the tail; the calibre of the duct increases from about the fifth decade onwards (Glaser et al 1987). As it reaches the neck of the gland, the pancreatic duct runs inferiorly and posteriorly towards the bile duct, which descends through the head of the pancreas. The two ducts unite and enter the wall of the descending part of the duodenum obliquely; a dilation, the hepatopancreatic ampulla (of Vater) is sometimes found at this union. The terminal part of the pancreatic duct contains a few mucosal folds that impede reflux of pancreatic juice (Purvis et al 2013). The length of the common pancreaticobiliary channel is variable and measures up to 5–7 mm in most individuals (Horaguchi et al 2014). An abnormally long common channel can occur in isolation or with congenital choledochal dilation and is associated with pancreaticobiliary reflux and an increased risk of biliary tract cancer (Kamisawa et al 2010a).
The accessory pancreatic duct (of Santorini) drains the superior part of the anterior portion of the head of the pancreas. Much smaller in calibre than the pancreatic duct, it is formed within the head of the pancreas from several lobular ducts and usually communicates with the pancreatic duct near the neck of the gland or near its first inferior branch (Kamisawa 2004, Kamisawa and Okamoto 2008). The accessory duct usually opens onto a small, rounded minor duodenal papilla that lies about 2 cm proximal to the major duodenal papilla (see Fig. 68.5A). If the duodenal end of the accessory duct fails to develop, the lobular ducts drain via small channel(s) into the pancreatic duct (Hernandez-Jover et al 2011). The anatomy of the pancreatic duct and accessory pancreatic duct can vary, reflecting variations in the development and fusion of the dorsal and ventral pancreatic ducts.
Patients who develop acute pancreatitis are more likely to have a poorly developed minor duodenal papilla: if this were not so, the patent accessory duct system could act as a secondary drainage mechanism for the main ductal system and thus prevent the elevation of intraductal pressure likely to cause acute pancreatitis (Kamisawa et al 2010b). One clinically important variation is pancreas divisum, which is seen in 5–10% of individuals (Rana et al 2012; see Fig. 68.5C,G). In this condition, normal duct fusion fails and the accessory pancreatic duct drains the entire pancreas, except that part of the head that develops from the ventral bud, via the minor duodenal papilla. The relatively small size of the minor duodenal papilla could impair pancreatic drainage, leading to recurrent pancreatitis.

Vascular supply and lymphatic drainage

Arteries

The pancreas has a rich arterial supply from branches of the coeliac trunk and superior mesenteric artery (Figs 68.6A–C; see also Fig. 64.4). The head of the pancreas and adjoining duodenum are supplied mainly by four arteries: anterior and posterior superior pancreaticoduodenal arteries via the gastroduodenal artery from the coeliac trunk, and anterior and posterior inferior pancreaticoduodenal arteries via the inferior pancreaticoduodenal artery from the superior mesenteric artery. The two anterior arteries supply the anterior aspects of the duodenum, head of the pancreas, and uncinate process, and join to form the anterior pancreaticoduodenal arcade. The two posterior arteries supply the posterior (dorsal) aspect of the head of the pancreas, adjacent duodenum and distal bile duct, and can join to form a posterior pancreaticoduodenal arcade.
The body and tail of the pancreas are supplied by multiple small direct branches from the splenic artery, including the dorsal pancreatic artery. Small arteries characteristically run along the superior and inferior borders of the gland, lying either in a deep groove or within the gland. They are fed along their length by the pancreaticoduodenal and splenic arteries. When the pancreas is transected during pancreatic surgery, bleeding from these vessels is avoided by ligating the superior and inferior borders of the pancreas using transfixion sutures prior to transection.
Posterior superior pancreaticoduodenal artery
The posterior superior pancreaticoduodenal artery usually arises as the first branch of the gastroduodenal artery at the superior edge of the superior (first) part of the duodenum. It runs to the right, anterior to the supraduodenal segment of the bile duct, before spiralling posteriorly around the pancreatic segment of the bile duct and descending on the posterior surface of the head of the pancreas. It gives branches to the duodenum, head of the pancreas, and bile duct before anastomosing with the posterior inferior pancreaticoduodenal artery to form the posterior pancreaticoduodenal arcade. The posterior superior pancreaticoduodenal artery can also give origin to the retroduodenal and supraduodenal arteries that supply the duodenum (Bertelli et al 1996a). The spiral course of the posterior superior pancreaticoduodenal artery reflects its embryonic development: it is the artery to the anterior aspect of the ventral pancreatic bud and the bile duct that subsequently rotate clockwise posterior to the duodenum and dorsal pancreatic bud (Bertelli et al 1997).
Anterior superior pancreaticoduodenal artery
The anterior superior pancreaticoduodenal artery usually arises as the smaller terminal branch of the gastroduodenal artery, together with the right gastro-omental artery. It originates posterior to the superior portion of the duodenum and runs inferiorly on the anterior aspect of the head of the pancreas at a variable distance medial to the groove between the descending duodenum and head of the pancreas. It passes inferiorly around the right or inferior border of the head of the pancreas, often piercing the gland to reach the posterior surface of the uncinate process, where it joins the anterior inferior pancreaticoduodenal artery to form the anterior pancreaticoduodenal arcade (Bertelli et al 1995).
Inferior pancreaticoduodenal artery
An inferior pancreaticoduodenal artery is present in most individuals. It usually arises either directly from the superior mesenteric artery at the inferior border of the pancreas or as a common vessel with the first jejunal artery (a pancreaticoduodenojejunal trunk) from the posterior or left aspect of the superior mesenteric artery (Bertelli et al 1996b, Horiguchi et al 2008). It runs to the right, posterior to the superior mesenteric vein, to reach the posterior surface of the uncinate process, where it divides into anterior and posterior inferior pancreaticoduodenal arteries. When it arises as a pancreaticoduodenojejunal trunk, the artery gives off a jejunal branch and then runs posterior to both the superior mesenteric artery and vein before dividing into its terminal branches. The inferior pancreaticoduodenal artery is at risk of bleeding during the retroperitoneal dissection of a Whipple procedure. Occasionally, it is absent and the anterior and posterior inferior pancreaticoduodenal arteries arise separately from the superior mesenteric artery.
The smaller anterior inferior pancreaticoduodenal artery runs to the right on the posterior surface of the uncinate process to join the anterior superior pancreaticoduodenal artery and form the anterior pancreaticoduodenal arcade. The larger posterior inferior pancreaticoduodenal artery runs on the posterior surface of the head of the pancreas, parallel and superior to the anterior inferior pancreaticoduodenal artery and joins the posterior superior pancreaticoduodenal artery to form the posterior pancreaticoduodenal arcade (Bertelli et al 1997).
Communicating arteries
Multiple small arteries run between the anterior and posterior pancreaticoduodenal arcades, either via the pancreaticoduodenal groove or through the substance of the gland. The largest and most consistent of these is the communicating artery (sometimes known as the middle pancreaticoduodenal arcade) that passes between the pancreatic duct and accessory pancreatic duct and connects the anterior pancreaticoduodenal arterial arcade and the posterior superior pancreaticoduodenal artery (Yamaguchi et al 2001; see Fig. 68.6C). This artery gives rise to most of the small arteries that supply the major duodenal papilla (Mirjalili and Stringer 2011).
Other arterial arcades in the head of the pancreas
Kirk’s arcade is formed by a right branch of the dorsal pancreatic artery that emerges on to the anterior surface of the head of the gland and anastomoses with a branch of either the gastroduodenal artery or the anterior pancreaticoduodenal arcade (Woodburne and Olsen 1951). It supplies the anterior surface of the head and neck of the gland.
Splenic artery
The splenic artery arises from the coeliac trunk and runs posterior to the superior border of the pancreas to the splenic hilum (see Fig. 68.6A). It gives off multiple small branches along its course that penetrate and supply the pancreas, including the dorsal pancreatic artery; the great pancreatic artery, arising approximately two-thirds of the way along the gland; and the artery to the tail of the pancreas, arising near the tail. These branches lie on or within the posterior aspect of the gland and often anastomose with the inferior pancreatic artery. Anatomical variations are not unusual, and the dorsal, great or inferior pancreatic arteries can be dominant in any one individual (Wu et al 2011).
Dorsal pancreatic artery
The dorsal pancreatic artery commonly arises from the initial 2 cm of the splenic artery, although it can also take origin from the common hepatic or superior mesenteric arteries or the coeliac trunk (Horiguchi et al 2008). It is short and gives off numerous branches, including a terminal left branch near the inferior border of the gland. Several right-sided branches run to the head of the pancreas, passing either posterior or anterior to the superior mesenteric vein to supply the posterior or anterior surface of the head of the pancreas, respectively; they anastomose with arteries of the pancreaticoduodenal arcade (Tsutsumi et al 2014). The terminal left branch anastomoses with the inferior pancreatic artery.
The length and course of the dorsal pancreatic artery depend primarily on its site of origin. When it arises from the splenic artery, common hepatic artery or coeliac trunk, it runs inferiorly on the posterior surface of the pancreas, whereas if it arises from the superior mesenteric artery, it runs superiorly. These variations should be taken into account during organ procurement because injury to the dorsal pancreatic artery, including at its origin, can jeopardize the pancreatic graft as a result of inadequate perfusion of the tail of the pancreas (Baranski et al 2016). The artery uniformly terminates near the inferior border of the pancreas close to the confluence of the splenic and superior mesenteric veins (Bertelli et al 1998).
Inferior pancreatic artery
The inferior (transverse) pancreatic artery commonly originates from the left terminal branch of the dorsal pancreatic artery. It runs to the left on the posterior surface of the gland close to its inferior border, gives multiple branches to the body and tail, and anastomoses with other pancreatic branches of the splenic artery. Occasionally, the inferior pancreatic artery originates from the gastroduodenal artery or the anterior superior pancreaticoduodenal artery, crossing the anterior surface of the head of the pancreas to reach the inferior border of the neck of the gland and then the tail. In such cases it may be the dominant artery to the body of the pancreas, and its injury or deliberate ligation during pancreaticoduodenectomy can cause ischaemia of the pancreas.
Arterial segmentation of the pancreas
The pancreas can be divided into two arterial ‘segments’ separated by a relatively avascular plane. There is disagreement about the definition of these segments. One view is that they are separated by a plane passing to the left of the superior mesenteric artery, such that the head and neck are supplied by branches of the gastroduodenal and superior mesenteric arteries and the body and tail are supplied by branches of the splenic artery (Busnardo et al 1988). An alternative view holds that the anterior segment consists of the superior part of the pancreatic head, neck, body and tail (derived from the dorsal pancreatic bud), and the posterior segment consists of the inferior part of the head and the uncinate process (derived from the ventral pancreatic bud) (Sakamoto et al 2000). In both schemes, the pancreatic duct crosses between the segments. Currently, segmental anatomy is of limited practical surgical significance.
Veins
Multiple veins drain the pancreas into the hepatic portal, superior mesenteric and splenic veins (Mourad et al 1994; Fig. 68.7). Although variations are common, the anterior superior pancreaticoduodenal vein typically joins either the confluence of the right gastro-omental and right colic veins (the gastrocolic trunk of Henle) or the right gastro-omental vein alone, to drain into the superior mesenteric vein at the inferior border of the neck of the pancreas (Mourad et al 1994, Lange et al 2000). The posterior superior pancreaticoduodenal vein drains cranially into the hepatic portal vein. Both anterior and posterior inferior pancreaticoduodenal veins usually drain directly into the posterior aspect of the superior mesenteric vein inferior to the uncinate process. These veins can be a major source of blood loss when the uncinate process is dissected off the superior mesenteric vein during a pancreaticoduodenectomy procedure. In most individuals the first and second jejunal veins form a single vein known as the first jejunal trunk that drains into the superior mesenteric vein (Ishikawa et al 2017). Veins from the body and tail of the gland drain directly into the splenic vein. An inferior (transverse) pancreatic vein sometimes runs along the inferior border of the pancreas to join the inferior mesenteric vein. A few pancreatic veins communicate with systemic veins in the retroperitoneum (veins of Retzius) and can form retroperitoneal varices in patients with portal hypertension.
Lymphatic drainage
The lymphatic drainage of the pancreas is extensive (see Fig. 63.14B), which partly explains the poor prognosis of individuals with pancreatic cancer. Lymphatic vessels start within the interlobular septa within the gland and join to form larger lymphatic vessels that follow the paths of local arteries (Deki and Sato 1988, O’Morchoe 1997). Lymphatics from the tail and body of the pancreas drain mostly into nodes along the splenic artery and inferior border of the gland, and from there to pre-aortic nodes between the coeliac trunk and superior mesenteric artery. Lymphatics from the neck and head drain more widely into nodes along the pancreaticoduodenal, superior mesenteric and hepatic arteries, and ultimately into pre-aortic nodes (Donatini and Hidden 1992, Cesmebasi et al 2015). There is no evidence of lymphatic channels within the pancreatic islets.

Innervation

The pancreas is innervated by extra- and intra-pancreatic plexuses of autonomic nerves (Love et al 2007, Zuo et al 2012). Preganglionic vagal efferents with cell bodies in the posterior nucleus of the vagus nerve reach the pancreas via hepatic, gastric and coeliac branches of the vagus nerve. They synapse with postganglionic parasympathetic pancreatic neurones that are distributed singly or clustered in ganglia in the interlobular connective tissue, lobules and islets. Postganglionic fibres ramify in the interacinar spaces and supply acinar cells and islets, modulating both exocrine and endocrine secretion (see Fig. 68.10). They reach the islets via the blood vessels: in marked contrast to mouse pancreas (widely used in experimental models analysing pancreatic function), relatively few parasympathetic axons terminate in the human endocrine pancreas (Pénicaud 2017) and endocrine cells of the human islet do not appear to be directly innervated (Rodriguez-Diaz et al 2012). Postganglionic parasympathetic neurones are also contacted by enteric neurones from the stomach and duodenum, sympathetic efferents and other pancreatic neurones and a network of interstitial cells (of Cajal) provides an additional link between autonomic nerves and pancreatic acini.
The cell bodies of preganglionic sympathetic nerves are located in the intermediolateral nuclei of the spinal cord (T6–T12); their axons travel in the thoracic splanchnic nerves and synapse in the coeliac and superior mesenteric ganglia. Postganglionic sympathetic nerves innervate the smooth muscle cells of vessels and ducts within the pancreas, causing vasoconstriction and inhibiting exocrine secretion.
Visceral afferents, travelling via parasympathetic and sympathetic routes, convey sensory information from the pancreas via the inferior (nodose) ganglion of the vagus nerve (terminating in the solitary tract), and via the coeliac plexus and greater thoracic splanchnic nerves (terminating in the dorsal horn of the spinal cord, T5–T9).
Extrapancreatic nerve plexuses are particularly abundant in the head and neck of the pancreas, where two plexuses have been identified (Yoshioka and Wakabayashi 1958). Pancreatic head plexus I originates from the coeliac ganglia and plexus and travels on the posterior surface of the head of the pancreas and along the medial margin of the uncinate process. Pancreatic head plexus II originates from the superior mesenteric ganglion and plexus and travels along the inferior pancreaticoduodenal artery on the uncinate process (Mochizuki et al 2010, Zuo et al 2012, Isaji et al 2018).
Perineural invasion is relatively common in pancreatic cancer and not only adversely affects prognosis, but may also be a factor in the pain associated with this disease (Bapat et al 2011).

Referred pain

Pain arising in the pancreas is poorly localized. Inflammatory or cancerous processes arising from the gland rapidly involve the tissues of the retroperitoneum that are innervated by somatic nerves and pain is localized to the lower thoracic spine, consequently patients complain of low back pain. Intractable pain from chronic pancreatitis or inoperable pancreatic tumours can be controlled by thermal or chemical ablation of the coeliac plexus.

Microstructure

The pancreas is composed of exocrine and endocrine tissues. The main tissue mass, forming 98% of the pancreas, is exocrine; the pancreatic islets that form the endocrine component are embedded in it (Figs 68.8–68.10).

Exocrine pancreas

The exocrine pancreas is a branched acinar gland (see Fig. 2.6) surrounded and incompletely lobulated by delicate loose connective tissue. Individual acini are composed of roughly spherical clusters of pyramidal cells that secrete digestive enzymes. A narrow intralobular duct lined by flattened or cuboidal centro-acinar cells originates from within each secretory acinus. Intralobular ducts, which release bicarbonate ions and water into their lumina, unite to form larger interlobular ducts lined by taller cuboidal and eventually columnar epithelium. The latter are surrounded by connective tissue septa containing smooth muscle, autonomic nerves and adipose tissue. Neuroendocrine cells are present among the columnar ductal cells, and mast cells are numerous in the surrounding connective tissue. A network of intralobular arteries, arterioles and capillaries forms around the terminal ducts, acini and the richly vascularized endocrine islets (Love et al 2007).

Acinar cells

Acinar cells have a basal nucleus surrounded by abundant basophilic rough endoplasmic reticulum and an apical region containing dense eosinophilic secretory zymogen granules. A prominent supranuclear Golgi complex is associated with large, membrane-bound granules containing the protein constituents of pancreatic secretion, including enzymes that are only active after release. Ganglionic neurones and cords of undifferentiated epithelial cells are also found within the acini. The structure of the exocrine pancreas and its functional regulation are summarized in Fig. 68.8.

Stellate cells

Pancreatic stellate cells are myofibroblast-like cells distributed in the exocrine part of the pancreas, mainly in the peri-acinar space, where their long cytoplasmic processes encircle the base of the acinus, and in perivascular and periductal regions of the pancreas. They are usually in a quiescent state but have been implicated in the pathogenesis of chronic pancreatitis and pancreatic cancer when activated (Omary et al 2007, Masamune and Shimosegawa 2013).

Endocrine pancreas

Pancreatic islets (of Langerhans) constitute the endocrine component of the pancreas. The human pancreas can contain more than a million islets distributed throughout the gland but most numerous in the tail. Each islet consists of a highly vascularized cluster of polyhedral cells arranged in a trabecular pattern, closely approximated to a dense network of fenestrated capillaries that enables substrates to be transported bidirectionally between the islet cells and the circulation (In’t Veld and Marichal 2010). Islets are innervated by autonomic nerve fibres that travel with the blood vessels and end blindly in the pericapillary spaces. True synaptic contact between these nerve fibres and islet cells has not been demonstrated, although it has been suggested that they have a role in islet function (Rodriguez-Diaz et al 2012). Specialized staining techniques are necessary to distinguish various islet cells, designated alpha, beta, delta, epsilon and pancreatic polypeptide (PP) or F cells. Beta cells secrete insulin and are the most abundant cell type (nearly 50% in human islets); alpha cells secrete glucagon (30–40%). Delta cells and PP cells are less common and secrete somatostatin and pancreatic polypeptide, respectively. Epsilon cells secrete ghrelin. Unlike murine islets, where beta cells form the core and are surrounded by alpha cells, human islets show no cell-type-specific localization. The various cell types are uniformly distributed along the microvasculature and have extensive interlinkages with other islet cells. Their general organization is shown in Fig. 68.10.

Acute pancreatitis

Acute inflammation of the pancreas can result from a variety of pancreatic insults, including gallstone obstruction of the hepatopancreatic ampulla, causing reflux of bile and pancreatic fluid into the pancreatic duct. This precipitates intraparenchymal activation of inactive digestive enzymes, leading to cell injury, an inflammatory cascade and destruction of pancreatic tissue.
The regional anatomy of the pancreas explains most of the clinical complications of acute pancreatitis. Irritation of the stomach contributes to gastric stasis and emesis inflammation of the superior mesenteric plexus can contribute to paralytic ileus; vascular inflammation can cause superior mesenteric or hepatic portal vein thrombosis, arterial bleeding or pseudoaneurysms and ischaemia of the transverse colon. Haemorrhaged blood can accumulate in the retroperitoneal tissues around the pancreas and track laterally to the flanks (Grey Turner’s sign), inferiorly to the inguinal ligaments (Fox’s sign), or via the lesser omentum and bare area of the liver to the falciform ligament and the skin around the umbilicus (Cullen’s sign), manifesting as diffuse bruising.
Acute peripancreatic fluid collections frequently resolve spontaneously as the inflammation in the pancreas subsides. Accumulation of fluid anterior to the pancreas, just deep to the posterior wall of the omental bursa, can result in a ‘pseudocyst’ containing amylase-rich fluid and debris but lacking a true epithelial lining (Banks et al 2013). A large pseudocyst bulges anteriorly into the omental bursa in contact with the posterior wall of the stomach, lesser omentum and gastrosplenic ligament. If it does not resolve spontaneously, the pseudocyst can be drained internally through the posterior wall of the stomach by endoscopic or open surgery, or externally by means of a percutaneous drain inserted under radiological guidance.

Chronic pancreatitis

Sustained injury to the pancreas can result in permanent damage, leading to parenchymal fibrosis and calcification within the parenchyma and the pancreatic ducts. This often causes chronic pain, diabetes mellitus and pancreatic exocrine insufficiency with steatorrhoea and malnutrition. Fibrosis can involve the bile duct in the head of the pancreas, leading to obstructive jaundice, and/or the splenic vein, causing splenic vein thrombosis and ‘left-sided’ portal hypertension.

Pancreatic tumours

Most pancreatic tumours are ductal adenocarcinomas derived from exocrine ductal epithelium. Up to two-thirds of such tumours are located in the head of the pancreas and often obstruct the pancreatic segment of the bile duct early in the course of disease, leading to obstructive jaundice. The appearance of a dilated bile duct and pancreatic duct on cross-sectional imaging (the ‘double duct’ sign) is characteristic of tumours of the head of the pancreas. Advanced tumours can invade the adjacent duodenum, causing obstruction of the gastric outlet and/or the hepatic portal or superior mesenteric veins posterior to the neck of the pancreas. A tumour in the body or tail of the gland often grows to a large size before presenting as an abdominal mass or with back pain and weight loss (Yeo et al 2002). Tumours of the uncinate process can cause early involvement of the superior mesenteric vessels and duodenum (O’Sullivan et al 2009). Peripancreatic and pre-aortic nodes can be involved, together with hepatic nodes, in tumours of the head of the pancreas, and nodes of the splenic hilum can be involved in tumours of the tail of the pancreas.
Surgery for tumours of the head of the pancreas usually involves combined resection of the head and duodenum (pancreaticoduodenectomy) as a consequence of their shared blood supply. The resectability of the tumour depends on the presence and extent of major vascular involvement, particularly the hepatic portal vein and superior mesenteric vessels. An accessory or replaced right hepatic artery running cranially on the posterior surface of the head of the pancreas and posterior to the supraduodenal segment of the bile duct is at risk of injury during such procedures (Turrini et al 2010). Recognition of this variation and preservation of the artery are essential to maintain the arterial supply to the right lobe of the liver. In distal or total pancreatectomy, spleen-preserving resections are generally undertaken if the underlying pathology does not involve the splenic vein lying in the groove on the posterior surface of the gland, taking care to control the multiple small pancreatic venous tributaries. In duodenum-preserving resection of the head of the pancreas, which is sometimes undertaken for chronic pancreatitis and benign pancreatic tumours, preservation of an adequate duodenal blood supply from the pancreaticoduodenal arterial arcades is important (Kimura and Nagai 1995). Stenosis of the coeliac trunk is common in elderly individuals with atherosclerosis. Here, the arterial blood supply to the liver is provided by the superior mesenteric artery through hypertrophied arteries of the anterior and posterior pancreaticoduodenal arcades. Care should be taken to avoid ligation of these branches during pancreatic surgery because this could cause hepatic ischaemia.

Pancreatic transplantation

The pancreas is usually transplanted together with the kidney (a simultaneous pancreas–kidney transplant) in patients with end-stage renal disease caused by diabetes mellitus. In selected patients, the pancreas alone can be transplanted to treat poor glycaemic control and hypoglycaemia unawareness (White et al 2009).
The donor pancreas is recovered from a deceased donor, usually as part of a multi-organ recovery procedure. The entire pancreas and the duodenal C loop (attached to the head of the pancreas) and spleen (closely related to the tail of the pancreas) are recovered to prevent injury to the vascular arcades in the pancreaticoduodenal groove and minimize handling of the pancreas during organ recovery (Abu-Elmagd et al 2000). The arterial supply for the donor pancreas is based on the splenic artery and the superior mesenteric artery, both of which are carefully preserved during the donor operation and reconstructed to a Y-shaped deceased donor iliac artery graft to enable a single arterial anastomosis to be performed in the recipient. The venous drainage is through the hepatic portal vein. The donor pancreas is implanted in the pelvis and the reconstructed artery is anastomosed to the external iliac artery. The hepatic portal vein of the graft can be anastomosed to either an iliac vein or the superior mesenteric vein of the recipient: there is no evidence to support the superiority of one technique over the other (Bazerbachi et al 2012). Drainage of the exocrine pancreatic secretions is into the ileum through a duodeno-ileal anastomosis or into the recipient urinary bladder through a duodeno-cystic anastomosis. Complications of the procedure result mainly from injury sustained by the donor pancreas during the organ recovery, storage and transplantation process, and include graft pancreatitis and graft necrosis leading to bleeding, thrombosis of vascular anastomoses and sepsis (Delis et al 2004, Troppmann 2010). In most patients, pancreas transplantation provides the potential for an insulin-free life and retards the development of diabetes-related complications (Gruessner et al 2012).
