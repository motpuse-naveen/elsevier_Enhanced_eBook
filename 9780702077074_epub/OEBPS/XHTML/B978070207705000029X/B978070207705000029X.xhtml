Chapter 29: Cerebellum

The cerebellum is the largest part of the hindbrain, recognized as a distinct division of the brain from the time of Herophilus (335–280 BC) and Galen (AD 131–200). It is situated in the posterior cranial fossa, beneath the tentorium cerebelli, the medial extension of the falx cerebri and the overlying occipital lobe and lies behind the pons and medulla, from which it is separated by the fourth ventricle (Figs 29.1–29.2). It is joined to the brainstem by three bilaterally paired major fibre tracts, the inferior, middle and superior cerebellar peduncles, through which it communicates with the remainder of the central nervous system. In adults, the ratio of the weight of the cerebellum to the cerebrum is about 1 to 10, in infants about 1 to 20. The adult human cerebellum has a volume of approximately 145 ml and weighs 150 grams, but its surface area is 40% that of the cerebral cortex. The great majority of cerebellar neurones are small granule cells, which are so densely packed that the cerebellar cortex contains approximately 70% of the 100 billion neurones in the central nervous system (Andersen et al 2012).
The basic internal organization of the cerebellum is of a superficial cortex overlying a core of white matter and deep cerebellar nuclei. The cortex is characteristically highly convoluted by numerous transverse furrows of varying depths that separate its surface into lobes, lobules and folia (leaflets). Aggregations of neuronal cell bodies embedded within the white matter form the fastigial (medial), globose (posterior interposed), emboliform (anterior interposed) and dentate (lateral) nuclei, collectively known as the deep cerebellar nuclei.
An earlier schema of the cerebellum regarded the cerebellum as being divided into three zones of connectional and functional organization (Jansen and Brodal 1940, Chambers and Sprague 1955a, 1955b). In this approach, the archicerebellum (vestibulocerebellum/flocculonodular lobe) has reciprocal links to vestibular nuclei either directly or indirectly and is concerned with eye movements and equilibrium. The paleocerebellum (spinocerebellum) in the vermis and paravermal regions receive cutaneous and kinaesthetic afferents from the spinal cord. The vermis is linked with the fastigial nucleus and influences the medial motor system through brainstem vestibular and reticular nuclei that send descending projections to the spinal cord, and is important for balance and equilibrium; the paravermal area is linked with the interpositus nuclei that project to the red nucleus and primary motor cortex and influences the lateral motor system which is important for the control of distal limb movements. The neocerebellum (pontocerebellum) is regarded as the lateral part of the cerebellum, receiving descending input from the cerebral cortex, projecting through the dentate nucleus back to thalamic areas that mostly project to motor and premotor cortices, and is important for fine motor control and perhaps motor planning and motor learning. These concepts retain some overall validity, but they have limitations in the light of evolving knowledge. The most salient are that the parasagittal organization of the cerebellum is exponentially more complex than this tripartite division; the anterior lobe–posterior lobe dichotomy of cerebellar function is a major organizing feature; pontine projections are not confined to the cerebellar hemispheres; the cerebellar incorporation into brainstem, diencephalic and cerebrocerebellar circuits extends considerably beyond the sensorimotor domains into the association areas and paralimbic cortices; and there are multiple representations of unimodal, multimodal and transmodal forms of sensorimotor, cognitive and affective systems within the human cerebellum.
The cerebellum is subdivided into modules, each consisting of a longitudinal cortical zone, a cerebellar or vestibular target nucleus, and a supporting olivocerebellar climbing fibre system. The longitudinal cortical zones are characterized by their connections as well as their immunohistochemical properties. The cerebellum receives topographically highly arranged input from peripheral receptors, spinal cord and brainstem, and from most of the cerebral cortex through two different afferent systems: mossy fibres and climbing fibres. It now appears that a fundamental role of the cerebellum is to enhance function in almost all domains, modulating behaviour, both motor and nonmotor, to optimize performance in a context-dependent manner.
Ideas on the involvement of the cerebellum in motor functions were derived mainly from movement disorders seen in experimental studies dating back to Rolando and Flourens (1824) (summarized by Luciani 1891), and in observations of patients with acquired cerebellar lesions or degeneration (Brown 1892, Marie 1893, Babinski 1899, Holmes 1939), who displayed characteristic gait ataxia (unstable balance and walking), dysmetria (disordered control) of the limbs, dysarthria (impaired articulatory clarity), and abnormal oculomotor control. The more recent observation that lesions of the cerebellar posterior lobes produce the cerebellar cognitive-affective syndrome in adults (Schmahmann and Sherman 1998) prompted the suggestion that the human cerebellum is also concerned with non-motor functions, which, together with the vestibulocerebellar and motor disorders, comprise the modern triad of clinical cerebellar ataxiology (Manto and Mariën 2015).

External Features and Relations

The cerebellum consists of two large, laterally located hemispheres united by a midline vermis (Fig. 29.3). It is a multiply folded structure; when viewed from the external surface, the fissures (the cerebellar equivalent of cerebral sulci) run horizontally and follow the curve of the cerebellar external surface. The cerebellum has three surfaces. The superior surface adjoins the tentorium cerebelli and projects beyond its free edge: the distinction between the vermis and hemispheres on the superior surface is indistinct, with shallow paramedian kinks in the transverse fissures likely reflecting indentations of the superior cerebellar artery. The superior surface slopes gently laterally and posteriorly as it reaches its margins. The transverse sinus borders the cerebellum at the point where the superior and inferior surfaces meet. The inferior surface of the cerebellum is characterized by a massive enlargement of the cerebellar hemispheres, separated from each other medially by a deep groove, or vallecula, which contains the dural falx cerebelli. The posterior vermis is a well-defined anatomical structure when viewed from posterior and inferior, named for its worm-like appearance by Vincenzo Malacarne (1744–1816) (Malacarne 1776), who also named the uvula and tonsils because of their resemblance to structures in the oral cavity. The posterior vermis is demarcated from the cerebellar hemispheres by deep paramedian sulci, although the hemispheres may extend medially and overlie the vermis. The inferior cerebellar surface lies against the occipital squama. The shape of the anterior cerebellar surface facing the brainstem is irregular, forming the roof of the fourth ventricle and the lateral recesses on each side. The cerebellar peduncles define the diamond shape of the ventricle when viewed from behind. Anterolaterally, the cerebellum lies against the posterior surface of the petrous part of the temporal bone.

Cerebellar fissures and lobules

The cerebellar surface is divided by numerous curved transverse fissures which separate its folia (the cerebellar equivalent of cerebral gyri) and give it a laminated appearance. The individual cerebellar folia group together as 10 lobules, arising usually from a common ray of medullary white matter. The lobules are continuous across the midline from one hemisphere to the other. They are distinguished from each other by named fissures, and they are grouped into three lobes: the anterior lobe (lobules I–V), posterior lobe (lobules VI to IX) and the flocculonodular lobe (lobule X) (Fig. 29.4; see Fig. 29.3). Because the cerebellar cortex has a roughly spherical shape, the true relations between its parts can sometimes be obscure. Thus, the most anterior lobules I/II of the cerebellar vermis lie very close to the most posterior vermal lobule X. The Roman numerals and subdivisions adopted by Schmahmann et al (1999, 2000) in the MRI Atlas of the Human Cerebellum (Fig. 29.5; see Fig. 29.2) and used here to designate the lobules are according to Larsell and Jansen (1972) and Bolk (1906) (Fig. 29.6) and replace earlier nomenclatures that were often conflicting or contradictory. The MRI Atlas enabled the development of probabilistic MR atlases of the human cerebellum and cerebellar nuclei (Diedrichsen et al 2009, 2011) used in functional imaging studies to correlate structure with function in health and disease.
Vermal lobules I and II constitute a single lamina of four or five shallow folia; their white core is continuous with the anterior (or superior) medullary velum lying anterior and inferior to the vermis. Lobule I is distinct from lobule II in some human cerebella and in lower primates. The vermal and hemispheric components of lobule I/II together were termed the lingula. The precentral fissure separates lobules I/II from lobule III (centralis) both in the vermis and the hemispheres. Lobule III is partially obscured by lobule IV when viewed from the superior aspect, but it is sizeable, and has a semilunar form with its wing (ala, in prior terminology) extending into the hemispheres on each side. Lobule III is the first distinct folium that is not attached to the anterior medullary velum. The preculminate fissure separates lobule III from lobule IV. The use of the term culmen (including both the vermis and the hemisphere) obscures the fact of the division of the culmen into lobules IV and V by the intraculminate fissure. This fissure is readily discernible on the hemisphere, but at the midline is continuous with a small fissure situated between the branches of a folium. Lobule IV usually contains one folium, and lobule V contains two. The deepest fissure in the vermis that is visible in mid-sagittal section is the primary fissure, which curves ventrolaterally around the superior surface of the cerebellum. It appears early in embryological development and marks the boundary between the anterior lobe (lobules I to V) and the posterior lobe (lobules VI to IX). Specifically, it separates lobule V from lobule VI in the vermis and in the hemisphere. The primary fissure, like other fissures within the anterior lobe, is progressively more difficult to discern on parasagittal sections as one moves laterally, becoming continuous with an undistinguished small fissure in the intermediate sectors of the hemispheres.
Lobule VI is sizeable and contains two large folia with medullary rays that take their origin off the medullary core. The vermal and hemispheric components of lobule VI have previously received different names: in the vermis, lobule VI was called the declive, and in the hemisphere it was called the lobulus simplex, among others.
The superior posterior fissure appears early in development and is visible on the superior surface of the cerebellum separating lobule VI from lobule VII. Vermal lobule VII is complex; its lateral extension in the hemispheres into what Lodewjck Bolk (1866–1930) called the ansiform lobule, forms a large part of the hemisphere in the human cerebellum. The massive expansion through evolution of the human cerebellum is a reflection in large part of the expansions of lobules VI and VII. The terms folium and tuber have been used to denote vermis lobule VII, but this fails to convey the complexity of their equivalent hemispheric extensions, as below.
The horizontal fissure appears relatively late in embryological development. It is prominent in the hemisphere, extending around the dorsolateral border of each hemisphere from the middle cerebellar peduncle to the vallecula, separating the superior and inferior surfaces. The horizontal fissure divides lobule VII into two sectors, with important differences in their hemispheric extensions. Vermal lobule VIIAf (identical to the folium) expands laterally to form crus I (of Bolk’s ansiform lobule, formerly known as the superior semilunar lobule). Vermal lobule VIIAt (formerly called the rostral half of the tuber of the vermis) is continuous in the hemispheres with crus II (of Bolk’s ansiform lobule, formerly known as the inferior semilunar lobule). The horizontal fissure therefore separates lobule VIIAf from VIIAt in the vermis, and crus I from crus II in the hemisphere. In some brains, lobule VIIAf may not be discernible at the midline, in which case the superior posterior fissure is bounded caudally by the ansoparamedian and not the horizontal fissure.
The ansoparamedian fissure can be traced across the midline separating lobule VIIAt and crus II from vermal and hemispheric lobule VIIB (previously termed the paramedian, or gracile lobule). The ansoparamedian fissure is not symmetrical in the hemispheres in all brains. The horizontal and ansoparamedian fissures sometimes meet on the posterior inferior cerebellar surface, so that crus II appears as a triangular lobe situated on the superior and posterior aspects of the posterior lobe, and on lateral parasagittal sections, crus I is adjacent to lobule VIIB. When the horizontal and ansoparamedian fissures do not meet, crus II continues laterally and forward onto the anterior surface of the cerebellum, and on lateral parasagittal sections crus II is adjacent to VIIB. These details are relevant for precise mapping of the human brain and can affect intra-and inter-individual functional mapping studies. Even when the folia at the lateral inferior cerebellar surface are asymmetric across the two hemispheres, they can be seen to be derived from medullary rays that are morphologically similar.
Lobule VIIIA and VIIIB in the vermis were formerly known as the pyramis, and in the hemispheres as the biventer. Thus, the prepyramidal/prebiventer fissure separates lobule VIIB from VIIIA and is named according to whether the lobule VIIB/VIIIA distinction is in the vermis (prepyramidal fissure) or hemisphere (prebiventer fissure). The intrapyramidal/intrabiventer fissure separates lobule VIIIA from VIIIB. The secondary fissure is interposed both at the vermis and in the hemispheres between lobules VIIIB and IX. In earlier terminology, vermal lobule IX is referred to as the uvula, and hemisphere lobule IX as the tonsil, and subsequently as the ventral paraflocculus (equivalent to the dorsal paraflocculus in monkeys). In the human, the folial loop of lobule IX is directed medially, contrary to the situation in most mammals, where the paraflocculus arches laterally. Note that the term cerebellar tonsil refers to a grossly visible anatomical structure at the inferior and medial cerebellum which is of great relevance in clinical neurology and neurosurgery (tonsillar herniation, Chiari malformation). Although the tonsil was previously equated with lobule IX, it also incorporates folia within lobule VIIIB, which means that the term is anatomically imprecise.
The posterolateral fissure separates lobules IX from X, and forms the boundary between the posterior lobe of the cerebellum and the flocculonodular lobe. Vermal lobule X (nodulus) is the last of the vermis divisions. Where its cortex ends it forms the attachment (taenia) of the ependymal roof of the fourth ventricle. Hemispheric lobule X (flocculus) is a small cerebellar lobule adjacent to the caudal surface of the middle cerebellar peduncle. It is located in the roof of the lateral recess of the fourth ventricle, and dorsal to the cochlear nuclei and the vestibulocochlear nerve which are ventral to the recess. Hemispheric lobule X appears as a double folial rosette; its dorsal leaf is known as the accessory paraflocculus of Henle (the ventral paraflocculus in monkeys), its ventral leaf represents the true flocculus. Between vermal lobule X (nodulus) and hemispheric lobule X (flocculus and accessory paraflocculus), the cortex is absent and the tissue is stretched out as the posterior medullary velum (inferior medullary velum) which forms the infero-posterior part of the fourth ventricle. The internal surface of the posterior medullary velum is the ventricular ependyma; its outer surface is covered by pia mater.

Cerebellar peduncles

Three pairs of peduncles connect the cerebellum with the brainstem (Fig. 29.7). The inferior cerebellar peduncle runs parallel to the major axis of the brainstem, medial to the middle cerebellar peduncle. It consists of an outer, compact fibre tract, the restiform body, and a medial, juxtarestiform body. The restiform body is a purely afferent system conveying fibres from the dorsal spinocerebellar tract and trigeminocerebellar, cuneocerebellar, reticulocerebellar and olivocerebellar tracts (see Fig. 29.24). The juxtarestiform body is mainly an efferent system, made up almost entirely of Purkinje cell axons destined for the vestibular nuclei and uncrossed fibres from the fastigial nucleus. It also contains primary afferent mossy fibres from the vestibular nerve and secondary afferent fibres from the vestibular nuclei. The crossed fibres from the fastigial nucleus pass dorsal to the superior cerebellar peduncle as the uncinate tract (the hook bundle of Russell) and enter the brainstem at the border of the juxtarestiform and restiform bodies.
The middle cerebellar peduncle (see Figs 29.7B–C) is the most lateral and by far the largest of the three. It passes obliquely from the basal pons to the cerebellum and contains the massive pontocerebellar mossy fibre pathway, composed almost entirely of fibres that arise from the contralateral basal pontine nuclei, with a small addition from nuclei in the pontine tegmentum.
The superior cerebellar peduncle (brachium conjunctivum) contains all the efferent fibres from the dentate, emboliform and globose nuclei, and a small fascicle from the fastigial nucleus. Fibres from the globose nucleus occupy a medial position; those from the emboliform and dentate nucleus are successively more lateral. Axons in the extreme ventrolateral portion of the peduncle arise from the superior vestibular nucleus and relay excitation from the anterior semicircular canal to the oculomotor nucleus (the excitatory vestibulo-ocular relay). Nucleo-olivary fibres of the interposed and dentate nuclei are located in a separate bundle, ventral to the peduncle. The superior cerebellar peduncle, including its nucleo-olivary component, decussates in the caudal mesencephalon (the decussation of the brachium conjunctivum). The decussation consists of dorsal and ventral portions. The dorsal portion contains fibres that occupy the medial two-thirds of the peduncle and start to cross the midline at the level of the inferior colliculus, ventral to the medial longitudinal fasciculus. The ventral portion contains fibres that occupy the lateral third of the peduncle, and decussate in the ventral tegmentum, immediately dorsal to the interpeduncular nucleus. Rostrally, at the level of the caudal red nucleus, the ventral and dorsal portions of the decussation merge and the entire decussation is displaced ventrally by the dorsal tegmental (tectospinal) decussation. After its decussation the peduncle divides into descending and ascending branches. The descending branch terminates in the medial reticular formation of the pons and the medulla and in the reticulotegmental nucleus (nucleus reticularis tegmenti pontis, NRTP). The nucleo-olivary fibres join the descending branch to terminate in the inferior olive in a strict orderly manner (see below). The much larger ascending branch distributes to the mesencephalon and the diencephalon; the red nucleus and the thalamus are its main targets. The fibres of the superior cerebellar peduncle always decussate to the opposite hemisphere, except in rare developmental disorders of axonal guidance (Spampinato et al 2008).
The superior cerebellar peduncle also conveys afferents to the cerebellum that arise in the spinal cord and course in the ventral spinocerebellar tract. These fibres reach the upper part of the pontine tegmentum, loop around the entrance of the trigeminal nerve and join the peduncle, uniting with the dorsal spinocerebellar fibres entering through the restiform body.

Internal Organization

White matter

On its cut surface, the characteristic appearance of the cerebellum is a central white matter core surrounded by multiple, deeply folded folia. The white matter core branches into the diverging medullary laminae, which occupy the central part of the lobules and then further branch out into their folia and subfolia. On sagittal section, the highly branched pattern of medullary laminae gave rise to the name arbor vitae, for its resemblance to the leaf of the coniferous arbor vitae or cedar tree (see Fig. 29.2). The white matter of the medullary rays contains the afferents to the cerebellar cortex, and the Purkinje cell axonal efferents from the cortex to the deep nuclei.
Fibres crossing the midline in the anterior medullary velum and the white matter core of the cerebellum constitute the cerebellar commissure, which contains the decussating afferent fibres of the inferior and middle cerebellar peduncles, and decussating efferent fibres from the fastigial nucleus. Note that the term commissure here denotes the crossing of the midline by cerebellar afferent and efferent fibres; there are no cerebellar cortico-cortical connections either ipsilaterally or between the two hemispheres.

Deep cerebellar nuclei

The medullary core contains the four deep cerebellar nuclei (Fig. 29.8). These were first described by Stilling (1864) as comprising, from medial to lateral, the fastigial, globose, emboliform and dentate nuclei. In rodent, cat and monkey brains the anterior and posterior interposed nuclei are equivalent in humans to the emboliform and globose nuclei respectively. The nuclei form two interconnected groups. The caudomedial group includes the fastigial nucleus located next to the midline and bordering on the fastigium (roof) of the fourth ventricle, and the closely adjacent globose nucleus. The rostrolateral group consists of the emboliform nucleus which is medially adjacent to the dentate nucleus.
The dentate is the largest of the deep cerebellar nuclei and has expanded most in evolution. It is an irregularly folded sheet of neurones, resembling a crumpled leather purse with a medially directed opening. The convolutions of the dentate nucleus are narrow rostromedially (microgyric) and contain large neurones, whereas the much wider ventrocaudal (macrogyric) part contains smaller neurones. This is important from the perspective of anthropology as well as cognitive neuroscience and behavioural neurology (see below). The dentate nucleus surrounds the hilum, a mass of efferent fibres arising from dentate neurones that forms the bulk of the superior cerebellar peduncle.
The cerebellar nuclei contain three types of neurones. Multipolar neurones have different sizes, with long ramifying dendrites and an axon that leaves the cerebellum through the superior cerebellar peduncle, the uncinate tract or the juxtarestiform body. These provide glutamatergic, excitatory input to the brainstem, thalamus and spinal cord. Some collaterals of these efferent axons enter the cerebellar cortex, where they terminate sparsely as mossy fibres. A population of small, inhibitory neurones gives rise to the pathway terminating in the contralateral inferior olive, the nucleo-olivary projection (Nelson et al 1989), travelling mostly in the superior cerebellar peduncle. These contain immunoreactivity for GAD (glutamic acid decarboxylase), the endogenous marker enzyme for GABAergic neurones, as well as CGRP- (calcitonin gene-related peptide)-like immunoreactivity. Local inhibitory interneurones in the cerebellar nuclei contain glycine, often colocalized with GABA (γ-aminobutyric acid). Purkinje cell axons are the main input to the neurones of the cerebellar nuclei: there are GABAergic inhibitory synapses on both dendrites and somata. Collaterals of climbing and mossy fibres terminate on proximal and distal dendrites and provide the cerebellar nuclei with glutamatergic excitatory input.
A collection of small, cholinergic neurones extends from the flocculus to the nodulus (hemispheric and vermal lobule X) in the roof of the fourth ventricle, invading the spaces between the nuclei. These form the basal interstitial nucleus (Langer 1985).

Cerebellar cortex

The cerebellar cortex is characterized by its regular, repeating, lattice-like histological structure (Figs 29.9–29.11). In contrast to the cerebral cortex, obvious local differences and architectonic heterogeneity do not occur and, with a few exceptions, different areas of the cerebellar cortex are largely indistinguishable. The same homogeneity is present throughout all mammals and, with only minor differences, throughout the vertebrates. The cerebellar cortex has three main layers, a Purkinje cell layer interleaved between the overlying molecular layer and the granule cell layer beneath. The three layers are interconnected by a complex circuitry with an architecture that is essentially invariant throughout. Cerebellar functional topography is entirely explained by the arrangement of the fibres that connect the cerebellum to other regions of the central nervous system. (For further reading, see Jansen and Brodal (1954), Braitenberg and Atwood (1958), Dow and Moruzzi (1958), Eccles et al (1967), Fox and Snider (1967), Llinas and Sotelo (1992), Larsell and Jansen (1972), Palay and Chan-Palay (1974), Ito (1984), Glickstein et al (1987), Strata (1989), Manto et al (2013).)

Purkinje cell layer

This layer is characterized by the presence of Purkinje cells (PC), one of the largest cells in the nervous system (Fig. 29.12) (Purkinje 1837). Smaller somata of epithelial (Bergmann) glial cells are located at this level. Clumps of granule cells and occasional Golgi cells penetrate between the Purkinje cell somata. There are estimated to be 25 million Purkinje cells in the human cerebellum (Andersen et al 2012), each with a pear-shaped soma, 30–35 μm in diameter and 50–70 μm in length. Individual Purkinje cells are separated by about 50 μm transversely and 50–100 μm longitudinally. This geometry occurs in its typical form in all vertebrate classes. The entire Purkinje cell soma, its dendrites and axonal initial segment, are covered with a neuroglial sheath, and the axon is myelinated. Axo-somatic contacts on the Purkinje cells penetrate this sheath, and the dendritic spines protrude through the sheath to contact the parallel and climbing fibres.
One or sometimes two large primary proximal dendrites arise from the apical pole (superficial pole, neck) of each Purkinje cell soma. They divide, usually into two major dendrites, from which an abundant arborization arises, with several orders of subdivision, that extends towards the surface, forming a flattened plate orientated perpendicular to the long axis of the folium. The fan-like appearance of the Purkinje cell dendritic tree is a defining feature. It measures 300–400 μm in the sagittal plane, but only 15–20 μm in the transverse plane. Approximately 90% of the Purkinje cell dendritic tree consists of terminal branches containing about 180,000 spines (Fox and Barnard 1957). They are not uniformly distributed: proximal first and second order dendrites have smooth surfaces with short, stubby spines which are contacted by climbing fibres, whereas distal branches have a dense array of short-necked dendritic spines on which the terminals of parallel fibres synapse. This differential proximal vs distal Purkinje cell spine density is under dual control: an intrinsic mechanism that promotes spine growth over the entire dendritic tree, and activity-dependent spine pruning by the climbing fibre at the proximal dendrite.
The subcellular structure of a Purkinje cell is essentially similar to that of other neurones. A distinguishing feature is the presence of subsurface cisterns below the plasmalemma of the soma and dendrites; these may penetrate into the spines and are often associated with a mitochondrion. The cisterns represent intracellular calcium stores which are important links in the second messenger systems of the cell. Excitatory (asymmetrical) synapses of the climbing fibres contact the proximal spines of the spiny branchlets; parallel fibres contact the distal spines of the spiny branchlets. Excitatory synapses are rare on the soma of a Purkinje cell. Inhibitory (symmetrical) synapses from basket cell axons and stellate cells and recurrent collaterals of the Purkinje cell axon contact the shafts of the proximal dendrites. The non-synaptic area of the surface of the soma and dendrites of a Purkinje cell is covered by lamellar protrusions of the Bergmann glial fibres.
The Purkinje cell axon arises from the pole opposite the dendrites and traverses the granular layer into the subjacent white matter. Its initial portion is narrow, unmyelinated and has the typical features of an initial axon segment. It is surrounded by the distal branches of the basket cell axons as the pinceau (paintbrush). Beyond the initial segment, about 50 μm beneath the Purkinje cell soma, the axon suddenly enlarges, acquires a myelin sheath and gives off collateral branches. The main axon descends into the white matter and forms a plexus in the deep cerebellar nuclei and, in the case of lobule X, the vestibular nuclei. Recurrent collateral branches of Purkinje axons form plexuses above and below their somata. These collaterals are orientated in the same plane as the Purkinje cell dendritic tree and the inhibitory synapses from basket and stellate cells. Purkinje cell axon recurrent collaterals terminate on neighbouring Purkinje neurones and on basket and Golgi neurones. Purkinje cells are inhibitory and use GABA as their main neurotransmitter, as well as the neuropeptides motilin, cerebellin and CGRP. They are the only efferent neurone of the cerebellar cortex and project to the deep cerebellar or vestibular nuclei.

Molecular layer

The molecular layer is about 300–400 μm thick. By ordinary histological technique it is almost featureless, contrasting with the immense cellular population of the granule cell layer. It is composed of dendritic arborizations of the Purkinje and Golgi cells, axons and parallel fibres of the granule cells, climbing fibres, radial fibres of the Bergmann neuroglial cells, and sparse basket and stellate interneurones. Purkinje cell dendritic trees extend towards the surface and spread out in a plane perpendicular to the long axis of the cerebellar folia. Dendritic trees of Golgi neurones lying in the superficial part of the granular layer reach towards the cerebellar surface. Unlike the flattened dendritic tree of the Purkinje cells, Golgi cell dendrites have the shape of a goblet and span the territory of many Purkinje neurones longitudinally as well as transversely. These dendrites receive synapses from parallel fibres. Some Golgi cell dendrites enter the granular layer where they are contacted by mossy fibre terminals.
The granule cell axon ascends into the molecular layer and bifurcates into the parallel fibre, one of the thinnest known vertebrate axons. Parallel fibres extend in opposite directions along the long axis of the folium and terminate on the dendrites of the Purkinje cells and Golgi cells that they encounter, and on the basket and stellate cells of the molecular layer. Climbing fibres are the terminals of olivocerebellar fibres which ascend through the granular layer to contact Purkinje dendrites in the molecular layer. Radiating branches of the large Bergmann glial cells give off processes that surround all neuronal elements except at the synapses; at the cortical surface their expansions join to form an external limiting membrane.
The cerebellar cortical interneurones were described by Ramón y Cajal (1909–10) (see Fig. 29.10). Basket and stellate cells are the inhibitory GABAergic interneurones of the molecular layer. They receive excitatory (asymmetrical) synapses from parallel fibres as they pass through their dendritic trees: their sparsely branched dendritic trees and the ramifications of their axons are located in a plane perpendicular to the long axis of a folium, i.e. in the same plane as the Purkinje cell dendritic tree and the ramifications of the Purkinje cell recurrent collaterals.
The basket cell has a pyramidal or oval shape, measures about 15 μm in length, and lies in the lower third of the molecular layer just above the Purkinje cells (Fig. 29.13A–B). Its dendrites extend up throughout the molecular layer in a fan-shaped field of 30 μm in the parasagittal plane, giving off relatively few branches, interdigitating with the dendritic fields of the Purkinje cells, and contacted by the parallel fibres. The complex axon of the basket cell has a horizontal course deep in the molecular layer just above the Purkinje cells, lying in the parasagittal plane perpendicular to the long axis of a folium (that is, perpendicular to the orientation of the parallel fibres and in the same plane as the flattened plate of the Purkinje cell dendritic tree).
The axon of each basket cell is about 1 mm in length and contacts approximately 9–12 Purkinje cells. As it moves away from its soma, the axon increases in size, giving off a succession of descending branches that envelope the Purkinje cell soma in an axonal sheath with numerous synaptic contacts over its entire surface, forming pericellular networks, or baskets. The terminal branches of the basket cell axons descend to surround the initial segment of the Purkinje cell axon in a dense plexus of convoluted and branching fibres measuring 15–20 μm across, with the appearance of a paintbrush or ‘pinceau’ (see Fig. 29.9D). This axo-axonic complex is unique in the mammalian nervous system. According to Palay and Chan Palay (1974) the pinceau is historically significant because it led Cajal to the view that nerve cells are in contact but not continuity with each other, and that impulses are directed from an axon to a cell body, essential tenets of the neurone doctrine. In addition to the descending collaterals, there are sparser ascending collaterals from the basket cell that synapse on the secondary and tertiary Purkinje cell dendrites. Branches from each basket cell axon also extend in the direction of the long axis of a folium to a further 3–6 rows of Purkinje neurones flanking the axon. Hence an estimated 72 Purkinje cell neurones may receive inhibitory synapses from a single basket cell.
Stellate cells have small cell bodies 5–10 μm in diameter (Fig. 29.13C–D). Their dendrites are contacted by parallel fibres and their axons terminate on the Purkinje cell dendrites. Superficial stellate cells in the upper reaches of the molecular layer generally have short, profusely branching dendrites. Their short axons lie within a narrow parasagittal plane perpendicular to the long axis of a folium, the same as that of the Purkinje cell dendritic tree. The deep, long-axon stellate cells in the middle part of the molecular layer have dendritic trees similar to their superficial counterparts. However, the axon is quite different because it can extend for a considerable distance in the parasagittal plane, up to 450 μm, and it provides ascending and descending collaterals early in its course. Unlike the basket cell axon that also has a long course in the plane of the Purkinje cells, the axon of the deep stellate cell only rarely enters the pericellular Purkinje cells plexus and does not participate in the pinceau.

Granule cell layer

The granule cell layer is 200–300 μm thick on folial summits and about 100 μm thick in the depths of the fissures. It contains granule cells, Golgi cells, Lugaro cells, unipolar brush cells and neuroglial cells. The granule cell is among the smallest neurones in the brain, with a spherical nucleus 5–8 μm in diameter, and a shell of cytoplasm containing a few small mitochondria, ribosomes and a diminutive Golgi complex (Fig. 29.14A). There are about 2.4 million per mm3 in the simian cerebellar cortex and 2–7 million per mm3 in the human cortex (Fox and Barnard 1957, Braitenberg and Atwood 1958). The number of granule cells in the human cerebellum has been estimated to be from 4.3 ×1010 (Zagon et al 1977) to about 7 ×1010 (Andersen et al 2012), making it the most numerous single neuronal element in the nervous system. There are about 3000 granule cells for each Purkinje cell (Lange 1975). Granule cells usually give rise to 3–5 dendrites, each about 10–30 μm in length, ending in claw-like terminal expansions that synapse with the terminals of mossy fibres and Golgi cell axons in the cerebellar glomeruli, visible by light microscopy in the intervals between groups of granular neurones. Cerebellar glomeruli are synaptic complexes wherein a central mossy fibre terminal synapses with the dendrites of granule cells and Golgi cells, and the granule cell dendrites receive synaptic contacts from Golgi terminals. The fine axons of granule cells pass from their superficial aspect into the molecular layer and branch at a T-junction to form the parallel fibres.
Golgi cells (Fig. 29.14B) are irregularly rounded or polygonal GABAergic, inhibitory interneurones: they number approximately 1 per 1.5 Purkinje cells in the human brain. Golgi cells contain the inhibitory amino acid glycine, as well as acetylcholine and leucine-5-enkephalin. Larger Golgi cells are 10–24 μm in diameter, and most lie in the upper half of the granular layer, below the level of the Lugaro cell and the infraganglionic plexus. Smaller Golgi cells are 9–18 μm in diameter and are generally located in the depths of the granular layer. Dendrites either radiate out from the soma or arise as one or two main trunks that give off subsidiary branches as they ascend to the outer zone of the molecular layer, predominantly at right-angles to the surface. Larger Golgi cells have either dendritic morphology, whereas smaller Golgi cells tend to have the radiating dendritic pattern. The Golgi dendritic tree is unlike that of the Purkinje, basket or stellate cell, which is flattened and confined to a single plane, but instead has a three-dimensional configuration, appearing much the same in transverse and longitudinal foliar sections. In both planes, the Golgi dendritic tree overlaps the territories of several Purkinje cells and neighbouring Golgi cells. Some Golgi dendrites do not enter the molecular layer, but divide in the granular layer to join the cerebellar glomeruli. The Golgi cell axon arises from the base of the soma or from a central dendrite, immediately dividing into a profuse arborization that extends through the entire thickness of the granular layer. The territory occupied by the axonal ramifications has a volume corresponding approximately to its dendritic tree in the molecular layer, overlapping with the axonal arborizations of adjacent Golgi cells. The main synaptic input to Golgi cell dendrites is from parallel fibres in the molecular layer. Purkinje cell recurrent collaterals and mossy and climbing fibres also terminate on their proximal dendrites and, more sparsely, on their somata. Golgi neurones are the principal inhibitory interneurones at the input stage of the cerebellum, providing feedforward inhibition through their axonal synapses with mossy fibres at the granule cell glomeruli and feedback inhibition at their dendritic synapses with parallel fibres.
The Lugaro cell is a fusiform cell measuring about 10 μm across and 25–30 μm in length (Fig. 29.15A) (Lugaro 1894a, 1894b). It is an inhibitory interneurone using both GABA and glycine as neurotransmitters. Its major axis lies parallel to the pial surface, horizontally or obliquely in the outermost third of the granular layer, sometimes alongside the somata of Purkinje cells or immediately above them. Its dendrites originate from the gradually tapering extremities at the two poles and extend horizontally for a considerable distance (up to 600 μm) at the level of the Purkinje cell bodies, mostly in the infraganglionic plexus formed by the recurrent axon collaterals of the Purkinje cells. Lugaro cell axons arise from the soma or a large proximal dendrite and form two types of axonal plexuses: a parasagittal axon ascends to the molecular layer and contacts the somata and dendrites of stellate and basket cells, alternatively, an axon lies in the transverse plane in the granule cell layer and contacts Golgi cells (Lugaro 1894a, 1894b, Palay and Chan Palay 1974, Lainé and Axelrad 1996, Berlucchi 2002). Excitatory input to Lugaro cells is from granule cell axons. The Lugaro cell is the major recipient in the cerebellum of serotoninergic input through volume transmission. Serotonin-induced activation of Lugaro cells causes synchronization of the activities of Golgi and granule cells over extended longitudinal domains, and of the simple-spike activity of Purkinje cells, thereby influencing the processing of mossy fibre information by the cerebellar cortex and modulation of the deep cerebellar nuclei (Dieudonné 2001, Berlucchi 2002).
The unipolar brush cell is the only known glutamatergic excitatory interneurone in the cerebellum, found particularly in vestibular regions in lobule X and the vermis, as well as in the dorsal cochlear nucleus (Altman and Bayer 1977, Braak and Braak 1993, Mugnaini and Floris 1994, Mugnaini et al 1997, 2011). It has a soma 9–12 μm in diameter and a single short dendrite ending in a tight brush-like tip of dendrioles that have extensive synaptic contact with mossy fibre rosettes (Fig. 29.15B). Its thin axon branches locally and synapses on granule and Golgi cells. The unipolar brush cell is thought to amplify vestibular mossy fibre input and provide feedforward excitation to granule cells.
Synaptic contacts between climbing fibres and the dendrites or cell bodies of cerebellar interneurones in the molecular or granular layers have not been observed. However, interneurones of the molecular layer can be activated by ‘spillover’ of glutamate from the climbing fibres (Szapiro and Barbour 2007, Galliano et al 2013). Golgi cells, therefore, provide feedback inhibition to the granule cells. Interneurones of the molecular layer provide feedforward inhibition to the Purkinje cells. Stellate cells (Mann-Metzer and Yarom 2000) and Golgi cells (Dugué et al 2009) are electrotonically coupled, although the extent of this coupling is not known. It may be restricted to the sagittal compartments that are one of the main features of the connectivity of the cerebellum, discussed below.
Neuroglial cells in the cerebellar cortex are specialized forms of protoplasmic astrocytes that envelop the somata of the Purkinje cells in a neuroglial sheath. Bergmann glial cells in the Purkinje cell layer give rise to two or more processes that branch in the parasagittal plane with up to five polarized main processes, the Bergmann fibres, giving the appearance of a candelabra (Fig. 29.16). These radial fibres ascend vertically over the full depth of the molecular layer, overlapping with fibres of neighbouring cells to form palisades and giving rise to small convoluted side branches that form sites of putative neuronal–glial interaction. The Bergmann glial fibres terminate beneath the pia as the external glial limiting membrane of the cortex. Bergmann glial cells are intricately involved in migration and development of the cerebellar cortex (Rakic 1971) (Ch. 3). In the mature cerebellum they regulate glutamatergic neural transmission and are important for extracellular ion homeostasis, synapse stability, plasticity, metabolic function and neuroprotection. They also contribute to information processing in the cerebellum (see De Zeeuw and Hoogland 2015 for review).
Fañanas cells are glial cells in the deeper molecular layer. Velate astrocytes have intersecting diaphanous veils that compartmentalize the granular layer. Rare smooth protoplasmic astrocytes are present in the molecular as well as the granular layers (Palay and Chan-Palay 1974). Oligodendroglia are seen mostly in the granular layer where there are more myelinated fibres, and in the cerebellar white matter. Glial cells play a role in supporting neuronal metabolism, controlling the extracellular potassium ion concentration, and clearing neurotransmitters from the extracellular space.

Cerebellar inputs

The neurones of the cerebellar cortex and nuclei are linked together in multiple repeating anatomical microcircuits (corticonuclear microcomplexes) that subserve the distinctive functional characteristics of the cerebellum. There are two major cerebellar inputs, the climbing fibre and mossy fibre systems. Monoaminergic fibres are a third contingent of afferent fibres, but they are less influential in determining the organization of the intracortical and corticonuclear connectivity.

Mossy fibres

The thick, heavily myelinated mossy fibres were so named by Ramon y Cajal because the ‘thickened ends of the mossy fibres are indented, or, more accurately, bristle with thick, short, divergent, varicose branches that are themselves modestly ramified. The ending as a whole resembles a piece of moss’ (Ramon y Cajal 1909–10/1995, Fig. 29.17). Mossy fibres enter through the three cerebellar peduncles depending on their site of origin. Most (up to 70%) run a transverse course to decussate in the cerebellar commissure, terminating bilaterally in the cerebellum. Like the climbing fibres, mossy fibres branch profusely, emitting 20–30 thin collateral branches that enter the white matter of the lobules and terminate in multiple, longitudinally orientated and symmetrically distributed aggregates of mossy fibre terminals in the granular layer (Wu et al 1999).
The terminal or synaptic arborizations of the mossy fibres, termed rosettes, are located along the course of the fibre in the granular layer, at the branch points, and at their sites of termination (Fig. 29.18). There are three kinds of mossy fibre rosettes. The most common type is an elongated cone that expands, emitting tubular and filamentous processes, and is found at the mossy fibre terminal in the outer third of the granular layer just beneath the Purkinje cell layer. Simple fusiform enlargements have globular contours and a few short, finger-like projections. Terminations with more complex shape have a massive central expansion out of which several appendages project.
Mossy fibres provide excitatory, glutamatergic afferents to the cerebellar cortex from most non-olivary sources. Their excitatory input is not to the Purkinje cell directly, but to the granule cells that provide excitatory input to the Purkinje cell dendrites. Mossy fibres activate α-amino-3-hydroxy-5-methyl-4-isoxazoleproprionate (AMPA), N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) and metabotropic glutamate receptors (mGluRs) on granule cells. The mossy fibre rosette is the central component of the granule cell glomerulus. Seen on light microscopy as pale islands between the granule cells, the glomerulus is a complex articulation between mossy fibre rosettes, the claw-like dendrites of the granule cells and Golgi cell axons. Each mossy fibre rosette makes excitatory synaptic contact with 50–100 dendritic terminals from up to 20 granule cells, while receiving inhibitory feedback from the descending axons of Golgi cells. Each mossy fibre branch synapses with 400 or more granule cells in one folium. If branches to adjacent folia are included, the number is probably several thousand. Conversely, each granule cell receives synapses from four or five different mossy fibre terminals. Collaterals to the deep cerebellar nuclei are derived from mossy fibres arising in the reticulotegmental nucleus, lateral reticular nucleus (LRN), and the spinocerebellar and vestibulocerebellar projections.
Cholecystokinin (CCK), calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP), corticotropin releasing factor (CRF) and leucine-5-enkephalin neuropeptides co-exist in many mossy fibres and some, mainly vestibulocerebellar mossy fibres, use acetylcholine as their neurotransmitter.

Climbing fibres

Climbing fibres arise exclusively from neurones of the inferior olive in the medulla oblongata and course to the cerebellum through the contralateral inferior cerebellar peduncle. Most olivocerebellar projections are contralateral, although a few axons project bilaterally by crossing the midline within the cerebellum. The parent climbing fibre splits into 7–10 thick branches in the cerebellar white matter, emitting thin collaterals to the deep cerebellar nuclei, before passing through the granular layer on their way to the molecular layer (Fig. 29.19). In the lower two-thirds of the molecular layer each branch of a climbing fibre lies closely opposed to the trunk and major branches of the dendritic tree of its target Purkinje cell, providing extensive direct excitatory glutamatergic synaptic contact with the stubby spines on the proximal dendrites of a single Purkinje cell in a one-to-one relationship (Fig. 29.20; see Fig. 29.12F). Each varicosity of the climbing fibre synapses with several dendritic spines, enabling 1000–1500 synaptic contacts between a climbing fibre and its Purkinje cell. The fine tendrils that branch off from the climbing fibres in the molecular layer make non-synaptic contact with ascending branches of the basket cell axons that enter the molecular layer, and with the dendritic trees of the stellate cells and the dendrites and somata of Golgi cells (Palay and Chan-Palay 1974, Sugihara et al 1999). Electrotonic coupling through gap junctions between the neurones of the inferior olive generates synchronous subthreshold oscillations (e.g. Leznik and Llinas 2005). Climbing fibres thus exert control over inhibition at both the input and output layers of the cerebellar cortex, regulating Golgi cell and stellate cell activity by releasing glutamate and activating ionotropic and metabotropic receptors through spillover-mediated transmission (Szapiro and Barbour 2007, Nietz et al 2017).
Climbing fibres use glutamate as their principal neurotransmitter, but there is chemical heterogeneity in the climbing fibre system: CCK is expressed in vermal lobules IV, VII and VIII, and in axons arising in the caudal part of the medial accessory olive. Enkephalin reactivity is present in the vermis, paramedian lobule and flocculus.
The olivocerebellar projection has a strict medio-lateral parasagittal zonal organization. The branches of a single olivocerebellar fibre innervate one or more sagittally orientated strips of Purkinje cells (Sugihara et al 2001) (Fig. 29.21; see Fig. 29.19). These strips correspond to microzones consisting of a narrow strip of Purkinje cells innervated by climbing fibres that share the same receptive field. Microzones with their Purkinje cells are considered to be the basic structural and functional unit of the cerebellar cortex (Andersson and Oscarsson 1978).

Monoaminergic inputs

Monoaminergic afferents to the cerebellum modulate the release of other neurotransmitters. Noradrenergic innervation arises from neurones in the caudal portion of the locus coeruleus as axonal collaterals of projections to the forebrain. These fibres synapse on granule cell dendrites and run adjacent to the parallel fibres to terminate on the Purkinje cell dendrites. Serotoninergic afferents to the cerebellum arise from the raphe nuclei and are present throughout all regions of the cerebellum. Histaminergic fibres arise from neurones in the tuberomammillary nucleus of the posterior hypothalamus and innervate most regions of the cerebellum. Dopaminergic inputs arise from the neurones of the midbrain including the ventral tegmental area. Cholinergic projections are present diffusely, particularly in the flocculonodular lobe and in lobules I and II, terminating on excitatory unipolar brush cells and on inhibitory interneurones.

Overview of cortical circuitry

The granule cell axon ascends in the cerebellar cortex through the dense granule cell layer and splits into two branches that travel in opposite directions along the long axis of a cerebellar folium, perpendicular to the ascending axon, and form the parallel fibres. The length of each parallel fibre branch is between 1 and 3 mm in the rat and cat, longer in nonhuman primates (6–8 mm) and probably no more than 10  mm in humans. Terminals located along the parallel fibre give it a beaded or varicose appearance; these terminals are the sites of synapses between the parallel fibres and the distal dendritic trees of Purkinje cells and the dendrites and somata of the stellate, basket and Golgi cells that they encounter along their course (Fig. 29.22). There is enormous convergence of mossy fibre inputs to individual Purkinje neurones; a dendritic tree of one Purkinje cell receives an estimated total of 175,000 synapses from different parallel fibres in the rat (Napper and Harvey 1988), although every parallel fibre may not synapse with the dendritic tree which it crosses. On its way to the distal arbors of a Purkinje cell dendritic tree, and before dividing to form the parallel fibres, the ascending axon of a granule cell makes synaptic contact first with dendrites of Golgi cells in the granular layer, and then with the spines of the proximal dendrites of Purkinje cells. Approximately 20% of the granule cell synapses on a Purkinje cell are made by the ascending segment of the granule cell axon, facilitating a more restricted influence on a single Purkinje cell than the parallel fibre–Purkinje cell synapses would allow.
A Purkinje cell generates two very different classes of action potentials mediated by its two principal afferents. Climbing fibre input to a Purkinje cell induces a complex spike with a very low frequency of 0.5 to 2 spikes per second, which is the same rate of firing as the olivary neurones from which the climbing fibre originates. The climbing fibre input to a Purkinje cell is thought to signal the occurrence of errors. In contrast, the mossy fibre input to a Purkinje cell induces a repetitive simple spike with a frequency between 50 and 150 spikes per second. The parallel fibre input is thought to provide information about incoming signals, such as direction and speed of limb movement: in the cognitive domain, this input may provide a Purkinje cell with the context in which specific behaviours occur.
These circuits are relevant to synaptic plasticity involved in learning and memory. Long-term depression (LTD) is characterized by the persistent depression of synaptic transmission from parallel fibres to the Purkinje cell that occurs when the parallel fibres are activated concurrently with the activation of climbing fibres to the same Purkinje cell. Long-term depression has also been noted in the climbing fibre–Purkinje cell synapse. Long-term potentiation (LTP) occurs in the mossy fibre–granule cell interaction (D’Angelo et al 2005) and at the parallel fibre–Purkinje cell synapse (Hansel et al 2001).
Purkinje cells receive inhibitory feedback from cortical interneurones (stellate, basket and Lugaro cells), all of which receive afferents from the parallel fibres and the recurrent collaterals of a Purkinje cell axon. The output from the cerebellar cortex is derived exclusively from the Purkinje cells, precisely organized, and directed towards the deep cerebellar nuclei and precerebellar nuclei. Purkinje cells in each lobule project to that part of the deep cerebellar nuclei that is closest to them. Thus, the vermis projects to the fastigial nucleus, the intermediate cortex to the globose and emboliform nuclei, and much of the lateral hemisphere projects to the dentate nucleus.
Climbing fibre microzones and the subjacent mossy fibre aggregates share the same receptive fields in regions of the cerebellum that receive somatosensory information from the periphery (Ekerot and Larson 1980, Ekerot and Jörntell 2003). A similar topographical relationship between microzones and mossy fibre terminal aggregates exists in other parts of the cerebellar cortex (Pijpers et al 2006). Parallel fibres disperse a localized mossy fibre input over a wide, mediolateral region of the molecular layer, and the localized functional topography is thought to be related, at least in part, to the more topographically focal synaptic inputs of the ascending axon of the granule cell (Cohen and Yarom 1998).

Cerebellar Functional Topography and Connectivity

Whereas cerebellar cortical architecture is essentially invariant throughout, the topographically arranged circuits that link the cerebellum with the spinal cord, brainstem and cerebral hemispheres enable the cerebellum to contribute to the many different aspects of nervous system function. This arrangement is fundamental to the idea that the architecture of a brain region determines the nature of the information processing that takes place in that region (Schmahmann and Pandya 2008), and the principle expressed in the notion of the universal cerebellar transform (UCT) (Schmahmann 2000), which is that the cerebellum performs the same computation for sensorimotor processing as it does for cognition, emotion, autonomic and vestibular functions. Superimposed upon this principle is the presence of highly organized and functionally specific heterogeneity of cerebellar connections with the spinal cord, brainstem and cerebral cortex (Brodal 1981, Ito 1984, Schmahmann 1996, 1997, 2000, 2001, Schmahmann and Pandya 1997a, Voogd and Glickstein 1998, Voogd 2004).
On a large scale, cerebellar functional topography distinguishes anterior and posterior cerebellar lobes. The cerebellar anterior lobe (lobules I to V) and parts of lobule VI, together with lobule VIII receive spinal afferents through the spinocerebellar tracts (Oscarsson 1965). They are also reciprocally interconnected with the motor cortices via the feedforward motor corticopontine projections (Brodal 1978, Hartmann-von Monakow et al 1981, Schmahmann et al 2004a) and through feedback to motor regions from cerebellar nuclei via the thalamus (Thach 1987, Kelly and Strick 2003). These cerebellar regions (i.e. anterior lobe, adjacent parts of lobule VI and lobule VIII) are also reciprocally linked with the medial and dorsal accessory nuclei of the inferior olivary complex, which in turn receive afferents from the spinal cord (Brodal 1981, Voogd 2004). The deep cerebellar nuclei that are linked with these cerebellar cortical areas are the anterior and posterior interpositus in the monkey (equivalent to the emboliform and globose nuclei in humans) and the dorsal part of the dentate nucleus. The cerebellar vermis and fastigial nucleus are interconnected with the vestibular and other brainstem nuclei engaged in midline body control, gait and equilibrium, as well as with brainstem nuclei linked with limbic and paralimbic cortical and subcortical regions (Schmahmann 2001).
In contrast to the connections of the anterior lobe and lobule VIII, the great expansion of the human cerebellum, i.e. the remainder of lobule VI, all of lobule VII (that includes lobule VIIA in the vermis and crus I and crus II in the hemispheres, lobule VIIB and most of lobule IX) has no connections with the cerebral cortical sensorimotor areas, and no spinal cord inputs from the spinocerebellar tracts (Brodal 1981, Voogd 2004). These areas are linked instead in a reciprocal feedforward and feedback manner with the association areas of the cerebral cortex concerned with higher order behaviour, namely, the prefrontal cortex, posterior parietal cortex, superior temporal polymodal regions, cingulate gyrus and posterior parahippocampal area (Schmahmann 1991, 1996, Schmahmann and Pandya 1989, 1991, 1992, 1993, 1997a, 1997b, Kelly and Strick 2003). The dentate nucleus of the cerebellum conveys the cerebellar posterior lobe efferents to these higher order regions of the cerebral cortex (Middleton and Strick 1994). The ventral and lateral parts of the dentate nucleus most involved in these projections (Dum and Strick 2003) have expanded through evolution together with the cerebellar posterior lobe and the cerebral cortical association areas (Dow 1974, Leiner et al 1986, Whiting and Barton 2003). The reciprocal interconnections between the inferior olivary nucleus and the cerebellar posterior lobe are distinct from those with the cerebellar motor regions, which are derived from the principal olivary nucleus, which has minimal or no spinal cord input (Sugihara and Shinoda 2004).
These anatomical connections set up a dichotomy of cerebellar linkage with the cerebral cortex, brainstem and spinal cord, and portray a complex connectional and functional heterogeneity. The sensorimotor cerebellum mostly in the anterior lobe, adjacent parts of lobule VI, and lobule VIII, appears to be an integral part of the distributed neural circuits that subserve the motor system, whereas the cognitive cerebellum in the posterior lobe, and the limbic cerebellum (likely represented principally in the vermis), are incorporated into the distributed neural circuits necessary for cognition and emotion.
On a smaller scale, the cerebellum is organized as a series of parallel, sagittally orientated modules (Voogd and Bigaré 1980, Voogd and Ruigrok 2012), each consisting of one or more Purkinje cell zones. From medial to lateral, zones A, B, X, C1, C2, C3, D1, D2 and Y can be distinguished. Climbing fibres from a subdivision of the contralateral inferior olive terminate on the Purkinje cells of a particular zone and also send a collateral innervation to the corresponding deep cerebellar nucleus (Groenewegen et al 1979). This collateral innervation is reciprocated by the, mainly crossed, nucleo-olivary pathway that originates from the small GABAergic neurones of the cerebellar nuclei (see Figs 29.19, 29.21). The outflow from the cerebellar cortex is directed to similarly topographically arranged regions in the fastigial, globose, emboliform and dentate nuclei. Efferents from the cerebellum to extracerebellar structures are conveyed exclusively through these nuclei, with the single exception of the cortex of the vestibulocerebellum (lobule X – flocculus and nodulus, and the vermal part of lobule IX – uvula), which projects directly to the vestibular nuclei. Cerebellar corticonuclear circuitry and feedback to the spinal cord, brainstem and cerebral hemispheres are thus arranged in a topographically precise manner respecting the zonal organization of the cerebellar cortex and its related deep cerebellar nuclei and inferior olivary complex.
The modular organization of the cerebellum appears very early during its development, before the emergence of its transverse fissures. Purkinje cells, born in the ventricular matrix of the cerebellar anlage, migrate to the meningeal surface, where they form a series of mediolaterally arranged clusters (Korneliussen 1968). During the later expansion of the cerebellar surface in the rostrocaudal dimension, reflecting the proliferation of millions of granule cells in the transient external matrix (the external granular layer), the Purkinje cell clusters increase in length and transform into Purkinje cell zones. The Purkinje cells become located in a monolayer and the original borders between the clusters are no longer visible. Purkinje cell clustering in the human brain does not differ from that in other species, with the exception of the immense size of the most lateral cluster, which is related to the anlage of the dentate nucleus (Fig. 29.23). This cluster develops into the D2 zone, the most lateral Purkinje cell zone, and is responsible for the large size of the human cerebellar hemisphere.
Cerebellar modules can be visualized because their Purkinje cell axons and climbing fibre afferents collect in compartments in the cerebellar white matter and are visible when stained for acetylcholine esterase (AChE) (see Fig. 29.21B). Some of the Purkinje cell zones are restricted to certain lobules; others span the entire rostrocaudal length of the cerebellum. Eight or nine of the modules can be recognized in the cerebellum of nonhuman primates and lower mammals (see Fig. 29.36). Purkinje cell zones differ in their climbing fibre afferents and cerebellar or vestibular target nucleus. Purkinje cells of the different zones also differ in their immunohistochemical properties (Voogd and Ruigrok 2012). A Purkinje cell-specific antibody, known as zebrin 2, is distributed in a pattern of zebrin-positive and zebrin-negative Purkinje cell zones (see Fig. 29.36D–E). This pattern has been shown to be congruent with the olivocerebellar and corticonuclear projection zones (Voogd et al 2003, Sugihara and Shinoda 2004). Many substances, such as the enzymes aldolase C, 5′ nucleotidase, protein kinase C and the metabotropic glutamate transporter 1A, colocalize with zebrin 2. Neurotransmission in different Purkinje cell zones may therefore differ: zebrin-positive Purkinje cells fire at a slower rate than the zebrin-negative cells (Zhou et al 2014). Apart from the parallel fibres, which may cross several Purkinje cell zones or microzones as they course through the molecular layer, there is no crosstalk between the modules. Parallel fibres are a key element in the integrative function of the cerebellum, but the relative independence of the cerebellar modules is an important difference between the cerebellum and the cerebral cortex, where different functional areas are interconnected through association fibres.

Afferent cerebellar fibres

Mossy fibre inputs to the cerebellum

Mossy fibre systems take their origin from multiple sites in the spinal cord and the brainstem, distribute bilaterally in the cerebellum and give off collaterals distributed in the mediolateral dimension that terminate as longitudinal aggregates of mossy fibre rosettes (see Fig. 29.18; Fig. 29.24A). These bands of rosettes are not continuous but are often restricted to either the apices or the bases of the folia. Exteroceptive components of mossy fibre systems terminate superficially, whereas proprioceptive systems terminate in the bases of the folia (Ekerot and Larson 1972) (Fig. 29.24B). The mossy fibre aggregates are not as distinct as the climbing fibre zones and often merge in the bases of the fissures. Mossy fibre aggregates of different systems interdigitate or overlap.
Spinal and trigeminocerebellar mossy fibre afferents
The spinal cord is connected with the cerebellum through spino-cerebellar tracts and through indirect mossy fibre pathways relayed by the lateral reticular nucleus. The spinocerebellar tracts are classified on the basis of their origin, their decussation within the spinal cord, their position in the lateral funiculus, their entrance route into the cerebellum and their lobular and zonal distribution.
The termination of the spinocerebellar, reticulocerebellar, cuneocerebellar and trigeminocerebellar tracts is restricted to the anterior and posterior motor regions of the cerebellum, i.e. to lobules III–V in the anterior lobe, lobule VI, parts of lobule VIIB and lobule VIII. These lobules also receive primary and secondary vestibulocerebellar inputs and pontocerebellar mossy fibres relaying information from cortical motor areas. Many of these mossy fibre systems terminate according to a somatotopical pattern (Fig. 29.24C). A very similar somatotopical organization occurs in the C1, C3 and Y climbing fibre zones that are restricted to the hemisphere of the same lobules (see Fig. 29.24).
The dorsal spinocerebellar tract transmits information from the ipsilateral lower limb. It contains proprioceptive fibres that arise from neurones in the posterior thoracic nucleus (Clarke’s column) in the thoracic and upper lumbar spinal cord, and exteroceptive fibres from the thoracic and lumbar dorsal horns. It enters the cerebellum in the inferior cerebellar peduncle and terminates mainly ipsilaterally in the vermis and hemisphere of the anterior and posterior lower limb regions.
The cuneocerebellar tract is the upper limb equivalent of the dorsal spinocerebellar tract. It takes its origin from the dorsal column nuclei, the proprioceptive component from the external cuneate nucleus, and the exteroceptive component from the internal cuneate and gracile nuclei. Both components terminate in the anterior and posterior upper limb regions, the proprioceptive component bilaterally in the bases of the fissures and the exteroceptive component ipsilaterally in the apices of the lobules of the hemisphere. The exteroceptive component has been shown to terminate in multiple longitudinal zones that are congruent with the climbing fibre zones of this region; these zones share the same detailed somatotopical organization as the C1, C3 and the Y climbing fibre zones (Ekerot and Larson 1980).
The ventral spinocerebellar tract is a composite pathway that informs the cerebellum about the state of activity of spinal reflex arcs related to the lower limb and lower trunk. Its fibres originate in the intermediate grey matter and the spinal border cells of the lumbar and sacral segments of the spinal cord, cross near their origin, and ascend close to the surface as far as the lower midbrain before looping around the emergent fibres of the trigeminal nerve to join the superior cerebellar peduncle. Most of these fibres cross again in the cerebellar white matter.
The rostral spinocerebellar tract originates from cell groups of the intermediate zone and horn of the contralateral cervical enlargement. It is considered to be the upper limb and upper trunk equivalent of the ventral spinocerebellar tract, but unlike its ventral counterpart, most of its fibres remain ipsilateral throughout their course and enter the cerebellum through both the superior and the inferior cerebellar peduncles terminating in the anterior and posterior vermis.
The central cervical spinocerebellar tract originates from a central cervical nucleus at high cervical levels (C1–C4). The tract terminates bilaterally in the bases of the fissures of the entire cerebellum, lacks a somatotopical organization and conveys labyrinthine information and proprioception from neck muscles (Matsushita and Tanami 1987).
Trigeminocerebellar mossy fibres originate from the ipsilateral principal sensory nucleus and nucleus of the spinal tract of the trigeminal nerve and terminate in the hemisphere in the anterior and posterior face regions in lobules VI and VIIB.
The distinct somatotopic organization of the anterior and posterior motor regions of the hemispheres is reflected in the termination of the exteroceptive components of the dorsal spinocerebellar, cuneocerebellar and trigeminocerebellar tracts. The organization is much less distinct for proprioceptive systems such as the central cervical spinocerebellar tract.
Reticulocerebellar mossy fibre afferents
In the reticular formation, the paramedian reticular nucleus, the lateral reticular nucleus and the reticulotegmental nucleus (nucleus reticularis tegmenti pontis, NRTP) all give rise to mossy fibres. Major collateral projections to the cerebellar nuclei arise from the lateral reticular nucleus and the reticulotegmental nucleus.
The paramedian reticular nucleus consists of cell groups at the lateral border of the medial longitudinal fasciculus in the caudal medulla. It receives fibres from the vestibular nuclei, the interstitiospinal and tectospinal tracts that descend in the medial longitudinal fasciculus, and from the spinal cord and the cerebral cortex, and projects to the entire cerebellum, with the possible exception of the paraflocculus.
The lateral reticular nucleus is in the lateral funiculus of the caudal medulla, surrounded by the ascending fibre systems of the lateral funiculus and the rubrospinal and lateral vestibulospinal tracts. It receives spinoreticular fibres that terminate in a somatotopic pattern within the entire lateral reticular nucleus, and descending fibres from the cerebral cortex. The dorsal part of the lateral reticular nucleus receives collaterals from the rubrospinal tract and projects to the ipsilateral cerebellar hemisphere. The ventral part of the lateral reticular nucleus receives a collateral projection from the lateral vestibulospinal tract and projects bilaterally, mainly to the vermis. The lateral reticular nucleus has strong projections as axon collaterals to the rostral part of the fastigial and emboliform nuclei and the medial pole of the globose nucleus (Wu et al 1999).
The reticulotegmental nucleus is a butterfly-shaped nucleus in the caudal half of the pontine tegmentum, next to the midline and dorsal to the pontine nuclei. It receives inputs from the cerebral cortex, tectum, nucleus of the optic tract and cerebellar nuclei via the crossed descending branch of the superior cerebellar peduncle and it projects to the cerebellum via the middle cerebellar peduncle. The medial visuomotor division of this nucleus receives visuomotor afferents from the frontal eye fields, the contralateral superior colliculus (through the tectopontine tract) and other visuomotor centres in the brainstem, and targets the visual vermis in lobule VII, crus I, the flocculus and the adjacent ventral paraflocculus. The bilateral projection of its lateral, motor portion overlaps similar motor projections from the pontine nuclei in the anterior lobe, lobule VI and lobule VIII. Mossy fibres from the reticular nucleus of the pontine tegmentum provide the cerebellar nuclei with a collateral innervation complementary to that of the lateral reticular nucleus. An uncrossed component of the tectopontine tract terminates in the dorsolateral corner of the pontine nuclei, where it overlaps with extrastriatal visual afferents.
Primary and secondary vestibulocerebellar mossy fibres
Primary vestibulocerebellar mossy fibres to the cerebellum are derived from vestibular ganglion cells that travel in the ascending branch of the vestibular nerve. They pass through the superior vestibular nucleus and the juxtarestiform body before entering the cerebellum and project directly to the ipsilateral vermis in the ventral part of lobule IX (uvula) and lobule X (nodulus) and to the ventral part of the anterior lobe and bases of the deep fissures of the vermis (Fig. 29.25). The flocculus (hemisphere lobule X) receives few, if any, vestibular fibres. Secondary vestibulocerebellar mossy fibres arise from the superior vestibular nucleus, the caudal portions of the medial and inferior vestibular nuclei, and the group X neurones, and terminate bilaterally in the same regions that receive primary vestibulocerebellar fibres with the exception of vermal lobule X (nodulus), and in hemisphere X (flocculus) and the adjacent ventral paraflocculus (the accessory paraflocculus of the human cerebellum) that lack a primary vestibulocerebellar projection. Some of the mossy fibres from the medial and inferior vestibular nuclei are cholinergic.
Corticopontocerebellar projections
The cerebral cortex is linked with the cerebellum in a two-stage feed-forward limb of cerebral cortical projections to the nuclei of the basis pontis (corticopontine projection), and the mossy fibre projection from the pontine nuclei through the middle cerebellar peduncle to the contralateral cerebellum (pontocerebellar projection). The reciprocal feedback pathway from the cerebellum to the cortex is mediated by the thalamus via cerebellothalamic and thalamocortical projections.
All corticopontine axons arise from lamina V pyramidal cells (Figs 29.26–29.27). Some corticopontine fibres are collaterals of axons that project to other targets in the brain or spinal cord: in the motor system it is likely that corticospinal fibres give off collaterals to the pontine nuclei (Ugolini and Kuypers 1986).
Tract tracing studies of white matter pathways in the monkey reveal that axons destined for the pons emanate from a given cortical area in a cord of fibres together with commissural fibres before separating as a distinct subcortical bundle. This subcortical bundle courses in the anterior limb of the internal capsule from prefrontal regions, or the sagittal stratum and/or posterior limb of the internal capsule from posterior regions (see Fig. 29.26). As thalamic fibres separate off from the subcortical bundle, the pontine fibres continue towards the cerebral peduncle. There is a common region through which the corticopontine fibres lead to the basis pontis, that is situated initially in the white matter above the mid-point of the lateral geniculate nucleus and then at the medial aspect of the lateral geniculate nucleus (see Fig. 29.27). These descending fibres demonstrate a certain degree of topographical organization, indicating that the corticopontine system consists of segregated and partially overlapping pathways that are, to some extent, distinguishable anatomically at each stage of their trajectory from origin to destination.
As the corticopontine fibres descend in the cerebral peduncle, they are topographically arranged such that those from prefrontal regions are situated most medially; those from premotor and then motor cortices occupy the middle third of the peduncle, and fibres from the parietal, temporal and occipital regions that are destined for the pons are in the lateral third of the cerebral peduncle. The relative area of the cerebral peduncle that contains fibres derived from the association cortices is larger in humans than in monkeys (as evidenced in comparative anatomy and tractography studies for the prefrontal cortex in particular (Ramnani et al 2006)) (Fig. 29.28), which appears to reflect the phenomenon of evolutionary pressure on interrelated cerebrocerebellar systems.
Within the pons, a given cortical area projects to multiple patches of pontine neurones distributed within both transverse and rostrocaudal dimensions. Some projections are arranged in rostrocaudal columns that further branch and coalesce. Each cerebral area commits fibres to a predictable trajectory in the cerebral white matter and cerebral peduncle, which leads to a unique mosaic of patches of terminations in precisely arranged regions of the basilar pons.
The motor projections are topographically organized, preferentially in the caudal half of the pons, lying close to the traversing corticofugal fibres (Fig. 29.29). In nuclei that receive multiple inputs, the terminations appear to interdigitate. Projections from the face region of the supplementary motor area (SMA) are most medial and include the median pontine nucleus. Projections from the ventral precentral gyrus (M1 face representation) are also medial, but lateral to those from SMA-face. M1 hand projections are in medially placed curved lamellae in the mid and caudal pons. Dorsal trunk projections are in medial and ventral locations, ventral trunk/hip projections encircle the peduncle in the caudal pons, and projections from the foot are heaviest caudally in laterally placed curved lamellae (Schmahmann et al 2004a).
Motor topography is also apparent in the human pons (Fig. 29.30). A topographical map in the human as determined by structure–function correlations in patients with stroke reveals that facial movement and articulation are localized to the rostral and medial basilar pons; hand coordination and dexterity are medial and ventral in rostral and midpons; arm coordination is represented ventral and lateral to the hand; leg coordination is in the caudal half of the pons, with lateral predominance. Gait is represented in medial and lateral locations throughout the rostral–caudal extent of the pons (Schmahmann et al 2004b).
The nuclei of the basis pontis pons receive prominent projections from unimodal and multimodal cerebral cortical association areas (Fig. 29.31). These occupy largely separate areas of the basis pontis in the rostrocaudal and transverse dimensions. The enlargement of the basis pontis along with the lateral cerebellar hemispheres and the ventrolateral dentate nucleus is in accord with the view that the major expansion in the cerebrocerebellar pathways reflect cerebellar linkage with the cerebral association areas rather than with the motor system.
In monkeys, prefrontal projections to the pons arise mostly in dorsolateral and dorsomedial prefrontal cortices and terminate in the rostral third of the medial pons. Posterior parietal projections arise from both gyral and sulcal cortices, including the multimodal caudal regions. Inferior parietal lobule projections to the pons are predominantly rostral, whereas those from the superior parietal lobule are seen throughout the rostrocaudal extent of the pons. These parietal projections are mostly in the intermediate and lateral sectors of the pons, with a degree of topographical organization determined by their site of origin. Temporal lobe projections arise from the multimodal regions of the cortex of the upper bank of the superior temporal sulcus, as well as from the superior temporal gyrus and supratemporal plane, and terminate in the lateral and dorsolateral pontine regions. Parastriate cortices at the dorsomedial and dorsolateral convexity, as well as the posterior parahippocampal gyrus, project to the lateral and dorsolateral pontine regions in a pattern that interdigitates with the other terminations in this area. The rostral cingulate cortex projects to medial pontine nuclei and the caudal cingulate cortex projects to more lateral regions. Pontine projections have also been shown in experimental retrograde studies to arise from the anterior insular cortex.
These connections provide the cerebellum with access to higher order information, enabling it to modulate cognitive and emotional processing.
There is a dichotomy in the pattern of corticopontine projections according to the dorsal (‘where’ pathway) versus ventral streams (‘what’ pathway) of cognitive processing. Dorsal stream areas concerned with visual motion and the peripheral visual field (the dorsal part of the prelunate gyrus, caudal lower bank of the cortex in the superior temporal sulcus, polymodal convergence zones in the lower bank of the cortex in the intraparietal sulcus, and paralimbic parts of the caudal inferior parietal lobule, as well as dorsolateral and dorsomedial prefrontal cortices concerned with spatial features of working memory) have projections to the pons. In contrast, in anatomical studies in monkeys, ventral stream cortices concerned with visual feature discrimination and the central visual field (ventral prelunate gyrus, rostral lower bank of the superior temporal sulcus, middle and inferior temporal gyri, lateral aspects of the posterior parahippocampal gyrus, and ventral prefrontal and orbitofrontal cortices with which these areas are interconnected) do not send projections into the basis pontis. These connections in monkeys suggested that the cerebellum plays a role in spatial awareness, and in attentional, auditory and linguistic functions subserved by the dorsal stream of cognitive processing. However, human imaging studies point to connections with medial and orbital prefrontal cortices, so there may be species differences in the corticopontocerebellar linkage and cerebellar engagement in functions subserved by the ventral stream.
Apart from the cortical projection to the basis pontis, there are also pontine inputs from the superior colliculus to the dorsolateral pons and to the reticular nucleus of the pontine tegmentum. The tectocerebellar pathway thus uses the pontine nuclei and the reticular nucleus of the pontine tegmentum as the link in its projection to the vermal visual area. Pontine inputs are also derived from the medial mammillary bodies.
The pontocerebellar projection is almost completely crossed. Projection fibres arise from the pontine neurones, course horizontally toward the midline in a focused aggregate, and fan out in multiple directions to course in the transverse pontocerebellar fibre bundles dispersed throughout much of the contralateral hemipons at the same rostrocaudal level as the site of origin. They then regroup to form a concentrated collection before coursing in the middle cerebellar peduncle to enter the cerebellum (Schmahmann et al 2004c) (Fig. 29.32).
The general principle of the rostrocaudal reversal in the pontocerebellar projection through the middle cerebellar peduncle was established by von Bechterew (1885) based on myelin stains in developing brain. The caudal pons (more heavily motor) is linked with cerebellar anterior and medial areas, whereas the rostral pons (more heavily associative) is linked with the lateral and caudal cerebellum (see Fig. 29.7B). Divergence and convergence in the corticopontine projection is further complicated by a similar arrangement in the pontocerebellar pathways. Pontine regions project to multiple cerebellar cortical areas; cerebellar areas receive input from a unique complement of neurones distributed throughout multiple pontine nuclei (Fig. 29.33). Cerebellar hemispheres receive a relatively greater pontine input than the vermis.
In monkeys, medial parts of the caudal pons project mainly to lobule V in the anterior lobe. The intrapeduncular nucleus projects to vermal lobule VIIIB, the paramedian lobule. These cerebellar regions contain sensorimotor representations of the upper and lower extremities. The dorsolateral pontine region and the reticular nucleus of the pontine tegmentum project to the dorsal paraflocculus, uvula and the vermal visual area. Neurones in the dorsomedial nucleus also project to the vermal visual area. Medial parts of the rostral pons project to crus I, and neurones in the medial, ventral and lateral pons send their axons to crus II.
Anatomical and physiological techniques in experimental animal models provide insights into the destination of cerebral cortical afferents in the cerebellum. The anterior lobe (particularly lobules IV and V) and the paramedian lobule (lobule VIII) receive afferents from pericentral motor and sensory cortices; crus I of the ansiform lobule receives inputs from premotor and prefrontal cortices; and crus II is linked with posterior parietal areas. One region of the cerebral cortex is distributed to numerous discrete sites in the cerebellar cortex, and conversely, multiple cerebral cortical areas contribute converging projections to common cerebellar areas. There are reciprocal loops of information processing between cerebral cortex and cerebellum: cerebral areas that project to discrete regions of cerebellum receive information back from those cerebellar areas (see Nieuwenhuys et al 2008, Voogd and Ruigrok 2012, Strick et al 2009, Schmahmann et al 2019 for reviews). See Fig. 29.34 for a simplified version of this projection (Glickstein et al 1985).

Climbing fibre afferents from the inferior olive

The subdivisions of the inferior olive, the sole source of climbing fibres, are summarized in Fig. 29.35. The three major nuclei are the principal olive and the medial and dorsal accessory olivary nuclei. The principal olive has a dorsal lamina, a bend and a ventral lamina with ventrolateral and ventromedial parts. The medial accessory olivary nucleus has rostral and caudal parts: its subnuclei, located dorsomedially, are the group beta (continuous medially with the dorsomedial cell column) and the dorsal cap of Kooy (continuous rostrally with the ventrolateral outgrowth and the ventral lamina of the principal olive). An intermediate portion of the medial accessory olivary nucleus is present in rat and monkey brains. The dorsal accessory olivary nucleus has rostral and caudal parts.
Connectional data for the inferior olivary nuclei, derived from experimental studies in the cat, rat and nonhuman primates, has defined different cerebellar zones (see Courville et al 1980, reviewed in Voogd and Ruigrok 2012). The A zone of the cerebellum is located next to the midline and extends over the entire vermis (Fig. 29.36A). It is composed of several zebrin-positive and zebrin-negative subzones that may be present over limited segments. Its climbing fibres are derived from the caudal medial accessory olive and it projects to the fastigial and vestibular nuclei. The X and B zones are restricted to the vermis of the anterior lobe and lobules VI and VIII. The X zone is narrow and separates the A zone from the B zone, which occupies the lateral vermis of these lobules. The X zone receives climbing fibres from the intermediate region of the medial accessory olive and projects to the interstitial cell groups located between the fastigial and posterior interposed nuclei. The B zone is innervated by climbing fibres from the caudal part of the dorsal accessory olive and projects to the lateral vestibular nucleus (Deiters’ nucleus). The dorsal accessory olive, the B zone and the lateral vestibular nucleus are somatotopically organized. In the B zone, the hindlimb is represented laterally and the forelimb is represented medially (Andersson and Oscarsson 1978). In rodents, Purkinje cells of the X and B zones are zebrin-negative.
The cerebellar hemisphere is composed of the C1–C3 and the D1, Y and D2 zones (see Fig. 29.36). Like the X and B zones, C1, C3 and the Y zone are restricted to the anterior lobe, the simplex lobule (HVI) and the paramedian lobule (HVIIB, the gracile lobule and HVIII, the biventral lobule). The Purkinje cells of these zones are zebrin-negative and thus appear as blank spaces in suitably immunostained histological sections of the anterior and posterior cerebellum (see Fig. 29.36E). The C1, C3 and Y zones project to the emboliform nucleus and receive their climbing fibre input from the rostral dorsal accessory olive. This subnucleus receives input from peripheral receptors through dorsal column and trigeminal pathways. The climbing fibre projections of the rostral dorsal accessory olive to the C1, C3 and Y zones and the emboliform nucleus are somatotopically organized. In each of the zones, the hindlimb is represented rostrally in the anterior cerebellum and caudally in the posterior lobe; the forelimb and face occupy more central areas (Ekerot and Larson 1979a, 1979b) (Fig. 29.37). This rostrocaudal distribution clearly differs from the mediolateral somatotopy in the vermal B zone. The somatotopical localization is extremely detailed, repeated in each of the zones. The C1, C3 and Y zones connect with motor centres in the brainstem and the cerebral cortex. The hemisphere of the anterior lobe, adjacent part of lobule VI, and lobule VIII are considered as the motor regions of the cerebellum. The C2, D1 and D2 zones extend beyond the anterior and posterior motor regions, where they interdigitate with the C1, C3 and Y zones over most of the rostrocaudal length of the cerebellum. In rodents, these zones are zebrin-positive.
The C2 zone receives its climbing fibre input from the rostral medial accessory olive and projects to the posterior interposed nucleus (Fig. 29.38). Somatotopical organization is lacking in the C2 zone. The D1 zone receives its climbing fibres from the ventral lamina of the principal olive and projects to the caudal dentate; the D2 zone receives its climbing fibres from the dorsal lamina of the principal olive and projects to the rostral dentate. The main connections of the C2 and the D zones are with the cerebral cortex. Sections of the C2 zone and the somatotopically organized part of the D2 zone located in the anterior and posterior motor regions of the cerebellar hemisphere are connected with motor, premotor and parietal cortical areas. Sections of the C2 and the D zones located in hemispheric lobules VII (lobule VIIA crus I, lobule VIIA crus II, and lobule VIIB) and lobule IX subserve visuomotor and a wide range of cognitive functions.
The modular organization of the vestibulocerebellum includes multiple Purkinje cell zones, innervated by climbing fibres from subnuclei in the inferior olive that transmit optokinetic and vestibular information. There are five Purkinje cell zones in the cortex of the flocculus and the adjacent ventral paraflocculus (Fig. 29.39; see Fig. 29.23).
In addition to the C2 zone located most medially in the flocculus and adjacent ventral paraflocculus, two pairs of zones occupy its lateral portion. Zones F1 and F3 connect with the oculomotor neurones in the vestibular nuclei that subserve the anterior canal vestibulo-ocular reflex (Fig. 29.40). The F2 and F4 zones connect with oculomotor neurones that subserve the horizontal canal vestibulo-ocular reflex. The flocculus and ventral paraflocculus receive vestibular mossy fibre input, relaying an efferent copy of the output of the vestibulo-oculomotor neurones. They also receive climbing fibre input, signalling retinal slip that occurs when the stabilization of the retina by the vestibulo-ocular reflex is incomplete. Retinal slip is perceived by two groups of neurones in the mesencephalon. In the horizontal plane, it is relayed by the nucleus of the optic tract. This nucleus, located in the pretectum, receives fibres of the contralateral optic nerve via the optic tract and projects to the dorsal cap of the inferior olive, located dorsomedial to the caudal medial accessory olive (see Fig. 29.35). The dorsal cap provides the F2 and F4 zones with climbing fibres. Retinal slip in the plane of the anterior canal is conveyed by the lateral and medial nuclei of the accessory system, part of a group of nuclei located on the periphery of the rostral mesencephalon that receive optic nerve fibres from the accessory optic tract (an offshoot of the optic nerve). These nuclei project to the ventrolateral outgrowth of the inferior olive, located immediately rostral to the dorsal cap (see Fig. 29.35). The ventrolateral outgrowth innervates the F1 and F3 zones. Repeated simultaneous activation of the vestibular mossy fibre-parallel fibre input and the climbing fibres, relaying retinal slip, induces plastic changes in the Purkinje cell output that compensates for the retinal slip. Combinations of the horizontal and anterior canal systems ensure compensation of retinal slip in all possible planes. Knowledge of this system has been instrumental in the concept that climbing fibres are carriers of error signals used in cerebellar learning (Marr 1969, Ito 1982).

Efferent connections of the cerebellar nuclei

The connections of the cerebellar nuclei with the brainstem, thalamus and spinal cord determine the sphere of influence of the cerebellar modules. The A, X, B and D zones project to the fastigial nucleus, the interstitial cell groups, the lateral vestibular nucleus and dentate nucleus respectively (see Fig. 29.36).

Fastigial nucleus

The uncinate tract is the major efferent pathway from the caudal part of the fastigial nucleus (see Fig. 29.39). Its fibres cross in the rostral part of the cerebellar commissure, pass dorsal to the superior cerebellar peduncle, enter the contralateral medial, superior and inferior vestibular nuclei laterally, traverse the nuclei and terminate in the medial reticular formation. Direct, uncrossed, fastigiobulbar fibres from the rostral part of the fastigial nucleus enter the vestibular nuclei medially via the juxtarestiform body (which passes along the lateral margin of the fourth ventricle); their termination in the reticular formation mirrors the termination of the uncinate tract (Batton et al 1977). The distribution of the fibres of the uncinate tract and the direct fastigiobulbar fibres is bilateral, but with a contralateral preponderance. Some crossed fibres can be traced caudally into the spinal cord.
The fastigial nucleus projects bilaterally to several subcortical motor centres from which descending brainstem pathways arise. This enables each hemivermis to influence different functional systems, including the medial and spinal vestibular nuclei and the medial bulbar reticular formation (which gives rise to the bilaterally descending medial reticulospinal tracts). Through these pathways the A zone of the vermis exerts a bilateral influence on ventromedially located spinal interneurones and motor neurones that innervate axial, truncal and proximal limb muscles (Kuypers’ medial motor system) (Lawrence and Kuypers 1968a, 1968b, Lemon et al 2012). Fibres of the uncinate tract that descend as far as the cervical cord terminate on the same neurones. The termination in the ventrolateral spinal nucleus is medial with respect to the projections of the dentate and interposed nuclei. The fastigial nucleus therefore influences motor centres in the brainstem that exert control bilaterally over the medial motor system of the spinal cord.
The caudal pole of the fastigial nucleus receives its Purkinje cell afferents from vermal visual areas in lobule VII that are involved in the long-term adaptation of saccades. Fastigial oculomotor projections are crossed and terminate in the rostral mesencephalic and pontine reticular formation that contains the horizontal gaze centre (paramedian pontine reticular formation), the vertical gaze centre (rostral interstitial nucleus of the medial longitudinal fasciculus), deep layers of the superior colliculus (bilaterally) and thalamic nuclei that probably include the frontal and parietal eye fields as their targets (Noda et al 1990).
Cerebellar influences on visceromotor systems are mediated by the projections of the fastigial nucleus to the parasolitary nucleus, a region bordering on the viscerosensory nuclei of the solitary tract; the dorsal visceromotor nucleus of the vagus nerve; central grey, serotoninergic raphe nuclei; and the noradrenergic nucleus of the locus coeruleus. The fastigial nucleus also influences visceromotor systems via projections of the vestibular nuclei and connections with the catecholaminergic nuclei of the brainstem and the hypothalamus (Zhu et al 2006).
Some fastigial nuclear fibres ascend along the superior cerebellar peduncle, separate from the peduncle at its decussation, and are distributed to the dorsal tegmentum, including the deep and intermediate layers of the nucleus of the tractus solitarius, the parabrachial nucleus, deep layers of the superior colliculus and intercollicular region, pretectal area, periaqueductal grey, interpeduncular nucleus and nuclei of the posterior commissure. The fastigial nucleus also projects to the ventral tegmental area, which sends a widespread dopaminergic innervation to the cerebral hemispheres: this fastigial projection has been shown to influence social cognition in a rodent model (Carta et al 2019). In the thalamus, fibres terminate in the contralateral internal medullary lamina, the intralaminar nuclei (paraventricular, paracentral, centromedian, parafascicular and central lateral), medial dorsal, ventrolateral and ventromedial thalamic nuclei. Ipsilateral projections recross in the commissure of the superior colliculus, the posterior commissure and the massa intermedia. The contralateral hypothalamus receives fastigial projections in the lateral, posterior and dorsal hypothalamic areas and the dorsomedial and paraventricular nuclei. These fastigial influences on autonomic and limbic circuits in the hypothalamus and thalamus are relevant when considering the cerebellar vermis and fastigial nucleus as nodes in limbic circuitry (Haines et al 1997, Schmahmann 1991, 2000).
Recurrent circuits involving the fastigial nucleus include excitatory efferents to the reticulotegmental nucleus and the GABAergic nucleo-olivary projection to the contralateral caudal medial accessory olive. The direct fastigiobulbar tract is an inhibitory, glycinergic system (Bagnall et al 2009).
Interstitial cell groups
The projections of the interstitial cell groups located between the fastigial and posterior interposed nuclei, the target nucleus of the X module, are directed to the vestibular nuclei, reticular formation, red nucleus and thalamus. They provide collaterals also to the spinal cord and superior colliculus (Bentivoglio and Kuypers 1982).
Lateral vestibular nucleus (Deiters’ nucleus)
The lateral vestibular nucleus (Deiters’ nucleus) is the target nucleus of the lateral vermal B zone. Despite its location outside the cerebellum, it might be better considered as one of the cerebellar nuclei because, unlike the other vestibular nuclei, it does not receive primary inputs from the labyrinth, but, like the deep cerebellar nuclei, it receives collateral innervation from the climbing fibres that innervate the B zone and it sends nucleo-olivary projections to the caudal dorsal accessory olive. The lateral vestibular nucleus gives rise to the lateral vestibulospinal tract. Through its projection to the lateral vestibular nucleus, the B zone, like the A zone, exerts an influence on ipsilateral interneurones and motor neurones in the anterior horn of the spinal cord that innervate axial, truncal and proximal limb muscles (Kuypers’ medial motor system).
The zones of the cerebellar vermis affect neurotransmission in the vestibulospinal and reticulospinal systems, controlling bilateral postural and vestibular reflexes of the axial and proximal musculature, and the oculomotor centres in the brainstem. The skeletomotor functions are located in the anterior vermis and posterior lobule VIII (the X and B zones are restricted to these lobules), and oculomotor functions are in lobule VII. Most caudally, lobule X (nodulus) belongs to the vestibulocerebellum and is considered below. Other functions, such as vegetative regulation, are subserved by the vermis but have not been studied in detail.
Emboliform (anterior interposed) nucleus
Climbing fibres innervating the C1, C3 and Y zones and the emboliform (anterior interposed) nucleus originate in the rostral half of the dorsal accessory olive. The latter receives somatotopically organized cutaneous input mainly through the dorsal column and trigeminal nuclei and contains a refined cutaneous map of the entire contralateral body surface (Gellman et al 1983). The corticospinal and rubrospinal tracts provide the dorsal column nuclei with collateral innervation.
The emboliform nucleus is the target of the C1, C3 and Y zones. The detailed somatotopic organization of these Purkinje cell zones is maintained in the nucleus, where Purkinje cells of different zones, but with the same climbing fibre input from a particular region of the body, project to a common set of neurones (see Fig. 29.37). Ascending axons from the anterior interposed nucleus enter the brachium conjunctivum, which decussates at the border of the pons and the mesencephalon. The ascending branch enters and surrounds the caudal half of the magnocellular red nucleus, then proceeds to terminate in the ventrolateral and ventral anterior nuclei of thalamus, which are linked with the contralateral primary motor and premotor cortices. The descending branch of the brachium conjunctivum terminates in the reticulotegmental nucleus. The entire system, including the magnocellular red nucleus and the primary motor cortex and their efferent tracts, is somatotopically organized. The reciprocal nucleo-olivary pathway from the emboliform nucleus terminates in the rostral half of the dorsal accessory olive: the emboliform nucleus does not participate in the recurrent cerebello–mesencephalon–olivary circuits.
The motor cortex and the caudal, magnocellular part of the red nucleus give rise to the two main descending motor systems: the corticospinal (pyramidal) tract that crosses the midline in the caudal medulla at the bulbospinal junction, and the rubrospinal tract that crosses the midline at its level of origin in the midbrain. The corticospinal tract provides the magnocellular red nucleus with collateral innervation. Both tracts terminate on laterally located spinal interneurones and motor neurones that innervate distal limb muscles. Control of the ipsilateral limbs by the pars intermedia of the spinal cord grey is therefore effected by the emboliform nucleus through two different pathways, the rubrospinal and the corticospinal tracts, which converge upon the same interneurones and motor neurones. During primate evolution, the corticospinal system increases in prominence at the cost of the rubrospinal system, which comes to occupy a subsidiary position in the human brain.
The role of the cerebellum in the motor system is closely allied to the circuitry of the C1, C3 and Y zones, and to the emboliform nucleus and its output systems. The double decussation of the brachium conjunctivum and the rubrospinal and corticospinal tracts is responsible for the clinical observation that lesions of the cerebellum affect the ipsilateral half of the body.
Globose (posterior interposed) nucleus
The globose (posterior interposed) nucleus is reciprocally interconnected with the contralateral rostral medial accessory olive. Its projections ascend and decussate in the superior cerebellar peduncle and terminate in a group of nuclei at the meso-diencephalic junction that includes the parvocellular red nucleus and the nucleus of Darkschewitsch in the central grey, the superior colliculus and thalamic nuclei that project to motor and premotor cortical areas (see Fig. 29.38). The nuclei at the meso-diencephalic junction give rise to ipsilaterally descending tegmental tracts that also terminate in the rostral half of the medial accessory olive, forming reciprocally organized loops.
The nucleus of Darkschewitsch is in a reciprocal loop by way of its fibres descending through the medial tegmental tract to the rostral medial accessory olive, which sends climbing fibres to the C2 zone and the globose nucleus. Motor and visual divisions can be distinguished in this system: the segments of the C2 zone located in the anterior and posterior motor regions of the cerebellum and the rostromedial globose nucleus receive input from the motor cortex, whereas visual and prefrontal input dominates in segments located in hemispheric lobules VII, IX (paraflocculus) and X (flocculus).
Dentate (lateral) nucleus
The shape of the dentate nucleus and the principal olivary nucleus change through evolution from compact nuclei to intricately folded sheets. In humans, the dentate nucleus has a rostromedial, microgyric, large-celled region, and a ventrocaudal, macrogyric, small-celled region (Dow 1974) (see Fig. 29.8). This corresponds to similar observations in monkeys, where the dentate consists of a rostromedial sector that is motor related, and a ventrocaudal component linked with nonmotor brain areas (Strick et al 2009) (see Fig. 29.38). The main projections from the dentate nucleus include the contralateral parvocellular red nucleus and thalamus. Group Y, a cell group located ventral to the dentate nucleus located within the floccular peduncle, projects to the contralateral oculomotor nucleus
The rostral dentate in lower mammals is the target of the D2 zone. Its dentate-olivary connections are with the dorsal lamina of the principal olivary nucleus. The rostral dentate receives projections from motor regions of the cerebellum and is somatotopically organized: the hindlimb is represented rostrally, then the arm and the face more caudally. It projects to the ventral, central and dorsolateral parts of the parvocellular red nucleus. Its thalamic targets are in the ventral intermediate and ventrolateral thalamic nuclei, which are reciprocally connected with the motor and premotor cortices (see Fig. 29.38D–E). Retrograde and transneuronal labelling reveals that the rostrodorsal dentate sends its efferents via the thalamus to the primary motor cortex (Wiesendanger and Wiesendanger 1985, Orioli and Strick 1989, Hoover and Strick 1999, Lu et al 2007), ventral premotor area (Orioli and Strick 1989), caudal part of the dorsal premotor area (Hashimoto et al 2010) and supplementary motor area (SMA proper) (Sakai et al 1999, Akkal et al 2007). Anterior and medial parietal areas also receive projections via the thalamus from the rostromedial dentate nucleus. The rostral dentate nucleus sends descending projections via the brachium conjunctivum to the reticulotegmental nucleus.
The caudal dentate is the target nucleus of the D1 zone, and its projections terminate in the ventral lamina of the principal olive. It receives its corticonuclear projections from lobule VII (hemispheric extensions of lobule VIIA as crus I and crus II, as well as lobule VIIB) and from lobule IX (paraflocculus/tonsil). The ventrocaudal dentate projects to the dorsomedial part of the parvocellular red nucleus. In the thalamus, it targets the medial parts of the ventrolateral thalamic nucleus, area X (Chan-Palay 1977), the parvo- and magnocellular anterior nucleus, and the lateral rim of the mediodorsal nucleus (Middleton and Strick 2001). These thalamic nuclei convey the projections to the cerebral cortical areas in the preSMA at the rostral part of the dorsal premotor area, prefrontal cortex in areas 46d and 9l (Middleton and Strick 1997, 2001), the frontal and supplementary eye fields (Lynch et al 1994, Lynch and Tian 2006) and the inferior parietal lobule area 7b (Clower et al 2001).
The recurrent pathways that link the parvocellular red nucleus by way of the central tegmental tract with the inferior olive, and via the olivocerebellar projection to the dentate nucleus, form the Guillain–Mollaret triangle. Projections from the rostrolateral and caudal parvocellular red nucleus course in the central tegmental tract to the dorsal lamina and bend of the principal olive, which provide climbing fibres to the caudal dentate and the cerebellar D2 zone. The dorsomedial parvocellular red nucleus sends its projections in the medial tegmental tract to the ventral lamina of the principal olive, which provides climbing fibres to the rostral dentate and the cerebellar D1 zone. The first published lithograph of a section through the pontine tegmentum (Stilling 1846), shows the central tegmental tract linking the dentate and recipient parvocellular red nucleus with the inferior olive to be one of the largest fibre systems in the brainstem (Fig. 29.41).
Single axon tracing studies of cerebellothalamic projections and thalamocortical pathways in monkeys demonstrated that there is considerable feedback from the cerebellum to cerebral association areas (see Fig. 29.41). This is consistent with the demonstration of associative projections into the cerebellum through the feedforward limb via the corticopontine pathways, and with the concept that regions of the cerebellum and cerebral cortex are reciprocally interconnected in circuits, or loops.
The cerebral cortical projections of the classic cerebellar recipient motor thalamic nuclei (pars oralis of the ventral posterolateral nucleus, VPLo, the caudal and pars postrema aspects of the ventrolateral nucleus, VLc and VLps, and nucleus X) are not confined to the motor region (Fig. 29.42). The upper bank of the superior temporal sulcus receives projections from the VLc and VLps nuclei; widespread regions of the posterior parietal cortex receives projections from the VLps nucleus, and the VLc projections to the parietal lobe are similar although less intense; nucleus X projects to both the upper and lower banks of the intraparietal sulcus; VPLo projects to both the gyral and sulcal cortices of the superior parietal lobule at the lateral convexity of the hemisphere. Further, in the frontal lobe, the prefrontal periarcuate areas are reciprocally interconnected with nucleus X, and with the VLc, and VPLo nuclei.
The cerebellar outflow is also directed to the medial dorsal thalamic nucleus in its paralaminar parts, the laterally situated pars multiformis and the more caudal pars densocellularis. These nuclei have reciprocal connections with the prefrontal cortex in area 8, area 46 at both banks of the principal sulcus, and area 9, as well as with the cingulate gyrus, posterior parietal cortex, and multimodal parts of the superior temporal sulcus. In addition, nonmotor thalamic nuclei, including the intralaminar nuclei, receive major cerebellar projections, particularly centralis lateralis (CL), as well as the paracentralis (Pcn) and centromedian-parafascicular (CM-Pf) complex. The CL nucleus, like other intralaminar nuclei, has widespread cortical connections including the posterior parietal cortex, the multimodal regions of the upper bank of the superior temporal sulcus, the prefrontal cortex, the cingulate gyrus, and the primary motor cortex. Pcn nucleus projections include the parahippocampal gyrus. These thalamic projections to higher order association areas originate not only in the dentate nucleus but also from parts of the interposed and fastigial nuclei (see Schmahmann 1996).
Trans-synaptic tract tracing studies using attenuated viral tracers further defined the feedback limb, showing that the morphological distinction between the phylogenetically older, dorsal magnocellular part of the dentate nucleus, and the more recently evolved ventral small-celled region have connectional and functional relevance. The primary motor cortex receives projections via the thalamus from dorsal portions of the dentate nucleus at mid-rostrocaudal levels and in the caudal portions of the emboliform nucleus where neuronal activity related to arm movement has been recorded (see Fig. 29.35). About 25–30% of the dentate nucleus projects to the primary motor cortex. In flattened maps of the dentate nucleus, neurones projecting to the arm representation of M1 are situated in a cluster in the dorsal portion of the dentate at mid-rostrocaudal levels; those to the leg representation are in the rostral pole of the dorsal dentate; and those projecting to the face representation are in caudal levels of the dorsal dentate (Strick et al 2009). Each M1 cortical area thus receives input from a spatially separate set of neurones in the dorsal part of the dentate nucleus. The premotor cortex receives input from the mid-rostrocaudal part of the dentate, ventral to the M1 projecting neurones. Frontal eye field projecting neurones are in the caudal third of the dentate which is correlated with saccadic eye movements. The prefrontal cortex (areas 46 and 9) receives projections from the ventral part of the dentate, mostly in its middle third rostrocaudally. Projections to the posterior parietal cortex, including the lower bank of the intraparietal sulcus, arise from neurones located ventrally in the caudal pole of the dentate. Projections to the anterior intraparietal area arise from neurones located dorsally in the dentate at mid-rostrocaudal levels (Clower et al 2001, Ugolini et al 2006, Strick et al 2009).
In humans, the D2 zone accounts for most of the cerebellar hemisphere, exemplified by its development (see Fig. 29.23) and by the size of the different components of its circuitry. The subdivision of the human dentate in rostrodorsal microgyric and caudolateral macrogyric portions would correspond with two major subdivisions of the D2 zone. Projections from the dorsal lamina of the principal olive to the D2 zone would be topographically arranged such that motor inputs are transmitted to the rostrodorsal microgyric dentate and segments of the D2 zone located in the motor regions of the cerebellum, and nonmotor inputs are directed to the caudolateral macrogyric dentate nucleus and segments of the D2 zone in hemispheric lobules VII and IX. This is in agreement with the clinical-anatomical observations of crossed cerebro-cerebellar atrophy in patients by Demolé (1927a, 1927b) and by Verhaart and Wieringen-Rauws (1950) that degeneration of the rostrodorsal, microgyric dentate occurred with large, chronic lesions involving the pre- and postcentral gyri, whereas lesions of more posterior parts of the hemisphere, involving parietal, occipital and temporal areas, resulted in atrophy of the ventrocaudal macrogyric dentate.
The understanding of the zonal organization of the connections of the inferior olive and the cerebellar nuclei is derived from studies in lower mammals, but knowledge of the connections of the cerebellar hemispheres that account for the bulk of the cerebellar cortex in the great apes and human remains incomplete. The demonstration by Fujita et al (2010) of the presence of D1 and D2 zones in the marmoset hemisphere supports the idea that the construction of the primate hemisphere is similar to that in rodents and carnivores, but the relative proportions of the marmoset hemisphere are not very different from rodents. The source of the climbing fibre projection to the D1 zone in rodents and primates is the ventral, non-convoluted lamina of the principal olive. Although the ventral lamina of the principal olive is prominent in lower mammals, if this subdivision is represented in the human inferior olive by its medial lamina, the contribution of the human D1 zone to the hemisphere would be very small (see Fig. 29.35). Its major output would be represented by the D2 zone, and projections to the convoluted dentate nucleus.
Explanations for the evolutionary increase in size of the dentate nucleus and its connections include the increasing complexity of the cortical motor system, which is a major target of the dentate nucleus, as multiple, interconnected premotor and posterior parietal areas involved in the preparation of movement converge on the primary motor cortex. However, converging lines of evidence from numerous avenues of investigation suggest that the increased size of the dentate nucleus and its interconnected cerebellar posterior lobe lateral hemispheres reflect increased connectivity with cerebral association and paralimbic areas that subserve non-motor functions, enabling a cerebellar role in the modulation of intellect and emotion (Leiner et al 1986, Schmahmann 1991, 1996, 1997, Middleton and Strick 1994, Stoodley and Schmahmann 2009a, 2010, Strick et al 2009, Schmahmann et al 2019). Trans-synaptic viral tracer studies in monkeys further reveal that cerebellar circuits have disynaptic linkage with the subthalamic nucleus, providing evidence for cerebellar-basal ganglia communication in both the motor and nonmotor domains (Hoshi et al 2005).

Neuroimaging of Human Cerebellar Structure and Function

Geographically distant areas of the cerebral cortex that are functionally coupled form intrinsic connectivity networks that subserve specific functions and can be studied with resting state functional connectivity MRI (rsfcMRI) (Habas et al 2009, Krienen and Buckner 2009, O’Reilly et al 2010, Buckner et al 2011). These cerebral networks map onto the cerebellum with topographic specificity and some degree of inter-individual variability. The motor networks map onto sensorimotor parts of the cerebellum in the anterior lobe and lobule VIII. The dorsal attention, ventral attention, frontoparietal, default mode and salience networks map onto focal areas within the posterior lobe of the cerebellum (Fig. 29.43). Among all the networks, the frontoparietal network for adaptive control is overrepresented in the cerebellum compared to the cerebral cortex (Marek et al 2018).
Task-based functional MRI within single subjects and at the group level provides independent confirmation of the cerebellar sensorimotor-cognitive dichotomy (Stoodley and Schmahmann 2009a, Stoodley et al 2010, 2012) (Fig. 29.44A). Tasks such as moving the arm or leg activate the primary sensorimotor cerebellum – anterior lobe and adjacent parts of lobule VI, as well as lobule VIII which is regarded (in the conceptualizing of Woolsey 1952) as the second sensorimotor area in the cerebellum. Cognitive paradigms activate topographically distinct regions within the cerebellar posterior lobe. Lobules VI and crus I are engaged in language and verbal working memory; lobule VI in spatial tasks; lobules VI, crus I and VIIB are activated by executive functions such as working memory, planning, organizing and strategy formation; and vermal lobules VI and VII are involved in emotional processing. Language is heavily right lateralized and spatial functions left-lateralized, reflecting crossed cerebro-cerebellar projections. Hemispheric lobules VI, crus I and VIIB appear to be involved in encoding generalized aversive processing. Threatening stimuli such as noxious heat and viewing unpleasant pictures activate these cerebellar regions together with limbic system structures – the anterior hypothalamus, subgenual anterior cingulate cortex and parahippocampal gyrus (Moulton et al 2011). There are fine-scale gradients within different cognitive networks, exemplified by the dorsal attention system. Visual working memory is encoded in hemisphere lobule VIIB, with spatial encoding (location of the stimulus) represented in medial portions of the lobule whereas memory load (number of stimuli) is encoded more laterally (Brissenden et al 2018) (Fig. 29.44B). This pattern is reflected also within the intraparietal sulcus with which cerebellum is interconnected (Schmahmann and Pandya 1989), with more posterior parietal regions representing location and anterior regions representing load.
Voxel-based lesion symptom mapping following cerebellar stroke shows motor topography in the nuclei and in cerebellar cortex confined to lobules II through VI (Schoch et al 2006). The study of Stoodley et al (2016) confirmed the dichotomy of motor ataxia arising from damage to lobules IV through VI, whereas the cerebellar cognitive affective syndrome, but not motor ataxia, occurred following stroke in lobules crus II through lobule IX (crus I was largely spared in patients in this study) (Fig. 29.44C).
Task-based and rsfc-MRI studies using a large dataset accumulated in the Human Connectome Project (HCP) confirm the double motor representation in the anterior lobe and lobule VIII (Guell et al 2018a). They also confirm and extend the demonstration with rsfc-MRI (Buckner et al 2011) of a triple representation of cognitive domains in the cerebellar posterior lobe. These three representations are in (i) lobules VI-crus I, (ii) crus II-VIIB, and (iii) lobule IX (Fig. 29.44D). Studies of intrinsic cerebellar functional connectivity (Guell et al 2018b) further reveal that the cerebellum demonstrates the same kind of sensorimotor-to-cognition hierarchical gradient pattern (Fig. 29.45) that has previously been identified in the cerebral cortex (Mesulam 2000). These fundamental organizing principles of cerebellar structure and function dovetail with the deeper understanding of cerebellar clinical neurology and neuropsychiatry (Schmahmann et al 2019).

Modulating cerebellar circuits alters cerebral cortical connections and physiology

The cerebellar areas that are incorporated into neural circuits subserving higher order functions and emotion are functionally relevant. By stimulating the cerebellum non-invasively using transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) it is possible to demonstrate that cerebellar modulation of cerebral–cortical circuits is anatomically and functionally specific.
Intermittent theta-burst TMS, which produces excitation, applied to vermal lobule VII results in increased connectivity within the dorsal attention network, but when applied to lateral parts of crus I/II it leads to increased connectivity in the default network (Halko et al 2014) (see Fig. 29.4). Inhibition-inducing continuous theta-burst TMS applied to lateral cerebellar hemisphere produces decreased functional connectivity with frontal and parietal cognitive regions, but connectivity with motor regions remains unaltered (Rastogi et al 2017). Cerebellar stimulation with intermittent theta-burst TMS has the shared physiological effect of increasing the temporal complexity of the cerebral cortical EEG pattern, but in a network-specific manner: vermal stimulation impacts high-beta/low-gamma-range oscillations, whereas lateral stimulation impacts low-theta and high-gamma-range oscillations (Farzan et al 2016). These TMS studies provide experimental evidence for the dysmetria of thought and universal cerebellar transform theories (Schmahmann 1991, 2000) that different subregions of the cerebellum modulate different cerebral cortical networks, and that the underlying nature of the cerebellar physiological effects on these different networks is similar.

The Triad of Clinical Ataxiology in Relation to Neuroanatomy

Three major clinical syndromes can be identified following cerebellar lesions. They are the cerebellar motor syndrome (Holmes 1939), the vestibulocerebellar syndrome (Ito 1984, Barmack and Yakhnitsa 2013) and the cerebellar cognitive affective syndrome (Schmahmann and Sherman 1998), each of which has its own distinct anatomical signature in the cerebellum. These may occur individually or together depending on the location of the injury. The timing of the onset and progression of the underlying disorder also influence the nature and severity of the presentation.

Cerebellar motor syndrome

The cerebellar motor syndrome, previously regarded as the sole manifestation of cerebellar disruption, is characterized by disturbed initiation, rate, rhythm and force of voluntary movements, the underlying basis of the incoordination (or ataxia, Greek, lack of order). Cerebellar lesions do not produce weakness but resting motor tone in pure cerebellar disease is decreased (hypotonic).
The somatotopy identified in experimental animal models and in functional imaging studies in healthy humans is reflected in structure–function correlation studies using voxel-based morphometry and lesion symptom mapping in patients with focal cerebellar lesions. Patients with lesions to the anterior cerebellar lobe and vermis often present with ataxia that may affect speech and deglutition, core muscles of the trunk and movement of their upper and lower limbs.
Speech is slurred, syllables are poorly articulated, cadence is slow and irregular, and rapid alternating buccal, palatal and lingual consonants are degraded. Dysarthria is compounded by impaired volume control, which is inconstant and not well modulated. Scanning speech was an older term to describe cerebellar dysarthria, but it does not fully capture the range of the characteristic deficits. Dysarthria in cerebellar developmental disorders differs in quality from that of the acquired and neurodegenerative ataxias, with a persistent childlike quality, as well as impaired prosody (the use of vocal intonation, stress and cadence). Cerebellar disorders affect the control of breathing, and ataxic respiration further degrades the quality of speech. Impaired control of the muscles of deglutition predisposes to dysphagia (impaired swallowing), causing choking on liquids and solids and the risk of aspiration pneumonia.
Truncal ataxia occurs with midline lesions, including titubation, or oscillation of the head and trunk. The patient with cerebellar ataxia has a widened stance and difficulty standing. In mild instances there is inability to stand in the tandem position, and in progressively more severe ataxia, inability to stand with the feet in parallel, or in the natural position, eventually needing to hold on to stationary objects or another person in order to stand. The cerebellar ataxic gait is staggering, uneven, irregular, and veers from side to side. Unilateral lesions cause stumbling towards the affected side.
Dysmetria (Greek, disordered timing or measurement) refers to the impaired ability to judge, regulate and control behaviour. It applies to both the motor and cognitive domains. Upper limb dysmetria is identified with tests such as the finger to nose test, or rapid finger following/mirror test, rapid alternating movements, and tests of finger dexterity such as tapping the index finger on the crease of the thumb. These reveal inaccurate and clumsy movements, jerking trajectory, overshooting (hypermetria) and undershooting the target (hypometria), oscillation at the fingers and wrists as well as more proximally at the elbows and shoulders when more severe, as well as overshoot, rebound and dysdiadochokinesis (dysrhythmic attempts at rapid alternating movements). Finger and hand dexterity for compound movements involving more than one joint are particularly affected. Lower limb dysmetria tested with the heel-to-shin test, reveals proximal overshoot as the heel is placed on the opposite knee, tremor of the heel on the knee, and slowing, jerking, or side-to-side movements as the heel moves down the shin. The attempt to draw a circle in the air with the legs is degraded by decomposition manifested as irregular or chaotic motions.
Postural tremor may be present in the arms, usually with activation. Cerebellar tremor is large amplitude, 2–3 Hz, and may involve many body parts. Rubral tremor from red nucleus lesions and its connections involves multiple joints, change directions and is frequently rotatory. Palatal tremor is slow and semi-rhythmic, resulting from lesions affecting the Guillain–Mollaret triangle (red nucleus – inferior olivary nucleus – deep cerebellar nuclei).
Ataxia rating scales assess and measure these areas of impairment, i.e. stance, gait, upper and lower limb control, and speech. Oculomotor deficits reflecting involvement of the vestibulocerebellum are also measured. The scales commonly in use are the International Cooperative Ataxia Rating Scale (ICARS: Trouillas et al 1997), the Friedreich’s Ataxia Rating Scale (FARS: Subramony et al 2005), the Scale for Assessment and Rating of Ataxia (SARA: Schmitz-Hübsch et al 2006), and the Brief Ataxia Rating Scale (BARS: Schmahmann et al 2009).

Cerebellar vestibular syndrome and the oculomotor cerebellum

The flocculus and the nodulus are known as the vestibulocerebellum because they maintain afferent and efferent connections with the vestibular system. They also belong to the functionally more comprehensive oculomotor division of the cerebellum, which includes lobule VII (visual vermis), uvula (dorsal vermal lobule IX), the ventral paraflocculus (the human accessory paraflocculus) and the dorsal paraflocculus (the human tonsil, particularly hemispheric lobule IX). The mossy fibre projection of the nucleus prepositus hypoglossi, a key element in the saccade-producing system, outlines the entire oculomotor cerebellum, with the exception of the dorsal paraflocculus (Belknap and McCrea 1988) (Fig. 29.46). The functions of the nodulus (vermal lobule X) are not exclusively oculomotor: it also influences labyrinthine and postural reflexes and vegetative systems.
The oculomotor cerebellum is involved in long-term adaptation of saccades, ocular stabilization reflexes and smooth pursuit (reviewed in Voogd et al (2012)) that permit bringing and maintaining images of objects of interest on the fovea. The flocculus/paraflocculus supports brief, high-frequency vestibular responses, sustained pursuit eye movements and gaze holding. The nodulus/ventral uvula enables sustained, low-frequency vestibular responses. The dorsal oculomotor vermis and its target in the posterior portion of the fastigial nucleus (the fastigial oculomotor region) are essential for saccades and the initiation of pursuit eye movements (Kheradmand and Zee 2011).
The vestibulo-ocular reflex (VOR) is an ancient reflex, present in both fishes and agnatha (jawless fish). It stabilizes the position of the retina in space during movements of the head by rotating the eyeball in the opposite direction. It is an open reflex, meaning that there is no time for a feedback connection that would compensate for inaccuracies in the execution of the reflex. This function is undertaken by the long-term adaptation of the reflex by the flocculus. The circuitry of the flocculus, similar to the vestibuloocular reflex, is organized on the coordinate system of the semicircular canals (Simpson and Graf 1981, Barmack and Yakhnitsa 2013). The vestibulo-ocular reflex consists of different components. One component connects the lateral (horizontal) semicircular canal, via oculomotor neurones in the vestibular nuclei, with the oculogyric muscles that move the eyes in a plane colinear with the plane of the lateral canal (see Fig. 29.40). The anterior semicircular canal influences the ipsilateral superior oblique and the contralateral inferior oblique muscles that move the eye in the plane of this canal.
Stroke in the territory of the posterior inferior cerebellar artery results in infarction of the vestibulocerebellum–flocculonodular region and lobule IX, producing abrupt-onset severe vertigo (sensation of movement in the absence of stimulus), almost always with nausea and vomiting. The vertigo makes it impossible to walk at the time of the acute event, but if the cerebellar motor areas are not damaged, there is no dysmetria of the limbs or dysarthria. Oculomotor abnormalities from cerebellar lesions produce visual disturbances including diplopia, oscillopsia and a sense of dysequilibrium. The range, complexity and varying combinations of oculomotor abnormalities on examination include deficits in gaze holding, reflex pursuit eye movements, voluntary initiation and direction of saccadic eye movements, and spontaneous or gaze-evoked nystagmus. The condition of isolated downbeat nystagmus, linked to a potassium channelopathy of the flocculonodular system, produces a cautious and unstable gait, likely a result of mismatched visual–vestibular interaction. Opsoclonus myoclonus syndrome is a dramatic impairment of oculomotor control with chaotic eye movements in horizontal and vertical directions in primary position, a heralding sign of a paraneoplastic syndrome that may be observed in neoplasms such as hypernephroma of the kidney in children, and the anti-Yo antibody syndrome directed at Purkinje cells in Hodgkin’s lymphoma and breast, ovarian and lung carcinomas.

Cerebellar cognitive affective syndrome

The cerebellar cognitive affective syndrome (CCAS) is characterized by impairments in executive function (planning, set-shifting, abstract reasoning, verbal fluency, working memory), often with perseveration, distractibility or inattention; visual–spatial disorganization and impaired visual–spatial memory; difficulties with language production including dysprosodia, agrammatism and mild anomia (Schmahmann and Sherman 1998). Language problems also include decreased verbal fluency (Molinari et al 1997), inability to detect one’s own errors in tasks such as the verb-for-noun generation paradigm (Fiez et al 1992, Stoodley and Schmahmann 2009b), and impaired metalinguistics, the use of language for the purpose of social communication (Mariën et al 2014, Guell et al 2015). Personality changes include blunting of affect or disinhibited and inappropriate behaviour, particularly following lesions of the vermis (the putative limbic cerebellum). The affective symptoms include impaired emotional and attentional control, and abnormal social skills that resemble symptoms typical of autism spectrum disorder and psychosis (Schmahmann et al 2007). The net effect of these disturbances in cognitive abilities is a general lowering of intellectual function. The CCAS arises from damage to the cognitive cerebellum in the cerebellar posterior lobe (lobules VI, VII, probably lobule IX), and is postulated to reflect dysmetria of thought analogous to the dysmetria of motor control from damage to the sensorimotor cerebellum (Schmahmann 1991, 1996, 2010, Schmahmann and Sherman 1998, Stoodley and Schmahmann 2009a, 2010, Stoodley et al 2012, 2016). The CCAS may occur separately or together with the cerebellar motor syndrome and the vestibular syndrome. It has been regarded as the third cornerstone of clinical ataxiology and eponymously named Schmahmann’s syndrome (Manto and Mariën 2015, Argyropoulos et al 2020). The defining features of the CCAS occur across disease types and in children as well as adults; a developmental form of the CCAS is present in children with hereditary or congenital disorders. The syndrome of paediatric postoperative cerebellar mutism, likely a profound apraxia of speech, may occur together with the CCAS as a consequence of accidental damage to cerebellar outflow pathways (the deep nuclei and the superior cerebellar peduncle) during surgery to remove posterior fossa tumours (Wisoff and Epstein 1984, Rekate et al 1985, Gudrunardottir et al 2016, Albazron et al 2019, Schmahmann 2020).
The affective component of the CCAS has been conceptualized as an acquired impairment of those functions commonly affected in neuro- developmental disorders such as autism spectrum and schizophrenia (Schmahmann et al 2007, Van Overwalle et al 2015). Conversely, cerebellar abnormalities are increasingly reported in neuropsychiatric conditions including schizophrenia, autism and depression (Schmahmann 1991, Fatemi et al 2012, Baumann et al 2015). The maturing field of the cognitive neuroscience of the cerebellum is addressing these possibilities in studies of complex behavioural repertoires that are far removed from sensorimotor domains, demonstrating, for example, granule cell activation during conditions of reward (Wagner et al 2017) and social cognition (Carta et al 2019), and elucidating the role of the cerebellum in learning and memory (Thompson 1983, Freeman 2015), fear conditioning (Supple and Leaton 1990) and empathy (Schmahmann et al 2007, Koziol et al 2013).
