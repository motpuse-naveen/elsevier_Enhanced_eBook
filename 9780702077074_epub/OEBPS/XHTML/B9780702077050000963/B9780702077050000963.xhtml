Commentary 9.2: A proposed novel action of psoas minor

Donald Anthony Neumann
Psoas minor is a curious muscle in humans, lacking a clear and universally agreed function and reported to be present in only 33–65% of individuals (Seib 1934, Gandhi et al 2013, Neumann and Garceau 2015, Tubbs et al 2016). This commentary proposes a novel functional role for psoas minor, with the aim of stimulating interest in this muscle, both anatomically and clinically.
Psoas minor is located retroperitoneally deep in the abdomen, running nearly parallel with and immediately anterior to the much larger psoas major (Fig. 9.2.1). Although structural variation is not uncommon, it typically originates proximally on the vertebral bodies of T12–L1 and the intervening intervertebral disc (Moore et al 2014; see Ch. 77). The relatively long tendon of psoas minor typically has a distal bony attachment to the iliopubic ramus (see Fig. 9.2.1): this attachment is usually medial to the anterior edge of the acetabulum and medial and slightly superior to the iliopubic eminence (Neumann and Garceau 2015). Therefore, unlike psoas major, psoas minor typically has a bony distal attachment to the pelvis, not to the femur, that likely accounts for its described functions as a weak flexor of the trunk or of the lumbar spine (Oatis 2008, Drake et al 2020). However, given its small muscle belly and the presence of other larger surrounding muscles that are capable of these actions, it seems unlikely that these are either the sole or dominant functions of psoas minor.

Anatomical connections between the tendon of psoas minor and the iliac fascia

In addition to the distal attachment of psoas minor to the pelvis, anatomy textbooks usually report a secondary attachment into adjacent fascia. In a cadaveric study, the tendon of psoas minor was found to be consistently fused with the structural ‘backbone’ of the iliac fascia, and the entire musculofascial entity was referred to as the psoas minor–iliac fascia complex (Neumann and Garceau 2015). Detailed measurements showed that the iliac fascia component of this complex draped directly over the distal, pre-tendinous portion of iliopsoas and the femoral nerve (Fig. 9.2.2A). The variably curved superior edge of the iliac fascia has been referred to as the iliopectineal arch (Agur and Dalley 2013), and this is the term that will be used throughout this commentary.
A composite tracing of the location of the iliac fascia obtained through study of cadaveric specimens is shown in Fig. 9.2.2B (Neumann and Garceau 2015). Of the 21 hips in which psoas minor was present, the muscle was attached distally to bone and to the iliac fascia in 19 (90.5%) (see vertical and horizontal red areas in Fig. 9.2.2B, respectively). In the remaining two hips (from the same cadaver) psoas minor was attached distally only to the iliac fascia (which was fully formed). Thus, in all cases, the tendon of psoas minor was firmly blended with the iliac fascia. The location of this tendon–fascial junction was on average 69.9 ± 9.0 mm medial and 29.8 ± 16.1 mm superior to the anterior–superior iliac spine (see Fig. 9.2.2B), placing the tendon–fascial junction immediately lateral and anterior to the sacroiliac joint.
As is apparent in the composite perspective in Fig. 9.2.2B, the arched superior edge of the iliac fascia (i.e. the iliopectineal arch) was generally concave, faced superiorly and could be pulled up and away from the underlying iliopsoas. The superior–medial aspect of the iliac fascia was attached to bone and crossed the sacroiliac joint in 11 hips (52.4%). Inferior to the sacroiliac joint, the fascia was attached to the inner pelvic brim and eventually became continuous with the pelvic attachment of the tendon of psoas minor (see Fig. 9.2.2B). The iliac fascia continued and was attached inferiorly to a point generally medial to the iliopubic junction, i.e. the junction between the anterior ilium and superior pubic ramus. En route, some fibres flowed posteromedially to interlace with the external fascia covering the superior part of obturator internus.
The lateral extent of the iliopectineal arch was attached to bone in a region immediately superior and inferior to the anterior superior iliac spine. However, the posterior side of the iliac fascia lacked a pelvic attachment. The anterior surface of the iliac fascia blended with the deep part of the inguinal ligament and the proximal fascia lata of the thigh. A near continuous sheet of iliac fascia therefore existed between the iliopectineal arch superiorly and fascia lata inferiorly, providing an open vertical aponeurotic sleeve that allowed unrestricted passage of the underlying iliopsoas and femoral nerve into the thigh (see Video 9.2.1 and Fig. 9.2.3). Although iliopsoas and the femoral nerve passed posterior to the iliac fascia, the femoral vein and artery (embedded within the femoral sheath) passed superficial to the fascia. The anterior surface of iliopsoas and the femoral nerve were in direct contact with the posterior surface of the iliac fascia, whereas the posterior surface of iliopsoas was in firm contact with a segment of the anterior pelvic brim.

Possible biomechanical and clinical significance of the psoas minor–iliac fascia complex

When present, the tendon of psoas minor is consistently attached to and reinforces the medial and central part of the iliac fascia (Neumann and Garceau 2015). Based on this anatomical connection, an active force produced by psoas minor could, to some degree, adjust the tension within the iliac fascia, and potentially within the proximal part of the fascia lata (see arrows in Fig. 9.2.4). In this way, psoas minor may provide some degree of mechanical stability to the underlying iliopsoas as it crosses over the femoral head and across the anterior pelvic brim. Such an active tensioning may confer useful biomechanical functions to iliopsoas as a hip flexor. Although speculative, it may be that the myogenic tensioning within the iliac fascia prevents an activated iliopsoas from ‘bowstringing’ away from the anterior pelvic brim, especially when strongly contracting to move the thigh into a high flexion angle. Excessive bowstringing of iliopsoas and its tendon away from the femoral head would increase the hip flexion moment arm (leverage) of the muscle, resulting in less rotation of the joint (flexion of the hip, in this case) per linear unit of muscle contraction. Such a counterproductive biomechanical scenario would exist for any muscle that bowstrings across a joint (Neumann 2017). Excessive bowstringing of the tendon of iliopsoas could reduce the range of available active hip flexion, which may limit the ease of activities that require end range flexion, such as putting on shoes and socks. Whether psoas minor has the physiological strength for generating enough active counter-tension in the iliac fascia to prevent iliopsoas bowstringing during hip flexion is not known. To add insight into this notion, Neumann and Garceau (2015) estimated that, on average, psoas minor could theoretically produce a maximal force of 21 N (2.1 kg) based on an average cross-sectional area of 52.5 mm2. Although this is an estimate, it does provide some context when considering whether psoas minor has the force potential to offer mechanical stability to the much stronger iliopsoas. Further study is needed to verify this biomechanical proposition.
The mechanism described in the previous paragraph could, in theory, also allow the psoas minor–iliac fascia complex to provide varying control of the medial–lateral position of the underlying iliopsoas as it runs across the femoral head, anterior capsule region, iliopectineal eminence and the pelvic brim. Could such control influence the contact stress between the distal region of iliopsoas and adjacent structures? This question is clinically relevant given the many poorly understood painful conditions or pathologies in the anterior hip region that may involve iliopsoas. Such conditions include iliopsoas ‘snapping hip’ syndrome (Byrd 2006, Lewis 2010, Philippon et al 2014); iliopsoas impingement (Dora et al 2007); iliopsoas bursitis or tendonitis (Blankenbaker et al 2006, Nunley et al 2010, Philippon et al 2011); and, possibly, acetabular labral pathology (Philippon et al 2014). An intriguing question arises as to the possible clinical consequences of not possessing a psoas minor, and therefore lacking the proposed active tensioning mechanism for the iliac fascia. Would absence of psoas minor predispose to developing painful iliopsoas-associated pathology?

A primary fascial-based stabilization role for a muscle: is this a novel concept?

Several examples exist within the body where a sheath of connective tissue passively stabilizes the position of underlying muscles or tendons. Consider for example the role of the fibrous lacertus for elbow flexion, the anular pulleys for digital flexion, or the extensor retinaculum for ankle dorsiflexion or wrist extension. This commentary proposes that a similar anatomical mechanism might exist for iliopsoas, with regard to its mechanical potential to bowstring away from the hip during high angle hip flexion (Yoshio et al 2002). It is arguable that this stability could be furnished not only by passive tension, as in the examples cited above, but also through force generated by psoas minor and transferred through the iliac fascia. In theory, sensory receptors within the psoas minor–iliac fascia complex may provide input to the central nervous system. As such, the nervous system may better determine the necessary motor activation of psoas minor to mechanically stabilize the iliopsoas tendon during active motion. Perhaps psoas minor and iliopsoas are activated ‘in phase’ with each other during vigorous movements involving high flexion angles, such as cycling or running up hill, which is an idea that remains unstudied.
The proposition that one muscle can serve as an active stabilizer for another, as postulated for psoas minor, is not unique. Examples include quadratus plantae, which may help guide the position of the common tendon of flexor digitorum longus in the foot, and palmaris longus as it stabilizes the palmar aponeurosis in the hand, a structural arrangement that prevents bowstringing of the extrinsic digital flexor tendons and median nerve within the carpal tunnel during extreme wrist flexion.

Closing remarks

Although not proven, it is proposed that psoas minor functions as a dedicated accessory muscle to iliopsoas. More specifically, the primary function of the psoas minor–iliac fascia complex is postulated to enhance the biomechanical stability and function of the underlying iliopsoas. Such a stabilizing role appears more likely than the traditional description of psoas minor as a trunk flexor.
Further study is warranted to test the hypotheses raised in this commentary. Such research could involve: (1) large-scale dissection studies to determine the cross-sectional area, morphological variation, pennation angle and innervation of the psoas minor–iliac fascia complex; (2) measurement of the strength and stiffness characteristics of the iliac fascia; (3) instrumenting the psoas minor–iliac fascia complex and cadaveric hips with force (strain) and kinematic measurement hardware; (4) computer and mathematical modelling; and (5) in vivo electromyographical or ultrasound analyses. One specific aim of this research would be to analyse whether psoas minor is physiologically capable of producing enough force to stabilize the position of iliopsoas across a range of hip positions. Ultimately, research may be justified to determine whether an association exists between the presence or absence of psoas minor and the likelihood of developing anterior hip pain and other associated pathologies involving iliopsoas.
Although this commentary has generated more questions than it has answered, it seeks to share a hypothesis with anatomists, kinesiologists, physiotherapists, surgeons and other clinicians who treat musculoskeletal problems involving the anterior aspect of the hip.
