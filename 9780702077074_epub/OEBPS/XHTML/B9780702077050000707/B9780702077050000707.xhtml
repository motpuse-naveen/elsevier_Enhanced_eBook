Chapter 70: Suprarenal gland

The suprarenal (adrenal) glands lie immediately superior and slightly anterior to the superior pole of each kidney (see Figs 72.5, 61.8). The glands are golden yellow in colour and are enclosed within the renal fascia, where they are separated from the kidneys by a small amount of loose connective tissue. Each gland possesses two functionally and structurally distinct areas: an outer cortex and an inner medulla derived from mesoderm and neural crest cells, respectively.
Macroscopically, the glands are slightly different in external appearance (Fig. 70.1). The right suprarenal gland is pyramidal in shape and has two well-developed lower projections (limbs), giving a cross-sectional appearance similar to a three-pointed star. The left is more semilunar in shape, flattened in the anteroposterior plane, and marginally larger than the right. The bulk of the right suprarenal gland sits on the superior pole of the right kidney and the bulk of the left suprarenal gland lies on the anteromedial aspect of the superior pole of the left kidney.
The dimensions of the suprarenal glands in adults have been measured in vivo from axial computed tomography (CT) (Vincent et al 1994). The mean maximum width of the suprarenal gland is approximately 50 mm, and the mean width of each limb of the gland (medial and lateral) is approximately 30-40 mm (Carsin-Vu et al 2016). No individual limb should measure more than 5 mm in transverse section. In adults, each suprarenal gland weighs approximately 5 g (the medulla contributes about one-tenth of the total weight) and has a volume of approximately 3–6 cm3 (Wang et al 2013).
At birth, the suprarenal glands are proportionately larger and are approximately one-third the size of the ipsilateral kidney. The cortex of each gland is expanded by a well-developed inner ‘fetal zone’, responsible for producing dehydroepiandrosterone (DHEA), the substrate for placental oestrogen in the fetus. The suprarenal cortex, specifically the fetal zone, shrinks immediately after birth and the medulla grows comparatively little. By the end of the second postnatal month, the weight of the suprarenal gland has decreased by more than 50%. The glands begin to grow again by the end of the second year of life and regain their weight at birth by puberty. There is little further weight increase in adult life.
Small accessory nodules composed mainly of cortical tissue, also known as ‘adrenal rests’, can occur in the areolar tissue near the suprarenal glands. They are also occasionally found in the spermatic cord, epididymis or testis (Altin et al 1992) and the ovary or broad ligament of the uterus. Ectopic suprarenal gland tissue can cause diagnostic confusion and is rarely the site of neoplastic change.

Right suprarenal gland

The right suprarenal gland lies posterior to the inferior vena cava, separated from it by only a thin layer of renal fascia and connective tissue. It also lies posterior to the right lobe of the liver and anterior to the right crus of the respiratory diaphragm and superior pole of the right kidney (Fig. 70.2). The renal surface of the right suprarenal gland overlaps the anterosuperior aspect of the superior pole of the right kidney, the two lower projections (limbs) of the gland straddling the kidney. The anterior surface has two distinct facets: a narrow medial facet that lies posterior to the inferior vena cava and a triangular lateral facet that lies in contact with the bare area of the liver. The most inferior part of the anterior surface may be covered by the peritoneal reflection of the coronary ligament, which also represents the upper aspect of the hepatorenal recess. Here, the gland lies posterior to the lateral border of the descending part of the duodenum. Below the superior border, near the anterior surface of the gland, the right suprarenal vein emerges from the hilum to join the inferior vena cava. This vein is short, which makes surgical resection of the gland or mobilization of the inferior vena cava potentially hazardous. It can be inadvertently avulsed from the inferior vena cava during surgery or, occasionally, by high-energy deceleration injuries. The posterior surface of the gland is divided into upper and lower areas by a curved transverse ridge. The larger upper area is slightly convex and abuts the respiratory diaphragm, whereas the smaller lower area is concave and lies in contact with the superior aspect of the superior pole of the right kidney. The medial border of the gland is thin and lies lateral to the right coeliac ganglion and the right inferior phrenic artery where the artery runs over the right crus of the respiratory diaphragm.

Left suprarenal gland

The left suprarenal gland lies close to the left crus of the respiratory diaphragm (Fig. 70.2). Its medial aspect is convex longitudinally, whereas the lateral aspect is concave because it is moulded by the medial surface of the superior pole of the left kidney. The superior border is sharply defined while the renal surface is more rounded. The upper part of the anterior surface is covered by the peritoneum of the posterior wall of the omental bursa, which separates it from the stomach and sometimes from the inferior border of the spleen. A small lower part of the anterior surface is not covered by peritoneum and lies adjacent to the pancreas and splenic artery. The hilum faces inferiorly from the lower part of the medial aspect. The left suprarenal vein emerges from the hilum and runs inferomedially to join the left renal vein. The posterior surface of the gland is divided by a ridge into a lateral area adjoining the kidney and a smaller medial area that lies in contact with the left crus of the respiratory diaphragm. The medial border lies lateral to the left coeliac ganglion and the left inferior phrenic and left gastric arteries, which ascend on the left crus of the diaphragm.

Vascular supply and lymphatic drainage

Arteries

The suprarenal glands have one of the highest arterial flow rates per gram of tissue (up to 5 ml/g/min) (Williams and Leggett 1989). Each gland is supplied by superior, middle and inferior suprarenal arteries, the main branches of which can be multiple (Fig. 70.3). These vessels ramify over the capsule before penetrating the gland to form a subcapsular arterial plexus, from which fenestrated sinusoids pass around clustered glomerulosa cells and between the columns of the zona fasciculata to a deep plexus in the zona reticularis. Venules from this plexus pass between medullary chromaffin cells to the central vein, which they enter between prominent bundles of smooth muscle fibres. Some relatively large arteries bypass this indirect route and pass directly to the medulla (Fig. 70.4). The arterial supply of the suprarenal gland is prone to considerable variation (Manso and DiDio 2000, Dutta 2010) and only the more common variants will be described here.
Superior suprarenal arteries
The superior suprarenal artery usually arises from the ipsilateral inferior phrenic artery and passes to the gland as four or five small branches. It occasionally arises from the abdominal aorta.
Middle suprarenal arteries
The middle suprarenal artery is usually single, but can be multiple or absent. It often arises from the lateral aspect of the abdominal aorta at around the level of the superior mesenteric artery and ascends slightly over the crura of the respiratory diaphragm to anastomose with the other suprarenal arteries on the surface of the suprarenal gland. The right middle suprarenal artery passes behind the inferior vena cava close to the right coeliac ganglion, whereas the left middle suprarenal artery passes close to the left coeliac ganglion, splenic artery and the superior border of the pancreas. The middle suprarenal artery can originate from either the ipsilateral inferior phrenic or renal artery.
Inferior suprarenal arteries
The inferior suprarenal arteries often contribute most of the arterial supply to the suprarenal gland. One or two arteries usually arise from the ipsilateral renal artery, but the inferior suprarenal arteries can originate from either the abdominal aorta or the ipsilateral gonadal artery.
Veins
The central vein emerges from the hilum of the suprarenal gland to form the suprarenal vein, which is usually single (Dobbie and Symington 1966). The right vein is very short and passes directly and horizontally into the posterior aspect of the inferior vena cava. An accessory right suprarenal vein is occasionally present and runs from the hilum superomedially to join the inferior vena cava above the right suprarenal vein. The short course renders the right suprarenal vein(s) vulnerable to injury or even avulsion from the inferior vena cava during surgery if undue traction is applied. The left suprarenal vein is longer and descends medially, anterolateral to the left coeliac ganglion, then passes posterior to the body of the pancreas. This vessel usually drains into the left renal vein and may receive the left inferior phrenic vein (Loukas et al 2005). Since the venous drainage from each suprarenal gland is usually via a single vein, infarction of the gland is more likely to be caused by injury to a suprarenal vein than to one of the suprarenal arteries.
Understanding the variant venous anatomy of the suprarenal gland is important to preclude haemorrhage during adrenalectomy. Cadaveric studies have shown little variation in suprarenal venous anatomy but suggest that variants are more common on the right (Cesmebasi et al 2014). However, a large surgical series of patients undergoing laparoscopic removal of the suprarenal gland found that 13% had variant venous anatomy for this structure (Scholten et al 2013). Common variants included an absent main suprarenal vein, accessory suprarenal veins, a branch to the inferior phrenic vein, and draining to the right hepatic vein on the right or, on the left, to the inferior vena cava.
Lymphatic drainage
Lymphatic vessels within the capsule of the suprarenal gland communicate with subcapsular lymphatics that drain medially to lumbar nodes (Merklin 1966, Skandalakis et al 2004). A few capsular lymphatics communicate with lymph vessels that pass through the respiratory diaphragm (Tokiwa et al 1993).

Innervation

Relative to its size, the suprarenal gland has a greater autonomic nerve supply than any other organ. The nerves are distributed throughout the gland, including around blood vessels, where they act to regulate blood flow; in the medulla, where they stimulate the release of catecholamines from chromaffin cells; in the cortex, where they can influence steroid hormone production (Tóth et al 1997). A suprarenal plexus lies between the medial aspect of each gland and the coeliac and aorticorenal ganglia. Cholinergic preganglionic sympathetic fibres travelling mainly in the greater thoracic splanchnic nerves reach the plexus and synapse on clusters of large medullary chromaffin cells. The precise spinal level of origin of these preganglionic fibres is uncertain and some may also travel in the lesser and least thoracic splanchnic nerves: cadaveric dissection studies in humans, both adult and fetal, have revealed significant differences not only in the origin and number of the splanchnic nerve rami but also in the communications between them (Naidoo et al 2001, Yang et al 2008, Loukas et al 2010). A smaller proportion of postganglionic sympathetic nerve fibres innervate cortical blood vessels. Other nerve fibres containing a variety of neurotransmitters have been identified within the suprarenal cortex and could be involved in modulating steroid hormone secretion (Li et al 1999). Experimental tracing studies in rats, guinea-pigs and marmosets found that the cell bodies of afferent nerve fibres arising in the suprarenal medulla were almost all located in dorsal root ganglia, and, less often, in the nodose ganglia of the vagus nerves (Coupland 1989, Parker et al 1993).

Microstructure and function

In section, the suprarenal glands have an outer cortex, which is yellowish in colour and forms the main mass. The suprarenal glands also have a thin medulla, forming about one-tenth of the gland, which is dark red or greyish depending on its content of blood (see Fig. 70.4; Fig. 70.5). The medulla is completely enclosed by cortex except at the hilum. The gland has a thick collagenous capsule from which trabeculae extend deep into the cortex. The capsule contains a rich arterial plexus that supplies branches to the suprarenal gland.

Suprarenal cortex

The suprarenal cortex is composed of a zona glomerulosa, zona fasciculata and zona reticularis (see Fig. 70.5). The outer, subcapsular, zona glomerulosa consists of a narrow region of small polyhedral cells arranged in rounded clusters. These cells have deeply staining nuclei and a basophilic cytoplasm containing a few lipid droplets. Ultrastructurally, they each have an abundant smooth endoplasmic reticulum that is typical of steroid-synthesizing cells. The broader, intermediate, zona fasciculata consists of large polyhedral basophilic cells arranged in straight columns, two cells wide, separated by parallel fenestrated venous sinusoids. These cells each contain many lipid droplets and large amounts of smooth endoplasmic reticulum. The innermost part of the cortex, the zona reticularis, consists of branching interconnected columns of rounded cells, each with a cytoplasm containing smooth endoplasmic reticulum, numerous lysosomes, and aggregates of brown lipofuscin pigment that accumulate with age.
Cortical cells produce several hormones: cells in the zona glomerulosa produce mineralocorticoids, e.g. aldosterone, which regulates electrolyte and water balance; cells in the zona fasciculata produce hormones maintaining carbohydrate balance, e.g. the glucocorticoid cortisol (hydrocortisone); and cells in the zona reticularis produce sex hormones, e.g. progesterone, oestrogens and androgens. The suprarenal cortex is essential for life and complete bilateral removal is lethal without replacement therapy.

Suprarenal medulla

The suprarenal medulla is composed of groups and columns of chromaffin cells (phaeochromocytes), separated by wide venous sinusoids and supported by a network of reticular fibres. Medullary chromaffin cells, so called from their colour reaction to dichromate fixatives, form part of the neuroendocrine system and are functionally equivalent to postganglionic sympathetic neurones. These cells store the catecholamines noradrenaline (norepinephrine) and adrenaline (epinephrine) and release them into the medullary venous plexus. Release is under preganglionic sympathetic control and is mediated by the sympathetic neurones that occur either singly or in small groups in the medulla.
Most medullary chromaffin cells synthesize adrenaline and store it in small granules with a dense core. The less numerous noradrenaline-secreting cells have larger granules with a dense eccentric core. Some cells synthesize both hormones. Chromogranin proteins package catecholamines within the granules, which also contain encephalins, opiate-like proteins that have endogenous analgesic effects under some circumstances. All of the cells are large, with large nuclei and basophilic, faintly granular cytoplasm. They form single rows along the venous sinusoids. Sympathetic axon terminals synapse with the medullary chromaffin cells on the surfaces that face away from the sinusoids.
The sinusoids are lined by a fenestrated endothelium and drain to the central vein and suprarenal vein. Under normal circumstances, little adrenaline or noradrenaline is released. Secretion of these substances increases in response to fear, anger or stress. Unlike the cortex, the suprarenal medulla is not essential for life.

Neoplastic lesions of the suprarenal glands

Several neoplastic lesions can be encountered arising from the suprarenal glands, including adenomas, phaeochromocytomas, myelolipomas, oncocytomas, cysts, adrenocortical carcinomas (ACC), and metastases from other malignancies. Many of these lesions require surgical resection, and therefore a firm understanding of suprarenal gland anatomy is critical for their successful treatment. In this section the most common and clinically relevant lesions of the suprarenal gland are reviewed.

Adenomas

Adenomas most commonly arise from the zona glomerulosa or the zona fasciculata of the suprarenal cortex. Up to 10% of incidentally detected suprarenal gland adenomas are functional and autonomously produce hormones (Mantero et al 1997). These lesions, which are composed of lipid-rich cells, commonly show low attenuation (less than 10 Hounsfield units) on unenhanced computed tomography (CT) (Young 2007, Johnson et al 2009). Alternatively, suprarenal adenomas can be detected on CT by their characteristic high degree of washout (greater than 60%) on delayed phase imaging performed 10–15 minutes after contrast administration. Suprarenal adenomas can also be reliably detected on chemical shift magnetic resonance imaging (MRI).
Functional adenomas arising from the zona glomerulosa cause a symptom complex known as Conn’s syndrome. This syndrome is characterized by hypertension, hypokalaemia and metabolic alkalosis. Bilateral adrenal hyperplasia can cause identical symptoms and must be distinguished from an aldosterone-producing adenoma, as the treatment of hyperplasia is medical with aldosterone-blocking medications, such as spironolactone, whereas aldosterone-producing adenomas require surgical excision. The two conditions can typically be differentiated on the basis of imaging findings or with selective suprarenal vein sampling, which is indicated in elderly patients who are prone to equivocal imaging findings that can lead to incorrect lateralization of the source of mineralocorticoid excess. Primary hyperaldosteronism is diagnosed by measuring morning plasma aldosterone and renin levels (Fassnacht et al 2016). A high ratio between the two hormones (typically defined as an aldosterone:renin ratio greater than 20) is indicative of disorders of aldosterone excess. Some recommend a salt loading test as further confirmation of the diagnosis.
Adenomas arising from the zona fasciculata can result in cortisol excess or what is known as Cushing’s syndrome. This syndrome is clinically characterized by central obesity, increased adiposity/rounding of the face (moon face), fat deposition between the shoulders (buffalo hump), hypertension, glucose intolerance, fatigue, abdominal striae and easy bruising. Functional adenomas causing cortisol excess also require surgical excision.
An autonomous cortisol-producing adenoma is diagnosed after observing an elevated cortisol level on an overnight 1 mg dexamethasone suppression test (Fassnacht et al 2016). This finding can be confirmed with the late-night salivary cortisol test or with a 24-hour urinary cortisol level. Other conditions that cause cortisol excess include exogenous steroid use, pituitary gland adenomas (also known as Cushing’s disease), adrenocortical carcinoma (ACC) and paraneoplastic syndromes. None of these alternative causes of cortisol excess will show suppression on the overnight 1 mg dexamethasone suppression test. However, Cushing’s disease will show suppression with a higher 2 mg dose of dexamethasone. Through a combination of imaging findings and metabolic testing, the precise cause of cortisol excess can typically be identified and addressed.
While rare, adenomas can also arise from the zona reticularis. These tumours can lead to excess production of sex hormones leading to virilization in women and feminization in men. Because of the rarity of these lesions, metabolic testing for sex hormones is not indicated in asymptomatic patients who present with an incidentally detected suprarenal gland tumour. However, it is worth noting that ACCs commonly present with symptoms of excess sex steroid production and patients presenting with related symptoms should be evaluated with an eye for this potentially aggressive malignancy.
Phaeochromocytomas
Phaeochromocytomas are catecholamine-producing tumours that arise from the medulla of the suprarenal glands. They are most commonly sporadic and unilateral; however, several hereditary syndromes can give rise to multifocal lesions. Syndromes associated with the development of phaeochromocytomas include von Hippel–Lindau disease, neurofibromatosis type 1, succinate dehydrogenase deficiency, and multiple endocrine neoplasia 2A and 2B.
Phaeochromocytomas commonly result in symptoms of catecholamine excess, including hypertension, tachycardia, headache and hyperhidrosis. On CT, phaeochromocytomas are highly vascular and typically display delayed contrast washout (Young 2007). On T2-weighted MRI, these lesions are markedly hyperintense and display the characteristic ‘light bulb sign’. Phaeochromocytomas have the potential for metastatic spread, which can be assessed with contrast-enhanced cross-sectional imaging (CT or MRI), or functional imaging with either 131I-metaiodobenzylguanidine single-photon imaging or 2-deoxy-2-[18F]fluoro-D-glucose positron emission tomography.
The presence of a phaeochromocytoma is confirmed with metabolic testing for plasma-free metanephrines or urinary fractionated metanephrines (Fassnacht et al 2016). Phaeochromocytomas require surgical resection with adrenalectomy. Prior to surgery, alpha-adrenergic blockade, most commonly with phenoxybenzamine, is needed to prevent an intraoperative hypertensive crisis.
Myelolipomas
Myelolipomas are benign non-metabolically active tumours of the suprarenal gland that are composed of trilineage haemopoietic precursor cells and mature fat. These lesions can be distinguished on CT from other suprarenal masses by the finding of large areas of macroscopic fat with negative Hounsfield units. Myelolipomas are most commonly asymptomatic and do not require treatment or follow-up imaging. Treatment in the form of surgical resection is only indicated for symptom management.
Adrenocortical carcinoma (ACC)
ACC is a rare primary malignancy of the suprarenal gland. Most cases of ACC are sporadic, unilateral and present at an advanced stage. Upwards of 50% of ACCs have hypersecretion of one or more of the cortical suprarenal gland hormones (Terzolo et al 2007). ACCs tend to be larger than other suprarenal gland tumours, with an average size of approximately 10 cm at presentation (Gröndal et al 1990). It is estimated that 10–20% of all suprarenal gland tumours greater than 4 cm are ACCs, so surgical resection should be considered for tumours meeting this size cut-off that are otherwise without the imaging hallmarks of a benign tumour (Young 2007, Azoury et al 2017). On CT imaging, ACCs tend to have irregular borders, calcifications and areas of necrosis. Additionally, extension into adjacent organs or the inferior vena cava is common.
Surgical excision is the mainstay of treatment for ACC. En bloc resection of the tumour and any involved organs should performed. A regional lymphadenectomy is also commonly performed for the purposes of disease control and tumour staging. Surgical resection is indicated for patients with localized and locally advanced disease. For patients with metastatic disease, primary tumour resection is indicated to alleviate symptoms. This is typically performed in combination with metastasectomy. Following surgical resection, consideration should be given to administration of adjuvant mitotane, a derivative of the insecticide dichlorodiphenyltrichloroethane (DDT), which inhibits steroidogenesis and remains the first line agent for metastatic ACC (Fassnacht et al 2018). The prognosis of ACC is quite poor, with 5-year survival rates often reported as less than 50% (Kim et al 2016).
Other metastases
The suprarenal glands are a common site for metastases from other malignancies. One study found that in patients with a history of a previous malignancy, approximately 50% of newly discovered suprarenal gland lesions were metastatic (Lenert et al 2001). Tumours that commonly metastasize to the suprarenal glands include melanoma, renal cell carcinoma, lung cancers and breast cancer. Metastatic lesions can be distinguished from primary lesions of the suprarenal gland through a combination of clinical history, imaging findings (lesions will be greater than 10 Hounsfield units on non-contrasted CT and will enhance) and percutaneous biopsy. Depending on the underlying malignancy and extent of metastatic spread, some metastases can be treated with surgical resection while others require systemic therapy.

Suprarenal gland excision

One or both of the suprarenal glands may be removed using ‘open surgery’, which remains the standard of care for patients with ACC who require en bloc resection of their tumour. For other lesions of the suprarenal gland requiring surgery, minimally invasive techniques are now generally preferred. The original laparoscopic approaches for removal of the suprarenal gland were described during the 1990s and have since been adopted throughout most of the world (Gagner et al 1992, Mercan et al 1995).
Both the open and minimally invasive techniques for removal of the suprarenal gland can be performed via a transabdominal or retroperitoneal approach. The retroperitoneal approach offers the advantage of remaining outside the abdominal cavity and so avoids encountering adhesions and potentially causing iatrogenic injury to other organs. Advantages of the transabdominal approach include a larger working space and more familiar anatomical relations. Ultimately, the choice of surgical technique is dictated by a combination of surgeon experience and patient-specific factors.
