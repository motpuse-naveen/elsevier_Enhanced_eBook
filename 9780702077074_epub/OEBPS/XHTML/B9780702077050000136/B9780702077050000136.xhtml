Chapter 13: Development of the heart and circulation

The early embryonic circulation is symmetric (Fig. 13.1). It is modified throughout development to produce a functioning heart and fetal circulation that is connected to the placenta, and changes rapidly at birth to accommodate disconnection from the placenta and the start of gaseous exchange in the lungs. Major restructuring of early vessels occurs as the embryo grows; anastomoses form and then disappear, capillaries fuse and give rise to arteries or veins, and the direction of blood flow may reverse several times before the final arrangement of vessels is completed. Embryonic cell populations arise and become restricted to a cardiovascular phenotype in spatially and temporally defined interactions during the earliest stages of development.
Cells from mesenchyme derived from the midline splanchnopleuric coelomic epithelium initially form both the myocardium and the endocardial lining. Later, this splanchnopleuric coelomic epithelium is also the source of the epicardium, the coronary arteries and the interstitial fibroblasts of the heart. (The epicardium, sometimes included in descriptions of the myocardium as ‘epimyocardium’, is not present at early stages of heart development.) A further cell population of head neural crest mesenchyme that arises from the region between the otic vesicle and the caudal limit of somite 3 later migrates to the arterial pole of the heart and contributes to the formation of the valves and aortic arch arteries (see Fig. 17.9).
The position of the developing myocardium is intimately related to the formation of the intraembryonic coelom and the relative movement of that tubular structure during neurulation and somitogenesis. The splanchnopleuric wall of the early midline intraembryonic coelom, directly adjacent to the endoderm, is a horseshoe-shaped proliferative epithelium that produces cardiogenic mesenchyme. It is termed the cardiac crescent or primary heart-forming field (Fig. 13.2) and is characterized by the expression of myocardium-specific markers, such as cardiac myosin and the cardiac transcription factor Nkx2-5. The area of the coelomic epithelium dorsomedial to the primary heart-forming field will later also add to the forming heart tube and is therefore named the secondary heart-forming field (see Fig. 13.2). These two myocardial fields may represent distinct myocardial lineages; they both express Islet1 but they sequentially add to different parts of the heart.
The endocardium that lines the cardiac lumen develops from the coelomic splanchnopleuric epithelium during stages 8 and 9. Individual endocardial cells arise from it close to the ventrolateral edges of the cranial intestinal portal and form an endocardial plexus between the splanchnopleuric coelomic epithelium and the foregut endoderm. This plexus of cells, formed initially by vasculogenesis and subsequently from angioblastic mesenchyme, is one of the earliest intraembryonic vascular precursors to appear. The cells express molecular markers of both the myocardial and the endothelial cell lineages, indicating that the endocardium has a different origin than other endothelial populations. The cells of the endocardial plexus aggregate to form two separate endocardial epithelial tubes that fuse across the midline, from the arterial pole to the venous pole, producing a single linear heart tube.
The development of the early heart is described below, followed by the formation of the early compartments of the heart and their later septation.

Formation of the Heart Tube

During the process of embryonic head folding, the midline splanchnopleuric coelomic epithelium (now cardiogenic epithelium) becomes positioned ventral to the foregut; the portion of the intraembryonic coelomic cavity it surrounds is the future pericardial cavity (Fig. 13.3). Populations of cardiac myocytes now arise from the ventral proliferating coelomic epithelium and surround the endocardial linear heart tube forming the early myocardium; these cells will have undergone restriction, differentiation and most will have left the cell cycle (Ch. 11). The secondary heart-forming field proliferates and adds cells to this bilaminar heart tube after embryonic head folding. It has been suggested that the secondary heart-forming field only contributes cells to those cardiac components that are required for the pulmonary circulation: namely, the right ventricle and outflow tract at the arterial pole, and the dorsal atrial wall and atrial septum at the venous pole. However, a type of secondary heart-forming field can also be distinguished in zebrafish and other chordates that lack a pulmonary circulation, implying that it is evolutionarily conserved independent of the presence of a pulmonary circulation (Sizarov et al 2016).
The pericardial cavity and myocardium gradually extend around the forming endocardial tube, leaving the dorsal mesocardium as a transient connection between the forming heart tube and the dorsal pericardial wall, analogous to the mesentery of the intestines (see Fig. 13.3C–D). The dorsal mesocardium is contiguous with the splanchnopleuric mesenchyme that surrounds the embryonic foregut and the developing lung buds and is the site of early mediastinal mesenchyme production. It disappears as a mesenteric entity in embryos with 8–12 somites. The partial breakdown of the dorsal mesocardium creates the transverse sinus of the pericardium that extends across the pericardial cavity from side to side, dorsal to the heart. Persisting dorsal mesocardium at the venous pole remains connected to the splanchnopleuric mesenchyme in which the pulmonary veins will develop; it is also confluent with the septum transversum mesenchyme, in which the liver will later develop.
By stage 10 (28–29 days postfertilization; embryonic stages 10–23 correlated to postfertilization days of development and postmenstrual weeks of pregnancy are shown in Fig. 23.3) a single endocardial tube is established that is almost completely surrounded by myocardial cells. This arrangement of an outer myocardial sleeve containing an inner endocardial tube constitutes the primary heart tube. The primary myocardium will not only give rise to the major parts of the conduction system of the heart, including the sinus node, the atrioventricular node, and the atrioventricular bundle, but also to part of the floor of the right atrium (the vestibule) and the entire atrioventricular canal. An enlarging space between the endocardial tube and the developing myocardium is filled by a meshwork of cardiac extracellular matrix proteins, produced by the cardiac myocytes, which promotes occlusion of some parts of the early heart tube during myocardial contraction and mechanical assistance for the generation of flow of early blood. There are heterochronic differences between species in these processes, for example, in the mouse, cardiac chambers start to form before a distinct linear heart tube has appeared.

Endocardial cushions

The extracellular matrix of the embryonic heart (see Fig. 13.3C–D), historically termed cardiac jelly, is composed of hyaluronic acid, hyaluronidase and fibronectin among other components. Inductive signals originating from the cardiac myocytes, such as BMP2 and TGF-β, lead to the activation of a subset of endocardial epithelial cells that will line the future atrioventricular canal and the proximal outflow tract; in the future left ventricle, endocardial cells do not undergo such an activation. The activated endocardial cells lose their cell-to-cell connections and undergo an epithelial to mesenchymal transition. Their expression of neural cell adhesion molecule (N-CAM) decreases and expression of substrate adhesion molecules such as chondroitin sulphate and fibronectin increases. Expression of type I procollagen is initiated and cytoskeletal re-arrangements facilitate cell migration into the cardiac extracellular matrix where they continue to proliferate. The cells and the locally accumulated extracellular matrix molecules, form the endocardial or cardiac cushions. Endocardial cushions initially function as valves in the embryonic heart: they give rise to the valves of the atrioventricular canal and the outflow tract and also supply the mesenchyme that is needed to complete the septation of the atria, ventricles and the outflow tract. This particular epithelial to mesenchymal transition may be the only example of a mesenchymal population derived from an endothelial lineage; endothelial to mesenchymal transformation may be relevant to later circulatory pathologies (Suzuki et al 2018, Kovacic et al 2019).

Looping of the heart tube

The early primary heart tube is initially bilaterally symmetric and has the form of an inverted ‘Y’. The caudal ‘legs’ of the Y are positioned on either side of the cranial intestinal portal, where they form the venous pole of the heart (Fig. 13.4A–C). The rostral ‘stem’ of the Y connects cranially with the future aortic arch arteries, forming the arterial pole of the heart. From the start, both poles are connected with the systemic vessels that run from the embryo, yolk sac and placenta. The pulmonary veins form later, their initial venous primordium becoming evident in the dorsal mesocardial mesenchyme. Myocardium from the secondary heart-forming field continues to be added to the heart, forming the smooth dorsal wall of the left atrium, and providing the site where the primary atrial septum will form.
The putative cardiac segments are not present in the early linear heart tube. The definitive cardiac chambers are formed by local differentiation and expansion, or ‘ballooning’, of the myocardial walls of the primary heart tube, indicating that cardiac myocyte identity is dependent on genetic, spatial and temporal signals and lacks a fixed ontogeny. Genetic fate map studies have shown that the primary heart tube will contribute to only two compartments: the legs of the inverted Y of the primary heart tube contain precursor cells of the atrioventricular canal, while the stem of the inverted Y contains myocardial precursor cells of the left ventricle (van Eif et al 2018). The atrioventricular canal precursors finally reside in the left ventricular free wall and the cells of the original embryonic left ventricle largely end up in the left face of the ventricular septum. The embryonic outflow tract contributes to the right ventricle.
The nodal/pitx2/cited-2 signalling pathway that determines the formation of the morphologically left-sided or right-sided features in the lungs, bronchial tree, liver and spleen is also expressed in the heart outflow tract and atrial appendages (Ocaña et al 2017). The lengthening heart tube bends ventrally and rightwards, concomitant with the partial breakdown of the dorsal mesocardium. This bend is now named the ventricular loop because the left ventricle will balloon from its outer curvature, the original ventral side of the straight tube; the inner curvature of this loop is the original dorsal side.
At the start of stage 10 the venous pole, the atrioventricular canal, the developing left ventricle and the outflow tract are all positioned symmetrically around the midline (Fig. 13.5A). As a result of the subsequent rapid lengthening of the cardiac tube, the atrioventricular canal moves in its entirety to the left (Fig. 13.5B), and the ventricular part of the heart tube loops to the right, thus placing the developing left ventricle on the left and the forming right ventricle on the right side of the midline of the body. The atrial floor, including the developing systemic sinus venosus and the atrioventricular canal moves to the right (Fig. 13.5C). The developing pulmonary vein remains anchored in the midline and after these manoeuvres, the atrioventricular canal again becomes positioned in the midline; these movements facilitate the appropriate connections of the developing muscular ventricular septum with the atrioventricular and outflow cushions. The orifice of the sinus venosus is now positioned to the right. From the outset, the muscular ventricular septum develops in line with the right side of the dorsal atrioventricular cushion so that the right atrium always has direct access to the developing right ventricle.

Cardiac gene expression, differentiation, contraction, conduction and automaticity

Cardiac differentiation and morphogenesis are intimately related processes, achieved by a still obscure transcriptional network. It is known that the cardiac transcription factor Nkx2–5, which has a homogeneous distribution over the embryonic heart tube, is generally important for cardiac-specific gene expression. In the human, mutations in this factor cause atrial septal defects and disturbances of atrioventricular conduction; in Drosophila, the heart will not form in its absence. The discovery of the involvement of locally expressed T-box transcription factors, provides some evidence of the further complexity of the patterning of the heart (Fig. 13.6). For example, whereas Tbx5 confers caudocranial positional information over the heart tube, determining the regional development of the cardiac chambers along these axes, Tbx2 and Tbx3 prevent regional formation of the cardiac chambers, permitting the myocardium in these regions to differentiate into the conduction system. Tbx18 is involved in the development of the sinus venosus while Tbx1 is necessary for the development of the outflow tract of the heart.
Most cardiac myocytes cease to divide after their differentiation to myocardium (Ahuja et al 2007). The primary heart tube continues to elongate as additional myocytes are recruited from the secondary heart-forming field. Hypertrophy of cardiac myocytes is not thought to be part of the process in this elongation as in mouse embryos, cardiac myocyte volume remains at approximately 2 ×10-3μm3 throughout gestation (de Boer et al 2012). Cardiac myocytes share a number of characteristic features that distinguish them from other cells: they are mononuclear, have sarcomeres and a sarcoplasmic reticulum and are joined to adjacent myocytes by the gap-junctional proteins connexin 40 and 43 (van Eif et al 2018). The expression of sarcomeric proteins is seen in primitive cardiomyocytes in stage 9 embryos. All cardiac myocytes share the capacity for producing an intrinsic cycle of electrical activity, i.e. the capacity to generate and conduct a depolarizing impulse that results in contraction (the cells are myogenic): the phenomenon is called automaticity, or pacemaker activity. Because the cells are electrically coupled the fastest pacemaking activity will take the lead. In embryonic hearts, the leading pacemaker is always found at the venous pole which corresponds to the sinuatrial node in the adult heart. There is evidence for the presence of a tertiary heart-forming field specific for pacemaker formation (Liang et al 2017).
All of the cardiac conduction system is myocardial in origin. The nodes, sinuatrial and atrioventricular, and the atrioventricular bundle constitute the central conduction system, while the bundle branches and their terminal ramifications, the Purkinje fibres, represent the peripheral ventricular conduction system. The development of the conduction system of the heart is intimately related to overall heart formation and the blood flow through the heart, embryo and placenta. The underlying gene-regulatory networks have been conserved during vertebrate evolution. Each phase of heart development shows different patterns of spontaneous electrical activation and contraction (Watanabe et al 2016).
Varying degrees of differentiation seen in early populations of cardiac myocytes can be categorized as forming primary, nodal, conducting and working myocardium (Table 13.1). There is a relationship between the degree of apparent differentiation and extent of the coupling between myocytes, and the occurrence of slow or fast wave propagation and corresponding contraction. All regions of the early embryonic heart tube have poorly coupled cells and display intrinsic automaticity (Video 13.1). This produces slow wave propagation of the depolarizing impulse along the heart tube and matching peristaltic waves of contraction that push the blood in an antegrade direction. That part of the early heart tube wall termed primary myocardium retains this phenotype; it shows poorly developed sarcoplasmic reticulum and sarcomeric structures. Poor electrical coupling and slow conduction seen in nodal myocytes is an absolute requirement for effective pacemaking because it allows the cells to build up sufficient electrical charge, which is then propagated through the surrounding myocardium. Cells that differentiate into working myocardium display virtually no automaticity. They have well developed sarcomeres and sarcoplasmic reticular structures, and contiguous cells show well developed gap junctions expressing connexins 40 and 43. The cells of the putative atrioventricular and peripheral ventricular conduction system have an ambiguous phenotype: they are well coupled, thus allowing fast conduction of the depolarizing impulse, but otherwise retain an embryonic phenotype. Myocytes of the atrioventricular bundles and Purkinje fibres have a phenotype between that of working and primary myocardium. Distinguishing conduction myocardium from working myocardium may suggest that the working myocardium does not conduct, while the conduction system conducts rapidly. However, to produce powerful synchronous contractions, the working myocardium must also conduct rapidly. Unambiguous morphological markers are often lacking in the early embryonic heart, which means that it is not possible to distinguish the various cells phenotypically; automaticity and the speed of conduction have therefore become important functional parameters with which to describe the development of the different parts of the heart.

Peristaltic hearts do not need valves, whereas chambered hearts require the presence of one-way valves at the inlets to, and the outlets from, the chambers. During development, specific regions of mesenchyme and cardiac extracellular matrix fulfil this function. The sequential arrangement of slow and fast conduction portions of the early myocardium is key to its early development. The fast conducting working myocardium of the atrial and ventricular chambers permits nearly simultaneous contraction, increasing the velocity of the forward pressure of blood. The slow conducting primary myocardium within the atrioventricular junction and outflow tract is adjacent to endocardial cushions formed from locally accumulated cardiac extracellular matrix. These regions have longer, slower closure and are thus able to function as sphincteric valves, preventing retrograde flow of blood. The longer closure permits discrete and successive contraction of the atrial chambers followed by the ventricular chambers: contraction in the ventricles at these early stages passes from base to apex. Conduction myocytes originate from part of a primary interventricular ring that becomes positioned at the atrioventricular fibrous ring, close to the interventricular septum in the adult heart (see Fig. 13.10). For further reading about the electrophysiology of the developing chick heart, see Watanabe et al (2016).

Compartments of the Heart

The future atrial and ventricular components of the developing heart can be distinguished once the primary heart tube has formed its ventricular loop (Fig. 13.7; see Figs 13.4, 13.5A). The region where the systemic venous tributaries join the primary heart tube is called the inflow tract of the heart. Within the primary heart tube, the developing atrial and ventricular chambers can be discerned as bulges that are separated by the atrioventricular canal. The ventricles connect to the outflow tract which, in a later stage, will septate and give rise to the pulmonary trunk and the aorta.

Inflow tract

Initially, blood flows into the heart via a sinus venosus, a confluence of bilateral umbilical, vitelline and cardinal venous channels (see Fig. 13.1). The sinus enlarges forming right and left sinus horns; a process under the control of the T-box transcription factor Tbx18. The common cardinal veins, the main contributors to the sinus horns, become surrounded by cardiomyocytes (van den Berg and Moorman 2011). With later development of the septum transversum and hepatic blood vessels, modifications of the cardinal system of veins and the terminal portions of the vitelline and umbilical veins form a main hepatocardiac channel that directs blood from the caudal part of the embryo towards the right horn of the sinus venosus (Fig. 13.8; see Figs 13.17, 13.21, 13.22). The sinus wall is thickened dorsally where the right and left sinus horns meet: this is the sinus septum that will give rise to the roof of the orifice of the future coronary sinus.

Inflow tract anomalies

The most common systemic venous anomaly is a persistent left superior vena cava that drains into the right atrium through an enlarged orifice of the coronary sinus (Povoski and Khabiri 2011): it can be caused by defects in the Robo1 and Robo2 receptors (Mommersteeg et al 2013). The inferior vena cava can have an interrupted abdominal course and drain to the heart via the azygos or hemiazygos venous system (see Figs 13.4, 13.18), an aberration found most frequently with left isomerism of the atrial appendages.

Sinuatrial junction

The sinus venosus becomes incorporated into the right atrium proper (see Fig. 13.8C), distinguished from the common atrium by a sinuatrial valve. The growth of the sinuatrial valve is slower than that of the surrounding myocardium and the valve leaflets lose their competence over time. The sinuatrial orifice becomes elongated and slit-like because of this differential growth. The two sinuatrial valve leaflets that flank it meet cranially to form the septum spurium. Later, a cranial part of the right sinuatrial valve leaflet, caudal to the septum spurium, becomes continuous with the crista terminalis, a ridge that projects from the atrial roof. More caudally, the right sinuatrial valve leaflet forms the valve of the inferior vena cava (Eustachian valve) and of the original left superior vena cava, later the valve of the coronary sinus (Thebesian valve). When this process is disturbed, a venous network (Chiari’s network) can develop from the right sinuatrial valve leaflet (Figueras-Coll et al 2017).
As development proceeds, the left-sided systemic venous tributaries diminish in size. The left common cardinal vein (later the left superior vena cava) forms the oblique vein of the left atrium (also known as the oblique vein of Marshall), while the remainder of the left sinus horn is incorporated into the left atrioventricular junction forming the coronary sinus (see Fig. 13.8C). The coronary sinus retains its own myocardial wall, histologically distinct from the left atrial wall, although some merging may take place.
At the sinus venosus side of the sinuatrial junction, cardiac nodal myocytes form a specialized sinuatrial node able to initiate the electrical impulses that activate the atrial myocardium and heart, and lead to its contraction. This node consists of distinct morphological regions and is supplied by a relatively large artery. It forms under the influence of Tbx18 and its differentiation is regulated by Tbx3. Later, when the dorsal wall of the sinus venosus lies in line with the atrial myocardium, lateral to the sinuatrial valve leaflets, and the sinuatrial valve has lost its competence, this region is termed the sinus venarum: it is smooth-walled and forms the dorsal wall of the right atrium. In adult humans, the sinus venosus myocardium contracts simultaneously with that of the right atrium and can therefore be considered to be incorporated functionally into the right atrium.

Sinus venosus anomalies

Interatrial communications can occur where the venae cavae join the sinus venosus and are most frequently noted for the superior vena cava (Jensen et al 2016). These defects are usually associated with drainage of the right pulmonary veins into the sinuatrial junction. Known as sinus venosus defects, their essential feature is a bi-atrial connection of the involved vena cava (Keyte et al 2014). Defective signalling by the guidance molecule Semaphorin 3d, which normally ensures that forming pulmonary veins connect to the left atrium, could underlie this malformation (Degenhardt et al 2013). An interatrial communication can occur through the mouth of the coronary sinus when there is a deficiency or absence of the walls that usually separate the left sinus horn or future coronary sinus from the left atrium (Ootaki et al 2003).

Common atrium

The working myocardium of the primitive atrium differentiates at the dorsal and lateral sides of the primary heart tube (Sylva et al 2013) (see Fig. 13.4D–G). The developing atrium then expands, ballooning in dorsal, lateral and cranial directions. The cranial expansion is pouch-shaped and forms the left and right atrial appendage (see Fig. 13.7A–B). Although it is possible to distinguish the left from the right atrial appendage around stage 14, the right being more extensive than the left, the atrium itself contains only a single cavity at this stage. The onset of primary septum formation is initially visible in the atrial roof. During atrial septation, the sinuatrial junction moves from a symmetric, midline position to a right-sided position (see Figs 13.5, 13.8, 13.20).

Right atrium

In the developing right atrium, the united remnants of the sinuatrial valve leaflets, caudal to the valve of the coronary sinus (Thebesian valve), continue to pass through the tissue that separates the orifice of the coronary sinus from the foramen ovale. These remnants form the tendon of Todaro (Ch. 57): the atrioventricular node of the fully formed heart is located at the end of this structure. The left sinuatrial valve leaflet blends with the right side of the atrial septum and there is usually no trace of it in the postnatal heart. The inner aspect of the sinus venosus myocardium and that of the newly forming mediastinal myocardium, derived from the dorsal mesocardium, is smooth, whereas the myocardium of the atrial appendages is ridged on its inner surface, forming the pectinate muscles.
The secondary heart-forming field myocardium of the atrial appendages is working myocardium and expresses the markers atrial natriuretic factor and connexin 40, like the ventricular myocardium at this stage. The mediastinal myocardium is connexin 40-positive but atrial natriuretic factor-negative. Connexin 40-negative primary myocardium marks the inflow to, and the outflow from, the initial atrial chambers as ‘rings’. The ring at the inflow defines the sinuatrial junction, and the atrioventricular canal ring forms the atrial outlets during development. The original atrioventricular canal is present within the definitive right and left atrial chambers as the atrial vestibules. These Tbx3-positive, connexin 40-negative rings initially lie parallel in the primary heart tube, but they become separated. The atrial floor or vestibule normally becomes Tbx3-negative and connexin 40-positive as a result of the ingrowth of myocardium from the secondary heart-forming field, while the sinus node and atrioventricular node, derivatives from the primary myocardium, retain their molecular phenotype. Connexin 40-negative remnants of the primary myocardium have been reported in the atrial floor and Tbx3-positive cardiomyocytes are found around the mouth of the coronary sinus: while these cardiomyocytes do not form a separate functional tract, they might be the substrate for arrhythmias.

Left atrium

Following the formation of the left atrial appendage and the primary atrial septum, the left atrium takes shape by the incorporation of mediastinal myocardium. While the tributaries of the sinus venosus approach the right atrium caudally and cranially, the differentiating pulmonary veins gain their entrance to the left atrial cavity through the dorsal mesocardium (see Fig. 13.3). The definitive topographical relationships between vessels and heart seen postnatally are therefore only established once differentiation of the pulmonary venous portal occurs.
Around stage 14, simultaneously with atrial septation, the pulmonary vein develops as a solitary channel from angiogenic cells derived from the dorsal mesocardium. This channel establishes continuity with the vascular plexus formed in the mediastinal mesenchyme around the developing lung buds. The initially solitary pulmonary vein, guided by Semaphorin 3d signals, later opens into the caudo-dorsal wall of the atrium adjacent to the developing atrioventricular junction, where a depression termed the pulmonary pit, flanked by the pulmonary ridges, has already been established (Degenhardt et al 2013).
The primary atrial septum develops from the right pulmonary ridge, confining the pulmonary venous orifice to the developing left atrium (see Fig. 13.11). The pulmonary vein initially branches within the dorsal mediastinal mesenchyme, its tributaries draining blood from the developing lung. With continuing development, the walls of the pulmonary venous channels become surrounded by myocardium; this process occurs up to and beyond the level of the second bifurcation. The veins then expand and their walls become incorporated into the roof of the left atrium, eventually forming the greater part of its cavity. The four pulmonary veins do not achieve their separate opening into the atrial roof until well after septum formation is completed, around postmenstrual week 11. The left half of the primary atrium becomes progressively restricted to the mature appendage. The myocardial sleeves that surround the pulmonary orifices taper off distally and become intermingled with fibrous tissue. In adult life, it is likely that this intermingling of myocardial and fibrous tissues forms the substrate for some forms of atrial fibrillation. Variations in the precise pattern of pulmonary venous drainage are quite common.
Anomalous pulmonary venous connections and cor triatriatum sinistrum
The developing common pulmonary vein migrates towards the heart before connecting to the pulmonary pit, but the correct connection may not occur (van den Berg and Moorman 2011). In total anomalous pulmonary venous connection, which occurs in about 7/100,000 neonates, no pulmonary veins enter the left atrium (Wu et al 2018) and the more primitive connection between the splanchnic plexus that surrounds the lungs with the right atrial inflow tract persists (Kao et al 2017): these vessels do not become surrounded by myocardium (Douglas et al 2009). In case of a partial anomalous pulmonary venous connection, one or more connections with the left atrium will be established alongside this primitive conjunction between cardinal veins and the splanchnic plexus. It has been suggested that these anomalies occur when there is a defect in the Semaphorin 3d signalling that would normally act as a repelling guiding factor (Degenhardt et al 2013).
In cor triatriatum sinistrum, a very rare anomaly, the left atrium is subdivided into two compartments separated by a full or partial membrane. The pulmonary venous confluence is guarded from the rest of the atrial body. There is evidence that mutations in the enzyme HYAL2 can lead to cor triatriatum sinistrum in humans (Muggenthaler et al 2017). The membrane might be an outgrowth of a ridge that is naturally found between the left superior pulmonary vein and the atrial appendage. Often this defect coincides with a septal defect, anomalous pulmonary venous connections or a patent ductus arteriosus.

Ventricles

The ventricles develop from the ventral side of the looping primary heart tube (see Figs 13.4, 13.7). The left ventricle develops from the rostral stem of the inverted, Y-shaped primary heart tube. The right ventricle develops later, downstream relative to the left ventricle, after more myocardium has been added to the cardiac tube from the secondary heart-forming field. As a consequence of the heart tube looping, the right ventricle is positioned to the right of the left ventricle, which is a prerequisite for forming the appropriate connections with the expanding atrial component of the heart. Unlike the atrial chambers, the morphological differences between the right and left ventricles are not part of the general asymmetry between the right- and left-sided organs of the body, but under the control of signalling pathways that determine caudocranial differentiation, such as retinoic acid and its downstream transcription factor Tbx5, which play a crucial role in this process. The Hand transcription factors also play a role in left ventricular growth and endocardial cushion maturation (Vincentz et al 2017).

Formation of the ventricular wall

The myocardium at the inner curvature of the looped heart, which is the original dorsal side of the primary heart tube, remains smooth-walled, whereas the myocardium at the outer curvature of the myocardial tube displays trabeculae as early as stage 10 (see Fig. 13.7C). By stage 17 the trabeculae have achieved a typical spatial organization, giving a sponge-like appearance to the internal aspect of both ventricles. Embryonic trabeculae are a few tens of micrometres in width, and diffusion with early luminal blood is sufficient to maintain cardiac cellular homeostasis, which means that the increase of cardiac mass by trabecular addition can continue in the absence of a coronary circulation (Fig. 13.9). Trabecular cardiomyocytes proliferate much less and exhibit more mitochondrial activity than compact wall cardiomyocytes, and are likely to be the main contributor to pressure generation. As development proceeds, a portion of the embryonic trabeculae on the luminal side of the ventricles stops proliferating and differentiates into the fast-conducting peripheral ventricular conduction system that includes the bundle branches and the Purkinje fibres (Fig. 13.10). A further portion, from the level of the atrioventricular junction to the apex of the heart, forms thicker adult trabeculae; the trabeculae in the right ventricle are coarser compared to those in the left. Over time the outer layer of ventricular myocardium will become highly proliferative and will form the thick compact layer of the ventricular wall.
Embryonic ventricular myocardium, encompassing both the trabeculae and exterior wall, possesses a specific molecular chamber phenotype: the myocytes express, among other proteins, the fast-conducting gap-junctional proteins connexin 40 and 43, and atrial natriuretic factor. During development, only the peripheral ventricular conduction system retains this molecular phenotype. The ventricular working myocardium loses connexin 40 and atrial natriuretic factor expression while retaining low expression of connexin 43. Changes in the expression of connexin 40 and 43 have been correlated with morphological development of the fetal heart. The increasing complexity of the helical disposition of ventricular myocardial fibres has been studied using high resolution diffusion tensor magnetic resonance imaging (Pervolaraki et al 2017).
Left ventricular noncompaction
In left ventricular noncompaction, the left ventricle exhibits particularly prominent trabeculae as compared to the compact wall. Although most cases of left ventricular noncompaction are thought to be developmental in origin, the process behind acquisition of prominent trabeculae is not clear: variation in trabecular prominence is seen during adult life, for example in pregnant women (Gati et al 2014). The condition can reflect failure of the trabeculae to compact, or excessive trabecular formation, or hypoplasticity of the compact wall (Anderson et al 2017, Jensen et al 2017) and can be associated with heart failure. Its uncertain aetiology is reflected in the diversity of current diagnostic criteria (Ivanov et al 2017).

Outflow tract

The outflow tract of the heart (truncus arteriosus) is formed by cells derived from the secondary heart-forming field and is confluent with the right ventricle. Initially, the outflow tract myocardium extends up to the pericardial reflections. As development proceeds, continuous addition of non-myocardial cells changes the position of the myocardium and cardiac extracellular matrix (cardiac jelly) relative to the outflow tract bend, a conspicuous morphological feature in the embryonic heart. The outflow tract is therefore a highly dynamic structure along which cells pass to contribute to the definitive right ventricle. By definition, the outflow tract is downstream of the ventricular loop, irrespective of the fact that, eventually, it will largely become incorporated within the definitive right ventricle (see Figs 13.4, 13.7). The method of incorporation of the outflow tract into the heart is different to that of the sinus venosus, because it involves movement of a structure, whereas the sinus venosus changes identity but otherwise shows no relative movement.
Within the inner curvature of the ventricular loop, the walls of the outflow tract and atrioventricular canal fade into one another without a clear boundary. It is within this ventricular part of the primary heart tube that the cushions of the atrioventricular canal and the outflow tract must achieve appropriate connections with the ventricular septum in order to separate the pulmonary trunk and the aorta.

Aortic arch anomalies

A series of aortic arch arteries develop from the outflow tract and conduct blood through the pharyngeal arches before joining the dorsal aortae; the temporal changes that occur in the arteries are outlined on p. 222 and described in detail in Chapter 17 (see Fig. 17.9). The persistence of a right aortic arch is caused by a failure of resorption of the embryonic fourth right pharyngeal arch artery (Kanne and Godwin 2010), and is found most frequently with Fallot’s tetralogy or with a persistent truncus arteriosus. It can also exist, together with a left aortic arch, in various combinations known as arterial rings, which compress the oesophagus, causing so-called dysphagia lusoria. For example, the right subclavian artery can sometimes arise from the arch of the aorta distal to the origin of the left subclavian artery and pass to the right, behind the trachea and oesophagus. This aberrant connection can be explained by the failure of fusion of the right common carotid artery and the right subclavian artery, caused by a fault in the right aortic arch involution.

Septation of the Embryonic Cardiac Compartments

The early embryonic heart is an undivided continuous tube. During development, septa are formed within the compartments of the heart. Initially they separate blood draining to the right side of the heart (oxygenated blood from the placenta and venous drainage from the body) and arterial flow from the left side of the heart (to the body and placenta); after birth they permit the smooth transfer of blood flow from the intrauterine to the extrauterine arrangement. Septa develop within the common atrium, in the atrioventricular canal, between the ventricles and within the outflow tract. Initially flow of blood on the right and left sides of the developing heart does not intermingle because flow is laminar. Pressures are similar in the right and left sides of the embryonic and fetal heart.
A number of shunts are open in the early heart and vessels because oxygenation is derived from the placenta and the pulmonary circulation is not functional in the embryo and fetus. Blood flow through the beating heart, the local pressures generated, and the shear forces created within the endocardial tube, form part of the epigenetic processes involved in heart development and the establishment of systemic and placental circulations.

Septation of the atrial chambers

Atrial septation is a process in which a valvular structure develops that initially directs the flow of placental blood to the body and allows separation of the two atrial chambers immediately after birth. It is typically described in terms of two septa that separate the endocardial and myocardial walls of the common atrium and two foramina that ensure continuous blood flow. Atrial septation starts at about stage 13 with proliferation of a crescentic ridge of mesenchyme in the atrial roof. This ridge, the septum primum, grows by active proliferation from the craniodorsal atrial roof towards the atrioventricular canal, just to the right of the entrance of the pulmonary vein (pulmonary pit) (Fig. 13.11A,D). Its leading edge is covered by a mesenchymal cap which is continuous with extracardiac mesenchyme (mediastinal mesenchyme) derived from the dorsal mesocardium that forms a dorsal mesenchymal protrusion. Under the influence of BMP signalling, the ventral horn of the crescentic septum primum will touch the ventral atrioventricular cushion, while the dorsal horn will fuse with the dorsal atrioventricular cushion. The primary atrial foramen (foramen or ostium primum) is therefore entirely surrounded by mesenchyme, subjacent to the atrial endocardium. Caudal to the advancing edge of the septum primum, the developing atrial chambers still communicate through the foramen primum (see Fig. 13.11A,D). The foramen primum diminishes in size as a result of continuous growth of the muscular primary atrial septum and the cranial part of the septum perforates by apoptosis. The perforations coalesce, producing a secondary atrial foramen (foramen, or ostium, secundum) that allows continued shunting of oxygen-enriched blood from the placenta to the left side of the heart and so to the head and brain. The foramen secundum forms during stages 15–17. The foramen primum is occluded in the median plane by stage 17, when the septum primum mesenchyme merges with the fusing atrioventricular (Fig. 13.11B,E). This tissue later differentiates to myocardium and forms a ridge that will become the limbus of the future fossa ovalis.
The secondary atrial septum (septum secundum) is reported to appear during stages 18–21 (O’Rahilly and Müller 1987), signalling the earliest development of the foramen ovale. Anderson et al (2002), however, note that invagination of the roof of the right atrium, forming a myocardial ridge, constitutes the septum secundum, and that this is not present until after stage 23 (see Fig. 13.11B,E). The free edge of the septum secundum overlaps the foramen secundum forming an oblique channel, the foramen ovale, between the now divided atria. Once these septa have formed, blood passes under the crescentic edge or limbus that is the crista dividens of the septum secundum, and flows obliquely towards and through the foramen secundum to the left atrium. This passage through the foramen ovale, persists throughout intrauterine life (see Videos 13.2, 23.1). In postnatal life, when left atrial pressure exceeds that in the right atrium, the septum primum will close against the septum secundum as a type of flap valve, completing atrial septation (Fig. 13.11C,F). On the right side of the foramen ovale, part of the septum primum remains underneath the rim of the septum secundum and becomes the fossa ovalis. Fig. 13.12 summarizes all the anomalies of septation.

Atrial septal defects

Worldwide, the reported prevalence of persistent communication between the atrial chambers through the foramen ovale is 1.64/1000 live births (13% of the most common congenital heart disease subtypes) (van der Linde et al 2011). It is often the result of failure of the flap valve (septum primum) to fuse completely with the infolded muscular rim of the septum secundum. This type of atrial septal defect is known as a persistent foramen ovale. When the flap valve is still able to overlap the rim, the communication is of little or no functional significance as long as the left atrial pressure is greater than the right, which is usually the case in healthy individuals. However, when the pressure in the right atrium increases during sneezing, when diving, or in the case of pulmonary hypertension, there is a risk of some blood entering the left atrium directly, bypassing the pulmonary circulation (Briggs et al 2012). If the flap valve (septum primum) does not cover the foramen ovale, or if it is perforated, a true septal defect exists (Geva et al 2014) (see Fig. 13.12A). A foramen secundum defect is present when the lumen of the left atrium is visible from within the right atrium; it can be the result of either a septum secundum that is too small or a foramen secundum in the septum primum that is too large. Foramen primum defects occur when the mesenchymal cap of the septum primum does not fuse with the atrioventricular cushions, resulting in an interatrial communication. Deficient growth of the dorsal mesenchymal protrusion may also result in foramen primum defects: this is an atrioventricular septal defect permitting exclusively atrial shunting (Jensen et al 2016, Wessels 2016).

Septation and appropriate positioning of the atrioventricular canal

The atrioventricular canal is initially a continuation of myocardium between the common atrium and the primitive ventricle. Four cardiac cushions are formed at the margin of this canal, namely: the left and right cushions, and the much larger ventral and dorsal cushions. The single orifice of the atrioventricular canal is divided by the approximation and later merger of the ventral and dorsal cushions. The dorsal atrioventricular cushion runs along the floor of the atrium. As it extends further dorsally it meets the dorsal mesenchymal protrusion (spina vestibuli) that connects with the mesenchymal cap that runs along the leading edge of the septum primum until it meets the ventral atrioventricular cushion (see Fig. 13.11). The dorsal atrioventricular cushion has a ventricular extension to the inner curvature of the heart tube that comes to lie on top of the developing muscular ventricular septum: much of the membranous septum of the ventricles is derived from this extension.
The two atrioventricular cushions fuse during stages 14–17, at which time they become known as the central fibrous body. Relative to the canal itself, the cushions are large, leaving narrow right and left slits for the passage of blood (Fig. 13.13). At the same time as the cushions fuse, the canal expands. This is seen most clearly on the lateral right side where the size of the right atrioventricular opening increases. Relative to the heart, the entire atrioventricular canal also shifts to the right (see Fig. 13.5), causing the dorsal cushion to overhang the ventricular septum into the developing right ventricle while the ventral cushion remains mostly on the left side.

Atrioventricular septal defects

Atrioventricular septal defects result from a failure of fusion of the endocardial atrioventricular cushions, leading to a common atrioventricular orifice and deficiencies of the adjacent septal structures (see Fig. 13.12B). Two major subgroups of atrioventricular defects are identified. The more frequent is complete atrioventricular defect, characterized by a common atrioventricular orifice and the potential for shunting through the septal defect at both atrial and ventricular levels (see Fig. 13.12B). The common atrioventricular orifice is guarded by a single common valve, with superior and inferior leaflets that bridge the scooped-out ventricular septum. The valve is tethered to both right and left ventricles. Although the left component of this valve is often interpreted as a ‘cleft mitral valve’, it bears no resemblance to the normally structured mitral valve because it has three leaflets where the ‘cleft’ forms the zone of apposition between the left ventricular components of the ventral and dorsal bridging leaflets. The less common incomplete atrioventricular septal defect has separate right and left atrioventricular orifices and shunting occurs only at the atrial level. Occasionally, when the bridging leaflets are attached to the underside of the atrial septum, shunting is minor and confined to the ventricular level (Briggs et al 2012).

Septation of the ventricles

Three distinct structures contribute to the formation of the ventricular septum: the atrioventricular cushions (including the protrusion of the dorsal atrioventricular cushion that lies on the ventricular septal crest), the muscular ventricular septum and the proximal parts of the outflow cushions. Separation of the right and left ventricles is heralded by the appearance of a caudal crescentic ridge within the ventricular loop (see Fig. 13.7). This is a persisting part of the Tbx3 and G1N2-positive primary heart tube myocardium, which means that the crest of the septum is the oldest portion of the developing ventricular septum. The ventricles (especially those parts that will become the apex of the heart) become enlarged as if they were expanding like balloons on either side of the muscular ventricular septum (see Figs 13.4, 13.7). Later, the dorsal part of the crest of the septum will become the atrioventricular bundle, while the atrioventricular node will form at the dorsal atrioventricular junction (see Fig. 13.10C). The ventral parts will largely disappear except the so-called ‘dead-end tract’ (Anderson et al 2016, de Vries et al 2016). The crest of the septum, covered by an extension of the dorsal atrioventricular cushion, together with the fused atrioventricular cushions themselves, bounds the ovoid interventricular foramen. The Tbx3 and G1N2-positive myocardium that surrounds this foramen marks a ring composed of primary myocardium which is smooth (see Fig. 13.10). Completion of ventricular septation is seen after a rightward shift of the atrioventricular canal and the positioning of the outflow tract between the separating atrioventricular canals so that it too overrides the developing ventricular septum. The foramen finally closes by formation of the membranous septum that completely separates the right from the left ventricle (see Fig. 13.13). During ventricular septation, transition from the early ventricular base-to-apex conduction activation to an apex-to-base conduction is initiated. Maturation of the ventricular conduction system, with concomitant development of the fibrous rings of the heart, continues throughout fetal development (Watanabe et al 2016).

Ventricular septal defects

Ventricular septal defects account for approximately two-thirds of all cardiac septal defects; the worldwide prevalence is 2.6/1000 live births (34% of the most common congenital heart disease subtypes) (van der Linde et al 2011, Xie et al 2017). The inappropriate formation and connection of the atrioventricular cushions to the muscular ventricular septum results in a perimembranous ventricular septal defect (see Fig. 13.12C). The membranous septum, and by extension a perimembranous ventricular septal defect, is closely associated with the area of insertion of the septal leaflet of the tricuspid valve. A perimembranous defect can also extend further into the ventricular outlet beneath the supraventricular crest that borders the right ventricle from the outflow tract. Perimembranous ventricular septal defects are often associated with the aortic orifice overriding the crest of the muscular septum, i.e. ‘overriding aorta’. When combined with pulmonary stenosis or atresia, this forms the basis for the syndrome of Fallot’s tetralogy. The last feature of the tetralogy, right ventricular hypertrophy, which developed in infancy in the untreated child, is now usually seen in cases where an affected child does not have timely surgical repair. The condition does not cause important haemodynamic imbalance during fetal life (Wohlmuth and Gardiner 2020). When the pulmonary trunk is normal or even dilated with this combination of anomalies, it is known as the Eisenmenger complex. Perimembranous defects can also occur with abnormal ventriculoarterial connections. Taussig–Bing anomaly occurs when, in a case of transposition of the great arteries, the pulmonary trunk overrides the muscular septum, causing both arteries to connect to the right ventricle and the ventricular septum is also defective. In perimembranous ventricular septal defects, the atrioventricular bundle and its right and left branches are still always found along the dorsal and caudal margins of the defect.
Less commonly, a septal defect may be found in the ventricular outflow tract underneath the conjoined facing leaflets of the aortic and pulmonary valves. Such juxta-arterial defects are doubly committed, in that they open beneath the orifices of both aortic and pulmonary valves. They are the result of the failure of formation of the free-standing muscular sub-pulmonary infundibulum, which links them to persistent truncus arteriosus anomalies, but the ventriculoarterial junctions are appropriately separated. These defects usually have a muscular dorsocaudal rim that protects the atrioventricular bundle: the defect can sometimes extend to the membranous septum.
A third type of ventricular septal defect occurs within the musculature of the septum. Defects may affect all parts of the septum and can be multiple, producing a so-called ‘Swiss cheese’ septum (Spicer et al 2014).

Septation and appropriate positioning of the outflow tract

The appearance of two outflow cushions is key to the separation of the outflow tract (truncus arteriosus). Together with the myocardial wall, the cushions spiral as they extend from their septal and parietal positions at end of the right ventricle to the developing aortic sac (see Fig. 13.13; Fig. 13.14). Within the developing aortic sac, a transverse wedge of tissue composed of dorsal mediastinal mesenchyme, the aortopulmonary septum, initially separates the origins of the aorta and the pulmonary trunk before their individual walls are formed. Meanwhile, neural crest cells migrate from the pharyngeal mediastinal myocardium into the outflow cushions. They do not populate the septum itself, but form the larger parts of the walls of the intrapericardial portions of the truncus arteriosus (Keyte et al 2014).
The outflow cushions start to fuse from stage 15. Fusion starts in the distal part of the outflow tract and continues gradually in the direction of the ventricular septum (see Fig. 13.14; Fig. 13.15A). Early on, the most proximal parts of the cushions remain unfused. The arterial valves and sinuses develop distally within the myocardial sleeve that surrounds this area. When the most proximal parts of the cushions fuse, they are invaded by cardiac myocytes (myocardium) and become continuous with the muscular ventricular septum, so joining the aorta to the left ventricle. Spread of myocardial cells also gives rise to the greater part of the supraventricular crest. In the right ventricle, the wall of the proximal myocardial outflow tract persists as the smooth-walled muscular sub-pulmonary infundibulum. Some myocardium also persists distal to the pulmonary valve. There is no distinct myocardial outflow tract present within the left ventricle in the postnatal heart: the myocardium that initially surrounded the entire aortic root is lost on the side of the non-coronary leaflet because a fibrous continuity develops between the mitral valve and the aorta.

Outflow tract septal anomalies

A number of outflow anomalies may occur if the outflow cushions either fail to develop or fuse in an inappropriate fashion. When the cushions fail to fuse, the result is a persistent truncus arteriosus, represented by an undivided arterial channel, guarded by a common arterial valve, positioned above and astride the free margin of the muscular ventricular septum (Fig. 13.16). This anomaly occurs frequently in DiGeorge syndrome and can be a result of a Tbx1 deletion (Rana et al 2014). Persistent truncus arteriosus is also likely linked to abnormal development of the secondary heart-forming field, which, by means of an overall shorter heart tube, leads to poor alignment of the aorta with the left ventricle. In this condition, there is a coexisting juxta-arterial deficiency of the ventricular septum; right and left pulmonary arteries usually arise via a confluent segment but can also take independent origin from the trunk, which continues as the ascending aorta. The common valve usually has three leaflets but may also have two, four or more.
Transposition of the arterial trunks (also referred to as transposition of the great vessels) is a condition in which the aorta arises from the right ventricle and the pulmonary trunk from the left. Better described as having discordant ventriculoarterial connections, such hearts can coexist with deficiencies of cardiac septation. They can also be found with discordant connections at the atrioventricular junctions, producing a congenitally corrected transposition. The developmental history of discordant connections remains unknown but may have similar signalling pathways that are involved in right atrial isomerism, the nodal/pitx2 pathways (Nakajima 2016).
Double outlet ventricle occurs when the greater parts of both arterial valves fall within the same ventricle, almost always the right. In these cases, in order to continue the circulation of oxygenated blood after birth, it is necessary that either the ventricular septum is deficient, or the ductus arteriosus remains patent with medication; in rare cases, the septal defect can close as a secondary event.

Cardiac Valve Development

Competent mesenchymal valves develop at the atrioventricular junction and between the ventricles and outflow tract. The valvular apparatus is largely composed of endocardium-derived mesenchyme. All leaflets and the chordae tendineae and myotendinous junctions initially form from internal endocardial projections that cover a myocardial basement membrane, matrix and mesenchymal cells.

Atrioventricular valves

The mitral and tricuspid valves are formed at the site of the atrioventricular canal. The endocardial cushions are located on the inside of the canal. The cushions occupy most of the canal, save two tiny lumina that connect the right atrium to the developing right ventricle and the left atrium to the developing left ventricle. On the outside of the heart, at the level of the atrioventricular canal, a groove forms which is covered by the developing epicardium (see p. 220). As the atrioventricular valves develop, the epicardium and the endocardial cushions fuse at the ventricular margin of the canal, disrupting the myocardial continuity. Only the prospective atrioventricular conduction axis is spared from disruption. The sites of myocardial disruption later form the anulus fibrosus, a collagenous structure that physically insulates the atria from the ventricles. If anulus fibrosus formation is disrupted, accessory strands of myocardium between the atria and ventricles may persist and can become aberrantly differentiated thereby becoming able to propagate the electrical impulse. This can lead to early excitation of the ventricles and can act as a substrate for re-entry ventricular tachycardias as seen in Wolf–Parkinson–White syndrome.
Although molecular lineage studies in the mouse have demonstrated that the atrioventricular cushions are initially entirely populated by endocardium-derived mesenchymal cells, they later receive a significant epicardial contribution. For example, the fibroblasts that make up the parietal valvular leaflets and the tendinous cords in the formed heart are derived from both the endocardium and the epicardium.
The atrioventricular valves develop as shelf-like projections from the margins of the expanded atrioventricular orifices. These projections contain both myocardium and endocardial cushion mesenchyme. They grow deep into the ventricular cavity initially as trabecular ridges. Part of these trabeculae will later remodel to form papillary muscles. Each valve leaflet develops functionally significant regional variations in surface texture while its core condenses as a collagenous fibrous lamina, supported either by the fibro-fatty tissue of the atrioventricular groove, or by the anulus fibrosus. In the mitral valve, the aortic leaflet forms before the mural leaflet. Both are fully delaminated from the myocardial wall by postmenstrual week 10. The formation of the inferior leaflet of the tricuspid valve is followed by the formation of the septal and, later, the anterosuperior leaflets. This process continues until at least postmenstrual week 13 (see Fig. 13.15B).

Atrioventricular valve defects

When the right atrioventricular junction (the connection between the right atrium and the right ventricle) does not expand normally during atrioventricular septation, the tricuspid valve remains atretic. Often a small dimple can still be observed within the right atrium where the connection should have been. Atresia of the tricuspid valve coincides with an atrial septal defect and a failure in right ventricle development. The left ventricle supports both the systemic and pulmonary circulation.
In Ebstein’s malformation, the septal and inferior tricuspid valve leaflets are attached abnormally deep into the right ventricular chamber (Paranon and Acar 2008). This often leads to right ventricular overload and valve regurgitation (Lombardi and Pluchinotta 2018). In rare cases, Ebstein’s malformation can coexist with atresia of the tricuspid valve; it does not coincide with hypoplasia of the right ventricle because the developmental process is different.
The mitral valve can exhibit several anomalies that result either in a valve stenosis or a valve regurgitation (Prakash et al 2017). Often, the papillary muscles or tensor apparatus are involved in these malformations (Séguéla et al 2011).

Aortic and pulmonary valves

The aortic and pulmonary valves are derived from the endocardial cushions within the myocardial outflow tract. These cushions become populated by neural crest cells, in addition to endocardial- and pharyngeal-derived mesenchymal cells, which helps them fuse across the tract. The myocardial wall surrounding the cushions then starts to split giving rise to the aortic and pulmonary roots. What remains of the fused cushions in each of the arterial roots will develop into the right and left adjacent pulmonary and coronary leaflets. The developing intercalated cushions, which form on opposite sides of the fused outflow cushions, will give rise to the non-adjacent leaflets of the aortic and pulmonary valve (see Fig. 13.14). In all these cushion-derived intrusions, the distal parts become excavated from the arterial wall to form the valvular leaflets. The valvular sinuses are found between the leaflets and the wall. The anulus fibrosus surrounding the atrioventricular valves also partially surrounds the outflow tract valves: only the pulmonary valve is not fully connected to it. The non-adjacent and left coronary leaflet of the aortic valve and the aortic leaflet of the mitral valve form a fibrous continuity with each other.
Neural crest cells play a role in the spatiotemporal regulation of the division of the outflow tract into the aortic and pulmonary arteries, and therefore of the development of the muscular sub-pulmonary infundibulum. Although proper migration of these cells is crucial for normal development of the outflow tract and the formation of arterial valve leaflets and sinuses, their exact role is largely obscure. Scarcely any neural crest cells remain in the adult heart, either in the leaflets of the arterial valves in the formed heart, or in the muscular sub-pulmonary infundibulum.

Arterial valve defects

Bicuspid aortic valve is the most common congenital cardiac anomaly and often results from the aberrant formation of a large leaflet from two leaflets (Hoffman and Kaplan 2002, Yener et al 2002). It is linked with an ascending aorta that is prone to dilatation and dissection later in life. The aetiology is not yet fully understood but may involve different extracellular matrix composition (Siu and Silversides 2010, Rambeau et al 2017).

Non-myocardial additions to the developing heart

The parietal pericardial wall surrounding the heart is lined with bi-potential mesothelium that will either add cells to the venous pole of the heart or differentiate towards the Tbx18-positive epicardial lineage. Bone morphogenetic proteins and fibroblast growth factors influence which lineage is ultimately followed. After heart looping and septation has started, epicardial mesenchyme is added to the external surface of the heart. The epicardium subsequently gives rise to the coronary vasculature and to the population of interstitial cardiac fibroblasts which not only produce extracellular matrix but also play a role in tissue repair and homeostasis (Tallquist and Molkentin 2017).

Epicardium

The epicardium develops from a protrusion of mesenchymal cells covered by mesothelium, the pro-epicardial organ (Simões and Riley 2018). This structure arises from the pericardial surface in the region of the sinus venosus during postfertilization week 5. The pro-epicardial cells migrate on the surface of the heart both by proliferation from their original location and by local directional outgrowth. The pro-epicardial cells successively spread over the myocardial surface of the developing atria, atrioventricular canal, ventricles and the proximal part of the outflow tract. The remnants of the pro-epicardium can be found in the area of the developing systemic venous tributaries up to stage 15 before it ultimately regresses.
After the formation of the epicardium, a distinct region, the sub-epicardial space or sub-epicardium, is formed between the developing epicardium and the outer myocardial layer. It is particularly pronounced at the atrioventricular junction and around the outflow regions of the heart tube and is populated by mesenchymal cells that are largely derived from the epicardium and that play an important role in the formation of the coronary vasculature.

Coronary blood vessels

Coronary arteries develop concomitantly with outflow tract septation during stages 14–16, by vasculogenesis from blood islands that coalesce into a plexus within the epicardial mesenchyme (Tomanek 2016). Plexus formation takes place before the formation of the arterial orifices in the aortic sinuses to which the coronary vessels ultimately connect. The coronary veins develop by a similar mechanism of plexus formation and mostly attach to the sinus venosus, although a small subpopulation will drain directly to the atrial chambers (Thebesian veins).
The coronary vessels themselves are formed by several precursor tissues. The endothelium is thought to arise from at least three different sources: the sinus venosus endothelium, the endocardium and, to a lesser extent, the pro-epicardium. The pericytes and smooth muscle cells arise from the epicardium and partially from the cushion endocardium. Neural crest cells can be traced to parts of the smooth muscle layer and fibroblasts arise mainly from the sub-epicardium.
The mature coronary vascular pattern is established by remodelling of the capillary plexuses and reduction in the number of arteriovenous anastomoses. Concomitant with the connection of the arterial plexus to the aorta, those vessels that acquire a tunica media and an adventitial covering persist and their luminal diameter increases (Sharma et al 2017), whereas those lacking a tunica media regress. Between postmenstrual weeks 16–19 the coronary arteries and veins increase in number and show thickening of the tunica media. During postmenstrual weeks 26–34 further expansion of the coronary vasculature occurs and elastic fibres are seen in the thickening tunica adventitia (Tomanek 2016).

Coronary artery anomalies

Ultimately, only solitary right and left coronary arteries remain. There are occasional anomalies in coronary artery morphology: small accessory branches of the coronary arteries take their origin directly from the coronary sinuses in one third of human hearts. For information on coronary artery anomalies see Spicer et al (2015) and Pérez-Pomares et al (2016).

Embryonic Blood, Blood Vessels and Early Circulation

Early formation of blood

Cellular elements

The cellular elements of the blood initially arise from the extraembryonic tissues of the secondary yolk sac (Ch. 8). The yolk sac wall is composed of an epithelial parietal hypoblast layer covered with an epithelial extraembryonic mesodermal layer, derived from extraembryonic mesoblast, and some intervening extraembryonic mesenchyme (Ch. 9). The yolk sac floats freely in the extraembryonic coelom (chorionic cavity) and after head and tail folding is continuous with the developing intraembryonic midgut within the embryo via a narrow vitelline duct (Ch. 10).
Evidence for the temporal phases of blood cell production is based mainly on experimental chick and mouse embryo studies. As both of these laboratory animals have very different developmental timescales and arrangements of extraembryonic tissues, any comparison to human development must be treated with caution. In the chick, the allantois becomes the site for gaseous exchange, and in the mouse, there is an extensive inverted visceral yolk sac and a haemotrichorial labyrinthine placenta. In humans, discoid haemochorial placentation provides gaseous and nutritional exchange and a separate yolk sac, which remains throughout gestation, can be identified at parturition.
Three overlapping waves of blood cell production have been identified, with differences between erythrocyte production and that of other cellular elements (Baron 2013). Lineage analyses of blood cells have revised some of the traditional views of the initial restriction of haemopoietic cell lines, e.g. populations of megakaryocytes and macrophages have been identified in the yolk sac wall at the same time as the earliest erythrocytes (McGrath et al 2015).
Erythrocytes arising in the first haemopoietic wave have conventionally been termed ‘primitive’, a term that is frequently used to indicate that embryonic cells are different from their adult counterparts: the acronym, EryP, primitive erythrocytes, is more useful. EryPs arise in the yolk sac wall within 3–4 weeks post fertilization in human embryos. The cells are nucleated and, in the mouse, very large (three times larger than fetal erythrocytes and 6 times larger than adult erythrocytes) (Kauts et al 2016). They express the zeta (ζ) and epsilon (ε) globin genes, which are consistently expressed in amniote embryos. EryPs enter the developing extraembryonic and intraembryonic circulations as a transiently amplifying pool of dividing cells, able to transport oxygen and carbon dioxide, and appear to have a role in vascular remodelling. They can leave the circulation, demonstrate surface adhesion molecules and form tight associations with other EryPs in the adjacent mesenchymal tissue surrounding early vessels. Over time, circulating EryPs continue to mature, as their cell diameter decreases and they lose nucleoli. In the mouse, EryPs undergo enucleation in the liver and remain in the fetal circulation until birth. In humans, EryPs are found in the stroma of placental villi at postfertilization week 4, and by week 5 the process of enucleation is first seen. Macrophages are present and proliferating in the chorionic plate before the fetoplacental circulation is established. In the process of EryP enucleation, groups of EryPs surround a macrophage, which contacts them via cytoplasmic projections and appears to engulf and digest their extruded nuclei (Van Handel et al 2010). The enucleated cells, reticulocytes, are found in the circulation, as are blood elements termed pyrenocytes, which are nuclei from EryPs with a surrounding cell membrane (McGrath et al 2008).
The second wave of erythrocyte production, also from the yolk sac wall, gives rise to EryD (definitive erythrocytes) from about postfertilization week 4 in humans. These cells are considered to arise from transient multipotent progenitors which will become restricted to erythroid and myeloid lineages (Baron 2013). In the mouse, EryD cells are found in the yolk sac wall, the developing liver and in the forming bone marrow. EryD cells are smaller than EryPs, they display different gene expression and requirements for erythropoietin, and different oxygen carrying capacities. Both types of early erythrocyte are present in the circulation during the second wave, and EryD cells rapidly outnumber EryPs.
The third wave of erythrocyte production of haemopoietic stem cells (HSCs), arises within the embryo at intraembryonic sites, including mesenchyme adjacent to large blood vessels, within the placental stroma and in the aorta-gonad-mesonephros (AGM) region. This latter region is subjacent to the intraembryonic coelom, particularly the lower ends of the pericardio-peritoneal canals and the upper posterior wall of the peritoneal cavity on either side of the mesentery of the midgut. It forms at the junction between somatopleuric and splanchnopleuric mesenchyme and is referred to as intermediate mesenchyme (Ch. 12).
In the mouse there is some evidence that cells with a future haemopoietic lineage arise from the caudal primitive streak (Fraser 2012). Mesoblast cells arising from the caudal primitive streak migrate rostrally and laterally and differentiate into the initial epithelial lining of the intraembryonic coelomic wall (see Fig. 10.3). These cells then proliferate producing the mesenchymal populations (splanchnopleuric, somatopleuric and intermediate). It is possible that the final stem cells for late fetal and future adult haemopoiesis remain in the coelomic wall epithelium until the requisite time. These haemopoietic stem cells do not differentiate in situ, but seed precursor cells to the liver, thymus and bone marrow, which subsequently produce all blood lineages; they have been found in human embryos in very small numbers around 5 postfertilization weeks (Baron 2013).
It was always stated that the embryonic liver develops precociously to become a haemopoietic site before the medullary cavities form in developing long bones. In the mouse, both EryP and EryDs mature and enucleate in the liver. EryDs, (which are zeta-globin negative) have been identified in human embryonic liver from 6.5 weeks post fertilization, equivalent to about stage 19, and enucleating cells have been identified in sub-sinusoidal spaces at 11 weeks post fertilization. These findings suggest that the fetal human liver may not support erythroblast enucleation until later in development than was previously thought (Van Handel et al 2010). Study of haemopoietic stem cells from human fetal liver and cord blood have produced genome-wide maps of the DNA methylation profiles of developing blood cells (Farlik et al 2016).

Plasma

It has been suggested that the rapidly expanding early cardiovascular system is filled with plasma, reflecting the movement of fluid from the intraembryonic coelom to the veins. The intraembryonic coelomic wall is largely composed of proliferating cells that produce the splanchnopleuric and somatopleuric mesenchymal populations; when not dividing, adjacent cells form tight junctions, indicating a barrier function of the epithelium (Guo et al 2014). However, the walls of a portion of the pericardioperitoneal canals are thinner, and possibly more permeable to coelomic fluid, at the time when the canals surround the hepatocardiac channels. The latter lie between the hepatic plexus and the sinus venosus; the hepatocardiac channel on the right side is a wider vessel whereas on the left it is more plexiform, with only a transitory connection to the sinus venosus. The differentiation of this specific coelomic region occurs just in advance of the expansion and filling of the right and left atria, at about stage 12. Fluid within the extraembryonic coelomic space, the chorionic cavity, which is continuous with the intraembryonic coelom, has been collected and examined in the early human embryo (Jauniaux and Gulbis 2000). There are few, if any, studies on the synthesis and circulation of proteins including plasma proteins, although both erythropoietin and thrombopoietin are apparently needed for embryonic erythrocyte production (Baron 2013).

Early formation of blood vessels

It was thought that blood cells and blood vessels were derived from the same precursor population, initially in the extraembryonic tissues, but lineage studies have shown that EryPs and endothelial precursors do not share a common progenitor (Baron 2013). Vasculogenesis and angiogenesis are involved in the formation of blood vessels lined with endothelium. Vasculogenesis is the process where vessels arise in situ in extra- and intraembryonic sites: vitelline vessels form within the yolk sac wall; umbilical vessels arise within the mesenchyme surrounding the allantois; and the endocardium and dorsal aortae arise from splanchopleuric mesenchyme derived from the epithelial coelomic wall.

Angiogenesis

Further vessel development occurs by a process of angiogenesis in which endothelial precursors, angioblasts, arising in splanchnic and somitic tissues, add endothelial sprouts and branches to earlier vessels. None of the main vessels of the adult arises as a single trunk in the embryo. A capillary network is first laid down along the course of each vessel and the larger arteries and veins are defined by selection and enlargement of definite paths in this network. Lymphatic vessels develop after the main arteries and veins are formed. They arise initially by angiogenesis from the cardinal veins and subsequently by proliferation of lymphangioblasts that form lymphatic capillaries.
Five stages of angiogenesis have been described: endothelial tip cell specification and sprout initiation; sprout elongation and local guidance, where the distal tip cell extends supported by proximal stalk cells that may divide; sprout elongation in response to cues from the extracellular matrix; lumen formation, initially by the confluence of intracellular vacuoles, followed by establishment of apical basal polarity of the cells of the endothelial sprout; and fusion of the tip cell to another sprout tip so that two developing tubes join to form an early vascular plexus (Kuijper et al 2007, Chappell et al 2012). The developmental processes of angiogenesis are similar to those seen in both neoplasia and acute inflammation in adult tissue. The literature on angiogenesis is extensive and the process will not be considered in detail here; the interested reader is directed to Herbert and Stainier (2011), Senger and Davis (2011), Beets et al (2013), Eichmann and Pardanaud (2014) and Hashimoto et al (2016).
Studies have considered the development of capillary beds within the surrounding mesenchymal tissues destined to become laminae propriae, but few have looked at how capillary beds form within tissues undergoing morphogenetic movements. Czirok et al (2011) discussed the temporal imperative for free-living embryos to develop rapidly in contrast to amniote embryos, which do not have to fend for themselves. They concluded that prior to the establishment of a circulation, amniote vascular patterns of development are not predetermined by a network of patterning genes and cell signalling pathways, but rather that the tissue movements during organogenesis and the realignment and tension that develop within extracellular matrix fibres may guide cell migration and the position of early vessels.
Extracellular matrix plays a critical role in angiogenesis and vessel stabilization in the embryo and in the adult. Differences in the composition and abundance of specific extracellular matrix components between the embryo and adult mean that there are likely to be significant differences in the process at different ages. Fibronectin is required for normal vascular development; its deposition is related to interactions between endothelial cells and pericytes, and local tensional forces that occur between blood vessel basement membrane and local extracellular matrix assembly (Davis and Senger 2005, Senger and Davis 2011). Early blood vessels are initially surrounded by a fibronectin-rich matrix that is later incorporated into the endothelial basal lamina along with laminin, a particularly early constituent. Several layers of fibronectin-expressing cells are seen around larger vessels, such as the dorsal aortae. The endothelium does not synthesize a basal lamina in those regions where remodelling and angiogenesis are active, and the mesenchyme around such endothelium does not express α-actin or laminin until branching has stopped and differentiation of the tunica media begins (after a stable vascular pattern has formed). It is not known how differentiation of pericytes and smooth muscle cells is induced; the majority of arteries accumulate medial smooth muscle from the surrounding mesenchyme.
In early development, the early vessels of the embryo are disproportionately large and their walls consist of little more than a single layer of endothelium. The cardiac orifices are also relatively large and the force of the cardiac contraction is weak and, consequently, the circulation is sluggish, despite the rapid rate of contraction of the developing heart. However, the tissues are able to draw nourishment not only from the capillaries but also from the large arteries and the intraembryonic coelomic fluid. The earliest blood cells are also very large; as the embryo grows the early vascular beds become more complex and narrowed and erythrocytes also become smaller.
There is an ongoing relationship between developing blood vessels, their response to haemodynamic forces and the mechanotransduction pathways involved. The flow of blood exerts pressure on the developing vessel walls; circumferential stress is generated when vessels stretch in response to pressure, and shear stress, the tangential frictional force of flowing blood against the endothelium, is detected by and produces epigenetic changes in that epithelium (Culver and Dickinson 2010). Three principles operate in embryonic vasculature: if the flow increases or decreases the blood vessel calibre increases or decreases to normalize the shear stress; if the blood pressure in the vessel increases or decreases the vessel wall thickens or thins respectively, mainly by the modulation of the amount of smooth muscle; increase in capillary pressure leads to new capillary growth (Roman and Pekkan 2012). Endothelial cells are mechanosensitive, they are able to respond to laminar and disturbed blood flows and are sensitive to blood viscosity. Very complex interactions occur throughout the development of the circulation; cardiac rate increases with development and cardiac output increases in proportion to the weight of the embryo, so that when dorsal aortic blood flow is matched to embryonic weight, blood flow remains constant over a more than 150-fold change in the mass of the embryo. The influence of blood flow on cardiac development, and the ways that computational fluid dynamics can be used to determine developmental responses to blood flow have been studied (Courchaine and Rugonyi 2018, Courchaine et al 2018). Routine postmenstrual week 20 4D ultrasound assessments have been correlated with fluid mechanics of blood flow through fetal hearts (Wiputra et al 2016). For further information on haemodynamic forces in vascular development, remodelling and vessel specification see Culver and Dickinson (2010) and Roman and Pekkan (2012).

Embryonic arteries

Early arteries

After head folding, the embryo has bilateral primitive aortae, each consisting of ventral and dorsal parts that are continuous through the first embryonic aortic arches. The ventral aortae are fused and form a dilated aortic sac. The dorsal aortae run caudally, one on each side of the notochord, and fuse from about the level of the fourth thoracic to the fourth lumbar segment to form a single definitive descending aorta in stage 10 (Fig. 13.17B). In general, more mature endothelial channels are seen in the rostral, more advanced regions of the embryo. More caudally, a changing capillary plexus constantly remodels, becoming confluent with the extraembryonic vascular channels developing in the walls of the yolk sac and allantois (within the connecting stalk). The dorsal continuation of the primitive dorsal aortae forms anastomoses with the vitelline vessels around the yolk sac, and more caudally with anastomoses forming around the allantois.
Initially, the dorsal aortae are the only longitudinal vessels present. Their branches all run at right-angles to the long axis of the embryo. Later, these transverse arteries become connected by longitudinal anastomosing channels that persist in part, forming arteries such as the vertebral, internal thoracic, superior and inferior epigastric, and gastroepiploic (gastro-omental). Each primitive dorsal aorta gives off somatic arteries (intersegmental branches to the body wall), a caudal continuation that passes into the body stalk (the umbilical arteries), lateral splanchnic arteries (paired segmental branches to the mesonephric ridge), and ventral splanchnic arteries (paired segmental branches to the digestive tube).

Aortic arch arteries

The aortic arch arteries arise from the truncus arteriosus and pass bilaterally around the developing pharynx, to join bilateral dorsal aortae (Fig. 13.17). Details of the fates of the aortic arteries are given in Chapter 17, Fig. 17.9. The arterial vessels supplying the head and brain in stage 17 are illustrated in Fig. 17.8 and details of the maturation of cerebral arteries are given in Chapter 14 (see Fig. 14.36).

Somatic arteries

The somatic arteries are intersegmental in position. They persist, almost unchanged, in the thoracic and lumbar regions, as the posterior intercostal, subcostal and lumbar arteries. Each gives off a dorsal ramus that passes backwards in the intersegmental interval and divides into medial and lateral branches to supply the muscles and superficial tissues of the back (Fig. 13.18). The dorsal ramus also gives off a spinal branch that enters the vertebral canal and divides into a series of branches that supply the walls and joints of the osteoligamentous canal, and neural branches to the spinal cord and spinal nerve roots. After giving off its dorsal ramus, each intersegmental artery runs ventrally in the body wall, gives off a lateral branch and terminates in muscular and cutaneous rami.

Lateral splanchnic arteries

On each side the lateral splanchnic arteries supply the mesonephros, metanephros (see Fig. 22.2), testis or ovary and the suprarenal gland. All these structures develop, in whole or in part, from the intermediate mesenchyme, later termed the aorta–gonad–mesonephros region. One testicular or ovarian artery and three suprarenal arteries persist on each side. The phrenic artery branches from the most cranial suprarenal artery, and the renal artery arises from the most caudal. Additional renal arteries are frequently present and may be regarded as branches of persistent lateral splanchnic arteries.

Ventral splanchnic arteries

The ventral splanchnic arteries are originally paired (vitelline) vessels distributed to the capillary plexus in the wall of the yolk sac. After fusion of the dorsal aortae, they merge as unpaired trunks that are distributed to the increasingly defined and lengthening primitive digestive tube. Longitudinal anastomotic channels connect these branches along the dorsal and ventral aspects of the tube, forming dorsal and ventral splanchnic anastomoses (see Figs 13.17, 13.18). Above the respiratory diaphragm, a variable number of ventral splanchnic arteries persist, usually four or five, and supply the thoracic oesophagus. The dorsal splanchnic anastomosis persists in the gastro-omental (gastroepiploic), pancreaticoduodenal and primary branches of the colic arteries, whereas the ventral splanchnic anastomosis forms the right and left gastric and the hepatic arteries. Below the diaphragm, the ventral splanchnic arteries are reduced to three: the coeliac trunk and the superior and inferior mesenteric arteries. As the viscera supplied descend into the abdomen, their origins migrate caudally by differential growth. Thus the origin of the coeliac artery is transferred from the level of C7 to the level of T12; the superior mesenteric from T2 to L1; and the inferior mesenteric from T12 to L3. The superior mesenteric artery extends along the axis of the midgut loop into the extraembryonic coelom from stage 14 until 9 postfertilization weeks. As the proximal limb of the midgut loop develops secondary and tertiary loops, the artery gives rise to a series of branches. At stage 23 eight branches have arisen from the intra-abdominal portion of the artery, with the first three supplying the most proximal portion of the midgut loop. The third branch is bifurcated and supplies the midgut at the umbilical opening. The distal parts of the intra-abdominal branches 5–8 extend into the mesentery within the extraembryonic coelom, and all of the remaining branches, 9–14, supply that part of the midgut loop outside the embryonic body. The distance between the individual branches 9–14 are longer than those between branches 5–8. A colic branch arises consistently on the cranial side of the artery between branches 7 and 9 (Hikspoors et al 2015).

Early vitelline arteries

Vitelline arteries form in the vitelline arterial plexus around the secondary yolk sac and become confluent with the dorsal aortae in stage 12. By stage 13 communication between the embryonic body and yolk sac is limited to a single vitelline artery arising from the splanchnic branches of the dorsal aorta and running rostral to the narrowing vitelline duct (see Fig. 13.17). During stage 14 the artery with the vitelline duct is a landmark for the apex of the midgut loop within the splanchnopleuric mesenchyme of the dorsal mesentery. The vitelline artery is not a patent vessel at stage 16: its proximal origin becomes the origin of the superior mesenteric artery (Hikspoors et al 2015).

Early umbilical arteries

Initially, the umbilical arteries are the direct caudal continuation of the primitive dorsal aortae (see Fig. 13.17). They are present in the body stalk (connecting stalk) before any vitelline or visceral branches emerge, indicating the dominance of the allantoic over the vitelline circulation in the human embryo. (On a comparative basis, the umbilical vessels are chorio-allantoic and therefore ‘somatovisceral’.) After the dorsal aortae fuse, right and left umbilical arteries arise from their ventrolateral aspects and pass lateral to the allantois in the connecting stalk (see Figs 13.17, 22.1). Later, the proximal part of each umbilical artery is joined by a new vessel that leaves the aorta at its termination and passes lateral to the allantois. This, possibly the fifth lumbar intersegmental artery, constitutes the dorsal root of the umbilical artery (the original stem forms the ventral root). The dorsal root gives off the axial artery of the lower limb, branches to the pelvic viscera and, more proximally, the external iliac artery. The ventral root disappears entirely and the umbilical artery now arises from that part of its dorsal root distal to the external iliac artery, i.e. the internal iliac artery.

Embryonic veins

Early veins

The early embryonic veins are often segregated into two groups, visceral and somatic, for convenience and apparent simplicity. The visceral group contains the derivatives of the vitelline and umbilical veins, and the somatic group includes all remaining veins. It should be noted that, with time, embryonic veins change the principal tissues they drain. They may receive radicles from obviously parietal tissues, which become confluent with drainage channels that are clearly visceral, and so form a compound vessel. The arrangement of the early embryonic veins is initially symmetric (see Fig. 13.17). The primitive tubular symmetric heart receives its venous return through the right and left horns of the sinus venosus, which are initially embedded in the mesenchyme of the septum transversum. Each horn receives vitelline, umbilical and the common cardinal veins (see Fig. 13.8). This symmetric pattern changes as the heart and gut develop and cardiac return is diverted to the right side of the heart.

Cardinal veins and somatic venous complexes

The initial venous channels in the early embryo have conventionally been termed cardinal because of their importance at this stage. The cardinal venous complexes are first represented by two large vessels on each side, the precardinal portion is rostral and the postcardinal is caudal to the heart. The two vessels unite to form a short common cardinal vein which passes ventrally to open into the corresponding horn of the sinus venosus (see Figs 13.1B, 13.17A; Fig. 13.19B). Further development of the precardinal veins directing the venous drainage of the head and arms to the right gives rise to the superior vena cava. Caudally, the postcardinal veins connect to a range of bilateral longitudinal anastomoses and venous confluences that cross the midline and successively direct the venous drainage from the lower limbs and body to the right, forming the inferior vena cava.
The precardinal veins join across the midline via an oblique interprecardial anastomosis (see Fig. 13.19B(i)) and undergo further remodelling as the head develops to form the dural venous sinuses (Ch. 17, p. 279; see Fig. 17.10). As the embryo increases in size, the postcardinal veins are insufficient channels to carry the venous return from the body wall and the developing mesonephros and gonads, and are supplemented by the bilateral longitudinal channels that anastomose with each other and with the posterior cardinal system. These channels are the subcardinal veins ventrally, and the subcentral, supracardinal, azygos line, and precostal veins more dorsally (Fig. 13.19A).
Postcardinal veins
The early postcardinal veins communicate across the midline via an interpostcardinal anastomosis (see Fig. 13.19B(ii)). This remains as an oblique transverse anastomosis between the iliac veins, and becomes the major part of the definitive left common iliac vein. It diverts an increasing volume of blood into the right longitudinal veins, which accounts for the ultimate disappearance of most of those on the left.
Subcardinal veins
Subcardinal veins (see Fig. 13.19B(i)) form in the ventromedial parts of the mesonephric ridges and become connected to the postcardinal veins by a number of vessels traversing the medial part of the ridges. They communicate by a pre-aortic intersubcardinal anastomotic plexus (the renal collar), which later forms the part of the left renal vein that crosses anterior to the abdominal aorta. On the right side, the subcardinal vein comes into intimate relationship with the developing liver. A cranial extension of the vessel establishes continuity with a corresponding new formation that is growing caudally from the right vitelline hepatocardiac (common hepatic) vein. In this way, on the right side, a more direct route is established to the heart and the prerenal (cranial) segment of the developing inferior vena cava.
Supracardinal veins
Supracardinal veins (see Fig. 13.19B(ii)) form dorsolateral to the aorta and lateral to the sympathetic trunk and take over the intersegmental venous drainage from the posterior cardinal vein. The supracardinal veins are also referred to as lateral sympathetic line veins or the thoracolumbar line. They receive the larger venous drainage of the growing body wall and communicate with the iliac veins caudally and with each other via the azygos lines and subcentral veins. The right supracardinal vein persists and forms the greater part of the postrenal segment of the inferior vena cava. The continuity of the vessel is maintained by the persistence of the anastomosis between the right supracardinal and the right subcardinal in the renal collar, a complete venous ring around the aorta below the origin of the superior mesenteric artery. The left supracardinal disappears, but some of the renal collar formed by the left supracardinal–subcardinal anastomosis persists in the left renal vein. Both supracardinal veins drain cranially into the subcardinal veins.
Azygos line veins
Azygos line veins form dorsolateral to the aorta and medial to the sympathetic trunk (see Fig. 13.19B(ii)). These channels, also referred to as the medial sympathetic line veins, gradually take over the intersegmental venous drainage from the supracardinal veins. The intersegmental veins now reach their longitudinal channel by passing medial to the sympathetic trunk, a relationship that the lumbar and intercostal veins subsequently maintain. Cranially, the azygos lines join the persistent cranial ends of the posterior cardinal veins.
Subcentral veins
Subcentral veins form directly dorsal to the aorta in the interval between the origins of the paired intersegmental arteries (see Fig. 13.19A). These veins communicate freely with each other and with the azygos line veins: these connections ultimately form the retro-aortic parts of the left lumbar veins and of the hemiazygos veins.
Precostal or lumbocostal venous line
A precostal or lumbocostal venous line, anterior to the vertebrocostal element and posterior to the supracardinal, is recognized by some authorities. The ascending lumbar vein is a possible derivative.

Further development of the somatic veins

The final arrangement of the embryonic abdominal and thoracic longitudinal cardinal veins may be summarized as follows. The terminal part of the left postcardinal vein forms the distal part of the left superior intercostal vein. On the right side, its cranial end persists as the terminal part of the azygos vein. The caudal part of the subcardinal vein is, in part, incorporated in the testicular or ovarian vein and partly disappears. The cranial end of the right subcardinal vein is incorporated into the inferior vena cava and also forms the right suprarenal vein. The left subcardinal vein, cranial to the intersubcardinal anastomosis, is incorporated into the left suprarenal vein. The renal and testicular or ovarian veins on both sides join the supracardinal–subcardinal anastomosis. On the left side, this connects directly to the forming inferior vena cava via an intersubcardinal anastomosis; the right supracardinal vein forms much of the caudal (postrenal) segment of the inferior vena cava. The left supracardinal vein disappears entirely. The right azygos line persists in its thoracic part to form all but the terminal part of the azygos vein. Its lumbar part can usually be identified as a small vessel that leaves the azygos vein on the body of the twelfth thoracic vertebra and descends on the vertebral column, deep to the right crus of the diaphragm, to join the posterior aspect of the inferior vena cava at the upper end of its postrenal segment. The left azygos line forms the hemiazygos veins. The subcentral veins give rise to the retro-aortic parts of the left lumbar veins and of the hemiazygos veins (see Fig. 13.19B(iv)).

Inferior vena cava

The inferior vena cava (see Fig. 13.19B(iv)) is a composite vessel that develops on the posterior abdominal wall dorsal to the developing peritoneal cavity. It forms as a consequence of the temporal remodelling of the successive venous complexes described above. The precise mode of development of its postrenal segment (caudal to the renal vein) is still somewhat uncertain. Its function is initially carried out by the right and left postcardinal veins which receive the venous drainage of the lower limb buds and pelvis, and run in the dorsal part of the mesonephric ridges, receiving tributaries from the body wall (intersegmental veins) and from the derivatives of the mesonephroi. Many of the changes seen in the development of the infrahepatic caval and azygos systems in the human may result from lateral to medial movement of the vessels as a consequence of the growth of the abdominal viscera (Hikspoors et al 2015).
Descriptions of venous development are very largely based on studies on animals, where the dimensions and final disposition of visceral organs differ from those found in humans. In some animals, the right postcardinal vein constitutes a large part of the postrenal segment of the inferior vena cava. In these cases, the right ureter, on leaving the kidney, passes medially, dorsal to the vessel and then, curving round its medial side, crosses its ventral aspect. Rarely, a similar condition is found in humans, and indicates the persistence of the right postcardinal vein and failure of the right supracardinal to play its normal part in the development of the inferior vena cava.
Only the supracardinal part of the inferior vena cava receives intersegmental venous drainage. The postrenal (caudal) segment of the inferior vena cava is on a plane that lies dorsal to the plane of the prerenal (cranial) segment. Thus, the right phrenic, suprarenal and renal arteries, which represent persistent mesonephric arteries, pass behind the inferior vena cava, whereas the testicular or ovarian artery, which has a similar developmental origin, passes anterior to it.
In summary, the inferior vena cava is formed from below upwards by the confluence of: the common iliac veins, a short segment of the right postcardinal vein, the postcardinal–supracardinal anastomosis, part of the right supracardinal vein, the right supracardinal–subcardinal anastomosis, right subcardinal vein, a new anastomotic channel of double origin (the hepatic segment of the inferior vena cava) and the cardiac termination of the right vitelline hepatocardiac vein (common hepatic vein).

Visceral venous complexes, ductus venosus and the portal vein

Early vitelline veins
The early vitelline veins drain capillary plexuses that develop within the extraembryonic splanchnopleuric mesenchyme of the secondary yolk sac (see Fig. 9.10). With head, tail and lateral fold formation, elongation of the rostral endoderm forms the foregut, the allantois is brought within the body cavity with the hindgut dorsally, and the midgut remains confluent with the yolk sac (see Fig. 10.10). At stage 12, derivatives from all these levels possess a venous drainage that is originally vitelline and enters the sinus venosus as the most medial of the vessels contributing to the inflow tract of the heart (see Fig. 13.8). The intraembryonic confluence of both vitelline veins descends between stages 14 and 18 as the heart descends.
Early umbilical veins
The early umbilical veins form in the mesenchyme surrounding the allantois and in the mesenchymal connecting stalk confluent with the developing placenta (see Figs 9.2, 9.10, 13.1). The human endodermal allantois is very small, projecting only into the (proximal) embryonic end of the connecting stalk, which is therefore regarded as precociously formed allantoic mesenchyme. Peripheral venules drain the mesenchymal cores of the placental chorionic villous stems and terminal villi. They are the radicles of a single vessel, the vena umbilicalis impar, that traverses the mixed mesenchyme of the connecting stalk to reach the caudal rim of the developing umbilical cord where it divides into primitive right and left umbilical veins. Each curves rostrally in the somatopleuric lateral border of the umbilicus, i.e. where intraembryonic and extraembryonic or amniotic somatopleure are continuous, lying lateral to the communication between both the intraembryonic and the extraembryonic coeloms. Rostrolateral to the umbilicus, the two umbilical veins reach, enter and traverse the junctional mesenchyme of the septum transversum and connect with septal capillary plexuses (Fig. 13.20). They then continue, entering their corresponding horn of the sinus venosus lateral to the terminations of the vitelline veins.
Later vitelline veins
From stage 13 the vitelline veins passing to the embryo from the yolk sac wall enter an intervitelline anastomosis (hepatic plexus) in the caudal part of the septum transversum before reaching the sinus venosus. This network is ventral to the future subdiaphragmatic end of the oesophagus, primitive stomach, the superior (first) and descending (second) regions of the duodenum and the remainder of the duodenal tube. The channel is sometimes termed suprahepatic because of the position of the hepatic primordium; as the latter expands, it becomes partly intrahepatic. The terminations of the vitelline veins from this time are termed hepatocardiac channels.
Development of the portal circulation is initiated by changes in the disposition of the duodenum and related changes to the intervitelline anastomotic veins. The duodenum develops from the caudal part of the foregut (that part of the gut with dorsolateral pericardioperitoneal canals), and the cranial part of the midgut. All portions are surrounded by splanchnopleuric mesenchyme: the portal circulation develops from angiogenic lineages in this tissue. Ventrally only the caudal part of the foregut is continuous with the ventral mesogastrium, derived from the cranial part of the septum transversum mesenchyme; the cranial midgut ventral surface lies free in the future peritoneal cavity. Dorsally, the duodenum is attached by splanchnopleuric mesenchyme to a dorsal mesoduodenum cranially and the dorsal mesentery of the midgut caudally.
Proximally a subdiaphragmatic (suprahepatic) anastomosis lies close to the foregut portion of the duodenum. As the cranial midgut portion of the duodenum lengthens, three more transverse duodenal intervitelline anastomoses, subhepatic (ventral duodenal), intermediate (dorsal duodenal) and caudal (ventral duodenal), become visible (see Fig. 13.20B). As can be inferred from their names, their relation to the duodenum alternates and it has become customary to describe the blood flow through these paraduodenal vitelline veins and their associated anastomoses as forming a figure of eight.
Distally, when the connection of the yolk sac to the midgut narrows, forming the vitelline duct, the vitelline veins also lengthen and continue on each side of the duct (see Fig. 21.2B). They lengthen even further when the midgut loop extends into the extraembryonic coelom. The left vitelline vein lies within the dorsal mesentery between the proximal and distal parts of the midgut loop. In stage 14 embryos, the right vitelline vein is cranial to the proximal midgut loop within the mesenchyme surrounding the vitelline duct; from stage 16 onwards it appears disconnected from this mesenchyme and free within the abdominal cavity alongside the vitelline duct. Its course forms the central axis of the primary midgut loop and marks the apex of the loop until the return of the herniated small intestine to the abdominal cavity (Hikspoors et al 2015).
Later umbilical veins
The further development of the vitelline veins and their anastomoses is closely interlocked with the growth of hepatic sinusoids in the septum transversum and changes to the umbilical veins. Evagination of hepatic ‘cords’ of presumptive hepatocytes from the endodermal foregut into the septum transversum mesenchyme initiates the formation of early plexiform connections of the hepatic sinusoids. As these expand laterally and extend caudally, they merge with the terminal connections of the vitelline and umbilical veins and subsume them within a series of complex branching vessels conducting blood in and out of the hepatic sinusoids.
Both deoxygenated and oxygenated blood enter the developing hepatic sinusoids, in the same way as the adult liver sinusoids convey portal and systemic blood. However, in the embryo and fetus, both blood supplies travel within veins. Vitelline veins from the extraembryonic yolk sac and the intraembryonic developing gut carry deoxygenated blood, and umbilical veins carry oxygenated blood from the placenta. All of these vessels branch and enter the liver via afferent hepatic veins, the venae advehentes. Blood leaves the hepatic sinusoids through four efferent hepatic veins, the venae revehentes; these are the terminal ends of the right and left vitelline veins, and the right and left umbilical veins, now termed hepatocardiac veins, all of which enter the sinus venosus (Fig. 13.21).
Changes to the circulation that involve returning venous blood to the right side via modifications of the cardinal system of veins occur contemporaneously with changes to the terminal portions of the vitelline and umbilical veins. The right umbilical vein regresses and atrophies during stages 14–15 (see Fig. 13.21). The hepatic terminal of the left umbilical vein retains some vessels that discharge into the sinusoids, but blood flow preferentially enlarges connections with the left half of the subhepatic (ventral duodenal) intervitelline anastomosis. These connections form a channel, the ductus venosus, which courses to the right half of the subdiaphragmatic anastomosis to join the right hepatocardiac channel (the termination of the right vitelline vein) and empty into the inferior vena cava (see Fig. 13.21; Fig. 13.22). The portal vein is derived from presumptive superior mesenteric and splenic veins that pass via the intermediate (dorsal duodenal) intervitelline anastomosis into the liver sinusoids via a right vena advehente. Blood in the umbilical vein supplies the left lobe of the liver and ductus venosus; the right lobe of the liver receives blood from the portal circulation. This arrangement means that the left lobe of the liver receives the most oxygenated blood returning from the placenta: the remainder (proportions change during the third trimester) passes into the ductus venosus and, via haemodynamic streaming, to the inferior vena cava and right atrium, then through the foramen ovale to the left atrium, to supply heart muscle and brain.

Embryonic Lymphatic Vessels

Lymphatic endothelial cells in lymphatic capillaries display a discontinuous basal lamina and basement membrane. They are held by anchoring filaments to the surrounding extracellular matrix. In the mouse, their differentiation starts with expression of Sox-18 and Prox-1, a Prospero homeobox transcription factor, in a subset of cardinal veins (Zhou et al 2010, Park et al 2011). Prox-1 is the most specific and functional lymphatic marker: its disruption leads to failure of lymphatic vessel development. Abnormal or absent lymphatic development is also seen with lack of vascular endothelial growth factor 3 (VEGFR3) and lack of angiopoietin 1, respectively (Yamashita 2007). Abnormalities of development and of remodelling of primary lymphatic vessels also occur if Akt, a serine/theonine protein kinase, is absent, possibly due to insufficient recruitment of smooth muscle cells to larger lymphatic vessels (Zhou et al 2010). An overview of the development of the mammalian lymphatic vasculature and genes involved, based on the mouse embryo, is given in Yang and Oliver (2014).
The earliest lymphatic vessels arise from budding of lymphatic endothelial cells from the cardinal veins to form lymph sacs (Eichmann et al 2005). Six early lymph sacs can be identified; two are paired (the jugular and the posterior lymph sacs) and two are unpaired (the retroperitoneal sac and the cisterna chyli). In lower mammals, an additional pair (subclavian) is present, but in the human embryo, these are merely extensions of the jugular sacs.
The jugular lymph sac is the first to appear, at the junction of the subclavian vein with the precardinal vein, with later prolongations along the internal and external jugular veins. The posterior lymph sac encircles the left common iliac vein, the retroperitoneal sac appears in the root of the mesentery near the suprarenal glands, and the cisterna chyli appears opposite the third and fourth lumbar vertebrae (Fig. 13.23). The lymph vessels bud out from the lymph sacs along lines that correspond more or less closely with the course of embryonic blood vessels (typically of the veins); many also arise de novo in the mesenchyme and establish connections with existing vessels (Fig. 13.24). The deeper plexuses are the first to be developed in the body wall and the wall of the intestine, and the vessels in the superficial layers are gradually formed by continued growth.
The thoracic duct is derived from left and right lymphatic trunks joined across the midline by anastomoses. The definitive duct consists of the lower portion of the right trunk, a transverse anastomosis and the upper portion of the left trunk. In contrast to the adult arrangement, the fetal thoracic duct is dilated at its origin from the cisterna chyli, and there is no associated lymphatic plexus draining the thoracic wall (Amore et al 2016). Perinatal and neonatal chylous visceral effusions are rare, but may be caused by hypoplastic or hyperplastic development of the lymphatic system. Chylothorax is the most common cause of pleural effusion in the neonate; primary chylous ascites is caused by anomalous development of the intra-abdominal lymphatic vessels (Bellini et al 2012).

Lymph nodes and lymphoid tissues

Lymph vessels can be seen in the embryo in the cervical region from stage 16. Lymph nodes provide regional proliferative foci for lymphocytes and have been identified from week 9. Early lymph sacs become infiltrated by lymphoid cells, and the outer portion of each sac becomes the subcapsular sinus of the lymph node. Morphological differentiation of medullary and cortical compartments has not been observed until the end of postfertilization week 10 (Tonar et al 2001). At the same time as these early lymph nodes are developing, the nasopharyngeal wall is infiltrated by lymphoid cells that are believed to herald the early development of the tubal and nasopharyngeal tonsils.

Fetal Circulation

As the cardiovascular system develops, the embryonic circulation is modified into a fetal circulation that is able to establish a separate pulmonary circulation at birth. The dorsal aortae persist on the cranial side of the third aortic arches as the internal carotid arteries (see Fig. 17.9). The dorsal aorta between the third and fourth aortic arches, the carotid duct, diminishes and ultimately disappears. From the fourth arch to the origin of the seventh intersegmental artery, the right dorsal aorta becomes part of the right subclavian artery, and caudal to the seventh intersegmental artery, the right dorsal aorta disappears up to the site where the thoracic aortae fuse. The remnants of the left carotid duct are found in the descending part of the aortic arch. The fused right and left embryonic dorsal aortae persist as the definitive descending thoracic and abdominal aorta.
Concomitant changes also occur in the early venous system. The precardinal veins enlarge as the embryo develops. They are further augmented by the subclavian veins from the upper limb buds, which become the chief tributaries of the common cardinal veins, gradually assuming a craniocaudal orientation as the thorax enlarges around the heart (see Fig. 13.19). These parts of the original precardinal vein, rostral to the subclavian veins, are now the internal jugular veins, and their confluences are the brachiocephalic veins. The right and left common cardinal veins are originally of the same diameter but the development of a large oblique transverse connection means that the left brachiocephalic vein carries more blood across from the left to the right. The part of the original right precardinal vein between the junction of the two brachiocephalic veins and the azygos veins will give rise to the upper part of the superior vena cava. The caudal part of this vessel, below the entrance of the azygos vein, is formed by the right common cardinal vein. The left precardinal and left common cardinal veins caudal to the transverse branching of the left brachiocephalic vein will disappear; the precardinal vein constitutes the terminal part of the left superior intercostal vein, while the common cardinal vein is represented by the ligament of the left superior vena cava and the oblique vein of the left atrium (vein of Marshall). The remainder of the left superior intercostal vein is developed from the cranial end of the postcardinal vein and drains the second, third and, on occasion, the fourth intercostal veins. The oblique vein of the left atrium passes downwards across the back of the left atrium to open into the coronary sinus, which represents the persistent left sinus horn.

Functioning fetal shunts

The adult circulation consists of higher pressure systemic and lower pressure pulmonary circulations, whereas the fetal circulation consists of systemic and placental circulations and a very limited high resistance pulmonary circulation (because the lung fields are not open). The ductus venosus, foramen ovale and ductus arteriosus are important conduits in the fetal circulation: the connection of a number of vascular ducts (shunts) and a valve between the atria enables the fetal circulation to change to the adult circuits within hours and days after delivery.
The placenta serves as the organ for fetal exchange of nutrients, gases and waste products of the blood, and in the second trimester receives 30% of the cardiac output (Kiserud and Haugen 2017). Fetal blood reaches the placenta via two umbilical arteries (carrying deoxygenated blood) and, in fetal life, returns by a single left umbilical vein (carrying oxygenated blood) because the right embryonic umbilical vein regresses. The left umbilical vein enters the abdomen at the umbilicus and angles upwards towards the liver in the caudal edge of the peritoneal layers of the original ventral mesentery, later termed the falciform ligament (see Fig. 21.6). The vein reaches the hepatic surface, joins the left branch of the comparatively small portal vein at the porta hepatis, and continues as the ductus venosus which ascends posterior to the liver to join the left hepatic vein near its termination in the inferior vena cava (Fig. 13.25; see Figs 13.20–13.22). In the second trimester about 70% of the blood in the umbilical vein passes into the liver and 30% passes into the ductus venosus (Kiserud and Haugen 2017).
The ductus venosus in the fetus does not appear to change size between postmenstrual weeks 13–17. During this time the portal sinus, which leads to the ductus venosus, has an average diameter of 2.6 mm and is lined by smooth endothelium. It ends at a thickening of subendothelial elastic fibres that form a protrusion into the ductal lumen, the shelf of the ductus venosus inlet. The ductus venosus is about 5.1 mm long (range 3.4–6.5) and has an average diameter of 1.4 mm at its inlet and outlet. It displays longitudinal endothelial corrugations supported by underlying elastin fibres and a single layer of longitudinally arranged smooth muscle cells that extends along the entire length of the ductus. Adrenergic nerve fibres have been identified in the wall of the ductus venosus at postmenstrual weeks 20–24; contraction in response to noradrenaline, acetylcholine and 5-HT has been demonstrated, as has dilation in response to NO, PGE1 and hypoxaemia (Kiserud and Haugen 2017). From the portal sinus to the subdiaphragmatic vestibulum, the ductus venosus progressively narrows and then dilates; the lumen is described as hourglass-shaped. It has been suggested that, unlike other veins, the ductus venosus acts as a high-pressure vessel: the narrowing of the inlet accelerates low-pressure, high-volume flow in the portal sinus, and the abundant elastic fibres in its wall propagate the wave by elastic recoil (Mavrides et al 2002). For details of the fetoplacental circulation see Kiserud and Haugen (2017).
Blood from the ductus venosus and hepatic veins joins the venous return from the lower limbs and abdominal wall in the inferior vena cava and enters the right atrium. The portocaval pressure gradient (blood pressure of the intra-abdominal umbilical vein) accelerates the blood in the ductus venosus to a velocity three times that of the umbilical vein and in the most favourable direction to open the foramen ovale valve and enter the left atrium (Kiserud and Haugen 2017). On ultrasound examination the foramen ovale can be seen with the septum primum, a flap-like valve, pushed to the left (see Video 13.2). From the left atrium, blood enters the left ventricle and from there flows to the aorta and is probably distributed almost entirely to the heart, head and upper limbs. Blood returning from the head and upper limbs enters the right atrium via the superior vena cava, flows through the right atrioventricular orifice into the right ventricle, together with the small amount of blood that is returned via the inferior vena cava, and then passes into the pulmonary trunk. However, because the fetal lungs are largely inactive, only a small amount of blood, 13% at week 20 and 21% at week 38, will flow from the right ventricle through the right and left pulmonary arteries to return to the left atrium via the pulmonary veins. The greater part of the outflow through the pulmonary trunk is carried by the ductus arteriosus directly to the aorta, where it mixes with any remaining blood that comes from the left ventricle. During the second half of pregnancy, the fetus maintains a combined cardiac output of 400 mL/kg/min; approximately two-thirds of the total fetal cardiac output is ejected by the right ventricle and the remaining third by the left ventricle (Kiserud and Haugen 2017, Fineman and Maltepe 2018).
The ductus arteriosus, a remnant of the left sixth pharyngeal arch artery, allows blood from the right ventricle to reach the arch of the aorta and thus bypass the fetal lungs (see Fig. 13.25; Fig. 13.26). In the fetus, it arises as a direct continuation of the pulmonary trunk at the point where it divides into right and left pulmonary arteries. It is 8–12 mm long, and joins the aorta at an angle of 30–35° on the left side, anterolaterally, below the origin of the left subclavian artery. The opening of the ductus arteriosus into the aorta is greatly elongated. Its diameter at its origin from the pulmonary trunk, when distended with blood, is 4–5 mm, which is nearly equal to the diameter of the adjacent ascending aorta (5–6 mm). Both arteries taper to a smaller diameter as they pass inferiorly and the aorta remains slightly larger (4 mm) (Fig. 13.27).
The ductus arteriosus differs from the other great vessels that arise from the heart because its tunica media contains smooth muscle rather than layers of elastin. The intima of the ductus arteriosus starts to thicken by accumulation of glycosaminoglycans beneath the endothelium. The left recurrent laryngeal nerve (which, at stage 16, is very large in relation to the aortic arch system) passes inferior to the ductus arteriosus.
The ductus arteriosus remains patent during intrauterine life due to synergistic interaction of high circulating PGE2 (prostaglandin E2) and low oxygen tension (Hung et al 2018). Fetal and neonatal ductal tissue produce PGE2 and to a lesser extent PGE12. Treatment for conditions such as polyhydramnios, where indomethacin is given, may cause premature intrauterine closure of the ductus arteriosus and consequent associated problems.

Antenatal ultrasound imaging of the fetal heart and circulation

The first trimester ultrasound scan to confirm successful implantation and a live conceptus will identify an embryonic heartbeat from postmenstrual week 6 (stage 10 embryo). The embryonic heart rate is about 175 beats per minute at stage 23.
In the routine second trimester ultrasound scan at 18–20 postmenstrual weeks, the evaluation initially checks that the fetal heart and stomach are both on the left and that the liver is on the right. A specific series of sectional views of the heart are recorded. In a four-chamber view of the fetal heart (observed up to about postmenstrual week 28), the following are checked: the heart occupies about one-third of the thorax in the anterior left quadrant of the chest; the two atria are of equal size as are the two ventricles and there is an intact interventricular septum; and the apex of the heart points to the left anterior thorax. In moving images, the ventricles show equal contraction, the two atrioventricular canals open equally and the foramen ovale flap valve can be seen flickering in the left atrium (see Videos 13.2, 23.1). The function and rhythm of the heart is recorded. The heart rate is about 140 beats per minute throughout fetal life, despite the increase in heart size over this period: heart rate increase is a sign of fetal distress. The blood flow through the heart can be demonstrated with colour flow (Fig. 13.28). After postmenstrual week 28 the right ventricle may look dilated compared with the left. The origin of the great vessels from their respective ventricles is assessed in transverse section, and the aorta is assessed longitudinally (see Fig. 23.5); it normally arises in the centre of the thorax and the vessels to the head and neck can be seen arising from the curve of the aortic arch.
In a healthy near-term fetus the ductus venosus has an hourglass shape. Ultrasound studies have shown that 30% of umbilical venous flow enters the ductus venosus at about postmenstrual week 20 declining to 20% during the last trimester (Kiserud and Haugen 2017). Blood flow in this fetal shunt regulates inflow to the right atrium through a constricted aperture that admits umbilical venous blood via a narrow, high velocity flow directly to the foramen ovale. In healthy fetuses the ductus venosus regulates the distribution of placental nutrients and oxygen by restricting the centralization of blood flow, and it can actively dilate in hypoxaemia (Kaimal 2019). Anomalous flow in the first trimester has been reported in Down’s syndrome and fetal growth restriction is associated with changes of flow through the ductus venosus. (For further details of ultrasound findings consult Coady and Bower (2015).)

Neonatal heart and circulatory changes at birth

At full term (notionally 40 postmenstrual weeks), the heart is situated midway between the crown of the head and the lower level of the buttocks and weighs about 20 g (see Fig. 23.14). Because the right ventricle has been working against the systemic pressure in the fetus, the pulmonary circulation not yet being active, the right ventricle is heavier at birth and continues to be so for up to 1–2 postnatal months. With the establishment of the pulmonary circulation, the work of the right side of the heart decreases, and the left side of the heart, particularly the ventricle, grows rapidly to meet the increased demand. The ventral surface of the heart is formed mainly by the right atrium and right ventricle, as in the adult. This surface is usually covered by the thymus, which may extend over the base of the right ventricle. Soon after birth, the fetal shunts of the foramen ovale and the ductus arteriosus and ductus venosus will close. The umbilical vessels will also occlude as the circulation of the neonate becomes independent of maternal oxygen supply after detachment of the placenta from the uterine wall.
Congenital heart disease has an overall prevalence of 8/1000 births (Tarabit et al 2018). Neonates with congenital heart disease are at higher risk of fetal growth restriction and preterm birth; infant mortality rates are reported to be 20% for congenital heart disease and 65% for congenital heart disease and preterm delivery (Laas et al 2017). Assisted reproductive techniques have been shown to affect the incidence of preterm delivery and of congenital heart disease. The incidence of preterm birth in cases of congenital heart disease has been reported as about 13% in spontaneously conceived neonates, and about 40% in conception following infertility treatments (Tarabit et al 2018).

Closure of the umbilical vessels

Umbilical arteries
The fetal umbilical arteries are continuous with the internal iliac arteries. Their lumen is 2–3 mm in diameter at their origin, when distended. They narrow as they approach the umbilicus, where there is a reciprocal thickening of the tunica media, reflecting an increase in the number of longitudinal smooth muscle and elastic fibres. The umbilical vessels lack a nerve supply in their extra-abdominal course. Before birth, there is a proliferation of connective tissue within the vessel wall. Within minutes of delivery, blood flow within the umbilical cord vessels decreases and the vessels constrict in response to handling, stretching, cooling and altered tensions of oxygen and carbon dioxide. After the cord is severed, the umbilical arteries constrict, preventing significant blood loss; thrombi often form in the distal ends of the arteries. Full closure of the umbilical vessels is mediated by several factors including prostaglandins, endoperoxides and serotonin. As oxygen tension increases shortly after birth, bradykinin, one of the kinin polypeptide hormones that induce contraction or relaxation of smooth muscle, forms in the blood of the umbilical cord. It is also formed and released by granular leukocytes in the lungs of the neonate after exposure to adequate oxygen. Bradykinin is a potent constrictor of the umbilical arteries and veins, and of the ductus arteriosus, but is also a potent inhibitor of contraction of the pulmonary vessels. The umbilical arteries obliterate from their distal ends until, by the end of postnatal month 2–3, involution has occurred at the level of the superior vesical arteries: the proximal parts of the obliterated vessels remain as the medial umbilical ligaments (see Ch. 21).
Umbilical vein
The umbilical vein in the neonate is 2–3 cm long and 4–5 mm in diameter when distended. It passes from the umbilicus within the layers of the falciform ligament, superiorly and to the right, to the porta hepatis, where it gives off several large intrahepatic branches to the liver and then joins the left branch of the portal vein and the ductus venosus. The thin-walled umbilical vein possesses a definite internal lamina of elastic fibres at the umbilical ring but not in its intra-abdominal course. The tunica media contains smooth muscle fibres, collagen and elastic fibres.
When the cord is severed, the umbilical vein constricts, but not as vigorously as the arteries. The rapid decrease in pressure in the umbilical vein after the cord is clamped means that the elastic tissue at the umbilical ring is sufficient to arrest any retrograde flow along the vessel. Before birth, there is a subintimal proliferation of connective tissue; after birth, the contraction of the collagen fibres in the tunica media and the increased subintimal connective tissue contribute to the transformation of the vessel into the ligamentum teres hepatis (round ligament of the liver). Obliteration occurs from the umbilical ring towards the hepatic end. No thrombi are formed in the obliteration process. For up to 48 hours after birth, the intra-abdominal portion of the umbilical vein can be easily dilated. In most adults, the original lumen of the vein persists through the ligamentum teres hepatis and can be dilated to 5–6 mm in diameter.
Closure of the ductus venosus
In the fetus, the ductus venosus is a direct continuation of the umbilical vein and arises from the left branch of the portal vein, directly opposite the termination of the umbilical vein. It passes for 2–3 cm within the layers of the lesser omentum, in a groove between the left lobe and caudate lobe of the liver, before terminating in either the inferior vena cava, or in the left hepatic vein immediately before it joins the inferior vena cava. In the area of transition into the portal sinus, the vessel wall contains circularly arranged smooth muscle fibres, collagen and abundant elastic fibres and its inner surface is ridged. After birth, with the loss of the umbilical blood flow, the blood pressure in the portal sinus drops, the width of the ductus venosus decreases and the inner edges are approximated. The remaining lumen becomes filled with cell-dense connective tissue that occludes the whole vessel. The ductus venosus is already closed in about one-third of neonates, for the remainder it closes between postnatal days 4 and 6, sooner in post-term and macrosomic neonates. Obliteration begins at the portal vein end in the second postnatal week, progresses to the vena cava and is complete by postnatal month 2–3. The fibrous remnant of the ductus venosus is the ligamentum venosum.

Closure of the foramen ovale

Within the heart, considered relative to the thoracic landmarks, the foramen ovale lies at the level of the third intercostal space (see Fig. 23.14). It is approximately 4–6  mm long and 3–4 mm wide and blood passes through it from the ventrally positioned right atrium dorsally and cranially to reach the left atrium. At birth, as pulmonary respiration begins, pulmonary blood flow increases dramatically and the left atrium now receives pulmonary venous blood under a relatively high pressure. A decrease in pressure occurs in the inferior vena cava as a result of the reduction of venous return concomitant with occlusion of the umbilical vein and ductus venosus, lowering the right atrial pressure. Left atrial pressure is now greater than that of the right atrium, and the foramen ovale is closed by apposition. Subsequent fusion of the primary septum to the rims of the secondary septum occurs, likely by fibrosis mediated by transition of the endocardium into mesenchyme. Although the foramen ovale closes functionally after pulmonary respiration is established, it does not become structurally closed until later: it is obliterated in fewer than 3% of infants 2 weeks after birth. In adults, fusion is sometimes incomplete, and the foramen remains probe-patent in up to one-third of all individuals. Almost invariably, this lack of fusion has no functional effect because the inequality of atrial pressures and the valve-like arrangement of the opening do not favour right to left shunting of blood.

Closure of the ductus arteriosus

At birth when a neonate takes its first breaths the lungs expand and the pulmonary resistance decreases. Blood flow from the right ventricle now passes along the pulmonary trunk to the lungs and the flow along the ductus arteriosus reverses, flowing from the aorta to the pulmonary trunk. Animal studies have shown that reversal of flow occurs rapidly after ventilation begins. Blood flow from the left ventricle travels via the aorta: the ductus arteriosus contributes almost 50% of total pulmonary blood flow in the neonate. The initial process of ductal closure consists of contraction of the smooth muscle cells and development of sub-endothelial oedema. Initial constriction at birth has been attributed to increased oxygen tension and decrease in prostaglandin E2 (Hung et al 2018). Other factors in the closure process include increased plasma catecholamine concentrations; suppression of prostaglandin I2 production; unresponsive prostaglandin E receptors; a synergistic role of prostaglandin F2a and oxygen concentrations; and a decrease in plasma adenosine concentration. The high oxygen tension of the reversed blood flow through the ductus is thought to initiate the synthesis of a hydroperoxy fatty acid that suppresses prostacyclin production, thus exposing the ductus to the contractile effects of prostaglandin endoperoxide. A neural factor may also be involved; the muscular wall has afferent and efferent nerve endings and responds to adrenaline (epinephrine) and noradrenaline (norepinephrine) within 10 minutes of the onset of ventilation; after 2 postnatal hours, the response to adrenaline falls to about 25%. The turbulence and increased shear stress of blood flow through the ductus apparently releases vasoconstrictive factors from the endothelium (Crossley et al 2009). For details of the molecular mechanisms regulating ductus closure see Hung et al (2018).
The first stage of ductal closure is completed within 10–15 hours, occurring in 50% of full-term neonates at 24 hours, in 90% by 48 hours and in 100% by 72 hours. Definitive closure usually takes 2–3 weeks, but can take up to 3 months before it is considered permanent. Several mechanisms have been implicated as mediators of permanent closure, including intimal cushion formation, proliferation and migration of smooth muscle cells, endothelial cell proliferation and production of extracellular matrix (Hung et al 2018). Following functional closure, the ductal smooth muscle cells undergo apoptosis, resulting in fibrosis and the formation of the ligamentum arteriosum that connects the left pulmonary artery (near its origin) to the aortic arch.
Patent ductus arteriosus
Patent ductus arteriosus can be defined as a failure of the ductus arteriosus to close within 72 hours after birth (Dice and Bhatia 2007). It affects 1 in 2000 live births in the United States and is seen most frequently in preterm neonates and in up to 70% of those born before postmenstrual week 28 (Clyman 2013, Gillam-Krakauer and Reese 2018). Persistent patency of the ductus arteriosus should be distinguished from delayed closure. The persistently patent ductus can be an obligatory part of the circulation when associated with transposition of the great arteries since it allows shunting: the patency may be ensured by appropriate medication. Developmentally, failure to close may be related to reduced intimal thickening, the presence of a thick elastic lamina, low levels of circulating prostaglandins and a low oxygen tension after birth. The condition affects 66% of infants with congenital heart disease born at high altitude (Chun et al 2019). Rubella infection and a genetic predisposition are associated with a patent ductus arteriosus. Patent ductus arteriosus is almost always found in Char syndrome, a mutation in TFAP2B, encoding a transcription factor in cardiac neural crest cells (Huang et al 2008).
Coarctation of the aorta
Smooth muscle cells extending to the aortic wall rather than remaining within the wall of the ductus arteriosus may be a cause of aortic narrowing in preductal or postductal positions. A constriction, the aortic isthmus, is sometimes present in the aorta between the site of origin of the left subclavian artery and the orifice of the ductus arteriosus that can persist as a coarctation of the aorta. This can be found as an isolated lesion, with a normally closed ductus arteriosus. When the ductus arteriosus remains open coarctation is more likely to be associated with additional lesions within the heart, often a bicuspid aortic valve (O’Brien and Marshall 2015). Coarctation of the aorta has an estimated incidence of 1 in 2500 live births, affecting males more than females, and is the fifth most common heart defect (Kenny and Hijazi 2011).

Catheterization of neonatal blood vessels

In the neonate, the blood vessels of the trunk and their associated visceral branches are relatively larger than the vessels in the limbs, an arrangement that favours central pooling of blood. Neonatal peripheral vessels are particularly small, with delicate walls that can be easily damaged, and consequently their cannulation poses much more of a challenge than is the case with their adult counterparts. Large vessels are in the same relative positions as in the adult but may correspond to different vertebral levels. Thus, although the bifurcation of the common carotid artery into internal and external carotid arteries typically occurs at the level of the upper border of the thyroid cartilage in both neonate and adult, the thyroid cartilage is relatively higher in the neonatal neck than it is in the adult. The renal arteries also arise at a higher vertebral level in the neonate, often that between the twelfth thoracic and first lumbar vertebrae, whereas they arise at the level of the inferior part of the body of the first lumbar vertebra/upper border of the body of the second lumbar vertebra in the adult. The abdominal aorta bifurcates into common iliac arteries at the upper border of the body of the fourth lumbar vertebra, rather than at the lower border of the body of the fourth lumbar vertebra, as typically occurs in the adult.

Umbilical arterial catheterization

Insertion of an umbilical catheter is undertaken to provide direct access to the arterial circulation. Arterial blood can be withdrawn repeatedly for measurement of oxygen and carbon dioxide partial pressures, pH, base excess and many other parameters of blood biochemistry and haematology. The indwelling catheter also enables the continuous measurement of arterial blood pressure. The catheter is inserted directly into either the cut end or the side of one of the two umbilical arteries in the umbilical cord stump that remains attached to the baby after transection of the umbilical cord at the time of delivery. The catheter tip is then advanced along the length of the umbilical artery, through the internal iliac artery, into the common iliac artery and, from there, into the aorta. In order to keep the catheter patent, a small volume of fluid is continuously infused through it. It is important for the tip of the catheter to be located well away from arteries branching from the aorta, to avoid potentially harmful perfusion of these arteries with the catheter fluid. Thus, umbilical arterial catheter tips are placed in the descending aorta either in a ‘high’ position, above the coeliac trunk but well below the ductus arteriosus, or in a ‘low’ position, below the renal and inferior mesenteric arteries but above the point where the aorta bifurcates into the two common iliac arteries. The length of catheter to be inserted can be estimated from charts relating the required catheter length to external body measurements, or from birth weight. Positioning of the catheter is assessed by means of radiographs of the abdomen or chest: a ‘high’ catheter tip should be located in the descending aorta somewhere between the levels of the sixth and ninth thoracic vertebrae, while a ‘low’ catheter tip should be at a level between the third and fourth lumbar vertebrae. Umbilical vein catheterization

The umbilical vein may be catheterized for initial central venous access in preterm babies and in term neonates to enable exchange and transfusion of blood, for central venous pressure measurement and for emergency vascular access. The catheter is inserted into the cut end of the umbilical vein and is advanced along the length of the vein, through the ductus venosus and into the inferior vena cava; the tip is placed between the ductus venosus and the right atrium. Positioning of the catheter tip is confirmed radiologically: it should be located just above the respiratory diaphragm at a point that is level with the ninth or tenth thoracic vertebrae. Estimation of the required catheter length can be determined from standard charts.

Central venous catheterization

Small-bore catheters can be fed into large central veins, superior or inferior venae cavae, just outside the right atrium, via needles or catheters inserted in the peripheral veins. Typically, the median cubital or basilic veins are used in the upper limb, the long saphenous vein at the medial malleolus in the lower limb, and the superficial temporal vein in the scalp. The tip of the catheter is sited at the entrance to the right atrium. The required catheter length is assessed from direct measurement of the distance between the proximal surface entry point in the limb or scalp to the right atrium, estimated at mid-sternal level.

Peripheral arterial puncture

It is common practice to insert a small-bore cannula into a peripheral artery in neonates receiving intensive care when either the umbilical artery is not accessible or there are clinical reasons to avoid cannulation of the umbilical vessels, e.g. if the umbilical cord has dried or in older neonates. Trans-illumination can be used to provide an outline of the artery to be cannulated. The peripheral arteries that are most commonly used are the radial artery, just proximal to the radiocarpal join on the palmar (volar) surface of the wrist, or the posterior tibial artery, posterior to the medial malleolus. The proximity of the ulnar nerve to the ulnar artery increases the risk of iatrogenic nerve damage associated with cannulation of the ulnar artery, and there is a higher risk of ischaemia with cannulation of this vessel. The relatively poor collateral circulation associated with the dorsalis pedis artery means that this artery is used only as a last resort. The axillary artery is now used in infants with difficult peripheral access; the brachial artery at the antecubital fossa, which was used previously, is rarely used because it has poor collateral circulation and the median nerve is in close proximity.
Confirmation that an adequate collateral circulation is present when cannulating the radial artery can be obtained by performing Allen’s test (Ch. 48). Alternatively, intact arterial flow can usually be confirmed, particularly in the preterm infant, by direct visualization of the arteries using trans-illumination. A cold light source is placed on the posterior aspect of the lower forearm and the shadow of the pulsating arteries can be seen on the anterior surface of the forearm.
