Commentary 1.1: Fluorescence microscopy today

A perspective
Dylan M. Owen, and M. Makarova
Fluorescence microscopes are some of the most widely used tools in current cell biology, either as systems within individual laboratories, or increasingly commonly, within centralized core facilities. The largely non-invasive nature of fluorescence imaging is its major strength, in that it allows the distribution of individual, specific protein species to be mapped at submicron resolution in living cells, tissues and even whole living organisms. Techniques developed in the 1990s and early years of the twenty-first century, including the development of fluorescent fusion protein constructs based on genetically encoded fluorescent proteins, advances in excitation and detector technology allowing new levels of specificity, speed and signal-to-noise ratio, and new image analysis and computing techniques for the manipulation and quantification of the resulting data, the most modern of which now use artificial intelligence and machine learning approaches, have collectively made fluorescence a standard research tool found within most laboratories working in the biological sciences.
A new wave of technological developments that have appeared in the past decade is being adopted by the biological sciences community. New fluorescent probes, optical technology and image processing algorithms have underpinned the development of super-resolution microscopy in which fluorescence images can be obtained with resolution approaching the molecular scale. In effect, this allows the mapping of all fluorescent molecules in a sample with nanometre accuracy, a possibility that is changing the way we think about what microscope images mean. Another major advance has been light-sheet-based imaging (Fig. 1.1.1). Its fundamental origins go back decades, but it is only now that light-sheet technology is becoming a vital tool that is especially useful for imaging large samples such as embryos and whole organisms, and in so doing, is threatening the status of the confocal microscope as the go-to system for most bio-imaging.
Fluorescent fusion constructs allow a protein species of interest to be genetically fused to a fluorescent protein so that it can be visualized in a microscope (Chudakov et al 2010). Originally, this meant fusion to the green fluorescent protein (GFP) from the Pacific jellyfish Aequoria victoria (Tsien 1998). For the development of this technology, the 2008 Nobel Prize for Chemistry was shared between Osamu Shinomura, who identified and purified GFP; Martin Chalfie, who created and imaged the first fluorescent fusion (Chalfie et al 1994); and Roger Tsien, who mutated GFP to create a whole palate of possible colours – a palate that is now vast (Shaner et al 2005, Giepmans et al 2006). Unlike immuno-staining, in which fluorescently tagged antibodies to the protein of interest are introduced into a cell, GFP is genetically encoded and is therefore compatible with live cell imaging, so allowing the investigation of protein dynamics under physiological conditions. The newest fluorescent protein technology includes photoactivatable and colour-switchable proteins, which are useful for tracking intracellular events (Patterson and Lippincott-Schwartz 2002). In parallel with the advances in fluorescent protein technology, work is ongoing on new, small organic dye molecules for use in immunostaining and other applications: the Janelia series of JF dyes are excellent examples of rationally designed fluorophores with superior photophysical properties that have largely rendered traditional dyes, such as FITC, obsolete.
Concomitant with the developments in labelling technology, detectors have become faster and more sensitive. The charge-coupled device (CCD) camera evolved into the electron-multiplying CCD (EM-CCD) camera, sensitive enough to detect individual photons and therefore to image and track single molecules within cells. Currently, compound metal-oxide semiconductor (CMOS) camera technology with very high frame rates and huge fields of view is transforming microscopy. Increases in sensitivity mean that scientific CMOS cameras are now even replacing EM-CCDs for single-molecule imaging applications. For point detectors such as those used on confocal and multiphoton systems, new hybrid detectors are being implemented, offering the Holy Grail of extremely high sensitivity and large dynamic range.
In conventional fluorescence microscopy methods (widefield epi-fluorescence, confocal, spinning disk, total internal reflection, etc.), the resolution of the final fluorescence image is limited to 200–300 nm by the diffraction of light, which means that two fluorescent objects separated by less than this distance would not be distinguishable as two separate entities. In 2014, the Nobel Prize for Chemistry was awarded to W. E. Moerner, Eric Betzig and Stefan Hell for developing super-resolution fluorescence microscopy methods. The diffraction limit was circumvented by three new methodologies that combined new fluorophores, optics and image analysis. Broadly speaking, these techniques are based on structured illumination microscopy (SIM) (Fig. 1.1.2A), in which a grid pattern of excitation light is projected on to the sample (Gustafsson 2000); stimulated emission depletion (STED) microscopy (Fig. 1.1.2B), in which a ‘doughnut’-shaped depletion beam is used to de-excite fluorophores and narrow the excitation spot used in confocal microscopy (Hell and Wichmann 1994, Vicidomini et al 2011); or single-molecule localization microscopy (SMLM) techniques (Fig. 1.1.2C) in which individual molecules are imaged and localized in sequence (Betzig and Chichester 1993). SMLM can be subdivided depending on the types of fluorophore used and how the signal is separated from the ensemble in order to achieve single-molecule imaging.
SIM achieves resolutions of around 100 nm and is compatible with conventional fluorophores and live cell imaging. STED typically achieves 50–100 nm resolution in biological samples, but is more challenging for live cell imaging because of the damaging laser powers used. SMLM delivers the highest resolution of 20–30 nm, but is relatively slow and therefore mainly used on fixed cells where new switchable fluorophores allow fluorescent molecules to be turned on stochastically. Individual molecules are then imaged and their coordinates recorded before the fluorophores are bleached and a new subset of molecules is activated. In this way, all molecules in the sample can be imaged in sequence, circumventing the diffraction limit. Using SMLM, the ability to acquire tables of the x, y and z coordinates of all individual fluorescent molecules, rather than images per se, requires new ways of thinking about the analysis and quantification of data sets, a challenge that is only just beginning. One of the newest implementations of SMLM uses complimentary DNA oligos to label molecules in the sample with fluorophores. Termed DNA points-accumulation for imaging by nanoscale topography (DNA-PAINT), it allows the serial imaging of target species and hence many-colour SMLM (Schnitzbauer et al 2017).
The newest developments in in vivo imaging are based on light-sheets (see Fig. 1.1.2). Originally termed SPIM (selective plane illumination microscopy), a flat sheet of excitation light is projected through the sample and imaged by a perpendicularly arranged lens (Huisken et al 2004), allowing high-resolution three-dimensional imaging at high speed and with minimal photo-damage to the sample. Modern implementations of light-sheet offer the advantages of confocal microscopy (e.g. optical sectioning) but with dramatically increased imaging speeds and low phototoxicity. Light-sheet therefore has numerous applications in whole, live organism imaging such as in fly embryos, nematodes and zebrafish development (Chen et al 2014). Light-sheet-based imaging is now being combined with super-resolution methods such as SIM and SMLM, with improvements in resolution, image quality, live-cell compatibility and imaging speed.
While work on new improved fluorophores, laser technology, optical components and processing algorithms continues, more radical breakthroughs in microscopic techniques and data analysis are likely. They will focus on further enhancements to resolution, e.g. the recently developed MINimal emission FLUXes (MINFLUX) method (Balzarotti et al 2017), imaging speeds and applicability to whole-organism imaging, e.g. Photoactivated Localization Microscopy (PALM), which is beginning to be applied to live cells when the biological structure is relatively stable (Shroff et al 2008) and the speed of STED has been greatly improved (Chmyrov et al 2013). There is no doubt that these advances will make fluorescence microscopy an even more valuable tool within the biological sciences.
