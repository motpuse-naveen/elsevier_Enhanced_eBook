Commentary 6.1: Nerve biomechanics

Kimberly S. Topp
Peripheral nerves are subject to physical forces as a result of body position and movement. Nonetheless, the biomechanical properties of these nerves permit continued electrical signalling when faced with reasonable physical demands. For example, when an individual moves from a standing to a sitting position, the sciatic nerve lengthens as it crosses the posterior aspect of the hip joint, its cross-sectional shape becomes elliptical and narrowed, and endoneurial fluid is forced proximally and distally away from the site of compression. The sciatic nerve glides proximally in the thigh towards the flexing hip joint and away from the knee joint (Topp and Boyd 2006).
When a nerve bed is elongated across a moving joint, the nerve is placed under longitudinal tension. The biomechanical response of that nerve may be documented in a load–elongation curve, measured in Newtons per millimetre (Haftek 1970). To enable comparisons to be made between nerves, the load–elongation curve may be translated into a stress–strain curve. Stress is defined as the intensity of force per unit cross-sectional area, and is reported in N/m2 or MPa (Pascal). Strain is defined as the ratio of change in length to the original length, and is often reported as a percentage.
Three distinct regions are seen in nerve load–elongation or stress–strain curves (Fig. 6.1.1). In the initial ‘toe’ region of the curve, application of very little tensile load or stress results in significant elongation or strain. In the ‘linear behaviour’ region, there is a correlation between the applied load or stress and the elongation or strain. In the ‘plastic’ region of the curve, minimal load or stress results in mechanical failure and discontinuity of nerve structures.
Several structural features of peripheral nerves may be related to segments of the stress–strain curve, and these features may vary between nerves requiring functional adaptation. Nerve in situ is under tension: the transition between the ‘toe’ region and the ‘linear behaviour’ region of the curve corresponds roughly with the in situ strain (Kwan et al 1992).
The elasticity seen in the initial part of the stress–strain curve is due to structures that make up visible, periodic light–dark bands that were initially described by, and named after, Felix Fontana (Fontana 1781). These bands vary in their angulation, width, spacing and periodicity. Studies of rat sciatic nerve using Fourier analysis have shown a linear relationship between increasing nerve strain and decreasing band frequency and axonal undulations (Love et al 2013). In vivo microdissection studies coupled with computer modelling suggest that layers of perineurial cells are possibly responsible for the short-wavelength, large-amplitude bands and that wavy spiralling nerve fibres in the endoneurium are responsible for the long-wavelength, small-amplitude bands (Merolli et al 2012), the two patterns merging to form the visible bands of Fontana. In a recent study, however, the nerves of Trembler-J mice, a model of Charcot–Marie–Tooth disease, were found to have altered bands of Fontana, resulting solely from unusually long, sinuous axons moving out of phase (Power et al 2015). Increasing strain in the ‘linear behaviour’ region of the stress–strain curve causes the bands of Fontana to disappear (Pourmand et al 1994). An illustration of functional adaptation is seen in a study of nerves in the feeding pouch of the rorqual whale (Lillie et al 2017). These highly extensible nerves demonstrate straightening of nerve fibres and connective tissues internal to the thick epineurium at the transition from ‘toe’ region to ‘linear behaviour’ region of the stress–strain curve. Further lengthening is achieved through unfolding of the thick, elastin-laden epineurium, a feature that differs from other nerves in less mobile regions of this animal (Vogl et al 2017).
Anatomical features are also known to contribute to the ‘linear behaviour’ region of the stress–strain curve. The slope of this region is termed Young’s modulus of elasticity, or stiffness in the case of the load–elongation curve. A steep slope indicates that the nerve is stiff: significant tensile force results in only a modest change in length. The modulus of elasticity or stiffness of an acellular nerve is comparable to that of fresh nerve, indicating that connective tissue fibres contribute to stiffness (Borschel et al 2003, Zilic et al 2016). Endoneurial fluid and the perineurial sheath that encloses the fluid compartment also contribute to stiffness. When a nerve is elongated, it undergoes transverse contraction (Walbeehm et al 2004): the reduction in cross-sectional area increases pressure in the endoneurial compartment and, consequently, stiffness of the nerve (Millesi et al 1995). As one might predict, nerve roots, which lack a perineurial sheath, demonstrate less stiffness than a peripheral nerve (Beel et al 1986). The differing biomechanical properties of nerve roots and nerves were investigated in a study of the neonatal brachial plexus (Singh et al 2018). There was less stiffness in the plexus roots, trunks and cords, compared to nerve branches or to the more frequently studied tibial nerve. External features, such as nerve branching, entering/exiting blood vessels, and paraneural tissue connections, resist elongation of the nerve in its nerve bed, and increase in situ nerve stiffness (Millesi et al 1995, Foran et al 2018).
In the ‘plastic’ region of the stress–strain curve, increasing longitudinal stress causes non-recoverable elongation and structural integrity is lost. The force per unit cross-sectional area at the point of mechanical failure is termed ultimate stress and occurs at ultimate strain. At this point, a nerve behaves like a viscous material (Haftek 1970). The velocity and direction of the applied load influence the result: dynamic loads are more likely than quasistatic loads to result in nerve rupture (Mahan et al 2018). Additionally, nerve damage is more likely to occur at lower strains with forces applied along the long axis of the nerve when compared to forces applied orthogonally, and forces applied orthogonally result in rupture at nerve branch points (Mahan et al 2018). Along the stress–strain curve, structures within the nerve become impaired in a hierarchical fashion. With increasing tensile load, there is first sliding and loss of tenuous connections in the interface between the layer of inner perineurial cells and the sheath of outer perineurial cells and associated epineurial tissues (Tillett et al 2004, Georgeu et al 2005). With continued load, the core of nerve fibres and inner perineurial cell layers is disrupted and function is impaired or lost: ultimately, the sheath of outer perineurial and epineurial tissues ruptures. Given that acellular nerve is of significantly lower tensile strength and ultimate strain than fresh nerve (Borschel et al 2003), the perineurial cell layer should be appreciated for its ability to withstand stress. Although definitive anatomical studies are lacking, it is likely that microscopic disruptions of perineurial cell–cell connections occur with increasing stress along the ‘linear behaviour’ region of the stress–strain curve, long before ultimate strain is reached.
Nerve is best described as a viscoelastic tissue because its bio-mechanical properties are time-dependent. In response to fixed tension over time, nerve demonstrates creep or elongation (Grewal et al 1996). Slow elongation allows for higher ultimate strain (Haftek 1970, Rydevik et al 1990). When stretched to a fixed length over time, nerve exhibits stress relaxation or a reduction in tension (Wall et al 1991, Driscoll et al 2002).
Returning to the example of the sciatic nerve, during movement from standing to sitting, the nerve bed is elongated posterior to the hip as forward movement of the trunk causes flexion at the hip joint. The sciatic nerve is exposed to tensile load or stress with resultant elongation or strain, the magnitude of which is greatest near the moving joint. Adjusting to these forces, segments of the nerve converge towards the flexing hip joint, with minimal motion in distant segments (Topp and Boyd 2006). Endoneurial pressure resists transverse contraction. When the seated posture is assumed and maintained, the nerve undergoes stress-relaxation – until the next change in position.
