Chapter 11: Overarching concepts in development

Genes in Development

Study of secondary oocytes before and after fertilization, and of the selection of embryos for implantation after in vitro fertilization, has identified the range of maternal genes ready for expression to ensure cleavage, morula and blastocyst formation. Embryonic genome activation occurs in the 4–8 cells stage (Niakan et al 2012) when embryonic cells express polarity genes, initiating cell–cell interaction (Mihajlović and Bruce 2017); by 6 days post fertilization the hatched blastocyst is able to interact with maternal tissue. A simplistic but useful concept likens these early developmental processes to a series of binary choices (see Fig. 8.1). Two cell phenotypes, epithelial and non-epithelial cells, are specified during cleavage and early compaction within the zona pellucida. All the relevant genes required to maintain these cell shapes and behaviours are upregulated at these very early stages.
Three lineages are set up at this time with the specification of trophoblast, hypoblast (primitive endoderm) and epiblast. Cells subsequently respond locally to their environment, specifically to other cells that are positioned lateral and basal to them. In the mouse embryo, the genes operating at these early times are given in Takaoka and Hamada (2012). The implanting blastocyst also expresses and secretes miRNAs (involved in post-translational gene expression) involved in interactions with the maternal endometrium which secretes similar molecules (Lian et al 2017): successful embryo–maternal cross-talk is vital for implantation.
Two related themes have emerged from experimental studies of development: the control of embryonic morphology has been highly conserved in evolution between vertebrates and invertebrates; this control involves families of genes coding for proteins that act as transcriptional regulators. Homeobox genes are believed to be responsible, at least in part, for the evolutionary origin of the embryonic body plan (Irie and Kuratani 2014) and for appropriate axial elongation of the embryo and limb specification (Deschamps and Duboule 2017). Experimental studies of transgenic animals in which the homeobox genes have been knocked out provide some evidence of their function. However, because developmental processes permit significant recovery from insult, some of the outcomes cannot be directly interpreted as demonstrating the effect of such gene loss. Other gene families required for normal development include the T-box family (helix–loop–helix transcription factors and Sox genes) and the signalling factors transforming growth factor beta (TGF-β), bone morphogenetic proteins (BMPs), fibroblast growth factor family (FGF), the Wnt family and Hedgehog signalling molecules. A range of cell receptor molecules are also required. For details on individual members within this range of factors, see Carlson (2014).
The successful culture of embryonic stem cells, the techniques of single-cell RNA-sequencing, analysis of all proteins expressed by early embryonic cells, the transcription factors they express, the molecules they secrete and those produced as metabolites, have generated extensive data on early genes and the control of sequential expression of genes specific to diverging cell phenotypes (Giacomini et al 2017).

Signalling between embryonic cells and tissues

Within the developing blastocyst, the position of cells relative to each other leads to epigenetic change. The acquisition of cell polarity is fundamental, because this specifies apical and basolateral domains, cell surface specializations, the positioning of intracellular organelles and cytoskeletal elements, and the position and nature of cell–cell junctions. The establishment cell junctional complexes between the outer, polarized, cells of the morula within the zona pellucida are the earliest embryonic cell interactions. The signals received by adjacent cells induces a change in their subsequent behaviour. Cellular signalling between cells and tissues occur via: direct cell–cell contact (gap junctions); cell adhesion molecules and their receptors; extracellular matrix molecules and their receptors; and growth factors and their receptors. The production of a basal lamina by epithelial cells (containing, e.g., laminin and fibronectin) and its attachment to underlying extracellular matrix molecules (collagen, proteoglycans and glycosaminoglycans) synthesized by mesenchyme cells constructs the tissues in which reciprocal signalling occurs (Fig. 11.1). In most developing tissues an epithelium and its basal lamina are supported by underlying mesenchymal cells, each contributing to developmental interactions. However, there are a few sites where two epithelial layers, a special epithelium and an endothelium, generate a shared basement membrane to which both epithelial phenotypes contribute. This arrangement is seen between type-1 pneumocytes and endothelium in the developing lung alveoli; between the podocytes and endothelium in the glomerulus of the developing kidney; and between astrocyte end feet and pial-derived endothelium in the developing brain (Fig. 11.2).
Combinations of all of these signalling routes are involved in development and their perturbation may result in developmental aberration. The transient production of, e.g., gap junctions is seen as epithelial somites are formed; between neuroepithelial cells within rhombomeres; and in the tunica media of the outflow tract of the heart. Mutations of the genes that code for extracellular matrix molecules give rise to a number of congenital disorders, e.g. mutations in type I collagen produce osteogenesis imperfecta; mutations in type II collagen produce disorders of cartilage; and mutations in fibrillin are associated with Marfan syndrome
Growth factors are distinguished from extracellular matrix molecules. They can be delivered to, and act on, cells in a variety of ways, namely: endocrine, autocrine, paracrine, intracrine, juxtacrine or matricrine (Fig. 11.3). Many growth factors are secreted in a latent form, e.g. associated with a propeptide (latency-associated peptide) in the case of transforming growth factor beta, or attached to a binding protein, in the case of insulin-like growth factors.
Other signalling mechanisms occurring between these cell arrangements include the transduction of the biomechanics of mesenchyme cell synthesized matrix. Stiffness of the matrix leads cells towards an osteoblastic developmental phenotype, whereas soft matrix leads to an adipocyte lineage (Heisenberg and Bellaïche 2013). Endogenous electrical fields are also believed to have a role in cell–cell communication and have been demonstrated in a range of amphibian embryos, and in vertebrate embryos during primitive streak ingression. Neuroepithelial cells are electrically coupled, regardless of their position relative to inter-rhombomeric boundaries.
The spatial and temporal distribution of a variety of cell adhesion molecules has been localized in the early embryo and displayed within the 3D tissue arrangement of embryos at a variety of stages (MGI mouse gene expression data search: eMAGE). The temporal appearance of these molecules correlates with a variety of morphogenetic events that involve cell aggregation or disaggregation.
It is important to realize that while developmental studies on laboratory species and in vitro studies of animal tissues indicate the range of factors operating in embryos, the spatial and temporal expression of these factors in human embryos will be different. Figure 8.2 illustrates the temporal differences in external features between human and some animal species. Differences in heterochrony (the time at which genes are expressed and specific structures develop), are easily appreciated between mammals and marsupials, where marsupial embryos are born with facial and upper limb development well in advance of mammals of a similar developmental age (Chew et al 2014). Although not so extreme, differences in heterochrony are significant between human and mouse embryos and any extrapolation of animal developmental sequences to human development should be avoided. Somite generation frequency is 5 hours in humans and 2 hours in mice, and other processes, such as progenitor cell population expansion, have different time lines, e.g. generation of motor neurones takes 2–3 weeks in humans but a few days in mice (Ebisuya and Briscoe 2018). The way in which embryonic cells and tissues count time is not yet clear, but analyses and correlation of specific heterochronic interspecies differences are now being investigated at the molecular level.

Induction and interactions

All cell signalling events form the basis of induction and are involved in cell–cell and tissue interactions. The sequential consequents of induction are described using specific terminology. The ability of a cell population (or tissue) to respond to an inductive signal is called competence and denotes the ability of the population to alter its initial behaviour as a result of the induction. After a cell population has been induced to develop along a certain pathway, it loses competence and becomes restricted. Once restricted, cells are set on a particular pathway of development and after a number of binary choices (further restrictions), they are said to be determined. Determined cells are programmed to follow a process of development that will lead to differentiation. The determined state is a heritable characteristic of cells and is the final step in restriction. Once a cell has become determined, it will progress to a differentiated phenotype if the environmental factors are suitable.
The process of determination and differentiation within embryonic cell populations is reflected by the ability of these populations to produce specific proteins. Primary proteins (colloquially termed housekeeping proteins) are considered essential for cellular metabolism, whereas proteins synthesized as cells become determined, and are therefore specific to the state of determination, are termed secondary proteins; e.g. liver and kidney cells produce arginase, but muscle cells do not. Fully differentiated cells produce tertiary proteins, that no other cell line can synthesize, e.g. haemoglobin in erythrocytes. The range of housekeeping, regulatory, and tissue-specific proteomes in adult cells is presented in the Human Protein Atlas (www.proteinatlas.org).

Permissive and instructive tissue interactions

Classically two types of cell and tissue interaction have been described, namely, permissive and instructive (Wessells 1977).
In a permissive interaction, a signal from an adjacent tissue is necessary for the successful self-differentiation of the responding tissue. This means that a particular cell population (or the matrix molecules secreted by the cells that it contains) will maintain mitotic activity in an adjacent cell population. Since a variety of different cell populations may permit a specific cell population to undergo mitosis and cell differentiation, no specific instruction or signal that might limit the developmental options of the responding tissue is involved; this signal therefore does not influence the developmental pathway selected and there is no restriction. The responding tissue has the intrinsic capacity to develop, and only needs appropriate environmental conditions in order to express this capacity. Permissive interactions often occur later in development, when a tissue whose fate has already been determined is maintained and stabilized by another.
An instructive (directive) interaction (induction) changes the cell type of the responding tissue, so that the cell population becomes restricted. Wessells (1977) proposed four general principles in most instructive interactions: in the presence of tissue A, responding tissue B develops in a certain way; in the absence of tissue A, responding tissue B does not develop in that way; in the absence of tissue A, but in the presence of tissue C, tissue B does not develop in that way; and in the presence of tissue A, a tissue D, which would normally develop differently, is changed to develop like tissue B.
These principles are exemplified during induction of the lens vesicle by the optic cup (see Fig. 15.1). An example of D is the experimental association of chicken flank ectoderm with mouse mammary mesenchyme, which results in the morphogenesis of mammary gland-like structures even though chickens do not normally develop mammary glands Wessells (1977).
Tissue interactions continue into adult life and are probably responsible for maintaining the functional heterogeneity of adult tissues and organs, as exemplified by the complex tissue heterogeneity, with sharply compartmentalized boundaries, that occurs in the oral cavity. The junctions between the mucosa of the vestibule and the lip, and between the vermillion border and the facial skin, are distinct boundaries of specific epithelial and mesenchymal differentiation, and are almost certainly maintained by continuing epithelial–mesenchymal interactions in adult life. Perturbation of these interactions throughout the body may underlie a wide variety of adult diseases, including susceptibility to cancer and proliferative disorders.
The interactions between adjacent epithelia and underlying connective tissue continue throughout embryonic and fetal life and extend into postnatal life. In the adult, these interactions also permit the metaplastic changes that tissues can undergo in response to local environmental conditions

Cell fates and stem cells

As populations of cells become progressively determined, they can be described within a hierarchy of cellular development as stem cells, progenitor cells, and terminally differentiated cells.

Stem cells

The definition of a stem cell has changed as newer methods for producing such cells in vitro have expanded. Within an embryo the earliest cells are totipotent and become restricted as development proceeds. In the adult, populations of stem cells remain within most tissues able to replenish the temporal loss of senescent cells and to respond to changes in environmental conditions by asymmetric division, where one daughter cell remains as a stem cell, while the other proceeds along a differentiation pathway. In proliferative cell division, which may be symmetric, the derived cells have identical determination.
Stem cell populations have been developed from a range of embryonic, fetal and adult cells (Fig. 11.4) and are now routinely used in in vitro culture for exploration of their properties and their reactions to possible therapeutic agents. Human embryonic stem cells (hESC) were first established in culture by Thomson et al (1998); embryonic and adult induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs) were established by Takahashi and Yamanaka (2006). All stem cells undergo passaging in culture (cells are grown to confluence then split into subcultures and regrown many times); specific growth factors are added to the culture media to promote cell restriction and determination. Precise control of levels of oxygen tension and growth factors in vitro (retroviral transduction of OCT4, KLF4m, c-MYC and SOX2), and forced changing of cell phenotype (mesenchyme to epithelial) have been used to produce a range of human induced pluripotential stem cell (hiPSC) types, e.g. cardiac myocytes, hepatocytes, neurones etc. (Takahashi and Yamanaka 2016, Chhabra 2017).
Initial cell culture studies were usually in 2D systems. Cultured cells are now grown in 3D in specific gels, on microfluidic chips, or in bioprinted shapes, where the culture medium and oxygen tension can be controlled. The metabolic products of such cultures have been analysed through proteomic analysis (metabolome or secretome). Cells grown in 3D culture will form organoids, where epithelial cells, supported by outer mesenchymal cells form hollow spheres. If these self-organizing cell lines are implanted into animals they continue to grow; if maintained in 3D culture they can be used for drug testing or examination of transcription factors they release. Exploration of the cellular interactions in such systems gives insights into how cells self-organize temporally and spatially (Simunovic and Brivanlou 2017). 3D culture of mouse embryonic stem cells with trophoblastic and hypoblastic (extraembryonic endoderm) cell lines has produced gastrulating embryo-like structures in vitro (Sozen et al 2018). These structures allow the exploration of gene expression, spatio-temporal modifications, transcriptional profiling, and comparison with in vitro embryonic development.
It is worth reflecting that changes from a perceived ‘normal’ behavioural repertoire seen in stem cell culture, as a result of changes to the culture conditions may lead to the misinterpretation of experimental results. Undifferentiated embryonic cells are seen only transiently in developing embryos (De Paepe et al 2014), yet hiPSCs, generated by resetting their epigenetic landscapes are used as ‘embryonic’ stem cells that can be pushed into specific developmental pathways by factors in the culture media. It has been noted that some of the epigenetic memories of such hiPSCs are not eradicated by their preparation and this may obfuscate hypotheses to explain their behaviours (Noguchi et al 2018). Such memory may also include a developmental clock. Heterochronic species-specific differences were conserved when hiPSC-derived neural progenitors were implanted into neonatal mouse brain: the hiPSCs divided for longer and took much longer to differentiate, reflecting their epigentic timeline for in vivo human corticogenesis (Espuny-Canacho et al 2013). The results from studies using implanted stem cell-derived tissues or from in vitro organoids, or the outcomes of metabolome and secretome analyses of stem cell-derived ‘gastrulating embryo-like structures’ (Sozen et al 2018), may provide answers to specific questions but these should not be extrapolated to represent what happens in whole embryo development.

Progenitor cells

Progenitor cells are already determined along a particular pathway. They may individually follow that differentiation pathway or may proliferate and produce large numbers of similarly determined progenitor cells that subsequently differentiate; neuroblasts or myoblasts are examples of progenitor cells.

Terminally differentiated cells

By virtue of their extreme specialization, terminally differentiated cells can no longer divide, e.g. erythrocytes and neurones. Apoptosis is a particular variety of terminal differentiation in which the final outcome is the death of the individual cells or cell populations. It occurs in the developing limb, where cells die along the pre- and postaxial limits of the apical ectodermal ridge, limiting its extent, and also between the digits, permitting their separation (see Ch. 19).

Morphogenesis and Pattern Formation

The outcomes of embryonic studies show that the process of expression of specific genes and transcription factors, the consequent synthesis of proteins, and movements of local cells or cell populations, is repeated in many different locations and at different times within an embryo. Correlation of these different but related processes informs overarching concepts of development that are applicable to many different cell populations and regions of an embryo. Over time, the meaning and use of some of the terms that describe such concepts have changed: the most recent ways of defining these terms are presented and attributed in what follows.
Interactions between cells are inferred when a change in the initial status of the cell (cells) is noted, e.g. the cell may undergo mitosis or apoptosis, change its glycocalyx protein receptors, or show signs of differentiation. The outcomes of any interaction can be specified by considering three subsequent characteristics of the cells involved: their spatial arrangement, i.e. where cells are in relation to other cells within their 3D cell population; their temporal state, i.e. the time since they were produced or expressed particular factors; their state of restriction or determination, i.e. their individual state at any one time, as a response to their neighbouring cells
Over time, the consequences of interactions are movements of cell populations that change the shape of an embryo. This overarching process is termed morphogenesis. It denotes the assumption of, and change in, form by the whole or part of a developing embryo: it is apparent in the whole embryo during gastrulation and in local cell population movements in branching morphogenesis, which occurs in developing lungs, kidneys and most glandular organs.
The development of branches from a tubular duct occurs over a period of time. Reciprocal interaction between the proliferating epithelium of the duct and its surrounding mesenchyme and extracellular matrix results in a series of clefts that produce a characteristic branching pattern (Fig. 11.5). During tubular and acinar development, hyaluronidase secreted by the underlying mesenchymal cells breaks down the basal laminae produced by the epithelial cells, which increases epithelial mitoses locally and results in an expanding acinus. Cleft formation is initiated by the mesenchyme, which produces collagen III fibrils within putative clefts. (If the collagen is removed experimentally, no clefts develop, whereas if excess collagen is not removed, supernumerary clefts appear.) The collagen acts to protect the basal lamina from the effects of the hyaluronidase, which means that the overlying epithelia have a locally reduced rate of mitosis. The region of rapid mitoses at the tip of the acinus is split into two, and two branches develop from this point.
Branching morphogenesis is an example of pattern formation, the process whereby the individual members of a mass of cells follow a number of different responses that are precisely related to each other, in an orderly manner in space and time. Conceptually, two separate components of pattern formation within branching morphogenesis have been noted (Salazar-Ciudad et al 2003): inductive mechanisms, which pattern via cell signalling, and mophogenetic mechanisms, which act on cell populations to change the spatial distribution of cells (a change of form) without changing the cell states (e.g. degree of restriction).
Inductive mechanisms seem to have a hierarchy, based on sequential gene expression; patterns generated in this way are similar to one another and limited in complexity. In contrast, morphogenetic mechanisms, which include the epigenetic context of cell populations, mechanical interactions between cells and also the ECM, and also the local properties of the ECM and its response to the local stresses, appear to generate more complex patterns using fewer genes.
A cell population with equivalent states of gene expression, noted as a genetic territory (Salazar-Ciudad et al 2003) can undergo an overall change in form. This has been termed a morphostatic mechanism: the population follows an autonomous and specific temporal sequence of behavioural changes specified by the expression of a new range of transcription factors, e.g. the meristematic growth of the developing limb bud (Ch. 19).
Morphodynamic mechanisms denote cell populations where genetic territories are changing at the same time as the cells are sending and receiving inductive molecular signals. Inductive and morphogenetic mechanisms are working synchronously and interdependently. Cells are acquiring positional information dynamically and, because of the different relative positions of each cell to its neighbour, the local interpretations of any inductive signals to a cell population is complex, as cells interact with changing molecular and biomechanical microenvironments over time. Such mechanisms can be seen in cerebral development (Ch. 14). For details of patterning in vertebrate development, see Tickle (2003).
The concept of pattern formation is used throughout all branches of embryology and development. It can be identified across species and used to explore the evolution of conserved cell interactions (Schlosser et al 2014). The prediction of patterning described mathematically by Turing (1952) where a cell producing two morphogens with different diffusion rates, one an activator and the other an inhibitor, has been applied to many developmental scenarios such as hair follicle patterning in developing skin, villous patterning in the gut and the development of stripes (Maini et al 2006, Heller and Fuchs 2015). Pattern formation can also be seen within in vitro culture of organoids (Deglincerti et al 2016, Ishihara et al 2017), demonstrating that the sequences of interactions, gene expression and signalling of cells in culture continue to follow the epigenetic programming of stem cells within their specified culture microenvironment.
Buckling morphogenesis is a process integrating further complexity, where two epithelia contribute to inductive interactions in three and four dimensions, with the transductive effects of the biomechanical properties of the combined tissues effecting subsequent developmental outcomes. This type of interaction is seen particularly in gut development, where joined linear sequences of epithelial: mesenchyme interactions are covered by a second epithelium. The elongation of the first tube is informed by its own genetic programme and also confined by the dynamic relationship with the outer epithelium. The biomechanical tension produced in the system informs regional lengthening, rate of growth of the original epithelial–mesenchyme interaction, and the local formation of complex looping. Differential growth between the epithelial layers in this system can give rise to complex coiling of the gut which can then slide into and pack closely within a restricted space. Although aspects of buckling morphogenesis can be predicted mathematically, the parameters for inclusion are extremely complex and not all of the mechanical properties of the tissues involved can yet be specified (Nerurkar et al 2017, Nerurkar et al 2019).

Experimental approaches to embryology

Study of the development of chimeric embryos, where small portions of an embryo are excised and replaced with similar portions of an embryo from a different species at the same stage, has provided significant advances in the understanding of conserved genes and interactive pathways. The technique was particularly effective in early chick/quail chimeric embryonic experiments because the nucleolus is especially prominent in quail but not in chick cells, allowing easy identification of quail cells within a chick embryo (Le Douarin 1969). Chimeric embryo experimentation confirmed the reciprocity of tissue interaction between the embryonic species, a phenomenon that had previously been illustrated, for a limited period, in co-cultures of embryonic avian and mammalian tissues. Somite development and vertebral formation have been studied in mouse–chick chimeras (Fontaine-Pérus 2000). The production, in vitro, of human–animal chimeric cell lines has also provided new ways of studying cellular pathways, as has the introduction of human artificial chromosome vectors into animal cells to study their interaction.
Techniques in which specific genes were targeted, removed or replaced (knock-out and knock-in studies) provided significant information on the actions of genes and the result of their perturbation in in vivo and in vitro experimentation. Culture of embryos, embryonic tissues and embryonic cells permitted investigation of the products of cell metabolism via perturbation of the culture media and analysis of culture metabolites, seen in proteomic, metabolome and secretome studies. Such techniques have also been used to study extraembryonic membranes, e.g. amnion and yolk sac.
Co-culture of different embryonic cell phenotypes has provided further information on the factors involved in instructive interactions between epithelial and mesenchymal cells, and has also allowed experimental examination of the extracellular matrix and its biomechanical properties (Hadden et al 2017). Three-dimensional cell culture has highlighted the self-organizing properties of mixed phenotypic embryonic cell populations and given rise to a plethora of studies where portions of embryos or embryonic stem cells are grown in mini-culture conditions with full control of the liquid and gaseous environment. Such ‘organ on chips’ culture systems are producing high volumes of data on the conditions under which such cell systems will flourish and the metabolites that are produced. However, these experimental results cannot be extrapolated to any in vivo embryonic development and care must be taken in generalizing the knowledge gained from them.
The current production of human induced pluripotent stem cells which can be encouraged along any line of development by the provision of specific growth factors in the culture media has expanded the possibilities of embryonic research. Specified cell phenotypes can be produced in culture and encouraged, within 3D systems, to form organoids with the view to further study of interactive developmental sequences, to implant such organoids back into animals to study their ability to grow in vivo, or to promote continued growth in vitro to produce portions of organs which could be transplanted into humans as future treatments.
Answers from these studies should not be assumed to add to our knowledge of normal development. Human embryos must not be grown in vitro for longer than 14 days after the point of fertilization; the present 14-day rule advocated in the UK by the Warnock Committee (Department of Health and Social Security 1984) was established at a time when human embryos could only be grown in vitro for a few days. Today, the purposeful culture of ‘synthetic human entities with embryo-like features’ (SHEEFs) (Aach et al 2017), multi-phenotype self-organizing gastruloids/organoids of gut, kidney or cerebral cortex (which can model diverse brain regions) created from hiPSCs, with the very real possibility of maintaining growth beyond the 14-day limit, poses significant ethical problems (Chan 2018). The International Society for Stem Cell Research (ISSCR) produced guidelines for the review, by a proposed human Embryo Research Oversight (EMRO) process, of ‘embryo-like structures that might manifest human organismal potential’ (ISSCR 2016). The production of these organoids enables researchers to study portions of embryos, in effect, at developmental stages much later than those at present permitted (Aach et al 2017). How the transnational dimension of any regulatory change ought to be managed and how this would affect the global dynamic of science needs careful thought and discussion (Chan 2018).

Development of Hierarchical Ontologies and Computer Modelling of Development

Bioinformatic databases use embryological ontologies to trace the lineage of organs and tissues to the earliest time and provide a temporal and spatial framework against which the sequential activation of genes preceding changes in cell and tissue morphology can be matched. They also provide a means of viewing all of the complex partonomy, lineage data and temporal information occurring within embryos within a specific timeframe and of linking these to adult anatomical tissue types. As understanding of the complexity of early developmental events expands, the use of ontological databases provide methods to correlate embryological information (limitations are discussed in Bard 2012).
Computer models have so far focused on single attributes of cells, such as their position, movement or forces transmitted through layers, e.g. cell position relative to a lattice, or the contact surfaces of a cell with other cells in 3D. Such models are more suited to densely packed epithelial cell populations than to mesenchymal populations where the viscoelastic properties of the glycosaminoglycans can be changed in three dimensions by mesenchymal cell–cell interaction or epithelial–mesenchymal interaction. Quantifying the development of complexity time in specific embryonic regions offers the opportunity to compare features such as gene expression, cell lineage and territories (Salvador-Martinez and Salazar-Ciudad 2017).
The exploration of data derived from analysis of the genomes of a range of animals including humans has benefited from ontologies and powerful computing algorithms. Internet websites contain the databases of genomes, spatial and temporal gene expressions, metabolomes and secretomes throughout the development of a range of invertebrate and vertebrate species (including Wormbase, Flybase, Mouse eMAP, KOMP (Knockout Mouse Project), and Human Developmental Studies Network – HUDSEN). Such ontologies assist embryological study, reveal explicit similarities in interspecies developmental processes and enhance the production of taxonomies of vertebrate evolution.
Until fairly recently the study of embryology has been based on detailed examination of histological sections of embryos at different developmental stages: an understanding of cells, tissues and extracellular spaces in 3D; and the use of specific staining techniques to compare sections of normal, perturbed or chimeric embryos. Today many computer programs based on cell or gene ontologies, devised and specified some years ago, are making predictions about developmental mechanisms. New research outcomes are curated and updated in the main by other computer algorithms, with human curation of only a small proportion of the uploaded data. The concepts used to explain developmental processes have evolved in step with an increased understanding of gene expression, common embryonic mechanisms, cell–cell and cell–matrix interactions, and newly discovered transitory embryonic cell populations. However, it is perhaps now no longer possible to modify how embryonic processes are (re)conceptualized in the light of new knowledge, because the language used when computer ontologies were first devised cannot be revised. The earlier freedom for exploration of concepts and theories, their revision in the light of new discoveries and the development of new thoughts and language, may no longer be possible. As computers are now able to devise their own algorithms, learning from their past searches, the original concepts and thoughts concerning embryological mechanisms and interaction may become changed in ways not yet apparent, and certainly not transparent.
Outcomes of studies based on analyses of Big Data in embryological studies and how they fit into the sum of knowledge may need caution in their interpretation (Hutter and Moerman 2015). If the input data (and underlying concepts) used to solve a problem are based on obfuscated and confused understanding, then the output may not answer the question investigated.
