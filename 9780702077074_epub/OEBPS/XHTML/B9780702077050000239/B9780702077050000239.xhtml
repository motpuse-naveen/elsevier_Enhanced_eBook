Chapter 23: Pre- and postnatal growth and the neonate

Prenatal Stages

Development of a human from fertilization to delivery at ‘full term’ averages 266 postfertilization days, or 9.5 lunar months (28 day units). It has long been customary to compute the length of a pregnancy, whether in a normal birth or an abortion, from the first day of the last menstrual period of the mother but, as ovulation usually occurs near the fourteenth day of a menstrual cycle, this ‘menstrual age’ is an overestimate of developmental age of about 2 weeks. Estimated in this manner, pregnancy averages 280 days from the last menstrual period or 10 lunar months (40 weeks).
The development of a human from fertilization to birth is divided into two periods: embryonic and fetal. The absolute size of an embryo or fetus does not afford a reliable indication of either its chronological age or the stage of its structural organization, even though graphs based on large numbers of observations have been constructed to provide averages. All such data suffer from the difficulty of timing the moment of conception in humans; in vitro fertilization techniques now accurately time the start of embryonic life (Ch. 8).
The embryonic period was graded or classified into developmental stages or ‘horizons’, on the basis of the internal and external features of approximately 600 sectioned embryos, by Streeter (1942, 1945, 1948). These sections formed the basis of the Carnegie system of staging. Streeter’s work and its continuation by O’Rahilly and Müller (1987), provided, and continues to provide, a sound foundation for embryonic study and a means of comparing stages of human development with those of the animals routinely used for experimental study, namely the chick, mouse and rat.
The postfertilization developmental events in embryonic stages 1–10 are shown in detail in Fig. 8.1. The stages up to stage 5 are concerned with implantation of the blastocyst. The external appearance of representative stages from stage 6 to stage 23, correlated with the greatest length in millimetres and approximate age in days is shown in Fig. 23.1. Estimations of embryonic length from fixed histological specimens may be 1–5 mm less than their equivalent in vivo estimates, reflecting the shrinkage caused by the preparative procedures used in embryological studies. O’Rahilly and Müller (2000, 2010) revised some of the ages that had previously been assigned to early Carnegie embryonic stages, pointing out that interembryonic variation might be greater than had been thought and that, consequently, some ages might have been underestimated. They noted that, as a guide, the age of an embryo could reasonably be estimated from the embryonic length within the range 3–30 mm, by adding 29 to the length. Recent use of ultrasound for the examination of human embryos and fetuses in utero has confirmed much of the staging data. However, assigning embryos to a particular stage and the estimated length of days that that stage encompasses remains subject to error. The revisions of age to particular stages given by O’Rahilly and Müller (2010) are followed as closely as possible in what follows.
Obvious external features provide some guidance to the changes occurring within embryos during successive stages. Somite number is related to early embryonic stages; once the number of somites is too great to count with accuracy, the degree of development of the pharyngeal arches is often used. External staging becomes more obvious when the limb buds appear. The upper limb bud is clearly visible at stage 13 (approximately 31–33 days post fertilization) and the acquisition of a distal paddle on the upper limb bud is characteristic by stage 16 (approximately 37–38 days post fertilization). At stage 18 (approximately 42–44 days post fertilization) the lower limb bud has a distal paddle, whereas the upper limb bud has digit rays that are beginning to separate. By stage 23 (approximately 53–58 days post fertilization) the embryo has a head that is almost erect and rounded, and eyelids are beginning to form. The limbs look far more in proportion and fingers and toes are separate. At this stage the external genitalia are well developed, although they may not be sufficiently developed for the accurate determination of the sex.
Stages of embryonic development include all aspects of internal and external morphogenetic change that occur within the embryo during that stage. They are used to convey a snapshot of the status of the development of all body systems within a particular timeframe. Fig. 23.2 gives an overview of the changes in the main body systems over time and also shows the systems and organs at risk at any specific developmental time and the range of anomalies caused by teratogenic insult at a particular time. Historically, the onset of bone marrow formation in the humerus in stage 23 was used by Streeter to indicate the end of the embryonic period and the beginning of the fetal period of prenatal life, after 53–58 days (about 8 weeks) postfertilization. Fig. 23.3 shows embryonic stages 10–23 correlated to postfertilization days of development alongside postmenstrual weeks. The ‘term’ of a pregnancy, i.e. its completion resulting in delivery, is considered clinically normal between 37 and 42 weeks.
In obstetric practice, the period of pregnancy is divided into thirds, termed trimesters. Within this time scale, starting from the last menstrual period, the fetal period commences during the end of the first trimester (Fig. 23.4). Neonates delivered before 37 weeks are called preterm (or premature); those delivered after 42 weeks are post-term. The use of postmenstrual weeks as a timescale for development has now been extended to those fetuses that are delivered before their predicted ‘term’. This terminology removes the ambiguous use of ‘gestation’,’gestational age’ or ‘gestational weeks’, and helpfully standardizes the terminology in studies that continue to document growth postnatally to 64 postmenstrual weeks (the equivalent of 6 postnatal months for a full-term infant born at 40 weeks). Some studies have used the term ‘corrected age’ for follow-up studies on preterm infants, calculated by subtracting the number of weeks born before 40 weeks’ gestation from the chronological age. It is of note that this method is now standardizing 40 weeks as ‘term’ and it is worth considering if all studies of term babies should have their age similarly adjusted if they were born at 38, 39 or 41 weeks.
The period from postmenstrual week 24 and up to 7 days after birth is termed the perinatal period (see Fig. 23.4). In the UK fetuses that are delivered and die before postmenstrual week 24 are considered to be miscarriages of pregnancy, although technological advances in neonatal care assist the delivery and support of infants younger than postmenstrual week 24. The neonatal period is considered to extend from birth to 28 days postnatally; the early neonatal period extends from birth to 7 days and the late neonatal period from 7 to 28 days.

Ultrasound staging

The predicted date of full term and delivery is revised after routine ultrasound examination of the fetus. Early ultrasound estimation of fetal age increases the rate of reported preterm delivery (delivery at <37 weeks) compared with estimation based only on the date of the last menstrual period, possibly because delayed ovulation is more frequent than early ovulation (Yang et al 2002).
The difficulty of correlating the appearance of a chorionic sac, embryo or fetus on an ultrasound scan, with age during the first trimester is related to the specificity of reporting the age. An age reported as within a specific postmenstrual week, e.g. week 12, will cover a period of 6 days (12 weeks 0 days up to 12 weeks 6 days, now noted as 12w0d and 12w6d). It is recommended that sonographic estimation of age should be given as postmenstrual weeks and days (i.e. 12 weeks indicates 12w0d) (Galan et al 2008).

First trimester scan

An early ultrasound scan will detect implantation and viability of the embryo once a heartbeat is detected, confirm multiple pregnancy and estimate the date of delivery. Cardiac activity can be identified by the sixth postmenstrual week (Galan et al 2008); note in Fig. 23.2 that this corresponds to approximately stage 10 (28–29 postfertilization days). Crown–rump length measurement, i.e. the greatest distance between the vertex of the skull and the ischial tuberosities, with the fetus in the natural curved position, is the most accurate predictor of the postmenstrual age of the embryo during the first trimester (Galan et al 2008). Greatest length exclusive of the lower limbs is independent of fixed points and thus simpler to measure, and was recommended by O’Rahilly and Müller (2000); it is generally taken to be the sitting height in postnatal life. Nuchal translucency is measured between 10 and 14 weeks to diagnose trisomy 21. The development of three-dimensional ultrasound scanning has enabled early detection of many anomalies that were previously diagnosed in the second trimester, including anencephaly, hydrocephalus and encephalocele (Pretorius et al 2014). Normograms of fetal spine growth constructed for a Taiwan population showed mean spine length increasing linearly between 11 and 14 weeks (Cheng et al 2010) and a tendency for spinal extension as early as 11 weeks.

Second trimester scan

Routine scanning at 18–20postmenstrual weeks is used to confirm the delivery date and assess not only the position of the placenta but also the presence of fetal anomalies that would require special intervention following delivery, such as cardiac defects, lung immaturity, renal agenesis, polyhydramnios and defects of the anterior abdominal wall (gastroschisis and exomphalos). Estimations are made of the following: biparietal skull diameter, taken through a plane of section that traverses the third ventricle and thalami; head circumference through a plane that traverses the third ventricle and thalami plus the cavum septi pellucidi anteriorly and the tentorial hiatus posteriorly; abdominal circumference through a plane where the transverse diameter of the liver is greatest, the appearance of the lower ribs is symmetric and the junction of the left and right portal veins is identified; and femoral length, which measures the ossified portions of the diaphysis and metaphyses (Fig. 23.5). Between 15 and 28postmenstrual weeks, the biparietal diameter is the most accurate index of fetal age and the expected date of delivery. Other measurements that may also be taken include the transverse cerebellar diameter and foot length. The amount and type of fetal movement, breathing movements and visceral functions, exemplified by bladder emptying, peristaltic action and colonic echogenicity, are noted. Gender can be identified later in development but this information is not always routinely passed on to parents. Three- and four-dimensional ultrasound scans are now routinely constructed (Video 23.1). For assessment of fetal wellbeing from ultrasound examination see Benson and Doubilet (2018).
Managing fetal anomalies
An outcome of routine ultrasound examination of embryos and fetuses for anomalies may be a change to the perinatal management, i.e. to the time, method and place of delivery of the fetus, or the parents may choose to terminate the pregnancy to minimize concerns about fetal and neonatal suffering and long-term disability. Termination may be chosen for severe, untreatable inherited metabolic disorders (e.g. Tay–Sachs disease), severe chromosomal anomalies (e.g. trisomy 13), lethal bone dysplasias, lethal anomalies such as anencephaly and other extreme neurological defects, and bilateral renal agenesis (Cass 2011). Improvements in prenatal screening and diagnosis have led to an overall increase in the prevalence of reported birth defects and overall lower perinatal mortality rates, reflecting increased early terminations of pregnancy (Cass 2011).
Magnetic resonance imaging
Advances in prenatal ultrafast magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) now provide further ways to detect fetal anomalies, capturing particularly clear images (Sepulveda et al 2012) (Fig. 23.6). For this reason, MRI is considered a useful adjunct to ultrasound imaging at 20–22postmenstrual weeks because it enables better management planning for known or suspected anomalies (Reddy et al 2014).
Fetal surgery
The use of routine ultrasound examination and MRI of fetuses has led to the development of a number of prenatal surgical interventions: e.g. to correct placental or membrane anomalies resulting in twin-to-twin transfusion and the production of amniotic bands (Deprest et al 2014); or to offer improved outcomes for meningomyelocele on the basis that the neural tissue may become secondarily damaged by exposure to amniotic fluid and mechanical traumatic injury during gestation (Adzick 2013, Cohen et al 2014, Danzer and Johnson 2014). Other conditions have also been treated prenatally with limited improvement in mortality. Congenital diaphragmatic hernia has been treated by percutaneous fetoscopic endoluminal tracheal occlusion from 25 to 33 postmenstrual weeks to prevent loss of lung fluid and enhance lung growth (Deprest et al 2004). Fetal airway patency has also been preserved during birth through perinatal ex utero intrapartum (EXIT) treatment, where a portion of the fetus is delivered through a hysterectomy incision for surgery while the fetus remains attached to the uteroplacental circulation, and the fetus is subsequently completely delivered after the airway procedure is completed (Deprest et al 2014).

Growth

The changes with age seen in the fetal period are part of a continuum termed growth, which extends postnatally to adulthood. Growth, which describes the velocity of change in size, has distinct phases. It is most rapid in utero, slower but relatively rapid in infancy, steady in childhood, rapid again during adolescence and thereafter very slow as adulthood is approached. It is measured throughout these periods, in a similar manner to that used for embryonic staging, i.e. by documenting increments in height and weight over time. Both genetic and environmental influences operate in exactly the same way as in earlier stages, with one or the other being dominant at any time point. Individual genetic potential can be fully realized at any stage of growth when environmental effects are neutral; conversely, negative environmental conditions and consequent epigenetic changes can prevent an individual from reaching their full growth potential.
Growth is documented in charts that portray different attributes of growth optimally for different end users, e.g. paediatricians, midwives, health visitors, endocrinologists, researchers etc. The recorded data is subject to detailed and specific statistical methodologies that ensure any graphs presented show smooth curves and are easy to follow. Specific terminology indicates the methodology used when collecting and presenting the data. A longitudinal study shows the successive measurement of individuals taken for the entire period of growth. A cross-sectional study collects measurements from different individuals and means (with their standard deviation – SD) are calculated for each time point, sometimes also for each sex.
A cross-sectional collection of weight or height from an adult population would produce a graph with a normal distribution with the mean measurement at the highest point. To display the change in weight and height in children, growth charts are presented with the weight or height plotted against time plus standard deviations each side of the mean (1SD, 2SD and 3SD), shown also as lines. Growth charts can also be constructed to show centiles (calculated from the mean and SD), so that the 50th centile (also known as the median), in the midpoint of the distribution, is flanked by lines showing the 3rd, 10th, 25th, 75th, 90th and 97th centiles. The charts are different because SD lines are delineated equally each side of the mean, whereas centiles are more widely spaced above the median than below. Both types of chart are often colloquially termed growth curves (Cole 2003, 2012).
The data from longitudinal and cross-sectional studies can be used to plot the increments in height or weight from one age to the next. This produces a velocity curve, which reflects a child’s state at any particular time much better than the growth curve, in which each point is dependent on the preceding one. The oldest published longitudinal study, still of great value today, was made by Count Philibert de Montbeillard on his son (Fig. 23.7). Comparison of the charts show clearly that although height increases over 18 years, the growth velocity decreases from birth onwards, with an acceleration between 13–15 years, during the adolescent growth spurt. Height and the corresponding growth velocity for the first 22 months of life, compiled from cross-sectional data is shown in Fig. 23.8.
The time at which data for growth charts were collected is important because secular change is now very apparent between older and more recent charts. Many of the growth charts acknowledged in the literature are based on meticulous studies from the 1940s and 1950s. They illustrate the reference weight and height for children born between or after the World Wars and later when food rationing was still in operation in the UK. They also reflect the social mores of those times when men were usually fed first, followed by the children, and women were fed last, factors that undoubtedly affected the growth of children at that time and the fetuses of low weight mothers.
Cross-sectional growth charts are particularly important in the management of public health issues, e.g. summarizing and comparing the nutritional status of different groups of children to explore the potential effects of socioeconomic status on growth. In this case the applicability of the chart to an individual child is less important. However, the reference population in which data are collected is important, as a growth chart may not be valid for an individual child who is not representative of the ethnic, socioeconomic or environmental factors pertaining to that reference population.
Prospective studies (e.g. INTERGROWTH 21st project, Papagerorgiou et al 2014), or longitudinal studies that exclude data from mothers or fetuses with pathological conditions, provide prescriptive standards against which individual fetuses growing in utero can be compared. The WHO multinational longitudinal fetal study (Kiserud and Haugen 2017), and studies from particular countries and regions, using specified racial and ethnic groups, provide reference ranges, reporting how some heterogeneous fetal populations grow over time; these studies include data from mothers or fetuses with pathological conditions (e.g. NICHD fetal growth studies, Buck Louis et al 2015, Grantz et al 2018). In both study types similar ultrasound anthropometric data are gathered (i.e. biparietal diameter (BPD), head circumference, femur length and abdominal circumference), and fetal weight is estimated. However, the inclusion criteria of participating mothers are different, since they may include fetuses that become growth retarded or that develop anomalies, and different statistical calculations may be applied to the data collected. It is important to recognize the different clinical utility of reference ranges and prescriptive standards (O’Gorman and Salomon 2018).
Growth has always been regarded as a regular process. Tanner (in Harrison et al (1964)) noted that the more carefully measurements were taken, the more regular the succession of points on a growth curve. However, a longitudinal study of growth measured weekly, semi-weekly and daily found that growth in length and head circumference occurred by saltatory increments, with a mean amplitude of 1.01 cm for length (Lampl 2002). Growth stasis, steep changes in growth and incremental growth have all been recorded in infancy, childhood and adolescence (Caino et al 2006). For a comprehensive account of all aspects of postnatal growth, the reader should consult Human Growth and Development (Cameron and Bogin 2012).

In utero

The relative growth of the placenta and fetus changes throughout development. Cellular hyperplasia is seen in the first trimester, hyperplasia and hypertrophy in the second trimester and hypertrophy alone in the third trimester (Baschat and Galan 2017). Placental growth plateaus in the second trimester, whereas fetal growth increases to 1.5% per day. Longitudinal growth and muscle development initially contribute to the weight gain. From 28 postmenstrual weeks fetal fat accumulates, and increases from 3.2% of fetal body weight at 32 postmenstrual weeks to 16% by delivery.
The fetal period is one of accelerated growth and differentiative processes that continue up to and beyond birth. Overall, the fetus increases in length from 30 mm to 500 mm and increases in weight from 2–3 g to more than 3000 g. Measured from the 9th to the 13th postmenstrual week, mean crown–rump length (CRL) increases almost linearly with age (Papageorghiou et al 2014) (Fig. 23.9). Throughout the period 13–20 postmenstrual weeks, the head and upper limbs of the fetus are still disproportionately large. The legs do not quite equal the arms in length proportionately, even by birth. The thorax broadens relative to the head, and the infra-umbilical abdominal wall shows a relative increase in area, so that the umbilicus gradually becomes more centrally situated. The rate of fetal growth slows from 36 to 40 postmenstrual weeks in response to the physical limitation imposed by the maternal uterus. This slowing of the growth rate enables a genetically larger child developing within a small mother to be delivered successfully.
The sequence of fetal weight gain and regional growth during the 28–38th postmenstrual week period has been identified across longitudinal and cross-sectional studies. Estimated fetal weight (EFW) standards for optimal fetal growth, derived from a function of head circumference and abdominal circumference, have been calculated (for details of the formulae used see Stirnemann et al 2017) (Fig. 23.10).
Placental weight at birth has routinely been measured and correlated with neonatal birth weight. An increase in placental efficiency is noted as an increased birth weight : placental weight ratio, with the reverse, a reduced birth weight : placental weight ratio suggesting reduced nutrient transfer per gram of placenta. Alterations in the availability of nutrients to the fetus at particular stages of pregnancy elicit adaptive responses by the fetus. These may ensure fetal coping but may also result in pathology in adult life; the nutritional status of pregnant females is therefore of fundamental importance for the health of the next generation. Under- or overnutrition during fetal life may lead to metabolic changes in childhood, adolescence and adulthood, and pass to successive generations (Maltepe and Penn 2018). Poor nutrition at critical stages of fetal life permanently alters the normal developmental pattern of a range of fetal organs and tissues, e.g. the endocrine pancreas, liver and blood vessels, resulting in their pathological responses to certain conditions in later adult life (Barker et al 1993, Catalano and Hauguel-De Mouzon 2011). Both high altitude and mild undernutrition, particularly during mid-pregnancy, cause an increase in placental size as an adaptive response. Although a larger placenta may be better able to deliver the full nutritional requirements of the fetus, the perfusion of a larger placenta is not without problems. Those pregnancies with relatively large placentas (low birth weight : placental weight ratio) are noted to increase the risk of hypertension in adulthood (Hayward et al 2016). It has been suggested that epigenetic placental changes may alter fetal blood flow and placental enzymes, the normal structure of the fetal vessel wall, or of its responses to circulating trophins, e.g. catecholamines or angiotensin II, and these prenatal changes continue into postnatal life. Undernutrition in later pregnancy does not produce the same sequelae and placental enlargement does not occur.

Alterations of fetal growth

Fetal growth restriction
Assessment of fetal growth aims to separate those constitutionally small fetuses, who might be expected to have a lower birth weight based on parental characteristics, but are growing normally and not at risk for adverse outcomes (70% of those with a birth weight below the 10th centile), from the remaining 30% of infants with a birth weight below the 10th centile who are growth restricted and at risk for increased mortality and perinatal morbidity (Baschat and Galan 2017). The reasons why some fetuses are classified as experiencing restricted growth, are multifactorial and complex involving maternal, placental and fetal factors operating over time. These factors include inter alia maternal hypertensive disorders, diabetes and malabsorptive disease, placental abruption and infarction, fetal genetic disorders and fetal infection.
Placental and fetal factors lead to symmetric and asymmetric patterns of fetal growth disturbance (Baschat and Galan 2017). In a symmetric growth pattern, decreased cell numbers and cell size in the first trimester, due to trisomies, genetic syndromes and viral infections, give rise to alterations in both body and head growth. In an asymmetric somatic growth pattern, uteroplacental insufficiencies, limiting nutrient supply and changing placental blood flow resistance, cause delay of lower body and abdominal circumference growth with absolute or relative sparing of head growth.
Ultrasound criteria are used to identify fetal growth restriction and sonographic estimated fetal weight (SEFW) is calculated. This is supplemented by sequential growth assessments and diagnostic tests, including Doppler assessment of the placenta, umbilical and fetal middle cerebral arteries. For Fetal Medicine Foundation reference ranges for the pulsatility index in these vessels (postmenstrual weeks 20–41) based on data from 72,387 pregnancies, see Ciobanu et al (2019). Ruiz-Martinez et al (2019) note wide discrepancies in published Doppler reference values and caution that these may result in significant variability in the management of growth-restricted fetuses and suboptimal outcomes. They recommend the reference ranges should be standardized. SEFW growth curves are generated from obstetric populations. Although some fetal biometry measurements are not considered accurately predictive for fetal growth restriction, a femur length : abdominal circumference ratio greater than 23.5 suggests growth restriction. This ratio is normally 22 at all gestational ages from 21 postmenstrual weeks to delivery (Baschat and Galan 2017).
Maternal cigarette smoking, alcohol consumption and illicit drug use can cause fetal growth restriction (Ross et al 2015).
Macrosomia
Growth in utero may result in fetal macrosomia as well as fetal growth restriction. The risk of fetal macrosomia, defined as an absolute birth weight greater than 4000 g, 4500 g or 5000 g, or as a customized birth weight centile greater than the 90th, 95th or 97th centile for the infant’s gestation age (Walsh and McAuliffe 2012, Schwartz et al 2014), is three times higher in females with poorly controlled gestational diabetes. The neonates have significantly increased fat mass but not lean body mass (Catalano and Hauguel-De Mouzon 2011). Obesity has become increasingly prevalent since the end of the twentieth century, leading to increases in maternal type 2 diabetes, hypertension, hyperlipidaemia, atherosclerosis and inflammation. In pregnancy, an observed maternal decrease in insulin sensitivity as pregnancy advances leads to increased nutrient transfer to the fetus. In response, the fetus increases insulin secretion, IGF-I production is stimulated, and fetal growth and fat deposition are promoted (Catalano 2014, Maltepe and Penn 2018). Obese females start their pregnancy with greater insulin resistance than females of normal weight, which means that their fetuses produce more IGF-I and continue to grow. However, the development of macrosomia is complex; it can occur in the absence of maternal hypoglycaemia where there is increased umbilical blood flow to the liver in the third trimester. High umbilical blood flow to the liver can lead to high fat content in the neonate, sustained for up to 4 postnatal years (Kiserud and Haugen 2017).
Fetal growth velocity has been computed from estimated fetal weight standards for singleton pregnancies undergoing ultrasound scans at 10w0d–13w6d, 16–22, 24–29, 30–33, 34–37 and 38–41 postmenstrual weeks (Grantz et al 2018). Mean EFW growth velocity at 16, 20, 30 and 35 postmenstrual weeks was 29 g/wk, 59 g/wk, 175 g/wk and 215 g/wk, with growth velocity peaking at 35 weeks (Grantz et al 2018) (Fig. 23.11). Considering the anthropometric measurements in this study, the average BPD growth velocity peaks at 13 postmenstrual weeks with a second slight acceleration from 19–22 weeks. Head circumference average growth velocity peaks at 14 weeks with a second acceleration from 19–21 weeks. Both decrease in growth velocity after this time. Average growth velocities for femoral and humeral length peak at about 15 weeks and then steadily decline. Abdominal circumference average growth velocity increases until 16 postmenstrual weeks, with a second acceleration from 27–31 weeks, after which growth velocity steadily declines up to 40 weeks (Fig. 23.12A–D).
There is a correlation between short maternal stature and macrosomia; macrosomic fetuses are at risk of shoulder dystocia and brachial plexus injuries during vaginal delivery. Adiposity at birth is related to obesity and metabolic dysfunction in childhood, which may be perpetuated through adulthood in an ongoing cycle through the generations (Catalano and Hauguel-De Mouzon 2011, Poston 2012). A range of risk factors for pre-, peri- and postnatal issues in maternal obesity is given in McGuire et al (2010).
Stillbirth
Stillbirth is classified as antepartum death occurring before the initiation of labour or intrapartum death occurring during labour and delivery. It is estimated that by 28 postmenstrual weeks approximately 1 in 250 pregnancies will result in a stillbirth in high-income countries and 1 in 22 pregnancies in low- and middle-income countries (Hirst et al 2018). Maternal age of 40 years or older doubled the risk of stillbirth compared to mothers aged 18–25 years. The epidemiology of neonatal mortality in Europe and the UK is presented in Macfarlane and Mugford (2012).
Assisted reproductive technique effects
A range of assisted reproductive techniques (ART) (Ch. 8) have been used and developed over the last 40 years and account for about 2% of births annually in Europe and America (Ginod et al 2018). It is becoming apparent that the mode of conception influences the epigenetic imprinting of offspring and that ARTs affect fetal growth kinetics, postnatal growth and adult risk of pathology. ART-conceived fetuses show higher CRL and EFW than reference charts during the first and second trimesters, but decelerated growth from the second trimester onwards with increased risk of low birth weight mortality (Fleming et al 2018, Ginod et al 2018).

Transition to Extrauterine Life

The sudden release of a fetus into a gaseous environment of varying temperature, with full gravity and a range of microorganisms, promotes the rapid maturation of some systems and the compensatory growth of others (in terms of responses to the effect of gravity, enteral feeding and exposure to microorganisms, respectively). Maturational processes involving local interactions and pattern formation still drive development at local and body-system levels in preterm infants. For details of the cardiovascular changes at birth see Chapter 13.
The type of birth, whether for preterm or term delivery, confers developmental outcomes on the neonate that affect its subsequent growth. Caesarean section for preterm delivery appears not to be associated with improved neonatal outcomes but is associated with increased risk of respiratory distress syndrome due to slower postnatal movement of fluid out of the lungs (Bellini et al 2006, Bhatta and Keriakos 2011, Werner et al 2012). The dramatic rise of this delivery method is not necessarily driven by improved neonatal outcomes because caesarean section has been shown to increase the odds of developing asthma and type 1 diabetes in later life by 20%. Meta-analyses indicate that the endocrine milieu during caesarean section may lead to epigenetic effects on hepatic and metabolic function and so affect immune function in adult life, leading, in some instances, to food allergy and obesity (Steer and Modi 2009, Hyde and Modi 2012, Song et al 2013).
Prior to birth, the gut is exposed to hundreds of proteins, metabolites and cytokines that are swallowed in amniotic fluid, and there is some evidence for the presence of bacterial species in amniotic fluid prior to delivery. Establishment of the gut microbiota by exposure to maternal vaginal and colonic bacteria during vaginal delivery leads to normal maturation of the gut wall. Gut colonization in preterm infants is delayed (Cilieborg et al 2012). Infants born via caesarean section may have primary gut flora disturbance for up to 6 months after birth and associated delay in postnatal immune development (Neu and Rushing 2011, Neu and Mai 2012, Matamoros et al 2013).
The establishment of enteral feeding has profound effects on early postnatal maturation. Human breast milk contains many bioactive substances that enhance gut maturity. It improves gastric emptying, encourages the growth of the microbiome and contains a range of proteins, including large amounts of secretory immunoglobulin A and cytokines (e.g. interleukin (IL)-10) (Jakaitis and Denning 2014). Feeding preterm infants human milk is associated with less feeding intolerance and is thought to provide protection against diabetes and obesity in later life (Valentine and Morrow 2012). Metabolomic studies on human milk fed to preterm and full-term neonates have shown that preterm milk contains higher concentrations of proteins and amino acids, and that the fatty acid content is higher in colostrum from mothers with neonates lower than the 20th centile for weight. The metabolome of milk from preterm delivered mothers achieves a similar composition to full-term delivered mothers by 5–7 postnatal weeks (Bardanzellu et al 2018). The rate of growth of infants fed breast milk for the first 6 months of postnatal life is different to that of formula-fed infants. Delivery of low-weight preterm babies, followed by parenteral feeding with no enteral nutrition, disrupts normal gut maturation and may lead to necrotizing enterocolitis (Wynn and Neu 2012, Neu and Pammi 2017). However, a balance with the gut microbiome is necessary; although full-term neonates born in tropical countries have similar villous height to those born in temperate climates, villous length in the small intestine shortens within 2–12 months of birth as a consequence of tropical enteropathy (Ramakrishna et al 2006). Human milk oligosaccharides, which are not absorbed in the proximal gut, are thought to act as probiotics by establishing interactions with the early gut microbiome so that specific bacterial species, able to metabolize sialylated breast milk oligosaccharides, can flourish, enhance gut barrier function and limit the expansion of enteropathogens (Charnonneau et al 2016). The physiological effects and importance of maternal–infant dyad interactions in establishing healthy postnatal life are complex and only now being investigated.

Preterm neonate

The way in which birth at a particular postmenstrual week of delivery has been classified into preterm birth age has been more complex than might be thought. The WHO has subdivided preterm birth into the categories of postmenstrual weeks in utero: extremely preterm <28 weeks; very preterm 28–32 weeks; late preterm 32–<37 weeks (Howson et al 2012). Term pregnancy has been defined as within the postmenstrual weeks period 37 completed weeks to less than 42 completed weeks; early term delivery 37–38 weeks; full term 38–40 weeks and post-term >42 weeks (Quinn et al 2016). Because the processes involved in initiating parturition are so complex, Villar et al (2012) recommend that five components should be included in any consideration of preterm birth: significant maternal conditions present before delivery; significant fetal conditions present before delivery; presence of placental pathological conditions; signs of the initiation of parturition; and the type of delivery. These factors involved in the pathogenesis of spontaneous preterm birth are discussed in Buhimschi and Norman (2014). Infection of the decidua, amniotic fluid, amnion or chorion, and clinical and subclinical chorioamnionitis have been reported in approximately 50% of preterm births (Maltepe and Penn 2018) (Ch. 9), whereas sterile inflammation occurs normally in the decidua, myometrium and cervix prior to parturition (Mesiano 2019). In 2010 more than 10% of all births were premature world-wide (Simhan et al 2014), the majority in south Asia and sub-Saharan Africa. In the United States, more than 70% of preterm births occurred between 34 and 36 postmenstrual weeks and 16% of preterm births occurred before 32 postmenstrual weeks (Simhan et al 2014). Preterm birth is the most frequent cause of infant mortality and accounts for one-third of infant deaths (for details of the risk factors for preterm birth see Simhan et al (2014)).
Previously, low birth weight fetuses and neonates were grouped statistically with preterm birth infants, however, improvement in sonographic estimation of fetal age means that perinatal and infant morbidities, which vary according to maturity and weight, can be addressed separately. A new definition of preterm birth syndrome has been proposed in publications from the Global Alliance to Prevent Prematurity and Stillbirth, which includes all births (live births, stillbirths and pregnancy terminations) occurring from 16w0d to 38w6d (Villar et al 2012, Kramer et al 2012, Buhimschi and Norman 2014). The rationale for the early time is because pathologies occurring between 16 and 20 weeks also affect later gestation; the rationale for the later time because births between 37 and 39 weeks are associated with greater morbidity than births after 39 weeks. The definition aims to consider early delivery and not disturbances of growth (see p. 371). The definition is based on a clinical phenotype of: (a) one or more conditions of the mother, placenta, or fetus; (b) the presence or absence of signs of parturition; (c) the pathway to delivery (spontaneous or caregiver initiated). The Global Alliance to Prevent Prematurity and Stillbirths recommends the use of this definition world-wide and acknowledges that its use will initially lead to higher reported rates.
It was previously assumed that the trajectories for extrauterine growth of preterm neonates would mimic that of intrauterine growth of fetuses matched for gestational age and of neonatal birth weights. The Preterm Postnatal follow-up study of the INTERGROWTH-21st Project has highlighted the differences between weight and head circumference of preterm infants and the WHO Child Growth Standards for term neonates (Villar et al 2015). The prospective studies within the INTERGROWTH project collected data from heathy women, with reliable menstrual dates and confirmatory ultrasound scan dating. Singleton pregnancies born preterm between 26 and 37 postmenstrual weeks were followed. The infants were breastfed or bottle-fed maternal milk daily, initially by nasogastric tube if required. Any neonates with congenital anomalies or with birth weights less than the 3rd centile were excluded, thus these growth charts are standards of postnatal growth of healthy infants, delivered earlier than expected. The charts plot postnatal growth up to 64 postmenstrual weeks, at which time the growth trajectories are similar to the WHO Child Growth Standards for term neonates (Fig. 23.13).

Full-term neonate

Details of the relative positions of the viscera and the skeleton in a full-term neonate are shown in Figs 23.14–23.16. The newborn infant is not a miniature adult, and extremely preterm infants are not the same as full-term infants.
Just as there are differences in the anatomical relationships of structures between the 40 postmenstrual week neonate, child and adult, there are major differences between 20 postmenstrual week and 40 postmenstrual week fetuses. The study of fetal anatomy at 20, 25, 30 and 35 weeks is vital for the investigative and life-saving procedures carried out on preterm infants today.
At birth, neonatal weight reflects the maternal environment, the number of conceptuses, the sex of the baby and the parity of the mother. Generally, full-term female babies are lighter than full-term males, twins are lighter than singletons, and later children tend to be heavier than the first born. The 10th to 90th centile ranges for the weight of a full-term infant at parturition can be seen in Fig. 23.10 (Stirnemann et al 2017). Body water accounts for 75–80% of this weight and a further 15–28% is composed of adipose tissue. Heavier babies have greater fat mass that can contribute to 46% of the variance in birth weight, although the relationship between lean mass and fat mass is not clear; heavier babies have thicker skinfolds (a measurement of fat mass) proportional to their size (Pomeroy et al 2014).
Different birth phenotypes have been correlated with different pathological sequelae. Infants who are thin at birth, with a low ponderal index (weight/length3), tend to develop a combination of insulin resistance, hypertension, type 2 diabetes and lipid disorders, whereas those who are short in relation to head size tend to develop hypertension and high plasma fibrinogen concentrations. These associations have been reported in low weight babies, rather than in those born prematurely. Some babies of average weight also develop cardiovascular pathology; they are either thin at birth and small in relation to the size of their placenta, or of average weight but short in relation to head size and have below average weight gain during the first year (Barker et al 1993). For more views on this concept consult Godfrey and Barker (2000), Ross and Beal (2008) and Kelishadi and Poursafa (2014).

Post-term neonate

Post-term delivery has been reported to affect the subsequent growth of boys more than girls. It is estimated that 8.1% of all live births in Europe and 5.6% of live births in the United States are born post-term (Beltrand et al 2012). In a Swedish study, post-term boys were heavier and longer than term boys at birth, although with similar ponderal indices. Post-term boys showed weight velocity changes and had higher BMIs than term boys by 3 years of age. There were no differences in weight gain throughout childhood in post-term and term girls. By 16 years post-term and term boys had similar height, but post-term boys were heavier. The rate of obesity (BMI >95th centile for age; BMI >85th centile for age) was 29% and 47% respectively in post-term boys and 7% and 13% respectively for term boys (Beltrand et al 2012).

Infancy and childhood

Preadult life is divided into five stages (Bogin 2012). Term delivery is considered normal between postmenstrual weeks 37 and 40. Postnatal growth is now described to 64 postmenstrual weeks to facilitate examination of the growth trajectories of preterm infants. The normal neonatal period can be defined as the first 28 postnatal days, 40–44 postmenstrual weeks. It is followed by infancy, which commences at postmenstrual week 45 and ends at about 36 postnatal months. During infancy weaning occurs and deciduous tooth eruption is seen. The WHO define infancy as ending at 12 postnatal months and the CDC have the subdivision of toddler extending from 1 to 3 years of age. Childhood extends from 3 to 7 years; there a midgrowth spurt, and eruption of the first permanent incisor and molar teeth. A human juvenile period begins at about 7 years of age; this ends in girls at about 10 years and in boys at about 12 years. Juveniles are prepubertal and in many industrialized nations the juvenile stage coincides with entry into traditional formal education.
After birth, there is a general decrease in total body water but a relative increase in intracellular fluid. Normally, a newborn loses about 10% of its birth weight by 3–4 days postnatally as a result of loss of excess extracellular fluid: by the end of the first year, total body water makes up 60% of the body weight. Growth in weight usually reaches its peak velocity after birth. Birth weight normally triples by the end of the first year, and quadruples by the end of the second year. Infants that have been breastfed for longer have slower weight growth velocities in the first months of life and their peak weight growth velocity occurs at a later age (Regnault and Gillman 2014). The peak velocity for length is reached at about 4 postnatal months (see Fig. 23.7). Body length increases from a neonatal range of 48–53 cm to about 75 cm during the first year after birth, by 12–13 cm in the second year and by 5–6 cm each year thereafter until puberty.
Hormonal changes that affect early postnatal life and subsequent growth rates in infants occur from birth to 6 postnatal months. This period has been termed mini-puberty (Ch. 22). It is associated with the rapid decrease of circulating placental hormones in the neonate and the activation of the HPG axis. The early surge in circulating testosterone in males during the first postnatal months influences somatic and adipose development in childhood. The growth velocity in weight and BMI at 8 postnatal weeks in boys is faster than girls; this corresponds to the time when median testosterone levels approach pubertal male values (Lanciotti et al 2018).
In 2006 the WHO published growth charts from birth to 5 years and 5–19 years compiled from children considered to be growing under optimal environmental conditions, i.e. they were breastfed, raised in environments that minimized poor diets and infection, and had non-smoking mothers. UK reference growth charts for boys and girls aged 2–18 years were published in 2012 by the Royal College of Paediatrics and Child Health (Fig. 23.17). Similar growth charts have been published for specified regions around the world, addressing racial and ethnic differences (Mansourian et al 2012, Khadilkar and Khadilkar 2015, Barzin et al 2018). As an example, Khadilkar and Khadilkar (2015) stress the importance of updating growth reference charts in countries experiencing major secular change in nutritional status and an increasing incidence of obesity. Compared to previous Indian growth charts, the revised charts show that Indian boys and girls are taller at all ages than they were 7 years ago. Using WHO guidelines, Khadilkar and Khadilkar lowered the weight centiles in comparison to previous charts which had included affluent children. The revised Indian Academy of Pediatrics charts are now considered more appropriate for use with Asian children. At birth South Asian children are among the lightest with a ‘thin-fat’ phenotype, i.e. small abdominal viscera, low muscle mass, and a relatively large proportion of body fat (Fairley et al 2013). Lower birth weight and fast early growth in Asian populations, both in Asia and in the UK, led to increased adiposity and increased cardiometabolic risk with a lower BMI (West et al 2013).
The pattern of postnatal growth in children is considered to be predictable: deviation from these patterns suggests growth disorders. Measurement of linear growth (supine length in infants and height in children) and growth velocity (as assessed from sequential height charts) show that linear growth velocity is most rapid in infancy, averaging 15  cm/year in the first two years. Children who grow in an environment that does not constrain their growth exhibit a pattern of growth that is mainly parallel to a particular centile, a phenomenon that has been termed homeorhesis or canalization (following the same imaginary ‘canal’ on the growth chart). Two-thirds of infants cross centile channels on linear growth curves (Cuttler 2016). After deviation from a centile (e.g. as a consequence of chronic disease in childhood, or the adolescent growth spurt), most children return to the same centile position in adulthood, a finding that suggests that this pattern is genetically determined within individuals (Cameron and Bogin 2012). Upward crossing of 2 centile lines in the first 6 months of life is more predictive of later obesity than upward centile crossing in any other 6-month interval in the first two years of postnatal life (Regnault and Gillman 2014).
From infancy to puberty the velocity of linear growth averages about 6 cm/year (Cuttler 2016). A target height, the child’s genetic potential, can be predicted by the midparental height plus 6.5 cm for boys and minus 6.5 cm for girls. (Mid-parental height is the average of both parents’ heights, plotted on the height centile chart at age 18 years after adjustment for sex). Syndrome-specific growth charts have been developed for aneuploidy and genetic anomalies that cause differences in the growth trajectory of children to adulthood, although there are few new growth charts for short stature children. For growth charts for Down’s syndrome consult Styles et al (2002) for UK data and Zemel et al (2015) for US data. For growth charts for Turner syndrome consult Lyon et al (1985) for UK data and Isojima et al (2010) for Japanese data.
Infants and children with chronic diseases and recurrent infections fail to achieve the genetic growth potential. ‘Catch-up growth’ describes growth above the normal rate expected for the specific age, seen on recovery from such conditions. It may restore the child to their original canal (channel) on the growth chart; if the disorder extends for many years and into puberty, it may not be completed. Information concerning individual growth may be retained as a memory in the epiphysial plates of long bones and in the chondrocyte resting zone and seems to influence structural and functional catch up in the epiphysial plate (Cuttler 2016).
Changes in weight during childhood can be secondary to changes in body water, muscle mass, total lean body mass and fat: weight is not synonymous with fat. Body mass index (BMI, weight/height2) can be calculated for children. Body fat increases from about 15% to 25% by 6 months of age and decreases to about 15% by 5 years. Weaning is usually initiated after the first 6 months of postnatal life (equivalent to 64 postmenstrual weeks); there is high risk of growth delay during this process that may affect weight. By 5 years of age the sex difference in body fat is about 2% increasing to 6% by 10 years. Although growth velocity plateaus between 5 and 10 years, a mid-growth or juvenile growth spurt is seen between 6 and 8 years in both sexes. It is small in terms of height, but larger for weight and skinfolds and has been attributed to an increase in adrenal androgenic hormone secretion (Lejarraga 2012).
Low weight in infancy and childhood at a given age may result in stunting, i.e. linear growth restriction (short height for age), or wasting, i.e. low weight for height (low BMI for age or reflecting recent weight loss). In neonates, these examples of alterations in growth are considered to be two different phenotypes with different timing, duration of causal insult and different prognoses (Victora et al 2015). Although the proportion of undernourished and underweight children declined globally from 25% in 1990 to 15% in 2015, it increased by about one-third in sub-Saharan Africa between 1990 and 2015. One-third of all undernourished children globally are now in sub-Saharan Africa, 39% of those stunted and 10% wasted (Akombi et al 2017).

Adolescence and adulthood

The juvenile period ends when puberty commences. Puberty is a short time period when there is a dramatic increase in sex hormone secretions and reactivation of the CNS for sexual development. It precedes adolescence, a developmental stage of 5–10 years during which there is a growth spurt in height and weight, eruption of the remaining permanent teeth, development of secondary sexual characteristics and sociosexual maturation (Bogin 2012). Humans seem to be the only species to have a quiescent interval between the rapid growth that takes place after birth and the adolescent growth spurt, a pattern of growth termed the Infancy/Childhood/Puberty (ICP) growth model (Stevens et al 2013). It has been suggested that this quiescent interval allows the acquisition of language, cultural inheritance and complex socialization before individuals pass through puberty and become sexually active. A neotenous heterochronic shift in the transcription of specific genes in the prefrontal cortex occurs in humans from early adolescence and corresponds to a period of substantial cortical reorganization that may be associated with the development of cognitive skills (Somel et al 2009).
Development-related gene expression correlates with three phases of human growth (infancy/early childhood; childhood/puberty; final height) in a development-dependent manner in human tissues (Stevens et al 2013) (Fig. 23.18). The outcomes of the Human Genome Project have been linked to anthropomorphic traits in meta-analyses of genome-wide association data that have identified genetic variants at hundreds of loci relevant to growth in pre-puberty, puberty and adulthood, particularly those associated with childhood and adult obesity (Genome-wide Investigation of ANThropometric measures – GIANT). Genome-wide association studies have shown genetic loci linking pubertal timing, height and growth with childhood obesity and genes involved with increases in BMI (Cousminer et al 2013, Locke et al 2015).
It is thought that the return to long bone growth seen in adolescence is a specifically human event (Stevens et al 2013). The adolescent growth spurt occurs at different times for different individuals, which means that nearly all children will cross centiles on reference charts of cross-sectional data. In an ‘early developer’, the height-for-age curve rises before that of their peers and levels off early. In a ‘late developer’, growth appears to slow but then accelerates and so their height-for-age curve rises after their peers have either ceased or nearly ceased growing (Cameron 2012). The increase in the growth velocity occurs between 10.5 and 11 years in girls, and 12.5 and 13 years in boys (Fig. 23.19; see Fig. 23.17). In both sexes, this spurt lasts for 2–2.5 years. Girls gain about 16 cm in height during the spurt, with a peak velocity at 12 years of age. Boys gain about 20 cm in height (mostly by growth of the trunk), with a peak velocity at 14 years of age, during which time they may be growing at the rate of 10 cm a year.
Growth in height continues at a slower rate after the adolescent growth spurt. Noticeable growth is said to stop at 18 years in females and 20 years in males; longitudinal studies have indicated an average figure of 16.25 years (females) and 17.75 years (males) with a normal variation of ± 2 years (Harrison et al 1964). Bone age is suggested to be a better predictor of pubertal milestones than chronological age (Cuttler et al 2016). A bone age of 11 years in boys and of 12 years in girls corresponds to the onset of puberty; menarche in girls occurs at a mean skeletal age of about 13 years. Second metacarpal length has been used to assess the impact of regional nutrition on development (Moro and Covino 2018).
Weight gain is more variable in the juvenile stage and adolescence. The onset of puberty is reported to be earlier in obese girls, although obesity per se does not affect the age of menarche (Li et al 2017). Juvenile weight increases by 2.25–2.75 kg annually until the adolescent growth spurt, when boys may add 20 kg to their weight and girls 16 kg. The peak velocity for weight gain lags behind the peak velocity for height by about 3 months. Body weight does not reach adult values until sometime after adult height is attained. Apart from the expected change in growth velocity that occurs during adolescence, it is now apparent that taller child height is prospectively associated with an elevated risk of obesity (Johnson et al 2012, Van Dommelen 2014) and that early attainment of peak height velocity may increase the risks for chronic disease in adulthood life (Regnault and Gillman 2014).
Longitudinal studies suggest that adult height peaks at the end of the third decade of life and declines with increasing velocity from the end of the fourth decade (Leon et al 1995). In a 10-year longitudinal study in England of individuals mostly aged over 55 years, physical stature declined by an annual rate of 0.08–0.10% for males and 0.12–0.14% for females, which would be a 2–4 cm reduction in height over the life course: reductions in height loss occurred simultaneously with deteriorating health (Fernihough and McGovern 2015).
Global estimates of the numbers of adults who are overweight or obese in adult life exceed the numbers of adults who do not have sufficient food (Mascie-Taylor and Goto 2007). Some of the consequences of the high prevalence of obesity in adults on future generational growth are now apparent. Oocyte maturation in obese women is affected by higher circulating concentrations of inflammatory cytokines: these oocytes are smaller, influencing periconception quality. Elevated BMI in men is associated with altered seminal fluid composition, sperm anomalies and decreased motility (Fleming et al 2018). Approximately one-third of adults of reproductive age (20–39 years) were obese in 2015–2016 in the United States (Hales et al 2017), with an annual increase of 2% (Adibi and Zhao 2017). It has been suggested that placental metabolism buffers the lipid environment of the obese mother, limiting lipotoxic effects on the fetus by placental lipid storage (Calabuig-Navarro et al 2017).
The Fetal Origins of Adult Disease hypothesis correlated low birth weight with increased adult risk of cardiovascular diseases; due to fetal anticipation of a similar future ex utero environment to that in utero followed by a childhood obesogenic environment (Barker 2013). The adaptations of this process to fetuses and children of obese parents are still to be investigated.

Integration of Types of Growth During Development and Life

The complex interaction between maternal and paternal genes from the earliest stages of development has been likened to a ‘parental conflict’ for maternal resources. Parentally imprinted genes are only a small proportion of the genome but are important in blastocyst implantation, placentation and developmental growth rates. For example, changes in some individuals to the 11p15 domain affects the future expression of Insulin-like Growth Factor 2 (IGF2) gene (if paternally imprinted) or the negative growth regulator PHLDA2 gene (if maternally imprinted); both conditions affect growth regulation and trajectory in early development (Moore et al 2015) (Ch. 8). Generally, paternally expressed genes enhance growth and maternally expressed genes suppress growth, the latter via genetic processes that prevent offspring becoming too large to be delivered normally because of obstructed labour (Butler 2009). Parental and neonatal phenotype and the prenatal environment may also affect the sexes differently; male fetuses grow faster and to a larger size and may place a greater demand on maternal physiology in stressful environments (Pomeroy et al 2015).
The various types of growth in the later prenatal months and in the postnatal period occur in differing patterns and at different rates. Under physiological conditions it appears that growth and duration of pregnancy are inversely related, with fast-growing fetuses delivering early and slower-growing fetuses having a longer time in utero (Kiserud and Johnsen 2009). High growth velocity at 16–22 weeks increases the likelihood for preterm birth (Lampl et al 2009). These differences in the rates of fetal growth affect the accuracy of estimations of fetal age.
The extent of tissue growth in an organ depends on the specific duration of multiplicative growth for the cell types contained within the organ (Fig. 23.20). Different cell populations complete their initial developmental proliferation and become differentiated at different times; the final stage of differentiation is usually the cessation of cell division.
Growth of a body can be described in two ways: isometric and allometric. Isometric growth implies a progressive proportional increase in all organs and systems with time; it does not occur in the developing embryo, which displays differential rates of growth. Allometric growth describes the differences in the relative rates of growth between one part of the body and another, and is most clearly seen in the changes in body proportion between fetuses, neonates, children and adults. Between 6 and 7 weeks after fertilization, the head is nearly one-half of the total embryonic length; it subsequently grows proportionally more slowly and it is one-quarter of the entire length at birth. During childhood, this pattern of growth continues with lengthening of the torso and limbs; in adults, the head is one-eighth of the total length of the body and lower limb length is the equivalent of four head lengths (Fig. 23.21). Relative limb length at birth is not associated with birth weight and growth-restricted fetuses show little difference in limb proportion. The limbs undergo accelerated postnatal growth relative to the head and trunk and distal limb segment length is especially sensitive to the postnatal environment (Pomeroy et al 2014). Undernutrition between 7 and 8 years of age causes relatively short legs in adults; adults with short stature caused by relatively short legs appear to be at greater risk for coronary heart disease and diabetes than those adults with longer legs (Bogin 2012, Pomeroy et al 2014). Growth of the liver, spleen, kidneys, skeletal and muscular tissues generally follows pre- and postnatal growth curves given for the entire body, whereas other tissues have very different growth rates. The brain, skull, lymphoid tissues and reproductive organs all show differing growth rates during childhood and adolescence (Kappelou et al 2006) (Fig. 23.22).
Differential bone growth rates during puberty can lead to temporal-specific associated pathology. For example, 30% of childhood fractures affect the radius, mostly the distal metaphysis, during the pubertal growth spurt, but the incidence of these fractures decreases rapidly thereafter (Rauch 2012). Local differential rates of bone growth are seen in bone growth plates, peripheral and central trabecular bone, and in bone remodelling. During prepubertal growth, the distal radial growth plate adds about 9 mm to the length of the radius per year and the spaces between the peripheral trabeculae are filled with mineralized bone. It has been suggested that incomplete trabecular coalescence during this rapid growth, and not bone remodelling (which takes at least 6 months to complete), leads to lower bone mineral density (especially in boys) and higher local metaphysial cortical porosity (Wang et al 2010, Rauch 2012).
Final adult height is determined by the time at which the epiphysial growth plates fuse and appears to be controlled by a mechanism within the growth plate itself rather than by a systemic mechanism (Baron et al 2015, Jee and Baron 2016). The length of the proliferative columns of chondrocytes within growth plates is correlated with the length of the limbs in mammals, and is longer in longer limb bones: the sensitivity of growth plates changes during development (Bogin 2012). Over the juvenile and pubertal time periods chondroblast number decreases, and the rate of cell division and extent of cell hypertrophy diminishes; the process is termed growth plate senescence. When chondroblast proliferation ceases, the growth plate is resorbed and replaced by bone. Growth velocity, which is at the equivalent of 82 cm/year from postmenstrual weeks 12 to 40, decreases linearly from birth to about 5 cm/year just before the onset of puberty. During puberty a growth spurt is driven by oestrogen and androgens and followed by further linear growth velocity decrease until cessation of chondrogenesis at the growth plate. Growth plates are sensitive to oestrogen which accelerates the loss of chondrocyte progenitor cells in the growth plate; early exposure to oestrogens, as in precocious puberty, causes early epiphysial fusion. Inflammatory illness and malnutrition in children slow linear growth and the rate of growth plate senescence: if the condition resolves, the growth plates function at a more rapid rate resulting in catch-up growth (Jee and Baron 2016). Skeletal dysplasias affecting stature are thought to reflect the molecular balance of the variable spatial and temporal expression of skeletal genes (Baron et al 2015).
