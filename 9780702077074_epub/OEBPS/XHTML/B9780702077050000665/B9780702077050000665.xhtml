Chapter 66: Liver

The liver is the largest of the abdominal viscera, occupying a substantial portion of the upper abdominal cavity. It occupies most of the right hypochondriac and epigastric regions and frequently extends into the left hypochondriac region as far as the left anterior axillary line (Fig. 66.1). As the body grows from infancy to adulthood, the liver rapidly increases in size. This period of growth reaches a plateau around 18 years and is followed by a gradual decrease in liver weight from middle age. The ratio of liver to body weight decreases with growth from infancy to adulthood. The liver weight is 4–5% of body weight in infancy and decreases to approximately 2% in adulthood. The size of the liver also differs between the sexes (being smaller in females) and body size (enlarging with fat deposition in obese individuals). It has an overall wedge shape, in part determined by the form of the upper abdominal cavity into which it grows. The narrow end of the wedge lies towards the left hypochondriac region and the anterior edge points anteriorly and inferiorly. The superior and right lateral aspects are shaped by the anterolateral abdominal and thoracic walls, and by the respiratory diaphragm. The inferior aspect is shaped by the adjacent viscera. The fibrous capsule of the capsule is no longer considered important in maintaining the shape of the liver; it allows expansion when the liver hypertrophies in response to disease, surgical resection or contralateral embolization of the hepatic portal vein or hepatic artery.
The liver is reddish-brown in colour. It assumes a more yellowish tinge with increasing fat (steatosis) and the edges become more rounded. These changes occur commonly in individuals who are obese and/or drink an excessive amount of alcohol. The liver gains a bluish tinge with venous outflow obstruction (Budd–Chiari syndrome) or congestive heart failure. The texture of the organ is usually soft to firm, though this depends partly on the volume of blood it contains and on its content of adipose and fibrous connective tissues.
The liver performs a wide range of metabolic activities required for homeostasis, nutrition and immune defence. For example, it is important for the removal and breakdown of toxic or potentially toxic materials from the blood; the regulation of blood glucose and lipids; the storage of certain vitamins, iron and other micronutrients; the synthesis of proteins and clotting factors; the metabolism of amino acids; and bile production. It is involved in a plethora of other biochemical reactions. Since most of these processes are exothermic, a substantial part of the thermal energy production of the body, especially at rest, is provided by the liver. The liver is populated by phagocytic macrophages, components of the mononuclear phagocyte system capable of removing particulates from the blood stream. It is an important site of haemopoiesis in the fetus; extramedullary haemopoiesis in the setting of haematological disease.
An account of the more common eponyms relating to the anatomy and surgery of the liver is provided in Stringer (2009).

Surfaces of the liver

The liver is usually described as having superior, anterior, right, posterior and inferior surfaces with a distinct inferior border (Figs 66.2–66.3). However, the superior, anterior and right surfaces are continuous and no definable borders separate them. It is more appropriate to group them as the diaphragmatic surface, which is mostly separated from the inferior, or visceral, surface by a narrow inferior border. At the infrasternal angle, the inferior border is adjacent to the anterior abdominal wall and accessible to physical examination by percussion, but not usually palpable except on deep inspiration. In the midline, the inferior border of the liver is near the transpyloric plane. In women and children the border often projects a little inferior to the right costal arch (margin).

Superior surface

The superior surface is the largest and lies immediately below the respiratory diaphragm, separated from it by peritoneum, except for a small triangular area where the two layers of the falciform ligament diverge. Most of the superior surface lies beneath the right superior aspect of the respiratory diaphragm, but there is a shallow cardiac impression centrally that corresponds to the position of the heart above the central tendon of the diaphragm. The left side of the superior surface lies beneath part of the left side of the diaphragm.

Anterior surface

The anterior surface is approximately triangular and convex and is covered by peritoneum, except at the attachment of the falciform ligament. Much of it is in contact with the anterior attachment of the respiratory diaphragm. On the right, the diaphragm separates it from the pleura and the sixth to the tenth ribs and costal cartilages, and on the left, from the seventh and eighth costal cartilages.

Right surface

The right surface is covered by peritoneum and lies adjacent to the right superior aspect of the respiratory diaphragm, which separates it from the right lung and pleura and the seventh to the eleventh ribs. The right lung and basal pleura lie superior and lateral to its proximal third, between the diaphragm and the seventh and eighth ribs. The diaphragm, the costodiaphragmatic recess lined by pleura, and the ninth and tenth ribs lie lateral to the middle and lower thirds of the right surface. Lateral to the lower third, the diaphragm and thoracic wall are in direct contact. Rarely, the right colic flexure and proximal transverse colon lie on a long mesentery over the right, anterior and superior surfaces of the liver; this can lead to misdiagnosis of pneumoperitoneum on a radiograph or very rarely cause symptoms (Chilaiditi’s syndrome) (Ch. 65).

Posterior surface

The posterior surface is convex, wide on the right but narrow on the left. A median concavity corresponds to the forward convexity of the vertebral column close to the attachment of the ligamentum venosum. Much of the posterior surface is attached to the respiratory diaphragm by loose connective tissue, forming the triangular ‘bare area’. The inferior vena cava lies in a groove or tunnel in the medial end of the bare area. To the left of the groove for the inferior vena cava, the posterior surface of the liver is formed by the caudate lobe and is covered by a layer of peritoneum continuous with that of the inferior layer of the coronary ligament and the layers of the lesser omentum. The caudate lobe is related to the diaphragmatic crura and the right inferior phrenic artery superior to the aortic hiatus, and is separated by these structures from the descending thoracic aorta.
The fissure for the ligamentum venosum separates the caudate lobe from the left lobe. It cuts deeply in front of the caudate lobe and contains the two layers of the lesser omentum. The posterior surface of the left lobe bears a shallow impression near the superior end of the fissure for the ligamentum venosum that is caused by the abdominal part of the oesophagus. The posterior surface of the left lobe to the left of this oesophageal impression is related to the fundus of the stomach. Together, these posterior relations make up what is sometimes referred to as the ‘bed’ of the liver (see Fig. 66.1).

Inferior surface

The inferior surface is bounded by the inferior edge of the liver. It blends with the posterior surface in the region of the origin of the lesser omentum, the porta hepatis and the inferior layer of the coronary ligament, and is marked near the midline by a sharp fissure that contains the round ligament of the liver (ligamentum teres hepatis; the obliterated fetal left umbilical vein). The gallbladder usually lies in a shallow fossa of variable depth: it can have a short mesentery or be completely intrahepatic. The quadrate lobe lies between the fissure for the round ligament of the liver and the gallbladder.
The inferior surface of the left lobe is related inferiorly to the fundus of the stomach and the superior aspect of the lesser omentum. The quadrate lobe lies adjacent to the pylorus, the superior part of the duodenum and the inferior part of the lesser omentum. Occasionally, the transverse colon lies between the duodenum and the quadrate lobe. To the right of the gallbladder, the inferior surface is related to the right flexure of the colon, the right suprarenal gland and right kidney, and the superior part of the duodenum (see Fig. 66.1).

Supports of the liver

The liver is stabilized and maintained in its position in the right upper quadrant of the abdomen by both static and dynamic factors. A three-tier classification of supporting structures has been proposed: the suspensory attachments of the inferior vena cava, hepatic veins, and coronary and triangular ligaments to the posterior abdominal wall (primary factors); the support provided by the right kidney, right colic flexure and duodenopancreatic complex (secondary factors); and the attachment of the falciform ligament to the anterior abdominal wall and respiratory diaphragm (tertiary factors) (Flament et al 1982). The surgical implications of these different factors are important for understanding the pathophysiology of blunt liver trauma and the stability of transplanted liver grafts. The inferior vena cava and the hepatic veins, especially the right hepatic vein, appear to be the most important anatomical structures supporting the bulk of the liver. Other factors that influence the position of the liver within the abdominal cavity include positive intra-abdominal pressure and the movement of the diaphragm during respiration.

Gross anatomical lobes

Historically, the liver has been divided on the basis of its external appearance into right, left, caudate and quadrate lobes, defined in part by peritoneal ligamentous attachments. Additional liver lobes have been reported but are rare.

Right lobe

The right lobe is the largest in volume and contributes to all surfaces of the liver. It is divided from the left lobe by the falciform ligament anteriorly and superiorly and by the ligamentum venosum and fissure for the round ligament of the liver inferiorly. On the inferior surface, to the right of the grooves formed by the round ligament of the liver and ligamentum venosum, the porta hepatis separates two prominences: the caudate lobe lies posterior and the quadrate lobe lies anterior. The gallbladder lies in a shallow fossa on the inferior surface of the liver between the quadrate lobe on the left and the right lobe on the right.

Left lobe

The left lobe is the smaller of the two main lobes, though it is nearly as large as the right lobe in young children. It lies to the left of the falciform ligament with no subdivisions. It is substantially thinner than the right lobe, having a thin apex that points into the left upper quadrant.

Quadrate lobe

The quadrate lobe is visible as a prominence on the inferior surface of the liver to the right of the groove formed by the round ligament of the liver. Although it is anatomically part of the right lobe, its vascular inflow is from the left branch of the hepatic portal vein and left hepatic artery and its biliary outflow is to the left hepatic duct. It lies anterior to the porta hepatis and is bounded by the fossa of the gallbladder to the right, a short portion of the inferior border anteriorly, the fissure for the round ligament of the liver to the left, and the porta hepatis posteriorly. Like the caudate lobe, its morphology varies among individuals (Joshi et al 2009).

Caudate lobe

The caudate lobe is visible as a prominence on the inferior and posterior surfaces to the right of the groove formed by the ligamentum venosum: it lies posterior to the porta hepatis and the hepatogastric ligament. To its right is the groove for the inferior vena cava. Above, it continues into the superior surface on the right of the superior end of the fissure for the ligamentum venosum. In gross anatomical descriptions, this lobe is said to arise from the right lobe but it is functionally separate.

Functional anatomical divisions

Current understanding of the functional anatomy of the liver is based on Couinaud’s division of the liver into eight functional segments (subsequently nine, then later revised back to eight), based on the distribution of portal venous branches in the parenchyma (Couinaud 1957). Understanding the intrahepatic biliary anatomy, especially of the right ductal system, was enhanced by contributions from Hjortsjö (1951) and Healey and Schroy (1953), who used the biliary system as the main guide for division of the liver (Fig. 66.4). All models use the branching pattern of either the portal and hepatic veins (the ‘French’ model) or the bile ducts (the ‘American’ model) as segmentation criteria, but differ in their nomenclature (Terminology Committee of the International Hepato-Pancreato-Biliary Association 2000, Strasberg 2005, Bismuth 2013, Strasberg and Phillips 2013, Makuuchi 2013, Majno et al 2014, Hikspoors et al 2017).
The liver is divided into four portal sectors (right anterior, right posterior, left medial and left lateral) by the four main branches of the hepatic portal vein. The three main hepatic veins lie between these sectors as intersectoral veins. The intersectoral planes are also called portal fissures (or scissures). Each sector is subdivided into segments (usually two) on the basis of their supply by tertiary divisions of the perivascular fibrous capsules (Glissonian sheaths) (see below).

Fissures and sulci of the liver

Knowledge of the fissures of the liver is essential for understanding liver surgery. Three major portal fissures, main, left and right portal fissures, not visible on the surface, run through the liver parenchyma and contain the three main hepatic veins. Three minor fissures, umbilical, venous and fissure of Gans (Rouvière’s sulcus, incisura hepatis dextra) are visible as physical clefts in the liver surface. Accessory fissures are rare. However, diaphragmatic sulci are seen in approximately 40% of livers and usually on the superior surface of the right lobe (Macchi et al 2003).

Main portal fissure

The main portal fissure (Cantlie’s line) extends from the midpoint of the fossa of the gallbladder at the inferior margin of the liver back to the midpoint of the inferior vena cava. It contains the middle hepatic vein and separates the liver into right and left hemilivers. Segments V and VIII lie immediately to the right, and segment IV immediately to the left, of the main portal fissure.

Left portal fissure

The left portal fissure divides the left hemiliver into left medial and left lateral sectors. It extends from the midpoint of the inferior edge of the liver between the falciform ligament and the left triangular ligament to the point that marks the confluence of the left and middle hepatic veins. It contains the left hepatic vein and separates the left medial (anterior) and left lateral (posterior) sectors; segment III lies immediately anteriorly and segment II posteriorly.

Right portal fissure

The right portal fissure divides the right hemiliver into right posterior and right anterior sectors. The plane of the right portal fissure is the most variable of the portal fissures: it runs approximately diagonally through the anatomical right lobe from the lateral end of the inferior border to the termination of the right hepatic vein. The portal fissure divides the right anterior sector (segments V and VIII) from the right posterior sector (segments VI and VII) and contains the right hepatic vein. It traverses the thickest portion of liver parenchyma that is commonly transected during liver resection.

Umbilical fissure

The umbilical fissure (fissure for the round ligament of the liver) separates segments II and III from segment IV and contains a major branch of the left hepatic vein (the umbilical fissure vein). It is marked anteriorly by the attachment of the falciform ligament and inferiorly by the round ligament of the liver, where it may be covered by a bridge of liver tissue extending between segments III and IV. When present, this bridge is usually avascular and can be divided safely with diathermy during surgery. The umbilical fissure also contains the umbilical portion of the left branch of the hepatic portal vein, segmental bile ducts converging to form the left hepatic duct, and the terminal branches of the left hepatic artery. The umbilical portion of the left branch of the hepatic portal vein offers direct surgical access to the left branch of the hepatic portal vein during surgery for cholangiocarcinoma of the porta hepatis and in operations to restore intrahepatic portal blood flow after hepatic portal vein occlusion. Knowledge of the arrangement of the vascular and biliary structures within the umbilical fissure is also essential when the liver is split for an adult and paediatric recipient and for live donor liver transplantation for a child recipient.

Venous fissure

The fissure for the ligamentum venosum (venous fissure) is directly continuous with the umbilical fissure on the undersurface of the liver. It contains the ligamentum venosum (the obliterated ductus venosus) and lies between the caudate lobe and segment II.

Fissure of Gans

The fissure of Gans (Rouvière’s sulcus) lies on the undersurface of the right lobe of the liver posterior to the fossa of the gallbladder and anterior to segment I. It occurs in over 80% of normal livers, often marks the variable site of division of the portal pedicle to the right posterior sector and is a useful landmark during laparoscopic cholecystectomy (Singh and Prasad 2017, Lockhart and Singh-Ranger 2018).

Sectors and segments of the liver

Sectors
Each sector of the liver contains between one and three segments: the right posterior sector contains segments VI and VII; the right anterior sector contains segments V and VIII; the left medial sector contains segment IV (and part of I); the left lateral sector contains segments II and III (Fig. 66.5). The segments are numbered clockwise from below, starting with segment I and ending with segment VIII (see Figs 66.4–66.5; Fig. 66.6).

Segment I

Segment I corresponds to the anatomical caudate lobe and additional adjacent parenchyma supplied by branches of the hepatic portal vein at the porta hepatis and arterial branches that arise from the left and/or the right hepatic arteries. Segment I lies posterior to segment IV; the caudate process extends to the left, posterior to the hepatogastric ligament. The segment straddles the inferior vena cava, with its left half directly adjacent to segment II and its right half wrapped to a variable extent around the retrohepatic portion of the inferior vena cava. The segment that wraps around the inferior vena cava most often includes a larger vein that drains directly into the inferior vena cava. In the European literature, segment I is subdivided into three parts, the caudate process, caudate lobe (Spiegel’s lobe) and paracaval portion; right and left subdivisions are recognized in Asia. The perivascular fibrous capsules to segment I arise from both right and left main capsules, which means that the segment receives vessels from both the left and right branches of the hepatic portal vein and hepatic arteries. The venous drainage of the caudate lobe is directly into the inferior vena cava by multiple small tributaries that usually arise from the lower third of the segment, occasionally from the middle third, but rarely from the upper third. The bile ducts draining the segment are closely related to the confluence of the right and left hepatic ducts (Ch. 67), such that excision of bile duct tumours affecting the porta hepatis of the liver usually requires removal of segment I.

Segment II

Segment II lies posterolateral to the left portal fissure, often has only one perivascular fibrous capsule, and drains into the left hepatic vein. Rarely, a separate vein drains directly into the inferior vena cava.

Segment III

Segment III lies between the umbilical fissure and the left portal fissure. It is supplied by one to three perivascular fibrous capsules and drains into the left hepatic vein: the veins of the falciform ligament can provide an alternative drainage route. Very rarely, the segment drains to the middle hepatic vein; when this occurs, reconstruction is required following a right hepatectomy involving resection of the middle hepatic vein and in split liver transplantation (Dar et al 2008).

Segment IV

Segment IV lies between the umbilical fissure and the main portal fissure, immediately anterior to segment I. It is supplied by three to five perivascular fibrous capsules, most of which arise in the umbilical fissure; their origins are often close to the fibrous capsules that supply segments II and III. Occasionally, segment IV is supplied by branches from the main left pedicle. Segment IV is typically divided into IVa superiorly and IVb inferiorly by two separate perivascular fibrous capsules. This subdivision is relevant to transverse hepatectomy, in which segments IVb, V and VI are removed along a transverse portal plane (Sugarbaker 1990). Segment IV usually drains into the middle hepatic vein, but it can also drain into the left hepatic vein through veins of the falciform ligament.

Segment V

Segment V is the inferior segment of the right anterior sector and lies between the middle and the right hepatic veins. Its size is variable, as are the number of perivascular fibrous capsules that supply it from the right anterior capsule. Venous drainage is usually into the right and middle hepatic veins but can be directly into the inferior vena cava via an inferior right hepatic vein.

Segment VI

Segment VI forms the inferior part of the right posterior sector, which is posterior to the right portal fissure. It is often supplied by two to three branches from the right posterior perivascular fibrous capsule. Occasionally, the perivascular fibrous capsule to segment VI arises directly from the right pedicle. Venous drainage is normally into the right hepatic vein but, as in segment V, can be directly into the inferior vena cava via an inferior right hepatic vein.

Segment VII

Segment VII forms the superior part of the right posterior sector and lies posterior to the right hepatic vein. The sheath to segment VII is often single. The venous drainage is into the right hepatic vein; occasionally, the segment drains directly into the inferior vena cava.

Segment VIII

Segment VIII is the superior part of the right anterior sector. The right anterior sectoral sheath ends in segment VIII and supplies it, after giving off branches to segment V. Venous drainage is to the right and middle hepatic veins.

Peritoneal attachments and ligaments of the liver

The liver is attached to the anterior abdominal wall, diaphragm and other viscera by several peritoneal ligaments.

Falciform ligament and round ligament of the liver

The liver is attached to the anterior abdominal wall by the falciform ligament, derived from the ventral mesogastrium in the embryo. The two layers of this ligament pass posteriorly and slightly to the right from the posterior surface of the anterior abdominal wall and the undersurface of the diaphragm in the midline, and are attached to the anterior and superior surfaces of the liver. On the most superior surface, the right leaf runs laterally and is continuous with the superior layer of the coronary ligament. The left layer of the falciform ligament turns medially and is continuous with the anterior layer of the left triangular ligament. The round ligament of the liver is the obliterated remnant of the left umbilical vein of the fetus; it runs in the inferior free border of the falciform ligament and continues into a fissure on the inferior surface of the liver. In fetal life, the left umbilical vein opens into the left branch of the hepatic portal vein and usually becomes obliterated soon after birth. However, it sometimes remains partially patent such that it can be dissected at the umbilicus, dilated and used to access the left branch of the hepatic portal vein in some individuals. Typically, the round ligament of the liver reopens in the setting of portal hypertension caused by cirrhosis of the liver, and it can become a sizeable collateral channel to the anterior abdominal wall.
The round ligament of the liver is important in abdominal surgery for several reasons. It is often divided in upper abdominal surgery to optimize access to the upper abdominal viscera or as the first step in mobilizing the liver. It is vascularized by numerous small arterial branches (mainly from the artery to segment IV) that anastomose with branches of the superior epigastric artery; it is therefore important to ligate or coagulate the ligament during its division. The round ligament of the liver is a guide to the segment III hepatic duct in hepaticojejunostomy formation, and to the left branch of the hepatic portal vein lying in the umbilical fissure during mesenterico-portal vein bypass (Vellar et al 1998, di Francesco et al 2014).

Coronary ligament

The coronary ligament is formed by the reflection of the peritoneum from the respiratory diaphragm onto the superior and posterior surfaces of the right lobe of the liver. A large triangular area of liver devoid of peritoneal covering, the so-called ‘bare area’, lies between the two layers of the coronary ligament. Here, the liver is attached to the diaphragm by loose connective tissue that is continuous inferiorly with the ‘anterior pararenal space’. On the right, the two layers of the coronary ligament converge laterally to form the right triangular ligament. On the left, the two layers become closely applied and form the left triangular ligament. The superior layer of the coronary ligament is reflected superiorly onto the inferior surface of the respiratory diaphragm and inferiorly onto the right and superior surfaces of the liver. The inferior layer of the coronary ligament is reflected inferiorly over the right suprarenal gland and right kidney and superiorly onto the inferior surface of the liver. Surgical division of the right triangular and coronary ligaments allows the right lobe of the liver to be rotated anteriorly and to the left to expose the lateral aspect of the inferior vena cava posterior to the liver.

Triangular ligaments

The left triangular ligament is a double layer of peritoneum that extends for a variable length over the superior border of the left lobe of the liver. Medially, the anterior leaf is continuous with the left layer of the falciform ligament, and the posterior layer is continuous with the left layer of the lesser omentum. The left triangular ligament lies in front of the abdominal part of the oesophagus and part of the fundus of the stomach: its division allows the left lobe of the liver to be mobilized for exposure of the abdominal part of the oesophagus and crura of the respiratory diaphragm. However, the left triangular ligament is also an important stabilizing factor for the left lobe after excision of the right lobe of the liver: its division destabilizes the left lobe so that it can rotate and displace into the space created under the right half of the diaphragm, compromising the venous outflow of the left lobe and/or portal venous inflow to the left lobe, with consequent liver dysfunction. If the left triangular ligament is divided, the left lobe should be stabilized by reattaching the falciform ligament to the anterior abdominal wall.
The right triangular ligament is a short structure that lies at the right lateral limit of the ‘bare area’ of the liver and represents the convergence laterally of the two layers of the coronary ligament.

Lesser omentum

The lesser omentum is a double layer of peritoneum that extends from the lesser curvature of the stomach and proximal duodenum to the inferior surface of the liver. Its free margin contains the portal triad (Ch. 62). Its attachment to the inferior surface of the liver is L-shaped. The vertical component follows the line of the fissure for the ligamentum venosum, the fibrous remnant of the ductus venosus. More inferiorly, the attachment runs horizontally to complete the L-shape in the porta hepatis. At its superior end, the left layer of the lesser omentum is continuous with the posterior layer of the left triangular ligament, and the right layer is continuous with the coronary ligament as it encloses the inferior vena cava. At its inferior end, the two layers of the lesser omentum diverge to surround the structures of the porta hepatis. A thin, fibrous condensation of fascia usually runs from the medial end of the porta hepatis into the fissure for the round ligament of the liver. This fascial condensation is continuous with the lower border of the falciform ligament where the round ligament of the liver re-emerges at the inferior border of the liver. Care should be taken when dividing the lesser omentum because it may contain a replaced or accessory left hepatic artery; when present, this invariably extends to the liver at the base of the umbilical fissure and can be identified by a pulsation in the lesser omentum close to that fissure.

Ligamentum venosum

The ligamentum venosum represents the obliterated venous connection that existed in the fetus between the left branch of the hepatic portal vein and the termination of the left hepatic vein (the ductus venosus). It is used as a guide to gain control of the left hepatic vein outside the liver during surgery. By dividing the ligament close to its insertion into the left hepatic vein and retracting it laterally, the angle between the left and the middle hepatic veins can be accessed.
This manoeuvre can be helpful in left liver resection with preservation of the caudate lobe, but if the caudate lobe is to be removed, the left hepatic vein (or more usually the confluence of the left and middle hepatic veins with the inferior vena cava) can be approached more posteriorly without division of the ligamentum venosum.

Porta hepatis, hepatoduodenal ligament and hilar plate

The porta hepatis is a deep transverse fissure on the inferior surface of the liver, between the quadrate lobe anteriorly and the caudate process posteriorly. It contains the hepatic portal vein, hepatic artery and hepatic nervous plexuses as they ascend into the parenchyma of the liver, and the right and left hepatic ducts and some lymph vessels that emerge from the liver. The hepatic ducts usually lie anterior to the hepatic portal vein and its branches, and the hepatic artery with its branches lies between the two (Figs 66.7–66.8). However, the right hepatic artery sometimes lies anterior to the common hepatic duct, a variation that is important during bile duct reconstruction by hepaticojejunostomy (Michels 1966). All these structures are enveloped within a sheath of loose connective tissue (perivascular fibrous capsule) that surrounds the vessels and bile ducts as they course through the liver parenchyma and is continuous with the fibrous capsule. The dense aggregation of vessels, supporting connective tissue and liver parenchyma just superior to the porta hepatis is often referred to as the ‘hilar plate’ of the liver (Kawarada et al 2000). Understanding the concept of the hilar plate is important in surgical approaches to the hilar structures, and division or lowering of the hilar plate is essential for optimum surgical access to the left hepatic duct.
The hepatic artery, bile duct and hepatic portal vein extend between the porta hepatis and the superior border of the duodenum in the free edge of the hepatoduodenal ligament, which forms the anterior boundary of the omental foramen (of Winslow). Rapid control of the vessels entering the porta hepatis (the hepatic pedicle) can be obtained by compressing them in the free edge of the lesser omentum (a ‘Pringle’ manoeuvre); this is conveniently done by dividing the lesser omentum immediately to the left of these structures and passing a tape around them through the omental foramen.
The left hepatic duct is extrahepatic as it descends obliquely along the undersurface of segment IV (the quadrate lobe) to the confluence of the hepatic ducts. Access to this extrahepatic segment of the left hepatic duct is particularly useful during a biliary-enteric bypass procedure to treat a stricture of the hepatic duct confluence. The right hepatic duct is more intrahepatic and extension of a hepaticojejunal anastomosis on to the right hepatic duct necessitates incision of the liver in the fossa for the gallbladder.

Perivascular fibrous capsules

The fibrous capsule of the liver (Glisson’s capsule) becomes condensed as the perivascular fibrous capsules (Glissonian sheaths) around the branches of the portal triad structures as they enter the liver and subdivide into segmental branches (Kuntz and Kuntz 2008). Thus, each bile duct, hepatic artery and hepatic portal vein is surrounded by a single fibrous capsule. Within each capsule, the hepatic portal vein is surrounded by loose areolar connective tissue, making dissection of the hepatic portal vein relatively easy. The fibrous tissue around the bile ducts and hepatic artery is tougher so dissection of these structures is more difficult. This capsular arrangement facilitates surgical control of the right and left vasculobiliary pedicles of the liver, as well as sectoral and segmental divisions in complex liver resections (Yamamoto et al 2012).

Vascular supply and lymphatic drainage

The hepatic artery, hepatic portal vein and hepatic veins supply or drain the liver. The portal vein and hepatic artery ascend in the lesser omentum to the porta hepatis, where each usually bifurcates. The common hepatic duct and lymphatic vessels descend from the porta hepatis alongside the hepatic portal vein and hepatic artery (see Fig. 66.8). The hepatic veins leave the liver via its posterior surface and run directly into the inferior vena cava.

Hepatic artery

During fetal and early postnatal life the common hepatic artery is the largest branch of the coeliac trunk, whereas in adults it is intermediate in size between the left gastric and splenic arteries. The common hepatic artery (Fig. 66.9) gives off the right gastric and gastroduodenal arteries (Fig. 66.9A). After originating from the coeliac trunk, it passes anteriorly and laterally, above the superior border of the pancreas, to the superior aspect of the first part of the duodenum. It is subdivided into the common hepatic artery, from the coeliac trunk to the origin of the gastroduodenal artery, and the proper hepatic artery, from that point to its bifurcation. Both parts ascend anterior to the hepatic portal vein and medial to the bile duct within the free margin of the hepatoduodenal ligament in the anterior wall of the omental foramen. The left and right hepatic arteries arise at a variable level below the porta hepatis. The right hepatic artery typically crosses posterior (occasionally anterior) to the common hepatic duct (Fig. 66.9B). Its usual course is toward the common hepatic duct: it bends inferiorly before crossing posterior to the duct and then bends back up toward the right hepatic duct lateral to the common hepatic duct and hepatic portal vein. Its close proximity to the bifurcation of the bile duct means that it is often involved or encased by bile duct cancers that arise at the bifurcation (hilar cholangiocarcinoma). The right hepatic artery is more frequently involved than the left hepatic artery in bile duct cancers of the porta hepatis. Occasionally, the right hepatic artery crosses anterior to the common hepatic duct and is more vulnerable to injury during biliary surgery. It almost always divides into an anterior branch supplying segments V and VIII and a posterior branch supplying segments VI and VII. The anterior division also often supplies a branch to segment I and the gallbladder. The proper hepatic artery sometimes divides at a more proximal level close to its origin; occasionally it divides distally, well to the left of the porta hepatis. The main significance of a proximal division is that the right hepatic artery can pass posterior to the hepatic portal vein (Launois and Jamieson 1993). The segmental arteries of the liver are macroscopically end arteries, although some collateral circulation occurs between segments via fine terminal branches.
Anatomical variants of the normal arrangement of the hepatic arteries are found in about one-third of individuals and are important to recognize because they are relevant to surgical and interventional radiological procedures (Michels 1966, Covey et al 2002, López-Andújar et al 2007, Saba and Mallarini 2011). An artery that supplies a part of the liver in addition to the normal artery supplying that part is defined as an accessory artery. A replaced hepatic artery is an artery that does not originate from an orthodox position and provides the sole supply to that part of the liver.
A replaced proper hepatic artery can arise from the superior mesenteric artery (Fig. 66.10) and can be suspected at surgery by a relatively superficial hepatic portal vein (reflecting the absence of a hepatic artery that would normally ascend in front of the vein). More commonly, a replaced or accessory right hepatic artery arises from the superior mesenteric artery (see Fig. 66.10; Fig. 66.11). In such cases, the variant artery runs in the lesser omentum posterior to the hepatic portal vein and bile duct and can usually be identified at surgery by palpable pulsation posterior to the hepatic portal vein. An accessory right hepatic artery can be injured during resection of the pancreatic head because it lies close to the hepatic portal vein. Occasionally, a replaced or accessory left hepatic artery arises from the left gastric artery, entering the liver through the umbilical fissure; this artery provides a source of collateral supply in cases where the arteries at the porta hepatis are occluded, but it can also be injured during mobilization of the stomach as it lies in the superior portion of the lesser omentum. Rarely, an accessory left or right hepatic artery arises from the gastroduodenal artery or directly from the aorta. The presence of replaced arteries can be life-saving in patients with bile duct cancer; because they are further away from the bile duct they tend to be spared from infiltration by the cancer, making excision of the tumour feasible. Knowledge of these variations is also essential when whole and split liver transplantation is performed, and their presence often requires reconstruction.
The most common anatomical variants are a replaced or accessory left hepatic artery that arises from the left gastric artery, or a replaced or accessory right hepatic artery that arises from the superior mesenteric artery, both occurring in 10–20% of individuals.
Variations in the intrahepatic arteries are common and can be important surgically. For example, the segment IV artery most commonly arises from the left hepatic artery, but in up to 30% of cases it arises from the right hepatic artery or the proper hepatic artery (Onishi et al 2000). The segment IV artery never arises to the right of the common hepatic duct, which means that if the right hepatic artery is divided to the right of the common hepatic duct, the arterial supply to segment IV is not endangered. Failure to recognize this variation can compromise the blood supply to segment IV following right hepatectomy, and it is especially important during right liver donation for live donor liver transplantation.

Veins

The liver has two venous systems. The portal venous system conveys blood from the majority of the gastrointestinal tract and its associated organs to the liver. The hepatic venous system drains blood from the liver parenchyma into the inferior vena cava.
Hepatic portal vein
The hepatic portal vein is formed posterior to the neck of the pancreas, usually from the convergence of the superior mesenteric and splenic veins (Fig. 66.12; see also Fig. 59.7). Its origin lies in the transpyloric plane between the inferior border of the body of the first lumbar vertebra and the superior border of the body of the second lumbar vertebra (Mirjalili et al 2012). The hepatic portal vein is approximately 8 cm long and ascends obliquely to the right, posterior to the superior (first) part of the duodenum, bile duct and gastroduodenal artery, and anterior to the inferior vena cava. It enters the hepatoduodenal ligament and ascends anterior to the omental foramen to reach the right end of the porta hepatis, where it divides into right and left main branches that enter the liver. In the lesser omentum, the hepatic portal vein lies posterior to both the bile duct and the hepatic artery. It is surrounded by the hepatic nervous plexus and accompanied by numerous lymphatics and some lymph nodes. The hepatic portal vein contains smooth muscle in its wall and, in experimental animals at least, has well-developed spontaneous contractions with frequencies between 0.01 and 1 Hz (Burt 2003). It is typically valveless.
The main extrahepatic tributaries of the hepatic portal vein are the left gastric vein, which ends in the left margin of the hepatic portal vein, and the posterior superior pancreaticoduodenal vein near the head of the pancreas. The hepatic portal vein divides into right and left branches at the porta hepatis of the liver (Fig. 66.13). The left branch of the hepatic portal vein has a longer extrahepatic course (4–5 cm) than the right branch of the hepatic portal vein, tends to lie more horizontally, and is often smaller in calibre. The horizontal portion runs along the inferior surface of segment IV and invariably gives branches to segment I and sometimes to segment IV. The left branch of the hepatic portal vein continues within the liver, giving off a segment II branch laterally before taking a more anterior and vertical course in the umbilical fissure, where it gives off branches to segments III and IV and receives the obliterated left umbilical vein (round ligament of the liver). Most of the portal venous supply to segment IV comes from the left branch of the hepatic portal vein and only occasionally from either the right branch of the hepatic portal vein or its branches to segments V or VIII. The right branch of the hepatic portal vein is only 2–3 cm in length and usually divides into a right medial (anterior) sectoral division supplying segments V and VIII, and a right lateral (posterior) sectoral division supplying segments VI and VII. The medial division can give a branch to segment I.
Hepatic portal vein variations usually involve the right branch (Covey et al 2004). When this branch is absent (10–15% of livers), the hepatic portal vein usually trifurcates into left portal, right anterior and right posterior sectoral veins. Absence of the right branch of the hepatic portal vein might be considered as a relative contraindication for split liver and live donor liver transplantations because this variation is often associated with a similar variation in biliary anatomy. The right anterior sectoral portal vein can arise from the hepatic portal vein, or the right posterior sectoral portal vein can originate from the left branch of the hepatic portal vein, variations that are important to remember during left-sided liver resection. When the bifurcation of the hepatic portal vein is absent, the hepatic portal vein enters the liver, giving off the right sectoral branches, and then turns left to supply the left lobe, a variation that presents an added complexity in major liver surgery (Chaib 2009).
Porto-systemic shunts
Increased pressure within the portal venous system can result in dilation of portal venous tributaries and reversed flow at sites of porto-systemic anastomoses. Common sites of porto-systemic shunts are listed in Table 66.1, and see also Sharma and Rameshbabu (2012).
Hepatic veins
The liver drains by three major hepatic veins into the suprahepatic part of the inferior vena cava and via numerous minor hepatic veins that drain into the retrohepatic inferior vena cava. The adult retrohepatic inferior vena cava is 6–7 cm long and is surrounded to a variable extent by segment I (Camargo et al 1996). The three major veins are located between the four sectors of the liver (see Fig. 66.6A; Figs 66.14–66.15). Thus, the right hepatic vein lies between the right anterior and posterior sectors, the middle hepatic vein lies between the right and left hemilivers, and the left hepatic vein lies between the left medial and lateral sectors. During hepatic resection, the surgeon should transect the liver parenchyma slightly to the left or right of the particular fissure that is being opened to avoid the main trunk of a hepatic vein.
Right hepatic vein
The right hepatic vein is the longest and largest hepatic vein. It is usually single, but occasionally remains as two trunks until it terminates by draining into the inferior vena cava. It runs in the right portal fissure between the right medial and lateral sectors. It drains the whole of segments VI and VII and variable proportions of segments V and VIII, depending on the extent to which these segments drain into the middle hepatic vein. The right hepatic vein is formed anteriorly near the inferior border of the liver and lies in a coronal plane through most of its course. It drains into the inferior vena cava near the superior border of the caudate lobe. Of the three major hepatic veins, the right hepatic vein is most variable in calibre, reflecting the variable contribution of the middle hepatic vein to the drainage of segments V and VIII and the existence of an accessory right inferior (30%) and/or middle hepatic vein (10%) (Fang et al 2012). The hepatic venous anatomy is particularly important in right lobe living donation and can be assessed preoperatively by computed tomography (CT) or magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), and/or by intraoperative ultrasound.
Middle hepatic vein
The middle hepatic vein lies in the main portal fissure between the right and left hemilivers. It usually joins the left hepatic vein and terminates in the inferior vena cava as a short common trunk; it ends as a single trunk in the inferior vena cava in fewer than 10% of individuals. The middle hepatic vein drains the central part of the liver and receives constant tributaries from segments IV, V and VIII. The vein from segment IV lies in a sagittal plane and enters the middle hepatic vein on its left side. The vein from segment VIII runs transversely into the right side of the middle hepatic vein and is sometimes large enough to be mistaken for the middle hepatic vein. Anteriorly, the middle hepatic vein drains some of segment V; the calibres of the tributaries draining segments V and VIII are variable. Intrahepatic venous anastomoses between the middle and right hepatic veins, particularly in segment VIII, have been reported in up to one-third of adult livers (Hribernik and Trotovšek 2014).

Left hepatic vein
The left hepatic vein lies between the left medial and left lateral sectors of the liver and drains segments II, III and occasionally IV. Small veins draining segment II and occasionally the superior part of segment IV, can drain directly into the inferior vena cava. The umbilical fissure vein, a major tributary of the left hepatic vein, usually runs between segments III and IV and contributes to their drainage. Occasionally, the vein draining segment III ends separately in the confluence of the left and middle hepatic veins. These variations in venous drainage are significant in split liver transplantation and live donor liver transplantation.
Minor hepatic veins
Segment I veins, ranging in number from one to five, drain directly into the inferior vena cava. Since this segment has a venous drainage independent of the rest of the liver, segment I often continues to drain effectively and undergoes compensatory hypertrophy in patients with Budd–Chiari syndrome, where the major hepatic veins are obstructed. There can be an accessory inferior or middle right hepatic vein, and several smaller ‘retrohepatic’ veins that drain the right lobe directly into the inferior vena cava. When present, these veins are surgically important, especially if they exceed 5 mm in diameter. They drain segments V and VI independently of the three major hepatic veins, which means that a tumour involving the latter can be resected safely as long as venous drainage from the accessory veins is preserved. In live donor and split liver transplantations, larger accessory veins must be individually anastomosed to the recipient inferior vena cava to ensure adequate venous drainage.
Transjugular intrahepatic porto-systemic shunt (TIPS) procedure for portal hypertension
In cases of extreme chronic portal hypertension a large calibre anastomosis between the portal and systemic circulations can be created within the liver parenchyma by inserting a stent between a large hepatic portal vein and a hepatic vein within the liver. The stent is introduced through a catheter inserted into the internal jugular vein and guided into the liver under radiological control (Siramolpiwat 2014). This large shunt relieves the severe portal hypertension but tends to worsen hepatic encephalopathy.

Lymphatic drainage

Lymph from the liver is rich in protein and is mostly a product of the hepatic sinusoids (Ohtani and Ohtani 2008). It passes via deep and superficial pathways to nodes superior and inferior to the respiratory diaphragm (Trutmann and Sasse 1994). Obstruction of hepatic venous drainage increases the flow of lymph in the thoracic duct.
Superficial hepatic lymphatics
Superficial lymphatics run in subserosal areolar tissue over the surface of the liver and drain in four directions. Lymphatics from most of the posterior surface, including the caudate lobe, drain into nodes alongside the inferior vena cava; a few lymphatics from the posterior surface of the left lobe pass towards the oesophageal hiatus and nodes around the cardia of the stomach. Lymphatics in the coronary and right triangular ligaments can pass directly to the thoracic duct. Lymphatics from most of the inferior, anterior and superior surfaces drain into hepatic nodes at the porta hepatis. A few lymphatics from the right superior surface accompany the inferior phrenic artery across the right crus of the respiratory diaphragm to drain into coeliac nodes.
Deep hepatic lymphatics
Fine lymphatics within the portal triads and around interlobular veins merge to form larger vessels. Some ascend through the parenchyma to pass through the caval foramen of the respiratory diaphragm and drain into nodes located in the inferior mediastinum, but most drain to lymph nodes at the porta hepatis.

Innervation

The parenchyma of the liver is supplied by nerves arising from the hepatic plexus that all enter the liver at the porta hepatis. Some nerve fibres also appear to arise from the right phrenic nerve and this might be the basis of referred shoulder pain in patients with biliary colic (Skandalakis et al 2004). The fibrous capsule of the liver is supplied by fine branches of the inferior intercostal nerves that also supply the parietal peritoneum, particularly around the ‘bare area’ and superior surface of the liver: distension or disruption of the fibrous capsule causes well-localized sharp pain.

Hepatic plexus

The hepatic plexus receives preganglionic parasympathetic fibres from the anterior and, to a lesser extent, the posterior vagal trunk, and postganglionic sympathetic fibres via the coeliac and superior mesenteric plexuses. Nerve fibres accompany the branches of the portal triad into the liver, penetrating as far as the hepatocytes. Aminergic, cholinergic, peptidergic and nitrergic nerve fibres have been identified and appear to be involved in hepatic metabolism and the control of sinusoidal blood flow (McCuskey 2004): the transplanted, denervated liver indicates that this role is not essential. Multiple fine branches from the plexus supply the extrahepatic bile ducts and gallbladder; parasympathetic fibres are motor to the smooth muscle of the bile ducts and gallbladder and inhibitory to the sphincter of the bile duct. Branches from the plexus also run inferiorly with the right gastric artery to contribute to the supply of the pylorus; with the gastroduodenal artery and its branches to reach the pylorus, proximal duodenum and pancreas; and with the right gastro-omental (gastro-epiploic) artery to provide a small contribution to the nerve supply of the stomach.
Referred pain
Pain arising from the parenchyma of the liver is poorly localized. In common with other structures of foregut origin, pain is referred to the epigastric region. Stretch or irritation of the fibrous capsule of the liver by inflammation or neoplasia produces well-localized ‘somatic’ pain. Pathology involving the diaphragmatic surface of the liver can be referred via the phrenic nerve to the right shoulder region (C3, C4, C5 dermatomes).

Microstructure

The liver is essentially an epithelial-mesenchymal outgrowth of the caudal part of the foregut, with which it remains connected via the biliary tree (Ch. 59). Most of the surface of the liver is covered by a typical serosa, the visceral peritoneum. Beneath this, and enclosing the whole organ, is a thin (50–100 μm) capsule of connective tissue, from which extensions pass into the liver as septa and trabeculae. Branches of the hepatic artery and hepatic portal vein, together with bile ductules and ducts, run within these connective tissue trabeculae (portal tracts). The combination of two types of blood vessel and a bile duct is termed a portal triad, and is usually accompanied by one or more lymphatic vessels and nerves (Fig. 66.16).
The liver parenchyma consists of a complex network of epithelial cells, supported by connective tissue and perfused by a rich blood supply from the portal vein and hepatic artery. The epithelial cells, hepatocytes, carry out the major metabolic activities, but additional cell types have storage, phagocytic and mechanically supportive functions. In the mature liver, hepatocytes are arranged mainly in plates (or cords when seen in two-dimensional sections) that are usually only one cell thick, separated by venous sinusoids that anastomose with each other via gaps in the plates (Fig. 66.17).
Bile is secreted by hepatocytes and is collected in a network of minute channels (canaliculi) between adjacent hepatocyte plasma membranes. Hepatocytes can therefore be regarded as exocrine cells, secreting bile into the gastrointestinal tract via the extrahepatic bile ducts. Their other metabolic functions involve complex biochemical exchanges with the blood.
The fetal liver is a major haemopoietic organ; erythrocytes, leukocytes and platelets develop from mesenchyme covering the sinusoidal endothelium.

Lobulation of the liver

The structural unit of the liver is the lobule: a roughly hexagonal arrangement of plates of hepatocytes, separated by intervening sinusoids that radiate outwards from a central vein, with portal triads at the vertices of each hexagon (Fig. 66.18; see Fig. 66.16). The central veins drain into the hepatic veins. In some species the classic lobular units are delimited microscopically by distinct connective tissue septa. However, the lobular organization of the human liver is not immediately evident in histological sections; the lobules do not have distinct boundaries, and connective tissue is sparse. The plates do not pass straight to the periphery of a lobule like the spokes of a wheel but run irregularly as they anastomose and branch.
Detailed studies of human liver, using three dimensional reconstruction and morphometric analyses combined with histopathological observations, have revealed a highly ordered arrangement of functional units, the liver acini. Each acinus is an approximately ovoid mass of tissue, orientated around the terminal branches of a hepatic arteriole and portal venule, with its long axis defined by two adjacent central veins. It includes the hepatic tissue served by these afferent vessels and is bounded by adjacent acini. Acinar functional heterogeneity is related to blood supply, thus the functional capacities of hepatocytes in a periportal zone (zone 1) differ from those in an intermediate zone (zone 2) or in a perivenous (pericentral or centrilobular) zone (zone 3) (Kietzmann 2017) (see Fig. 66.16C).
The acinar definition of hepatic microarchitecture has clarified the interpretation of liver histopathology, particularly in relation to zones of hypoxic damage, glycogen deposition and removal, and toxic injury, all of which are related to the direction of blood flow.

Blood supply

Preterminal hepatic arterioles within the acini convey arterial blood to the sinusoids, mostly via a fine capillary plexus that drains to branches of the hepatic portal vein. A small proportion of arterial blood passes directly to the hepatic sinusoids, bypassing these capillary plexuses. Sinusoids thus contain mixed venous and arterial blood. Central veins from adjacent lobules form interlobular veins that unite as hepatic veins and drain to the inferior vena cava.

Hepatic plates (cords)

The endothelium of the sinusoids is fenestrated and lacks a continuous basal lamina, enabling the endothelial cells to act as a dynamic blood filter (Fraser et al 1995). The sinusoids are separated from the plates of hepatocytes by a narrow gap, the perisinusoidal space (of Disse), that is normally 0.2–0.5 μm wide but which distends in hypoxic states. The space contains interstitial fluid, the microvilli of adjacent hepatocytes, hepatic stellate cells, fine collagen fibres (mostly type III with some types I and IV), and occasional non-myelinated nerve terminals.
A network of minute, interconnecting biliary canaliculi (approximately 1 μm in diameter) runs between the hepatic plates. The canaliculi are formed by the apposed plasma membranes of adjacent hepatocytes, sealed by tight junctions that isolate the canalicular lumen (and bile) from adjacent intercellular space. Canaliculi conduct bile to the bile canals (of Hering), trough-like structures lined by cholangiocytes and hepatocytes within the liver lobules. Each canal collects bile from multiple canaliculi and empties into a bile ductule (lined by cholangiocytes) that drains into an interlobular bile duct within the portal tracts (Roskams et al 2004, Saxena and Theise 2004). The flow of bile is thus towards the periphery of the lobule, in the opposite direction to the centripetal blood flow.

Cells of the liver

Resident cells of the liver include hepatocytes, hepatic stellate cells (perisinusoidal cells; fat-storing cells; cells of Ito), sinusoidal endothelial cells, stellate macrophages (Kupffer cells), the epithelial cells of the biliary tree (cholangiocytes), hepatic stem cells, hepatic natural killer lymphocytes (pit cells) and connective tissue cells of the capsule and portal tracts (see Fig. 66.16B).

Hepatocytes

About 80% of the volume of the liver and 60% of its cellular population is made up of hepatocytes (parenchymal cells) (see Fig. 66.18). They are polyhedral, with 5–12 sides, and measure 20–30 μm across. The nuclei are round, euchromatic and often tetraploid, polyploid or multiple, with two or more in each cell. The cytoplasm typically contains a considerable amount of rough and smooth endoplasmic reticulum, many mitochondria, lysosomes and well-developed Golgi apparatus, all features indicating high metabolic activity. Glycogen granules and lipid vacuoles are usually prominent. Numerous large peroxisomes and vacuoles containing enzymes, e.g. urease in distinctive crystalline forms, indicate the complex metabolism of these cells. Their role in iron metabolism is reflected by the presence of storage vacuoles containing crystals of ferritin and haemosiderin.
The cell surface facing the sinusoids exhibits numerous microvilli, approximately 0.5 μm long, creating a large surface area (approximately 70% of the hepatocyte surface) exposed to blood plasma. Elsewhere, hepatocytes are linked by numerous gap junctions and desmosomes. The lateral plasma membranes of adjacent hepatocytes form microscopic channels, bile canaliculi, which are specialized regions of intercellular space formed by apposing grooves in the membranes and sealed from extraneous interstitial space by tight junctions. Four types of transmembrane molecules (occludins, claudins, junctional adhesion molecules and Coxsackie virus and adenovirus receptors) constitute the core units of the functional protein complexes of the tight junctions (Lee 2012) (see Fig. 1.27). Individual tight junction complexes, either alone or in combination, are involved in signalling pathways in health and disease. Numerous membrane-bound exocytotic vesicles cluster near the lumina of the canaliculi because the secretion of bile is targeted at the canalicular plasma membrane. The tight junctions surrounding the biliary canaliculi prevent bile from entering the interstitial fluid or blood plasma, thereby creating a blood–bile barrier.

Hepatic stellate cells

Hepatic stellate cells are much less numerous than hepatocytes. They lie within the perisinusoidal space between the sinusoids and the hepatocyte plates. Irregular in outline, they are characterized by numerous cytoplasmic lipid droplets in which the fat-soluble vitamin A is stored. They secrete most of the intralobular matrix components, including collagen type III (reticular) fibres and are a significant source of growth factors involved in liver homeostasis and regeneration. They play a major role in pathological processes (Yin et al 2013). In response to liver injury hepatic stellate cells become activated and predominantly myofibroblast-like. They are responsible for the replacement of damaged hepatocytes with collagenous scar tissue, hepatic fibrosis, seen initially in zone 3 around central veins. Fibrosis can progress to cirrhosis, where the parenchymal architecture and pattern of blood flow within the liver are both destroyed, with major systemic consequences.

Sinusoidal endothelial cells

Hepatic venous sinusoids are generally wider than blood capillaries and are lined by a thin but highly fenestrated endothelium that lacks a complete basal lamina. The endothelial cells are typically flattened, each with a central nucleus, and are joined to each other by junctional complexes. The fenestrae are grouped in clusters with a mean diameter of 100 nm, allowing plasma direct access to the basal plasma membranes of hepatocytes. Their cytoplasm contains numerous transcytotic vesicles.

Stellate macrophages

Stellate macrophages (Kupffer cells) of the liver are derived from circulating blood monocytes and originate in the bone marrow. They are long-term hepatic residents and lie within the sinusoidal lumina attached to the endothelial surface. They can be found in both the centrilobular and periportal zones but are typically more concentrated in the periportal zones: their function and structures differ depending on their location. They form the largest population of the mononuclear phagocyte system responsible for removing cellular and microbial debris from the circulation and for secreting cytokines involved in defence: they have been implicated in the pathogenesis of various hepatic diseases, including endotoxin tolerance, liver transplantation, nonalcoholic fatty liver disease, and alcoholic liver disease (Li et al 2017). They remove aged and damaged red blood cells from the hepatic circulation, a function normally shared with the spleen but fulfilled entirely by the liver after splenectomy.

Hepatic stem cells

Hepatic stem cells are undifferentiated, pluripotent cells and are understood to reside around the bile canals; they are derived from the ductal plate in fetal livers. Their progeny are small epithelial cells (8–18 μm) with large oval nuclei and scanty cytoplasm, expressing both biliary epithelial and hepatocyte markers (Roskams et al 2010).
